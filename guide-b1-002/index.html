Lab Note B1-002
Unity · 01 — Overview

Gain structure and the signal path, taught from zero

Between the moment a sound is made and the moment it reaches your ears, its volume is changed in about ten different places. This Lab Note names every one of those places, teaches you what to set it to, and gives you one word — unity — to hang the whole chain on. It is the slow companion to Lab 001 — Signal Path & Gain Staging.

The one principle

Principle

Every stage in the signal path either changes the level or leaves it alone. Set every stage you do not need to change to unity — change nothing — and then the only places the level moves are the places you chose. A chain you can read is a chain you can fix.

That is the spine of the whole note. Unity is the "I left this alone" setting. When everything you did not touch is at unity, a problem can only live in the one or two places you did touch — and that is what turns "it sounds bad somewhere" into "the input trim is too hot."

What you'll build

A clean signal path

  • One instrument routed source → interface → Live → group → master with every stage justified
  • Built on the MOTU M4 + Ableton Live 12, fed by the MicroFreak and Digitakt II
  • ≥ 6 dB of headroom at the master and no clip anywhere in the chain

The skills you'll practice

  • Setting the M4 input trim correctly — not too hot, not too cold
  • Knowing the difference between gain, volume, and level
  • Telling analog input clip apart from digital 0 dBFS overs
  • Keeping a working level around −12 dBFS so mastering has room

Why a level, not a sound

The beginner instinct

  • Record everything as loud as possible "so it's strong"
  • Fix a quiet track by pushing the fader, not the source
  • Treat the red light as a single warning, not as four different problems in four different places

The opposite discipline

  • Decide one working level and aim every stage at it
  • Leave deliberate room at the top — headroom is not wasted space
  • Fix level at the stage that owns it: trim at the interface, gain in the box, fader only for balance

The skill transfers to every track you ever make. A clean gain structure is invisible when it works and is the cause of half of all "my mix sounds amateur" problems when it does not. Get it right once, here, on one instrument, and you carry it into the whole workshop. Do less, but make each stage deliberate.

How to read this Lab Note

Work the pages in order. Listen trains your ear on what clean and broken gain sound like. Decisions is the theory — gain vs volume vs level, unity, headroom, clipping. Setup wires the M4 rig for clean gain. Palette hands you the five "moves" for changing level. The build is the long, exact, step-by-step from one source to the master. Reflection turns the result into one carry-forward rule and one proof gate.
