Unity · 05 — Palette

The five moves for changing level

There are exactly five places you change level in this chain, each with a job it is good at and a job it is wrong for. This page names all five, gives the starting number for each, and tells you when to reach for which. Use the right move for the right reason and the chain stays clean.

The reference numbers

The targets every move aims at
Working levelpeaks ≈ −12 dBFS
Single-part ceilingnever above −6 dBFS
Master headroom at the end≥ 6 dB below 0 dBFS
The hard wall0 dBFS — never touched while tracking or mixing
Unity0 dB of gain — "change nothing"
The order they appear in the path

Trim → clip gain → utility gain → fader → sends. They run left to right down the signal path. Each one owns a different job. The discipline is to fix a level at the earliest move that owns it, and leave the rest at unity.

Move 1 — the M4 input trim

Move 01

Gain at the boundary — set the recorded level

What it is for
  • The only move that sets the level actually recorded. Everything else just rebalances what the trim captured.
  • Turn it up until the loudest part of the source peaks around −12 dBFS on Live's input meter, and the M4's clip LED never lights.
  • Too cold (trim too low) and you'll be tempted to make it up downstream — that raises the noise floor. Too hot and you clip the converter permanently.
Trim — what each setting feels like
Clip LED flickersToo hot. Permanent damage on the peaks. Turn down now.
Peaks ≈ −12 dBFSCorrect. Healthy level, plenty of headroom, clip LED dark.
Peaks ≈ −40 dBFSToo cold. You'll amplify hiss to use it. Turn up at the trim.
  • The one rule: the trim is the only place you make a quiet source louder. If a recording is weak, fix it here, not at the fader.

Move 2 — clip gain

What it is for

  • A per-clip level offset, applied before the track's effects
  • Matching two takes recorded at slightly different levels
  • Trimming an already-recorded clip toward −12 without touching the fader

Starting value & when

  • Starts at 0 dB (unity) — change only with a reason
  • Use it to fix the recording you already have
  • Not for live balance — that's the fader's job

Move 3 — Utility gain

What it is for

  • A Utility device dropped on a track to add or remove gain anywhere in the effect chain
  • Driving the next effect harder or softer without moving the fader
  • Mono-ing a signal or controlling width while you're there

Starting value & when

  • Gain at 0 dB (unity) by default
  • Place one before a saturator to set how hard you drive it
  • Place one after to bring the level back to unity, so the effect changes tone, not loudness
  • The "trim in, trim out" trick. Utility before an effect to drive it, Utility after to restore the level — now you can A/B the effect honestly, because it isn't just louder. This is how you keep gain staging clean inside a track, not only at the input.

Move 4 — the track fader

What it is for

  • Volume out of the channel — balancing one part against another
  • Riding a level over time as automation
  • The last stage on a channel, after all effects

Starting value & when

  • Starts at 0 dB (unity)
  • Move it to balance parts, never to rescue a cold recording
  • If you're pulling every fader way down, the master is too hot upstream
The fader's one forbidden job

Never use the fader to make a too-quiet recording louder. Pushing the fader up amplifies the signal and its noise floor together — the hiss from the listen page. A quiet recording is fixed at the trim, upstream, where the noise hasn't been added yet.

Move 5 — send levels

What it is for

  • How much of a track is sent to a return (reverb, delay)
  • Setting the amount of effect without changing the dry track's level
  • Sharing one reverb across many tracks at different amounts

Starting value & when

  • Starts at −∞ (off) and is raised to taste
  • Pre- or post-fader changes whether the send follows the fader
  • Watch the return's own meter — reverb tails eat headroom too
  • Sends are gain too. A return channel sums every send into it and can clip just like any other. Reverb and delay tails pile up in your headroom — meter the returns, not just the tracks.
  • Post-fader by default. Post-fader means the send follows the track fader, so pulling a part down also pulls its reverb down — usually what you want.

The five moves, side by side

Which move owns which job
M4 trimSets the recorded level. The only place to make a source louder. Aim −12 dBFS.

The palette rule

Each move has one job. Set the recorded level at the trim; match clips with clip gain; drive effects with Utility; balance with the fader; add effect with sends. Reach for the wrong move — the fader to fix a cold take, the trim to balance a part — and you trade a clean problem for a noisy one.

Before the build
  • I can name all five moves and the one job each owns.
  • I know the trim is the only place to make a source louder.
  • I'd use clip gain to match takes, not the fader.
  • I'd trim into a saturator with Utility and trim back out after.
  • I'd never push a fader to rescue a cold recording.
  • I know sends feed returns that can clip, and I'd meter them too.
