Lab Note B1-002
Unity · 06 — The build

Build one clean gain structure, stage by stage

Now we touch the gear. We route one instrument from the oscillator all the way to the master, setting and justifying the level at every stage, then break the chain on purpose in four places to learn each fault's fingerprint. Every step is small and has a decision checkpoint. Keep the setup page and the palette open in other tabs.

Step 01

Open the template and start everything at unity

New set
  • ⌘ N. Save as unity_b1_002.als in a dedicated folder.
  • ⌘ , → Audio: device MOTU M4 in and out, sample rate 48000 Hz, buffer 256 samples.
  • Input Config: enable mono input 1 (MicroFreak) and stereo 3/4 (Digitakt). Rename them in plain language.
Confirm unity everywhere downstream
  • Create one audio track, ⌘ R to name it MF SOURCE. Set its In to Ext. In channel 1, Monitor In.
  • Track fader at 0 dB. Clip gain at 0 dB. Master fader at 0 dB. No effects, no Utility, no limiter.
  • Drop one metering plug-in on the master, set to show peaks. This is your dashboard for the whole build.
Decision check
  • Is every digital stage at 0 dB — fader, clip gain, master?
  • Is the master metering plug-in the only thing on the master, with no limiter?
  • Can I name what unity means and why I'm starting there?
Step 02

Stage one — the source and instrument output

Make a steady sound to gain-stage on
  • On the MicroFreak, choose a preset that sustains — a held pad or a long bass note — so the level is steady enough to read on a meter. A short pluck is hard to gain-stage on.
  • Set the MicroFreak's rear master volume to about 75 %. This is the instrument-output stage. It should be healthy but not maxed — leave the box a little room too.
  • Hold a middle-register note. Confirm the M4's input 1 meter on the front panel moves.
Why 75 % and not 100 %
  • A synth driven to its own maximum often distorts at the output before the interface even sees it. Around 75 % gives a strong, clean signal and leaves the interface trim room to do the fine setting.
  • This is the first place level is set — and already a decision you can justify: strong source, not maxed source.
Decision check
  • Does the M4 input 1 meter move when I hold the note?
  • Is the MicroFreak's own output strong but not maxed (≈ 75 %)?
  • Can I justify the source level in one sentence?
Step 03

Stage two — set the M4 input trim

The most important knob in the chain
  • Start the M4 input 1 trim fully down. Hold the loudest note you'll play.
  • Raise the trim slowly while watching Live's MF SOURCE input meter. Stop when the peaks sit around −12 dBFS.
  • Play your loudest, most aggressive note. The M4's clip LED must stay dark. If it flickers, you are clipping the converter — turn the trim down until it never lights.
The level is now set, once
  • The recording's level is now entirely owned by this one trim, with everything downstream at unity. That is the whole point — one place to set, one place to check.
  • Write the trim position and the peak reading in the gain ledger: M4 In 1 trim · peaks −12 dBFS · clip LED dark.
Decision check
  • Do my loudest notes peak around −12 dBFS in Live?
  • Does the M4 clip LED stay dark even on the hottest note?
  • Did I set this level at the trim and nowhere else?
Step 04

Record the source and confirm the noise floor

Print a take
  • Arm MF SOURCE, monitor Auto. ⌘ ⇧ R and record about 30 seconds of held notes with a few silent gaps between them.
  • Stop. Leave clip gain at 0 dB — the level should already be right from the trim, with nothing to make up.
Listen into the gaps
  • Solo the take and turn the phones up to your marked level. In the silent gaps, listen for hiss under the silence. With the trim set correctly, the gaps should be clean — that is a healthy noise floor.
  • This confirms the recording captured a strong signal so you never have to amplify it (and its noise) later.
Decision check
  • Are the silent gaps clean, or is there hiss I'd amplify if I pushed the fader?
  • Did I leave clip gain at unity because the level was already right?
  • Do the peaks of the recorded clip still sit near −12 dBFS?
Step 05

Stage three — gain inside the track with Utility

Trim in, trim out
  • Add a Saturator to MF SOURCE, then a Utility before it and another after it.
  • Use the first Utility's Gain to drive the saturator harder or softer. Use the second Utility's Gain to bring the level back down to unity afterward.
  • Now bypass and enable the saturator. Because the after-Utility restored the level, you hear the tone change, not just a loudness jump — an honest A/B.
Why this is gain staging, not mixing
  • Driving an effect is a gain decision: how hard the signal hits it changes the result. Setting that drive with Utility, and restoring the level after, keeps the channel's output at the level you intended.
  • If you skipped the after-Utility, the saturator's added harmonics would also push the level up — and you'd "fix" that at the fader, undoing the clean structure. Keep gain at the gain stage.
Decision check
  • When I A/B the saturator, do I hear tone change rather than just more loudness?
  • Is the channel's output level back near where it was before the effect?
  • Did I drive the effect with Utility, not with the fader?
Step 06

Stage four — the group bus

Route to a group
  • Select MF SOURCE and ⌘ G to wrap it in a group named SYNTHS. The group fader starts at 0 dB — unity, summing without adding gain.
  • Watch the group's meter. With one track in it the group level matches the track. As you add tracks later, the group will sum them — and can climb toward the ceiling.
The bus is a gain stage too
  • Anything summed can clip. If the group meter ever nears 0 dBFS, the fix is to pull the group fader down a touch — or better, to have set each track lower so the sum lands right. Headroom on the bus is headroom for the master.
  • Leave the group at unity for now with one source; the discipline is to watch it, not to nudge it without reason.
Decision check
  • Is the group fader at unity, summing without adding gain?
  • Is the group meter well below 0 dBFS, leaving room for the master?
  • Do I understand the group is just another place level can climb?
Step 07

Stage five — the master and the headroom check

Read the master
  • Play the source through. On the master metering plug-in, read the peak number. With everything set at −12 dBFS and unity downstream, the master should peak comfortably below −6 dBFS.
  • Confirm at least 6 dB of headroom remains below 0 dBFS. That gap is what mastering will use; protect it.

Decision check
  • Does the master keep at least 6 dB of headroom?
  • Can I name the level at every stage and why it's set there?
  • Is there a single stage doing a job that belongs to another?
Step 08

Break it on purpose — the four clipping loci

Create each fault, one at a time, then undo it
  • Interface input. Turn the M4 trim up until the clip LED lights and Live's input goes red. Record two seconds. Listen: harsh, square. Now pull the trim back down — but notice the recorded clip is permanently damaged. This is analog clip: scissors, not a calculator.
  • Track / device input. Push a Utility's Gain hard into the saturator so the device input overs. Hear it spit, then pull the Utility back. Recoverable — the level was just a big number inside Live.
  • Group / bus. Raise the group fader until the group meter hits 0 dBFS red. Hear the sum break up, then pull it back. Recoverable, and a warning of what happens when many tracks sum.
  • Master / output. Raise the master fader past 0 dBFS. The whole mix overs. Pull it back — recoverable here, but in mastering this is exactly the no-headroom mix that gets squashed.
Fill the clipping diagnosis table
Four loci — name each fingerprint
Interface inputM4 clip LED · permanent · fix at the trim or source
Track / device inputLive channel red · recoverable · fix the Utility / clip gain feeding it
Group / busGroup meter red · recoverable · lower the sum or the group fader
Master / outputMaster red · recoverable now, fatal for mastering · keep headroom
Decision check
  • Can I tell analog input clip (permanent) from digital overs (recoverable) by what survives?
  • Can I point at which meter told the truth for each fault?
  • Have I returned every stage to its justified level afterward?
Step 09

The three-level recording — hear what level decides

Record the same phrase three ways
  • Record the same held phrase three times: trim down so it peaks ≈ −40 dBFS; trim correct at ≈ −12 dBFS; trim too hot so the clip LED lights.
  • Level-match all three to the same loudness, then compare. The −40 take, brought up to match, drags its noise floor up with it. The hot take is harsh on the peaks. The −12 take is clean and full.
Defend one
  • Write one sentence: which take you'd keep and why. The answer is the −12 take — strong enough to need no rescue, with room above for everything to come.
  • This is the whole lesson made audible: level is a decision at the source, and the trim is where it's made.
Decision check
  • At matched loudness, can I hear the noise floor rise on the −40 take?
  • Can I hear the harshness on the hot take?
  • Can I defend the −12 take in one sentence?
Step 10

Add the stereo case and lock the structure

Gain-stage the Digitakt stereo pair
  • Create DT DRUMS, In Ext. In 3/4. Play a pattern and set the level from the Digitakt's one Main Volume so both L and R peak ≈ −12 dBFS.
  • Confirm both sides meter equally. A mismatch means a half-seated TRS cable — a wiring fault, not a gain one. Route DT DRUMS to its own DRUMS group.
Recheck the master with both sources
  • Play synth and drums together. The master now sums two groups — watch it climb. If it nears −6 dBFS, pull each group down a touch so the sum keeps ≥ 6 dB of headroom. Balance with the group faders, set level at the trims.
  • Save the gain ledger: one row per stage, nominal and actual. Save the set as a template — your clean structure, reusable on every track.
Final decision check
  • Do both Digitakt channels meter equally at −12 dBFS?
  • With both sources playing, does the master still keep ≥ 6 dB headroom and never clip?
  • Can I trace one signal from source to master and justify the level at every stage out loud?
