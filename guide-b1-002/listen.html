Lab Note B1-002
Unity · 02 — Listen

Hear what level does — and what breaks it

Gain structure is silent when it works, so the only way to learn it by ear is to learn its failures. Before any gear, train your ear on what a clean chain sounds like and on the three ways it goes wrong: a rising noise floor, hard clipping, and squashed dynamics. Once you can hear them, you can fix them.

The four questions to ask of any sound

Loop a clear 30–60 second section of whatever you are auditioning — a record, your own take, a test tone. Answer all four in one short sentence each. Describe the behavior, never the mood.

The frame

Four questions, one sentence each

Ask, in order
  1. Is there a hiss or hum under the sound when it goes quiet?
    Why it matters

    A noise floor that rises and falls with the quiet parts is the sound of a source recorded too cold then turned up. The interface trim was too low, the fader did the work, and the noise came up with the signal.

  2. Is the top of the sound flattened — does it sound harsh, square, or crackly at the loud moments?
    Why it matters

    That is clipping: a level pushed past the ceiling so the wave's peaks are sliced off. Done by accident it sounds cheap; the point of training the ear is to recognise it instantly so you never ship it unmeant.

  3. Do the loud and quiet parts still feel different, or has everything been flattened to one loudness?
    Why it matters

    Squashed dynamics are the sound of no headroom — every stage pushed so hard that limiting clamps the life out of the music. The kick and the pad end up the same size, and the track stops breathing.

  4. If you turned it down to match a clean reference, would it still sound right?
    Why it matters

    Louder always sounds better for the first few seconds. The honest test is to level-match. A clean chain survives being turned down; a chain that only impressed because it was loud falls apart.

Three sounds to learn cold

You can make all three on your own bench in five minutes — and you will, on the build page. For now, the goal is only to attach a name to each sound.

The faultWhat it sounds likeWhere it lives in the chain
Rising noise floor A hiss or hum that swells under the signal, loudest in the quiet bars. The sound is clean but "thin" and surrounded by grime. Source or interface trim set too cold, then the gap made up with the Live fader or clip gain. The noise was always there; you amplified it.
Hard clipping A harsh, square, crackly edge on the peaks. On a sine it turns round and reedy; on drums it spits and tears. Analog clip at the M4 input (red clip LED), or digital 0 dBFS overs inside Live. Same red light, two different chips, two different fixes.
Squashed dynamics Everything one loudness. The kick no longer punches; the quiet parts are dragged up; the track is loud but lifeless and tiring. No headroom left for the master. A limiter clamps the whole mix because every stage upstream was already maxed out.

And one sound to learn as the target

A clean chain — the reference

  • Quiet parts are silent underneath, not hissy
  • Loud parts have an edge of air above them — they are not touching the ceiling
  • The kick still punches above the pad; dynamics are intact

How to confirm it

  • Pause the track — what disappears was the music, what stays is your system noise
  • Turn it down to a clean reference's level — it should still sound right
  • Watch the meter sit around −12 dBFS with the peaks never near 0
  • Listen on the rig you'll build on. The DT 990 Pro is open-back and honest — it will show you the noise floor and the harshness of clipping that laptop speakers hide. Loop, don't scrub; a noise floor only reveals itself across the quiet bars.
  • Level-match before you judge. Any comparison made at two different volumes is a lie. Match loudness first, then ask which one is actually cleaner.
  • Block 1 has a full listening list. Lab 001 collects ten records that each make a deliberate choice about volume — quiet as depth, clipping as texture, noise as melody. Work that list in Lab 001; this page is only about hearing the faults.

The one principle you take from listening

Carry this into the build

Every gain fault you can hear is a level set in the wrong place. Hiss is a level made up too late; clipping is a level pushed too far; squash is a level with no room left above it. The cure is never "fix it in the mix" — it is to find the stage that owns the level and set it there, once, at the source.

Before you move on
  • I can name a rising noise floor by ear and say what causes it.
  • I can hear hard clipping on both a sine and a drum hit.
  • I can tell squashed dynamics from a sound that is simply loud.
  • I always level-match before deciding which take is cleaner.
  • I have one sentence: a clean chain is quiet underneath and has room on top.
