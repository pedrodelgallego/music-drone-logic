Lab Note B1-002
Unity · 07 — Reflection

Fault, carry-forward, principle

Most of the learning happens here, not while turning the trim. Answer slowly, write the answers down, and turn the result into one usable rule you carry into every track and the rest of Block 1.

How you know it worked

Success signals
  • You can trace one signal from source to master and name the level at every stage.
  • The recording's level was set once, at the M4 trim, with everything downstream at unity.
  • The silent gaps in the take are clean — no noise floor to amplify later.
  • The master keeps at least 6 dB of headroom and nothing clips anywhere.
  • You can tell analog input clip (permanent) from a digital over (recoverable) by what survives.
  • At matched loudness, the −12 take clearly beats the −40 and the hot take.
  • Each of the five moves was used for its one job, none for another's.
  • Both Digitakt channels meter equally — a stereo pair gain-staged from one control.
Failure signals — the faults to name
  • A recording was cold, so you pushed the fader to make it up — and brought the hiss with it.
  • The M4 clip LED was ignored, and a take came out permanently damaged on its peaks.
  • You confused a digital over with analog clip and "fixed" the wrong stage.
  • The master had no headroom left, so the whole mix sat pinned at the ceiling.
  • An effect was driven with the fader instead of Utility, breaking the clean structure.
  • A group or return clipped on the sum because each part was set too hot.
  • One Digitakt channel was quieter than the other — a wiring fault read as a gain fault.
  • You couldn't say what a stage was set to, because it was never started at unity.

Reflection questions

  1. Which single stage did the most to fix or break your level? Was it the one you expected?
  2. Did you ever reach for the fader to rescue a cold recording? Where, and how did you catch it?
  3. Could you tell analog input clip from a digital over by ear, or only by which meter went red?
  4. How much headroom did the master finish with? Did any single part cross −6 dBFS?
  5. In the three-level test, what did the −40 take sound like once you matched its loudness?
  6. Did "trim in, trim out" let you hear the saturator as tone rather than as loudness?
  7. Where did a level climb that you didn't expect — a group, a return, the sum of two parts?
  8. Did starting every stage at unity actually make a fault easier to locate? Give one example.
  9. What is the smallest change — one trim, one fader — that would most improve the next take?
  10. Of the faults on the listen page, which one did your own chain produce before you fixed it?

Carry-forward

Write your carry-forward rule

Finish this sentence in your logbook and reuse it next time: "If a recording is too quiet, I will fix it at the trim before I touch a single fader." A carry-forward rule is a fault turned into a reflex.

  • Keep the template. Your unity Live set is now a reusable object. Start every track from it and you begin with a known, clean gain structure instead of from scratch.
  • The system-diff sentence: "I thought the path worked like ___; after building it, I found it worked like ___." Most of the time the surprise is one stage that was never at unity, sitting wherever it was last left.
  • Next in Block 1: gain structure is the foundation that everything else stands on. Final principle

The best gain structure is the most boring one: every stage you didn't need to change sitting at unity, one level set at the source, and deliberate room left on top. Invisible when it works, and the cause of half of all "amateur" mixes when it doesn't.

Proof gate — the one thing you must demonstrate

Route one instrument from source to master with every stage at a justified level — at least 6 dB of headroom at the master and no clip anywhere in the chain — and explain each gain decision out loud, stage by stage. If you can play the take, point at the meter, and say why each level is where it is, this Lab Note is complete.
