Lab Note B5-003
Feedback · 01 — Overview

Feedback as a safety-critical routing system

A feedback loop is a path that feeds its own output back into its input. Build the ceilings first, open the loop second. This note treats controlled feedback as the engine of a dub-techno track — repeats that grow, smear, and dirty themselves into new timbres — and treats the runaway as the failure mode that the whole discipline exists to prevent. The musical-versus-runaway line is a single number: loop gain at unity. Stay just below it, and put a hand on the kill.

The goal

A dub-techno track built on a loop

  • A 6–8 minute original where the feedback loop is the lead instrument, not an effect
  • Made with Ableton Live 12 Suite, Arturia MicroFreak, and Elektron Digitakt II
  • Fully original — the dub-effect angle, not a copy of any reference

Fundamentals you'll practice

  • Send-into-itself routing, loop gain, and finding unity by ear
  • Filtered feedback, gain ceilings, soft-clip limiting in the loop
  • The safety discipline: limiter, monitor down, kill route in reach
Core objective

Understand the single dividing line between music and disaster — loop gain at unity — and learn to live just below it while keeping one deliberate gesture that touches the edge.

Safety first — read before patching

A self-oscillating loop can go from inaudible to full-scale in under a second. Set a limiter ceiling, turn the monitors down, and keep one hand on the kill before you raise any loop gain. This is not caution for its own sake — runaway feedback through near-field monitors or headphones is genuinely loud enough to hurt.

Why this exercise matters

The beginner instinct

  • Turn the feedback knob all the way up and "see what happens"
  • No filter in the loop, no limiter, monitors loud
  • Steer a runaway with a mouse instead of a fader

The opposite discipline

  • Build the ceiling, the filter, and the kill before raising gain
  • Live just below unity; visit the edge only as a hands-on gesture
  • Let the loop's slow decay do the evolving — do less, hear more

This is the space/dub-effect angle on feedback: controlled loops inside a mixer or DAW, feeding wet signal back through filters and saturation until the repeats become their own instrument. No-input mixer performance — where the desk feeds entirely on itself — is a separate, more dangerous discipline covered in its own note.

Principle

Below unity it is music; at unity it is runaway. Build the ceilings first, open the loop second.
