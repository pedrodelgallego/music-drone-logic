Feedback · 03 — Decisions

Theory, discipline, and the choices that matter

A feedback loop has exactly one parameter that decides whether it is music or disaster: loop gain. Everything else — filters, saturation, ceilings — exists to shape where and how fast that gain acts. Understand the mechanism, then make the small number of decisions that define your track before you touch a knob.

Musical feedback vs runaway — the same mechanism, two outcomes

Musical feedback

  • Loop gain just below unity — energy decays a little on each pass
  • A filter in the loop removes a band per cycle, so the tail evolves and dies
  • Repeats build to a peak, then recede on their own
  • You can let go of the knob and the sound resolves

Runaway

  • Loop gain at or above unity — energy grows on each pass
  • No filtering, so a resonant frequency compounds without limit
  • Climbs to full-scale and stays there until you intervene
  • Let go of the knob and it gets worse, not better
  • The single dividing line is loop gain. Loop gain is the product of every gain in the round-trip path: send level, return level, any Utility trim, the wet/dry of the device, and the self-send amount. Below unity (net energy lost per pass) it is music. At or above unity (net energy gained per pass) it is runaway.
  • Filtering lowers the effective gain in specific bands. A filter in the loop removes energy from the bands it attenuates, so those bands can never compound — which is why filtered feedback stays musical at settings that would otherwise scream. Resonance is the opposite: it adds gain at one frequency, which is exactly why a resonant peak is the first thing to run away.
  • Time only sets the rhythm, not the safety. Delay time decides whether repeats are slap, eighth-note, or a long swell. It does not change whether the loop is stable — that is gain alone.

The safety discipline — non-negotiable, set every time

Build the ceilings before you open the loop
  • Limiter on the master with a hard ceiling at -3 dBFS — the backstop, not the sound.
  • Monitor level down before you start; bring it up only once the loop is stable.
  • A filter in the feedback path (HP 200 Hz, LP 4 kHz) so no single band can compound unchecked.
  • A Utility with a negative trim inside the loop — a guaranteed decay even if other knobs creep up.
  • One mapped knob or fader for loop gain, and you know which direction is "down."
  • A kill route you can hit instantly: mute the return, or a master mute on the controller.
  • Headphones off or low for the first test — never audition a fresh loop at performance volume.
Why this is safety-critical, not just good practice

A loop crossing unity doesn't get gradually louder — it climbs exponentially, often to full-scale in under a second, and it does not stop on its own. Through near-field monitors or headphones that is a genuine hearing-damage event. Every ceiling below is a layer that buys you time to reach the kill.

The decisions that define your track

Decision 01

What is the seed?

The source that feeds the loop
  • A dub-techno track usually feeds the loop from a sparse source: a single chord stab, a rimshot, or a filtered bass note every few bars. The loop fills the rest.
  • Choose a seed with a clear onset and a short body — the loop will do the sustaining. A pad fed into a loop just turns to mud.
  • Decision: one seed, played rarely. Resist the urge to feed a busy part into the loop; the repeats need silence to bloom into.
Decision 02

Where does the loop live?

Send-into-itself, in software or hardware
Pick one home for the loop
Ableton return self-sendRecommended. A return holding Echo + Reverb sends back into its own input. Fully recallable, every gain on a mapped control.
Compact-mixer aux loopThe original dub patch — aux return trimmed back to the aux send. Tactile, but no undo and no recall. Higher stakes.
Digitakt II internal delay feedbackSelf-contained and rhythmic, but the feedback maxes below true unity by design — safe, less wild.
MicroFreak filter self-oscillationA different kind of feedback — the filter resonating into a sine. Useful as a seed, not as the main loop.
  • Decision for this build: the Ableton return self-send, because recall and a mapped safety fader matter more than hardware feel while you're learning the edge.
Decision 03

What is in the loop, and in what order?

The processing decides the character
  • Filter placement: a band-limiting filter (HP + LP) keeps the loop from compounding sub and air. Put it early in the loop so every pass is band-limited before anything else acts.
  • Saturation: a saturator with an output ceiling turns loud repeats into compression and dirt rather than climb — soft-clipping is its own limiter. This is what makes the dub wash sound aged.
  • Resonance / character: a touch of resonant filtering gives the howl; keep it modest, because resonance is gain.
  • Decision: HP/LP filter → saturator with ceiling → delay+reverb → Utility trim, in that order, before the loop closes.
Decision 04

How close to unity do you live?

The gain target, decided in advance
  • Find unity by ear: raise loop gain until repeats stop decaying. Mark that knob position. Then back off about 10 % — that is your home.
  • The performance gesture is a brief, hands-on push toward unity at the track's peak, then an immediate pull back. Decide where in the arrangement that gesture lands before you record.
  • Decision: home at ~90 % of unity; one scripted bloom toward ~98 % at the peak, never holding at or above 100 %.
The performance, not the accident

The thrilling part of feedback is touching unity for a moment — letting the loop bloom right to the edge, then pulling it back. That is a deliberate gesture on a control you trust. Building toward that edge by accident, with no ceiling and no kill, is the failure this whole note exists to prevent.

Decision check
  • Can you state your seed, your loop's home, your processing chain, and where the bloom lands — in one sentence each, before patching?
  • Is there a filter in the loop so no single band can compound? A loop without filtering is a loop waiting to scream.
  • If you let go of every control, does the sound decay to silence on its own? If not, you are above unity.
  • Is loop gain on a fader you can pull down instantly, or are you steering a runaway with a mouse?
