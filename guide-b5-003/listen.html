Lab Note B5-003
Feedback · 02 — Listen

Listen for the loop

Before you patch anything, train your ear on records where feedback is the instrument. In dub, the engineer rides the echo's feedback knob like a fader — repeats swell to the edge of runaway, then get pulled back at the exact moment they'd take over. Listen for that gesture. The point is not to copy these tracks but to recognise the sound of a loop being played: a repeat that grows, smears, filters itself down, and dies on its own.

What to listen for

The growing repeat

  • A snare or chord stab that echoes louder than the source for a moment
  • Repeats that thicken and smear instead of cleanly decaying
  • The bloom toward unity — then the hand pulling it back

The filtered tail

  • Each pass loses a band — repeats get darker and hollower as they fade
  • High-passed echoes that never thicken the low end
  • Resonance that howls just shy of self-oscillation, then recedes

Reference lessons

Each of these block-5 listening lessons isolates one facet of controlled feedback. Open them, listen with the ear-targets above, then come back and build something that is yours.

Listen 01

Dub feedback as performance

The desk played like an instrument
  • The Upsetters — Blackboard Jungle Dub — Lee Perry's foundational dub. Listen for the spring-reverb crashes and the tape-echo feedback ridden by hand, repeats blooming to the edge and snapping back.
  • Mad Professor — Dub Me Crazy — a later, cleaner take on the same patch. The feedback gesture is deliberate and recurring; map your ear to the moment the engineer commits to the bloom.
  • Carry-over: the feedback knob is a fader, not a setting. You will map yours to a physical control in Setup for exactly this reason.
Listen 02

Feedback as texture in techno

The loop becomes the groove
  • Pole — Modul — feedback and digital-fault crackle treated as rhythm. The decaying loop is the percussion; nothing else needs to enter.
  • Listen for how a single seed event regenerates into a whole bar of movement — the generative property you'll exploit in the build.
  • Carry-over: a loop just below unity can fill space that would otherwise need three more parts. Do less, let the loop evolve.
Listen 03

Dub-techno space and the long tail

Adjacent listening in the block
  • Basic Channel — Phylyps Trak and Maurizio — M5 — the chord-stab-into-cavernous-space template that filtered feedback feeds directly into.
  • Rhythm & Sound — Carrier and Deepchord presents Echospace — The Coldest Season — listen for how the wet tail is its own instrument, evolving long after the source stops.
  • See the full block index: Block 5 — Space & dub.
Decision check
  • Can you point to the exact second a repeat blooms toward unity and the hand pulls it back?
  • Can you hear the loop losing a frequency band on each pass — getting darker as it dies?
  • Could a single seed event, fed back, replace two of the parts you were planning to add?
  • Are you listening to copy, or to learn the gesture so you can perform your own?
