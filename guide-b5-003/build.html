Lab Note B5-003
Feedback · 06 — The build

Build a dub-techno track on a feedback loop

A complete, end-to-end build where the feedback loop is the lead instrument. Every step is small, every step has a decision checkpoint, and every gain and cable is spelled out. The first step is not music — it is the ceiling. Keep the setup page open in another tab; it is the wiring reference this page assumes, and keep the palette open for the recipe ranges.

Safety first — every session, before sound

You will raise loop gain many times today. Each time, the limiter must be set, the monitor must be low, and the kill must be in reach. If at any point the loop climbs and won't stop, hit the kill — do not try to "ride it down" with a mouse. Re-read the discipline if you skipped Decisions.

Step 01

Build the ceiling and the kill

Project and master backstop
  • ⌘ N for a new Live Set. Tab to Arrangement. Tempo 126, 4/4. Set the time ruler to Time Ruler Format → Time so the arrangement reads in m:ss.
  • On the Master, drop a Limiter. Ceiling -3 dBFS, Lookahead 1.5 ms (fastest). This is the catch, not the sound — you should rarely see it engage in normal play.
  • Add a Spectrum after the limiter so you can see a band start to compound before you hear it run away.
  • Turn your monitor controller down to a conversational level. Headphones off or at minimum for the first loop test.
Audio & clock preferences
  • ⌘ , → Audio: Input/Output MOTU M4, 48000 Hz, buffer 256.
  • Link, Tempo & MIDI: enable Track and Sync on the Digitakt II output row so Ableton is the clock master; enable Sync on the MicroFreak. Confirm both follow play/stop at 126 before continuing.
  • ⌘ , → Sends: enable Allow Sends to be assigned to other return tracks. Without this, the self-send is impossible.
Tracks and the kill
  • ⌘ T three audio tracks: DT DRUMS, MF SEED, LOOP PRINT (the last one will record the loop's output later).
  • ⌘ ⌥ T two return tracks: A — LOOP, B — TAP.
  • ⌘ M → map a Launchpad pad to A — LOOP's Activator (track on/off). This is your instant kill — pressing it silences the entire loop in one tap. Verify it works now, before there is anything dangerous in the loop.
  • ⌘ S → save as feedback_b5_003.als in a dedicated project folder.
Decision check
  • Is the master limiter at -3 dBFS and the monitor turned down?
  • Does the mapped kill pad silence A — LOOP in one press? Test it twice.
  • Can you see the Spectrum, so a compounding band is visible as well as audible?
Step 02

Build the loop device chain

A — LOOP devices, in exact order
The chain on A — LOOP (filter first, trim last)
1 — EQ EightHP 200 Hz 24 dB/oct; LP 4 kHz 24 dB/oct. Every pass is band-limited before anything else acts.
2 — Auto FilterLow-pass, resonance 20 % to start. This is the "character" filter you'll sweep and automate. Map its cutoff later.
3 — SaturatorSoft Sine, Drive +3 dB, output ceiling on. Loud repeats compress and dirty instead of climbing.
4 — EchoSync on, 3/16, the Echo's own Feedback only 20 %, HP its input at 250 Hz. Dry/Wet 100 %.
5 — Hybrid ReverbPlate or Hall, decay 3.5 s, Dry/Wet 100 %.
6 — UtilityGain -6 dB. The guaranteed per-pass loss — your hard floor of safety even if the self-send creeps.
  • Two Echo feedbacks exist now: the Echo device's internal feedback (keep low, 20 %) and the loop's feedback (the self-send you build next). Keep them mentally separate — the loop feedback is the one that can run away.
Close the loop through B — TAP
  • B — TAP holds no devices. Set its Audio To → A — LOOP. It is just the wire that carries the loop output back to the loop input.
  • On A — LOOP, show its sends. The send to B — TAP is the loop-gain control. Set it to -inf for now.
  • ⌘ M → map a Launchpad fader to that send. Mapping Min -inf, Max 0 dB. Push the fader to the bottom and confirm that is "off." Exit map mode.
Before the next step

You now have a loop that can run away, but the self-send is at -inf, the Utility loses 6 dB per pass, the filter is band-limited, the saturator is ceilinged, the limiter catches at -3 dBFS, and the kill is mapped. Five layers. Only now is it safe to raise gain.

Step 03

Make the seed on the MicroFreak

A sparse chord stab
  • Shift + press Type to init a fresh patch. Oscillator Harmo (organ-like, controllable). Paraphonic on — you'll hold two notes.
  • Tune to a minor third + root, e.g. D2 + F2. Filter cutoff 11 o'clock (dark), resonance low. ENV attack 0, decay/sustain/release short — a stab, not a pad. The loop does the sustaining.
  • The seed must have a clear onset and a short body. A long source fed into a loop just turns to mud — the silence between stabs is where the repeats bloom.
  • Save the patch as SEED STAB.
Route the seed into the loop
  • Select MF SEED. Audio From → Ext. In 1 (MicroFreak), Monitor In. Gain-stage so a stab peaks around -12 dBFS.
  • On MF SEED, set its send to A — LOOP to about -9 dB. Keep the track fader itself modest — most of the sound will come back through the wet loop, not the dry seed.
  • Play one stab. With the loop self-send still at -inf, you should hear a single dub echo decay and stop. That confirms the dry-to-loop path before any feedback exists.
Decision check
  • Is the seed a short stab with a clear onset, not a sustained pad?
  • With self-send at -inf, does one stab produce a clean, decaying echo and then silence?
  • Is the dry seed quiet enough that the wet loop can be the star?
Step 04

Find unity, then back off

Raise the loop gain by hand
  • Monitor low. One hand on the loop-gain fader, one near the kill pad. Play a stab, then stop playing — let the loop carry alone.
  • Raise the loop-gain fader a hair at a time. Listen: at first repeats decay quickly; as you climb they decay slower; then there is a point where a repeat is as loud as the one before it. That is unity. The Spectrum will show a band holding flat instead of falling.
  • The instant a repeat is louder than the previous one, you are above unity — pull the fader down immediately. You do not need to visit there.
  • Find unity, then back the fader off about 10 %. Mark this position (a piece of tape, or note the dB value). This is home — the dub-wash recipe from the palette.
If it ever won't stop

Hit the kill pad. Then pull the loop-gain fader to the bottom. Then bring the kill back up. Diagnose with gain at home, never by chasing a runaway. A loop that surprised you once will do it again at the same fader position — respect the mark.

Confirm the decay floor
  • At home, play one stab and take both hands off everything. The wash must decay to silence on its own within a few seconds. If it doesn't, you are at or above unity — lower the fader or increase the Utility loss to -8 dB.
  • This hands-off decay test is the single most important check on this page. Repeat it any time you change a loop parameter.
Decision check
  • Do you have a marked "home" fader position about 10 % below unity?
  • Hands fully off: does the loop decay to silence on its own? (If not, you are above unity.)
  • Did you reach for the kill at any point — and did it work instantly?
Step 05

Build the dub-techno foundation on Digitakt II

A minimal, swung pattern
  • Digitakt II project FEEDBACK LAB, pattern A01, clocked to Ableton at 126. Four tracks: T1 KICK, T2 HAT, T3 RIM, T4 SUB.
  • T1 KICK: trigs on 1, 5, 9, 13, short dark kick tuned to D so it agrees with the seed. T2 HAT: offbeats 3, 7, 11, 15, p-lock alternating volume. Add 52 % swing.
  • T3 RIM: one hit on step 11, sometimes — set a probability of 35 %. T4 SUB: a sine-ish sub on D1, trigs on 1 and 9 only, sidechained later.
  • Route DT DRUMS Audio From → Ext. In 3/4. Drums stay dry — they are the steady grid against which the wet loop drifts. No send to the loop from the kick.
Decide what feeds the loop
  • Send only the rim (T3 RIM, via a Digitakt FX send or a separate Ableton track send) into A — LOOP at -12 dB. The kick and sub must never enter the loop — sub in a feedback loop is the fastest route to a low-frequency runaway, and the HP at 200 Hz is only your second line of defense.
  • So the loop is fed by two sparse sources: the MicroFreak stab (occasional) and the rim (probabilistic). Both leave long silences for the wash to bloom into.
Decision check
  • Are kick and sub kept entirely out of the loop?
  • Does the groove feel like dub-techno — sparse, swung, spacious — rather than busy?
  • Do the loop's sources leave enough silence for the repeats to be heard?
Step 06

Shape the three loop characters

Map the recipe ranges to the arrangement
  • You built the loop at the dub-wash home in Step 04. Now rehearse moving it to the other two palette recipes by hand, so you know the gestures before recording.
  • Filtered tail: raise the loop-gain fader from home toward 70–85 % of unity while pulling the Auto Filter cutoff down from 4 kHz toward 800 Hz. The repeats darken and tunnel away. The filter guarantees the decay even at this higher gain — confirm with the hands-off test.
  • Resonant howl: from home, raise the Auto Filter resonance toward 50 % and sweep its cutoff slowly. A pitched howl emerges. Keep loop gain lower here (50–65 % of unity) because resonance is adding the rest of the energy. Hand stays on the gain fader.
The resonant howl is the high-risk recipe

Resonance adds gain at one frequency — the band most likely to run away. Never combine resonance above 55 % with loop gain above ~70 % of unity, and keep the LP filter active above the howl. If the howl starts to climb, drop resonance first, then gain.

Decision check
  • Can you move between all three recipes by hand without the loop ever climbing?
  • For the filtered tail, does pulling the cutoff down reliably bring the tail to silence even at high gain?
  • For the howl, is your hand on the gain fader the entire time?
Step 07

Arrange the loop as the lead

The eight-minute arc, sections and times
Section → time → loop behaviour at 126 BPM
INTRO — 0:00Loop at dub-wash home, fed by occasional stabs only. No drums. The room establishes itself.
KICK — 1:00Kick + sub enter dry. Loop unchanged. The dry grid meets the wet wash.
GROOVE — 2:00Hat + rim enter; rim begins feeding the loop probabilistically. Wash thickens with the rim's drift.
TAIL — 3:00Shift toward the filtered-tail recipe: loop gain creeps up, Auto Filter cutoff automated down. Repeats darken.
BLOOM — 4:30The peak. The scripted gesture: ride loop gain toward ~98 % of unity for 4–8 bars, then pull back. Reverb send blooms. Nothing new enters.
STRIP — 5:30Loop gain back to home, filter reopened. Drums thin to kick + hat. The strip-back makes the bloom retroactively bigger.
OUTRO — 6:30Drums out. Loop fed by the last few stabs, gain easing down so the wash decays to silence over the final 60–90 s.
Print the loop as audio
  • The loop's output is generative and won't recall perfectly — so capture it. Set LOOP PRINT Audio From → Resampling (it records the post-fader master, or set its input to the A — LOOP output if you want it isolated).
  • Arm LOOP PRINT, monitor low, and record the full performance while you ride the gestures live. The dry drums and seed are already recallable; this print preserves the un-repeatable wet evolution.
  • Two takes. Keep the one where the bloom felt most controlled, not the loudest.
The bloom is a performance, not an accident

The peak at 4:30 is the whole point of the track: a deliberate, hands-on push toward the edge and a confident pull back. It works because every ceiling is already in place. If you find yourself hoping the loop will calm down, you've crossed from performance into accident — pull the gain to home.

Decision check
  • Does the peak arrive because the loop bloomed, not because a new element was added?
  • Did you record the loop print, so the un-repeatable wet evolution survives?
  • Through the whole take, was your hand on the gain fader and the kill in reach?
  • Does the outro decay to silence on its own once your hands leave the controls?
Step 08

Mix, verify, and export

Balance around the loop
Starting faders — the loop print is the centrepiece
DT Drums (dry)-9 dB (peaks ~ -6 dBFS)
MF Seed (dry)-15 dB — the dry stab is just an onset
Loop print (wet)-8 dB — the lead instrument
A — LOOP return (live)mute once the print is recorded, to avoid double feedback
Master peak≤ -3 dBFS (limiter is the catch, not the loudness tool)
  • Once LOOP PRINT holds the take, mute the live loop return so you are not running a second feedback path during the mix. The danger is over; the recording is just audio now.
Verification passes
  • Low-end check: the loop print should hold nothing below 200 Hz (the in-loop HP). Confirm on Spectrum; if there's sub in the wet, the HP was bypassed during the take — re-print.
  • Mono check: flip a master Utility to mono — the wet reverb wash will narrow but must not vanish or comb harshly.
  • Decay check: the outro must reach true silence. A wash that never quite ends reads as a mistake, not a tail.
  • Quiet listen: play it once at conversation volume. The dub-wash relationship between dry grid and wet loop should still read.
