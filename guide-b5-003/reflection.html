Feedback · 07 — Reflection

Fault, carry-forward, principle

Most of the learning happens here, not while making the track. With feedback there is a second layer to reflect on: not only "did it sound good," but "was it ever out of control." Answer both, slowly, in writing.

How you know it worked

Success signals
  • The loop is the lead instrument — muting it makes the track collapse, not just thin out.
  • The repeats evolve: they thicken, darken, or howl, rather than cleanly copying the source.
  • The peak arrives because the loop bloomed, not because elements were added.
  • At every moment, taking your hands off the controls let the sound decay to silence.
  • You stayed below unity except for one deliberate, hands-on gesture.
  • The kick and sub never entered the loop; the low end stayed clean.
  • You can explain why each gain, filter, and ceiling was set where it is.
Fault signals
  • The loop crossed unity unintentionally and you chased it with a mouse instead of the kill.
  • You monitored a fresh loop at performance volume.
  • There was no filter in the loop, so a band compounded and screamed.
  • Sub frequencies built up because the kick or sub was feeding the loop.
  • The repeats just copied the source — no evolution, because there was no processing in the loop.
  • The wet loop wasn't printed, so the take is gone and can't be recovered.
  • The track needed new elements at the peak instead of the loop's own bloom.

Eleven reflection questions

  1. Where exactly was your "home" gain relative to unity, and how did you find it?
  2. Which recipe — dub wash, resonant howl, filtered tail — carried the most of the track?
  3. Did the in-loop filter do its job: was the low end clean and no single band runaway?
  4. Did you ever reach for the kill? What triggered it, and did it work instantly?
  5. Did the bloom at the peak feel like a performance or like a near-accident?
  6. Could you let go of every control at any moment and trust the sound to resolve?
  7. What fed the loop, and did those sources leave enough silence for the repeats?
  8. Did the loop evolve the source, or merely echo it? What in the loop caused the evolution?
  9. Was the master limiter the catch (rarely engaging) or the crutch (always working)?
  10. If you handed your patch to someone else, could they raise the gain safely from your notes?
  11. What would you change in the safety discipline next time — before the music?
Carry-forward

The discipline you built here — ceiling first, filter in the loop, gain on a fader, kill in reach — is the foundation for the more dangerous no-input mixer note, where the desk feeds entirely on itself with no external source at all. Carry forward the habit of building the ceilings before opening the loop; there, it is not optional.

Final principle

Below unity it is music; at unity it is runaway. The best feedback track is not the one that screamed loudest — it is the one that bloomed right to the edge, on purpose, and came back. Build the ceilings first, open the loop second.
