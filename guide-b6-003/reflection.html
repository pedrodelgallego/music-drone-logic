Chance Operations · 07 — Reflection

Separate what chance decided from what you decided

The learning in chance music happens here, with the roll log open. The point is not whether the track is good — it's whether you can still say, line by line, which decisions were yours and which you handed off, and whether the hand-off was honest. Answer slowly. Write the answers down next to the log.

How you know it worked

Success signals
  • You can write the complete, finite list of everything chance was choosing between.
  • Removing every chance hand-off still leaves a coherent track.
  • Every possible outcome of every procedure was one you'd sign your name to.
  • You auditioned at least three runs per decision and curated, rather than settling for the first.
  • The roll log is complete enough to reproduce the exact track.
  • Chance decided structure — notes, perc, texture, order — never foundation.
  • The kick, key, tempo, form, and peak were never rolled.
  • The track sounds intentional even though you didn't choose the specifics.
Failure signals
  • You can't list what chance was choosing between — the fence was never drawn.
  • Chance decided whether a core element existed at all.
  • Most outcomes needed rescuing — the field was too wide.
  • You kept the first roll because re-rolling felt like effort.
  • You used chance to avoid deciding at all, not to avoid a specific safe habit.
  • The roll log is missing, so the track can't be reproduced or defended.
  • The result sounds arbitrary because there was no boundary to read it against.

Reflection questions

  1. Which chance procedure produced the result you were most glad you didn't choose yourself?
  2. Which fence was too loose — where did a roll surface something you'd not sign your name to?
  3. Did any hand-off secretly decide foundation rather than structure?
  4. Which procedure did you settle on rather than curate? Be honest.
  5. Did the best-of-three discipline change what you kept, or did you keep the first run anyway?
  6. If you handed the constitution and roll log to someone else, could they rebuild the track?
  7. Where did chance break a loop you'd otherwise have predicted bar-for-bar?
  8. Would the track survive with all chance removed? If not, what crossed the line?
  9. What would you fence more tightly next time?
The fault to watch for

The characteristic failure of chance music is the wide fence: a field so broad that most outcomes need fixing, so you quietly start "fixing" them — which is just composing by hand while pretending the dice did it. The honest tell is in your log. If you re-rolled to find a usable result, the fence was too loose. If you re-rolled to choose between several you liked, it was right. Curation between good options is the work; rescuing bad ones is a confession.

Carry forward

Cage relocated authorship from "which note" to "which field of notes." Carry that move into everything: a self-playing patch, a tuning system, a live set. The skill is never the randomness — it's the discipline to compose the boundary first, log what the coin did, and curate what survives. Decide where your authorship lives, then let the coin work below it.

Final principle

Chance is a composer only when you have already decided what it is allowed to compose. The dice are never the instrument — the boundary is.
