Lab Note B6-003
Chance Operations · 02 — Listen

Three composers who handed off the pick

Before you roll anything, listen to people who already did. Each of these pieces is built from a different chance device — an oracle, a pair of dice, a tape splice decided by hand-cut lengths — but they share one architecture: a human composed the field, and something other than the composer chose within it. Open the rep lessons alongside this page and listen for the boundary, not the randomness.

The case studies

Three reference lessons live in Block 6. Listen to each one asking the same question: what did the composer decide, and what did they refuse to decide?

Cage — Music of Changes (1951)

  • Charts of sounds, durations, dynamics; the I Ching picks cells
  • The chart is the composition; the oracle is the executor
  • Open the rep lesson →

Cage — Williams Mix (1952)

  • Tape collage: chance decides splice lengths and which of six sound categories
  • A score that is literally a cutting pattern, not a set of notes
  • Open the rep lesson →

Mozart (attrib.) — Musikalisches Würfelspiel

  • A dice game: every bar pre-composed, the dice only choose the order
  • The tightest possible boundary — every outcome is guaranteed to work
  • Open the rep lesson →

A fourth, optional

  • Imaginary Landscape No. 4 — twelve radios, chance-determined dials
  • Hear how a wide, untamed field becomes the risk this whole note guards against

What to listen for in each

Piece → chance device → the boundary that makes it work
Music of ChangesCharts of cells; coin tosses mapped through the I Ching hexagrams choose which cell sounds, when, and how loud. The pianist plays a fully fixed score — the indeterminacy happened at the writing desk, not in performance.
Williams MixEight tracks of magnetic tape, six categories of sound (city, country, electronic, manual, wind, small). Chance operations decide splice lengths and category per fragment. The categories are the fence; the splice pattern is the roll.
Würfelspiel176 pre-written one-bar minuet fragments in a numbered table. Two dice per bar pick a row; the column is the bar position. Every face of the dice maps to a bar that was composed to fit — the tightest field in the lesson.
Imaginary Landscape No. 4Twelve radios, two performers each: one on tuning, one on volume. Chance determines the dial positions, but the content is whatever is broadcasting. This is the cautionary one — the field includes "silence" and "an ad read" with equal weight.
The listening question

In the first three pieces every possible outcome was usable because the composer fenced the field first. In the fourth, the field is the open airwaves — and you can hear the difference between bounded indeterminacy and raw chance immediately. That contrast is the entire subject of this Lab Note.

Two ways the same idea sounds

Bounded — every roll is keepable

  • Mozart: any sequence of those 176 bars is a coherent minuet
  • Cage's charts: any cell is a sound he chose to allow
  • You curate between good options, never away from bad ones

Unbounded — most rolls need rescuing

  • Open radios: the result can be silence, traffic, or a hit single
  • The intention is structural, but each outcome carries none of it
  • You spend the session fixing instead of choosing
  • How to use these: don't copy a note, a splice, or a bar. Copy the architecture. Decide what your charts contain — your three allowed bass notes, your two texture samples, your handful of section orderings — and let the device pick.
