Lab Note B6-003
Chance Operations · 05 — Procedures

A palette of chance procedures, each with a worked outcome

Six procedures, ordered from the tightest fence to the loosest. Each one names the device, the boundary you author, the exact mechanics, and a worked roll — what actually happened the first time it ran, so you can hear the difference between a usable result and one that needs a re-roll. Steal these, then re-draw the fences for your own track.

Procedure A — The Würfelspiel (dice choose section order)

Procedure A

Pre-build six variants, let a die order them

The boundary you author
  • Build six 16-bar variants of the same section — call them V1–V6. Same key, same kick, same energy band. They differ only in which allowed bass notes land and which perc condition is active.
  • Every variant must be one you'd happily keep on its own. That is the whole fence.
Mechanics
  • Roll a six-sided die once per 16-bar slot across the body of the track. Log the sequence on paper.
  • Constraint to keep it musical: never play the same variant twice in a row — re-roll a repeat. This is a rule you author on top of the chance, exactly as Mozart's table forbids nonsensical adjacencies.
Worked roll
First run — eight slots, die results logged
Slot 1 → 4V4 (sparse, perc off)
Slot 2 → 4repeat — re-rolled → 1 (V1, root-heavy)
Slot 3 → 6V6 (G1 emphasis, perc 1:2)
Slot 4 → 2V2 (C2 lift)
Slot 5 → 5V5 (perc condition fires)
Slot 6 → 1V1 again — legal, not consecutive
Slot 7 → 3V3 (longest gaps)
Slot 8 → 6V6 to close the body
  • Outcome: a body that breathes V4 → V1 → V6 → V2 → V5 → V1 → V3 → V6. Every slot is keepable because every variant was. This is bounded indeterminacy working exactly as intended.

Procedure B — The single probability trig

Procedure B

One trig that plays sometimes

The boundary you author
  • One perc trig on T3, step 8, set to a 35 % condition. The trig either plays its authored sound or stays silent — those are the only two outcomes.
Mechanics
  • Hold the trig, turn the trig-condition knob to 35 %. The Digitakt rolls its own coin each pattern cycle.
  • Record a 4-minute pass so the trig has dozens of chances to land. Over 4 minutes at 124 BPM that trig faces roughly 60 coin tosses.
Worked roll
  • Across one 4-minute take the trig fired on bars 3, 9, 10, 17, 26, 31, 38, 44, 52, 59 — clustered, then sparse, in a way no human would program and no listener can predict.
  • Outcome: the loop stops being a loop. Lower the percentage for a rarer heartbeat; raise it past 60 % and it reads as "always with occasional gaps", which is a different, weaker effect.

Procedure C — Bernoulli coin chooses the sample

Procedure C

A coin in the control path picks A or B

The boundary you author
  • Two texture samples, both in key, both at the same level and decay. The kick and hat are fixed; only which texture the trig loads is up to the coin.
Mechanics — two ways
  • On the Digitakt: conditional sample lock — hold the texture trig, lock sample slot A; on a copied pattern lock slot B; alternate the patterns with a chance follow action or by a die roll per cycle.
  • In VCV: a guaranteed gate into a Bernoulli Gate at 0.5; output A triggers sampler voice A, output B triggers voice B. The rhythm never changes — only the timbre does.
Worked roll
  • Over 32 cycles the coin chose A 18 times, B 14 times — close to fair, with a run of four B's around the midpoint that gave the section an accidental "movement".
  • Outcome: a texture that shimmers between two timbres with no detectable pattern. Because both samples were pre-vetted, every cycle was usable — no re-roll needed.

Procedure D — Quantized random note (the n-way pick)

Procedure D

Chance chooses among three allowed notes

The boundary you author
  • Exactly three pitches: A1, C2, G1. Exactly three steps the bass may land on (1, 7, 11). Everything else is silence the kick owns.
Mechanics — three ways
  • VCV: a random voltage → sample-and-hold clocked once per bar → quantizer set to A-minor with only those three degrees enabled → MIDI to MicroFreak.
  • Code: Prand([0,3,7], inf) in SuperCollider, or n "<0 3 7>?" in TidalCycles — the list literal is the fence.
  • Analog: roll a die, 1–2 = A1, 3–4 = C2, 5–6 = G1; log it, play it in.
Worked roll
  • One bar's S&H sequence, logged: A1, A1, G1, C2, A1, G1, C2, C2 over eight bars. Note the two C2's in a row near the end — tension that resolves nowhere, which is exactly the hypnotic effect.
  • Outcome: a bassline you'd never have written by hand but recognise as entirely yours, because you wrote the three notes it's drawing from. Re-roll if a bar lands on the root every time — that means the seed got lucky toward "safe".

Procedure E — Live per-note chance inside a fixed clip

Procedure E

Each note carries its own probability

The boundary you author
  • A fixed MIDI clip — say a hat or a chord stab. The notes, pitches, and timing are composed. The only thing in play is each note's Chance value in Live 12's note editor.
Mechanics
  • Select a note, open the Note tab, set its Chance below 100 %. Structural notes stay at 100 %; ornamental ones drop to 40–70 %.
  • The clip is the field; the chances are bounded per note. Print to audio once a run sounds right — Live re-rolls every loop, so capture the take you curated.
Worked roll
  • A 1-bar hat clip: downbeats at 100 %, the three offbeat ghosts at 50 %. Across 16 bars the ghosts thinned and thickened on their own; bar 11 happened to drop all three, leaving a held breath.
  • Outcome: human-feeling variation with zero programming. The danger: too many sub-100 % notes and the groove's identity dissolves — keep the load-bearing hits certain.

Procedure F — Wide field (the cautionary one)

Procedure F

Random pitch, no quantizer — what not to do

The (absent) boundary
  • Random CV straight to pitch, full range, no scale, no degree limit. The field is "any pitch", which is to say there is no field.
Worked roll
  • Eight bars produced two notes in key, three glaringly out, two sub-audible, one ear-splitting. Every bar needed rescuing.
  • Outcome: noise. This is here so you can hear the boundary's job by its absence. Add the quantizer and three-degree limit from Procedure D and the same random source becomes musical — the source didn't change, the fence did.
Decision check — for every procedure above
  • Can you write the complete list of outcomes this procedure chooses between? If not, tighten until you can.
  • Did you audition at least three runs before keeping one? Curation, not the first roll.
  • Remove the chance entirely — does a coherent track remain? It must.
  • Is every possible outcome one you'd sign your name to? If a single one isn't, the fence is too loose.
Carry forward

Procedures A through E differ only in fence width and where the pick lands. Procedure F is the same machinery with the fence removed — proof that the boundary, not the dice, is the instrument.
