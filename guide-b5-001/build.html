Lab Note B5-001
Delay · 06 — The build

Build a dub where delay is the composition

A complete, exhaustive build of a 6–8 minute dub-style track at 124 BPM in which the delay returns carry the music. The dry parts are deliberately sparse — most of what the listener hears is echo. Every send, feedback percentage, filter frequency, and throw is spelled out. Keep the setup page and delay recipes open in other tabs.

Step 01

Project, returns, and the dry/wet philosophy

Open the set
  • ⌘ N, Tab to Arrangement view. Tempo 124, signature 4/4.
  • Right-click the ruler → Time Ruler Format → Time so positions read in m:ss. The arrangement plan in Step 09 is written in minutes.
  • ⌘ S → save as dub_b5_001.als in a dedicated project folder. File → Collect All and Save at every session's end.
Create exactly the tracks this dub needs
  • ⌘ T for audio tracks: DT DRUMS, MF BASS, STAB, SKANK, MELODICA. Five dry parts — that is the entire instrument list, and it is intentionally small.
  • ⌘ ⌥ T three times for returns: A — DUB ECHO, B — SLAP, C — SPRING.
  • Colour the dry tracks one family and the returns a contrasting one. You will be reading the return lanes more than any others in this build.
Dial the three returns (from the recipes)
Return A — DUB ECHO (Echo, recipe 1/4)
Sync / timeOn, 3/16
Feedback60 % resting
StereoPing-Pong
Feedback HP / LP250 Hz / 4 kHz
Dry/Wet100 %
Return B — SLAP (Echo, recipe 2)
Sync / timeOff, 110 ms
Feedback12 %
Stereo / HPMono, panned right; HP 300 Hz; Dry/Wet 100 %
Return C — SPRING (Echo + short reverb, recipe 3 wash)

The thesis of this build

Every return is 100 % wet. You will never touch a Dry/Wet on a return again. From here on, every musical decision is made on a send — and the sends are the composition.

Step 02

The dry foundation — drums and bass, kept clean

Digitakt II — the one-drop foundation
  • On Digitakt II, project DUB B5, pattern A01, four tracks: T1 KICK, T2 RIM, T3 HAT, T4 PERC.
  • Confirm clock: press play in Ableton, Digitakt shows 124 and follows transport. One clock master, always.
  • Programme a one-drop feel — the dub backbone. The kick does not sit on every quarter; it leaves space, and that space is where the echoes will live.
The 16-step foundation
Kick (T1)Steps 3 and 11 — the one-drop; downbeats 1 and 9 are deliberately empty
Rim (T2)Step 7 only — this is the hit you will throw into the echo
Hat (T3)Offbeats 3, 7, 11, 15, micro-timed a hair late; p-lock alternating velocity
Perc (T4)One sparse tick, 25–50 % probability, so it surprises
Keep the foundation dry on record
  • On Digitakt, set the delay/reverb sends on T1–T4 to zero for the recorded pass — except one deliberate p-lock (below). The space in this track is created in Ableton, not baked in.
  • Record a 4-minute pass into DT DRUMS (Ext. In 3/4, Monitor Auto). Peaks just under -6 dBFS.
  • The one baked throw: on T2 RIM step 7, p-lock the Digitakt's own delay send up — so that single rim is the seed of a hardware throw inside the recorded audio. Everything else stays bone dry.
MicroFreak bass — deep, simple, dry
  • Init a patch. Oscillator Wavetable, Wave ~25 %, monophonic. Filter cutoff at 11 o'clock, low resonance. Short envelope.
  • Sequence 3 notes in A natural minor, rooted A1, landing on the kick's offbeat steps so bass and kick interlock rather than collide.
  • Record 4 minutes into MF BASS, hands off the knobs. The bass never goes to any delay — it stays dry and central, the anchor the echoes float over.
Decision check
  • Is the foundation genuinely sparse — are there empty downbeats for the echo to fill?
  • Are kick and bass dry, with nothing sent to any delay? Solo them; they should sound naked.
  • Does the one-drop feel like it is leaving room rather than missing a beat?
Step 03

The stab — the part that lives in the delay

Design a short, percussive chord
  • MicroFreak, paraphonic on. Play a two- or three-note minor chord (A–C–E). Very short envelope — a stab, not a pad. It should be gone in under 200 ms.
  • The stab is rhythmically sparse on purpose: it plays once or twice a bar, on an offbeat. Its repeats will fill the rest.
  • Record a 4-minute pass into STAB, dry.
Wire the stab's sends — this is the move
STAB track sends
Send A (DUB ECHO)Performed — flat at -inf, thrown on chosen bars (Step 06)
Send B (SLAP)Constant -15 dB — a permanent slap fattens the dry stab
Send C (SPRING)-inf until the breakdown, then opened (Step 07)
  • The constant slap (B) makes the dry stab feel wide and present without ping-pong. The throw (A) is the gesture. Two delays, two jobs, on one source — exactly the send model from page 03.
STAB (dry) → B const → SLAP (width) + A thrown DUB ECHO (tail) → mix → Master
Decision check
  • Is the stab short enough that its repeats read as separate hits, not a smear?
  • Does the constant slap widen it without you hearing a distinct echo?
  • With Send A flat, does the dry stab feel sparse and waiting — leaving room for the throw to do the work?
Step 04

The skank and the melodica voice

SKANK — the offbeat chord chop
  • A second, brighter MicroFreak chord on the offbeats (steps 3, 7, 11, 15) — the reggae skank. Bright filter, very short.
  • Record into SKANK. Send B (SLAP) at -12 dB constant; no throw — the skank's job is rhythmic glue, lightly slapped, never thrown.
MELODICA — the voice that duets with the echo
  • MicroFreak, a single-note lead patch (try the Harmo or KarplusStr engine for a reedy, melodica-like tone). Play a slow, sparse phrase — three or four notes, long gaps.
  • Record into MELODICA. The gaps are essential: the delay answers into them. This is the Augustus Pablo duet — the line and its echo trade places.
  • Send C (SPRING) at -9 dB constant, and Send A (DUB ECHO) performed on phrase endings. The repeats of the last note of each phrase become the answer.
The duet rule

Never play over your own echo. Phrase, then rest, and let the delay fill the rest. If the dry line is busy, there is no room for the echo to speak — and the echo is the second musician.

Step 05

Tune the feedback so tails dissolve

Set the tail length on Return A
  • Solo STAB. Throw a single stab into Return A by hand. Count the repeats before silence.
  • At 60 % feedback you should hear roughly six to eight repeats over about two bars. Fewer? Raise toward 70 %. A wall that never decays? You are too high — lower it, do not reach for the return fader.
  • Confirm each repeat is darker than the last. If they stay bright, the in-loop low-pass is not engaged — fix it now; this is the difference between dub and noise.
Verify the two-knob distinction on your own ears
Prove it to yourself once
Raise feedback 60 → 75 %Tail gets longer — more repeats. Presence unchanged.
Raise return fader -10 → -4 dBEchoes get louder, same number of repeats. Length unchanged.
If you confused themYou raised the fader hoping for a longer tail and drowned the mix. Return both to start and set feedback first, level second.
Check the low end stays out
  • Solo Return A. Throw the bass into it for a second (then undo). Does sub energy enter the repeats? It should not — the 250 Hz feedback HP exists to keep the echoes airy.
  • If the kick or sub leaks into the tail, raise the HP to 300 Hz. The delay must never compete with the dry low end.
Decision check
  • Six-to-eight darkening repeats from one throw — yes or no?
  • Can you make the tail longer with feedback and louder with the fader, independently?
  • Is the low end absent from the repeats?
Step 06

Perform the throws — the composition itself

Map the sends to hardware
  • ⌘ M for MIDI map mode. Click STAB Send A → tap a Launchpad pad. Set the mapping Min/Max to -inf / 0 dB so a full press is a full throw and release is silence.
  • Map four send throws total and no more: STAB → A, RIM (DT DRUMS) → A, MELODICA → A, and SKANK → A for rare surprises.
  • Exit map mode. You now play the delays like an instrument.
The throw vocabulary — a per-bar plan
When to throw what (per 8-bar cycle)
Bar 1, step 7 rimShort throw — rim into the tail, one bar of trailing repeats
Bar 4, stabThrow the stab; it answers across bars 4–5 while the dry stab rests
Bar 6, melodica phrase endThrow the last note; the echo completes the phrase into bar 7
Bar 8, full throwSpike several sends at once on the turnaround — a wash that resolves the cycle
  • Automate the send, never the feedback, for every one of these. Feedback is set; the throw is on/off of the path.
  • Each throw is a one-step spike: -inf → 0 dB on the exact trig → -inf immediately after. Anything longer feeds multiple hits and the tail smears.
Record the performance
  • F9 Arrangement Record. Play the full length and ride the four pads, following the throw plan but leaving room to react.
  • Throw slower and rarer than feels natural. A throw every bar is too many; the silence between throws is what makes each one land.
  • Two takes. Keep the one with more restraint. After: right-click each send lane → Simplify Envelope to clean the spikes while keeping the shape.
Decision check
  • Mute every dry track for 16 bars and listen to the returns alone: do the throws still imply the groove and the chord changes? In a dub track, they must.
  • Is any throw landing on top of the dry hit it came from, doubling it? Move the throw to a bar where the dry source rests.
  • Could you remove a third of the throws and lose nothing? Then remove them — restraint is the lesson.
Step 07

The feedback swell — a riser made of echo

Automate feedback for the transition only
  • This is the one place you automate feedback rather than a send. On Return A, ramp Feedback 60 % → 92 % over 8 bars approaching the breakdown, then snap it back to 60 % on the downbeat of the drop.
  • Hold a steady stream of sends going in during the ramp so there is material to regenerate. The existing repeats pile into a swelling, darkening wash — a riser with no new element.
  • The in-loop low-pass keeps the runaway warm. Without it the swell screams; with it, it blooms.
Open the SPRING wash in the breakdown
  • At the drop, cut the dry drums. Open STAB and MELODICA Send C (SPRING) from -inf to -6 dB over 4 bars.
  • Now the only thing left is dry stab/melodica hits and their long, hazy dotted-eighth repeats in the spring return — the track dissolves into pure delay. This is the section that proves the thesis.
  • Bring the kick back on a bar line and pull Send C back to -inf over 4 bars; the haze recedes as the groove returns.
  • Mind the lurch. Do not change Return A's time (3/16) anywhere — only its feedback. Changing sync'd time while it rings pitches the in-flight repeats. Feedback automation is safe; time automation is not.
  • Watch the master meter during the swell. 92 % feedback blooms a second after the last send. Keep the master ≤ -3 dBFS through the peak of the swell.
Step 08

Print the tail and reuse it as texture

Capture a pass of pure repeats
  • New audio track ECHO PRINT, Audio From → the output of A — DUB ECHO (route the return to this track). Arm, ⌘ ⇧ R, record 16 bars of throws.
  • You now have the echo as committed audio — something a live return can never give you.
Mutate the printed tail
  • Clip view → Reverse: the repeats now swell toward the hits — anticipation built from the track's own DNA.
  • Or Warp Texture mode, half-speed, for a ghostly under-layer. HP at 250 Hz so it sits beneath without muddying.
  • Place sparingly under the breakdown. One reversed echo-tail is a texture; a wall of them is clutter.
Step 09

Arrange — the throw timeline is the structure

Mark the map
Section → time → bar at 124 BPM
INTRO — 0:00Bar 1 — drums + bass dry; one rim throw per cycle, nothing else
SKANK IN — 1:00Bar 32 — skank enters with constant slap
STAB IN — 2:00Bar 63 — stab enters; first thrown stabs into Return A
MELODICA — 3:00Bar 94 — the melodica duet; phrase-end throws
SWELL — 4:20Bar 134 — feedback ramp 60 → 92 % over 8 bars
BREAKDOWN — 4:45Bar 147 — drums cut, SPRING wash opens, pure delay
RETURN — 5:45Bar 178 — kick back, haze recedes, throws resume
OUTRO — 6:45Bar 209 — strip to bass + dub echo trailing; fade over final 30–45 s
Editing craft
  • Cut on kick steps; 4-beat crossfades over any seam. Consolidate finished sections with ⌘ J.
  • The energy curve is drawn almost entirely by throw density and feedback, not by adding parts. From STAB IN to SWELL, the only thing that grows is how often and how hard you throw.
  • The BREAKDOWN adds nothing — it removes the dry drums and lets the delay stand alone. That subtraction is the climax.
Decision check
  • Play 2:00 → 4:45 and ask: does the build come from throws getting denser, not from new instruments arriving?
  • Does the breakdown feel bigger because the drums left and the echo remained?
  • If you muted Returns A and C across the whole track, would less than half remain? That is the pass condition for this build.
Step 10

Mix, mono-check, and export

Balance with the returns as instruments

DT Drums (dry)-10 dB

Return A — DUB ECHO-9 dB (presence; set after feedback)

Master peak≤ -3 dBFS, including the swell bloom
The checks that matter for a delay track
  • Mono check: Utility on the master, flip to mono every few minutes. The ping-pong returns are the suspects. If Return A collapses, lean on Return B's mono slap and pull A's width down — Return B was built as exactly this insurance.
  • Low-end check: solo each return; confirm no kick or sub in the repeats. The feedback HP should have handled it; if not, raise it.
  • Pile-up check: through the densest throws, do the tails darken and clear, or do they accumulate into mud? If they pile, lower the feedback LP toward 3 kHz so each pass loses more highs.
  • Quiet check: listen once at low volume. The throws should still read as gestures, not as a constant wash.
