Delay · 03 — Decisions

Routing decides the sound

A delay is not a knob you turn up. It is a path you build: where the signal leaves, how much comes back, where the repeats sit in stereo, and what they sound like by the time you hear them again. Decide the routing first — the sound is downstream of the route.

Insert vs send — the decision that defines everything

Delay as an insert

  • The whole track passes through the device; Dry/Wet sets the blend
  • One source, one delay — the repeats inherit that source's EQ and dynamics
  • Use when the delay is part of the instrument's identity — a dubbed-out stab, a slap on a vocal
  • Cheap on routing, expensive on flexibility — you cannot feed two sources into it

Delay on a send / return

  • A copy is tapped off and routed to a return track running 100 % wet
  • Many sources share one space; the send knob is the per-track mix control
  • The return is a printable bus — you can EQ, saturate, and automate the repeats alone
  • This is the dub mixer model: the throw lives on the send, the colour lives on the return
The rule

On a return, the device is always 100 % wet — the send amount is the mix. The instant you find yourself riding Dry/Wet on a return, you have confused an insert with a send.

Which route, when — for this dub track
Main echo (the architecture)Send / return. Every part can throw into it; you perform the sends; you can print and process the tail.
A slap that defines one soundInsert. The short doubling is part of that instrument and never needs to be shared.
A second, contrasting spaceSecond return. A long ping-pong on B, a tight mono slap on C — sources choose by sending to one, both, or neither.
Hardware throw on Digitakt IIThe Digitakt's delay is effectively a per-track send; p-lock the send amount on chosen trigs — that is the throw, baked in.

Feedback amount vs return level — two knobs that both get "louder"

This is the single most common delay mistake. Beginners reach for one knob when they mean the other. They are not the same control and they fail in opposite ways.

Two ways to make a delay "more"
Feedback amountHow many times a repeat re-enters the line. Controls length of the tail and density. Past ~100 % it self-oscillates and runs away.
Return level (or send level)How loud the wet path sits in the mix. Controls presence, not length. Turning it up never causes runaway.
Symptom of confusionYou want longer trails, so you raise the return — and the mix drowns in echoes that never decay, when what you wanted was more repeats, not more level.
The opposite confusionYou want the echo more present, so you crank feedback — and now it never stops, smearing every later bar, when all you wanted was to hear the few repeats you already had.
The fixSet feedback for the tail length you want, then set return level for how present that tail is. Two passes, two knobs, in that order.
Source → send → Return: Echo 100% wet ↺ feedback Return fader → mix → Master
  • A mental model. Feedback is "how long does the ball keep bouncing." Return level is "how close is the ball to the microphone." You can have a long bounce that is quiet, or a short bounce that is loud — they are independent, and confusing them is what muddies mixes.

Sync'd vs free — clocked rhythm or analog drift

Sync'd (note divisions)

  • Echo's Sync on, times in 1/4, 3/16, dotted-eighth
  • 3/16 and dotted-1/8 are the dub staples — they pull against the kick instead of doubling it
  • Locks the repeats to the groove; the delay becomes a rhythm part

Free (milliseconds)

  • Time set in ms; nudging it slightly off the grid is a deliberate effect
  • Modulating delay time creates pitch wobble — analog tape and BBD character
  • Echo's Modulation + Wobble sections add this drift; small amounts only
Dub-useful sync divisions at 124 BPM (1 bar = 1935 ms)
1/4484 ms — the echo doubles the beat; safe but obvious
3/16363 ms — the classic dub throw; lands just before each beat and tumbles forward
dotted 1/8363 ms — same arithmetic as 3/16, framed as a dotted note; the rolling stagger
1/8242 ms — busy; better as a slap than a long throw
dotted 1/4726 ms — long, spacious; good for melodica-style answers between phrases
  • The lurch trap. Changing a sync'd delay time while it rings causes a pitch jump on the in-flight repeats. If you automate delay time, do it during a silence in the source, or switch Echo to a fixed time and crossfade between two returns instead.
  • When to go free. Choose ms time when you want the echo to drift off the grid — a hair fast or slow makes it breathe. The cost is that it no longer locks to the kick, so keep free-time delays sparse or on non-rhythmic material.

Ping-pong & width — stereo from a mono source

Echo stereo behaviour
Stereo mode = Ping-PongRepeats alternate hard L / R. Wide and hypnotic, but it is the first thing to collapse in mono.
L/R offsetDifferent delay times per side — looser, more analog than strict ping-pong; still mono-risky.
Width controlPull width down on the return to keep some stereo movement without an empty centre in mono.
Mono-safe alternativeA single-tap mono delay panned slightly, with a short reverb for width — survives the mono fold every time (see page 05).
Test it now, not at the mix

Drop a Utility after the delay return and flip it to mono for ten seconds. If the ping-pong vanishes or comb-filters into a thin whoosh, the width was decorative. Decide whether you can afford it before it is load-bearing.

Filtered feedback — why dub delays decay instead of piling up

A delay with flat feedback accumulates energy and turns to mud. The trick is to remove a little band on every pass, so each repeat is darker and thinner than the last — the tail dissolves instead of clogging.

Repeat in → HP 250 Hz → delay line → LP 4 kHz → repeat out ↺ each pass
Echo feedback filtering on the return
High-pass in the feedback path200–300 Hz — keeps low end out of the repeats so the kick and bass stay defined
Low-pass in the feedback path3–5 kHz — each repeat loses highs, fading into a warm blur instead of a hissing pile
Echo's Filter sectionEngage the in-loop HP/LP — not just an EQ after the device, which filters once; this filters every pass
Saturation in the loopA touch of drive makes loud repeats compress and dirty as they regenerate — the tape-echo character that keeps feedback musical
Decision check
  • Is this delay an insert (part of one instrument) or a send (a shared, printable space)? Can you state which, and why?
  • When you wanted "more delay," did you reach for feedback (length) or return level (presence) — and was that the right knob?
  • Sync'd or free? If sync'd, are you on a division that pulls against the kick (3/16, dotted-1/8) rather than doubling it (1/4)?
  • Does the feedback path filter on every pass, so the tail darkens and dies? Or does it pile up flat and muddy?
  • Flip the delay return to mono: does anything important disappear? If yes, is that width worth losing on a phone speaker?
