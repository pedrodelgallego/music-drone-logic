Lab Note B5-001
Delay · 02 — Listen

Listen to the architects

Dub is where delay stopped being an effect and became composition. Before you route a single cable, listen — not for the song, but for what the echo is doing: when it enters, how long it rings, what frequencies survive the trip, and how the mixing engineer plays the send like an instrument.

Three references — study the throw, not the tune

These are listening targets only. Each rep lesson in the workshop breaks one track down step by step. Open them, but bring back a principle — never a pattern. Your track must be fully original.

King Tubby — "Dub Fi Gwan"

  • The mixing desk as instrument: whole parts vanish, then a single snare is thrown into a long tail
  • Listen for the send being ridden by hand — the delay enters and leaves as a gesture, not a setting
  • Notice how dry the bass and drums stay; the space is created by what is removed
  • Workshop rep lesson →

Scientist — "Your Teeth in My Neck"

  • A younger engineer pushing feedback further — repeats that almost self-oscillate, then are pulled back
  • Listen for filtered feedback: each repeat is darker and thinner, so the tail dissolves instead of clogging
  • Hear delay used as transition — a throw covers the seam where a part drops out
  • Workshop rep lesson →

Augustus Pablo & King Tubby — "King Tubby Meets Rockers Uptown"

  • Melodica and echo as a duet — the delay answers the phrase, becoming a second voice
  • Listen for sync'd repeats pulling against the one-drop, never doubling the downbeat
  • The reverb and delay interlock: short slap into long space, two depths at once
  • Workshop rep lesson →

What to listen for, specifically

An ear-training checklist for delay
EntryDoes the delay run constantly, or is it thrown in for one hit then gone? Count how often the echo is actually present.
TimeAre the repeats locked to the groove (sync'd) or drifting against it (free / tape)? Tap along — do echoes land on the offbeat or wander?
FeedbackHow many repeats before silence? Two? Eight? Does the tail darken as it fades, or stay bright and pile up?
StereoDo repeats bounce L/R (ping-pong) or sit centred? Check on one earbud — what collapses?
FrequencyIs there low end in the echoes, or only mids and highs? Almost always the lows are filtered out of the repeats.
RoleIs the delay decoration, or is it carrying a bar on its own while the dry parts rest? In dub it is usually the latter.
The listening exercise

Pick one reference. Play 90 seconds. On paper, mark every moment the delay enters and every moment it leaves. You will end up with a timeline of throws — that timeline is the composition, and it is what you are learning to author.

  • Why dub, for a techno course? Dub invented the vocabulary every later genre borrows: send-based echo, the throw, filtered feedback, the mixer as a playable instrument. Learn it at the source and it transfers to hypnotic techno, dub techno, and ambient untouched.
  • Resist the copy reflex. The temptation after listening is to recreate the exact delay time and feedback of a track you love. Don't. Take the behaviour — "the snare gets thrown into a darkening tail every fourth bar" — and rebuild it from scratch in your own key, tempo, and palette.
