Lab Note B8-003
Teaching the Method · 01 — Overview

Prove you own a method by teaching it to a stranger

The last test of mastery is not whether you can make the piece again — it is whether someone who was not in the room can. In this note you pick a method you already own, take it apart, and write a lab note that teaches it to a stranger: intention before gear, a reproducible mechanism, a decision check at every fork. Then you hand it to a real person, watch them fail, and revise. This is a deep-reference companion to Block 8 · Authorship.

The one principle

Principle

You only own what you can hand over. A method you can run but cannot transfer is a habit, not knowledge. Writing it down for a stranger forces every instinct you skip unconsciously into the open — and the gaps the stranger trips on are the exact edges of your understanding.

The goal

A teaching artifact you wrote

  • One method you own, deconstructed into reproducible steps
  • Written for a stranger — no shared room, ear, or taste assumed
  • Tested on a real person, then revised against what broke

Skills you'll practice

  • Stating intention and refusals before naming any gear
  • Turning instinct into numbers, ranges, and orders
  • Writing decision checks a reader can answer without you
  • Reading a failed rebuild as a fault in the note, not the reader
Core objective

Produce a lab note that lets a stranger rebuild one of your methods and recognise the result as yours — and be honest about the part of it that no document can carry.

Why teaching is the test

The comfortable illusion

  • "I made it, so I understand it"
  • "Anyone could follow what I did"
  • "The steps are obvious once you have the gear"

What the stranger reveals

  • The clock master you never wrote down
  • The ranges you set "by feel" and cannot name
  • The order of operations you do without thinking
  • The refusals you assumed everyone shares

This is the close of three years of building. The skill transfers far beyond music: a method you can teach is a method you can revisit, defend, and improve. The lab note outlives the track.

Principle

If the reader needs to ask you a question to proceed, that question marks a gap in the note — not a flaw in the reader.
