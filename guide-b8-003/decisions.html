Lab Note B8-003
Teaching the Method · 03 — Decisions

What makes a method teachable

Before you write a single step, settle the decisions that shape the whole note. What separates a teachable method from a habit? What is the line between an intention and a recipe? What does reproducible actually require? And what is honestly lost the moment a practice becomes a document? Decide these now — they are the spine of the build.

Decision 1 — Method or habit?

A method

  • You can state its goal before you touch anything
  • The same inputs reliably give the same kind of result
  • You can name why each step is there
  • It survives being written down for someone else

A habit

  • You do it the same way but cannot say why
  • Results vary and you blame "the mood"
  • Steps exist because that is how you learned it
  • When you write it down, half of it goes missing
The test

Try to teach it. The act of writing it for a stranger is the only reliable way to tell a method from a habit. A habit collapses into "and then you just feel it." A method has a reason at every fork — and the reasons are what you write down.

Decision 2 — Intention vs recipe

A recipe lists actions. An intention explains the result those actions serve. A note that is all recipe produces a copy that works once and cannot adapt. A note that is all intention produces a vague gesture the reader cannot reproduce. You need both, in order.

The same step, three ways
Pure intention"Make the bass breathe under the kick." — true, but the reader cannot act on it.
Pure recipe"Compressor, sidechain from kick, 4:1, attack 1 ms, release 120 ms." — reproducible, but the reader has no idea what they are listening for.
Intention then recipe"Make the bass swell back between kicks like breathing — sidechain it to the kick at 4:1, release 120 ms, then tune the release by ear until the swell is audible." — teachable.
  • Lead with intention. The reader must know what they are aiming at before a knob is named, or the recipe is just superstition.
  • Close with a checkable result. Pair every recipe step with what the reader should hear or see, so they can confirm they hit the intention — not just that they performed the actions.

Decision 3 — What reproducibility actually requires

Decision 3

Spell out what you skip by instinct

The four things strangers always get wrong
Write these down even when they feel obvious
The clock masterWhich device runs the timing. You stopped noticing; the stranger has two devices both trying to be master and nothing locks.
The signal pathWhat plugs into what, in what order. "Route it to the bus" assumes a routing the reader has not built.
The ranges"Open the filter" is a habit. "Open from 600 Hz to 3.5 kHz over 40 bars" is teachable. A number can be reproduced; a vibe cannot.
The order of operations"EQ before reverb" matters and you do it without thinking. Written out, it stops being luck.
Ranges, not vibes
  • Replace every "until it sounds right" with a number, a range, or a checkable observation. "Until the gain-reduction meter shows about 3 dB per kick" is reproducible; "until it pumps nicely" is not.
  • Where a value genuinely must be judged by ear, say so explicitly and give the reader the listening test that tells them they are done. Do not disguise a judgement as a fixed number, and do not disguise a fixed number as a judgement.
Decision check
  • Have I named the clock master and the full signal path in writing?
  • Is every "open it / turn it up / make it bigger" replaced by a range or a checkable result?
  • Where order matters, did I say so — and say why?

Decision 4 — What is lost in documentation

Be honest about the limit. A lab note transfers the system completely and the voice only partly. Knowing the difference — and writing the difference into the note — is the last piece of mastery.

What survives

  • The constraints, the routing, the parameter ranges
  • The order of operations and the decision checks
  • The refusals, which travel cleanly as written rules
  • The reproducible mechanism at the core

What is lost

  • The years of ear that hear a sweep is a hair too fast
  • The taste that chose this constraint over a hundred others
  • The accidents you kept because they felt right
  • The in-the-moment call of "this take, not that one"
Reframe

What is lost is not a failure. A note that transferred everything would clone you; a note that transfers the system invites the reader's voice into the gap. That gap is where their authorship begins. Your job is not to close it — it is to mark it honestly so the reader knows it is theirs to fill.

Decision 5 — Who is the stranger?

Decision 5

Define the reader before you write a word

Name the reader's floor and ceiling
  • Floor: what can you assume they already know? "Knows their DAW's transport and how to arm a track" is fair. "Knows my exact sidechain trick" is not — that is the thing you are teaching.
  • Ceiling: what must you not assume? They do not share your room, your monitors, your ear, or your history with this gear. Every place you lean on shared context is a place the note will break.
  • Write the reader profile at the top of your note as one sentence. It governs every later decision about what to spell out.
Decision check
  • Have I picked a method that is genuinely a method, not a habit?
  • Does my plan lead with intention and close every step with a checkable result?
  • Have I committed to writing down the clock, the path, the ranges, and the order?
  • Can I state, in one sentence, who the stranger is and what they cannot be assumed to know?
  • Have I decided what the note honestly cannot transfer — and am I at peace with marking that gap rather than faking it?
