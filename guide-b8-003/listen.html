Lab Note B8-003
Teaching the Method · 02 — Listen

Four methods that survived being written down

Before you write your own teaching artifact, study people who turned a method into a document a stranger could run. Each of these is a different answer to the same question: what part of a practice survives transfer, and what is lost? Read each linked Block 8 lesson, then run the prompt against your own method.

How to listen this time

The frame

Do not listen for the sound. Listen for the instruction. Ask of each work: could a stranger reproduce this from what was written down? Where did the author trust the reader, and where did they pin everything to a rule? That boundary is the whole craft of teaching a method.

The four references

Listen 01

Steve Reich — Music as a Gradual Process

The teaching move
  • Reich did not publish a score for a single piece — he published the mechanism: a process so audible and so completely determined that performer and listener hear the same thing unfold. The method is the music.
  • This is the cleanest model for your note: state a process that runs itself once started, so the reader's hand is guided by the rule, not by your taste.
  • Note what he refuses — hidden structure, secret decisions, anything the listener cannot follow in real time. The refusals are part of the teaching.

Open the Block 8 lesson →

Run this against your method
  • Is there a single mechanism at the core of your method that, once set running, produces the result? Name it in one sentence.
  • What in your method is "audible process" — reproducible — and what is a private decision the reader cannot follow?
Listen 02

Pauline Oliveros — Sonic Meditations

The teaching move
  • Oliveros taught with plain-language instructions anyone could follow with no gear and no training: listen, breathe, respond. The method is entirely verbal, entirely reproducible — and the result still differs in every room.
  • This is the proof that intention before gear works. There is no equipment to hide behind; the instruction is the intention, stated so cleanly that a stranger enacts it correctly while still bringing themselves.
  • Study how she leaves room. The instruction is precise about what to do and open about what it will sound like. That gap is deliberate, not vague.

Open the Block 8 lesson →

Run this against your method
  • Could your method survive losing the gear names — stated as intention and action alone? If not, you are teaching the gear, not the method.
  • Where will you be precise about the action and open about the result? Mark that boundary on purpose.
Listen 03

Daphne Oram — An Individual Note

The teaching move
  • Oram wrote a whole book to transfer a way of thinking about sound, not a single technique. It is prose, analogy, and first principles — teaching the model behind the method so the reader can generate methods of their own.
  • This is the upper limit of documentation: she transfers the worldview, knowing the specific machine (Oramics) cannot be rebuilt by most readers. She teaches what survives — the reasoning — and lets the rest go.
  • Watch how she explains why before how on every page. The why is the part that travels; the how is local to her studio.

Open the Block 8 lesson →

Run this against your method
  • Behind your method is a model of how sound behaves. Can you state it in a paragraph the reader could apply to gear you have never touched?
  • Which parts of your note are universal reasoning, and which are local to your exact rig? Label them — the reader needs to know which is which.
Listen 04

Brian Eno — A Year with Swollen Appendices

The teaching move
  • Eno's diary is the honest opposite of a clean tutorial: it shows the method in motion — the false starts, the boredom, the accident kept because it felt right. It teaches by exposing the working life around the technique.
  • This is the corrective to over-polished documentation. A note that hides every dead end teaches a fiction. Eno shows that the path was messy and the method still transfers — because the reasoning behind each choice is on the page.
  • Note what a diary cannot carry: the ear that decided, in the moment, that one take was the one. He records the decision; he cannot transfer the judgement. That is what is lost.

Open the Block 8 lesson →

Run this against your method
  • Which accident is baked into your method that you would have to admit was luck? Will you teach it as a rule, or name it as a judgement the reader must make for themselves?
  • What does your method look like in motion — the failures you skip when you tell the clean story? Decide which of those the reader needs to see.

The pattern across all four

What each one transferred

  • The mechanism (Reich) — a process that runs itself
  • The intention (Oliveros) — action stated cleanly, gear-free
  • The model (Oram) — the reasoning behind the method
  • The working truth (Eno) — the path, not just the result

What none of them could transfer

  • The years of ear that set the ranges
  • The taste that chose this constraint over others
  • The in-the-moment judgement of "this take, not that one"
  • The reader fills these with their own — and should
  • The common move is order: all four put the why before the how. Intention, model, or mechanism first; the specific actions second; the gear last or never.
  • The common honesty is the gap: each author knew exactly what their document could not carry and did not pretend otherwise. That honesty is what makes the rest trustworthy.
