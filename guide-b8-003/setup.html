Lab Note B8-003
Teaching the Method · 04 — Setup

The rig you document, and the template you write into

Two setups matter here. The first is the studio your method runs on — the same rig as the rest of these notes — because you must describe it precisely enough for a stranger to map onto their own. The second is the writing setup: the teaching-artifact template, and the discipline of capturing a method as you run it so nothing is lost to memory.

The rig you are documenting

The reference signal path
MicroFreak → MOTU M4 in 1 → Ableton ← Digitakt II in 3/4
  • This is the rig the whole course assumes: Ableton Live 12 Suite as the clock master and recorder, Arturia MicroFreak mono into M4 input 1, Elektron Digitakt II main L/R into M4 inputs 3/4, monitored on DT 990 Pro.
  • Ableton sends MIDI clock and transport out; the Digitakt and MicroFreak receive. Exactly one clock master — this is the single fact a stranger most often gets wrong, so it is the first thing your note must state.
Why the rig matters to a teaching note

A method written against a specific rig is reproducible; a method written against "your setup" is not. But the reader's rig will differ. So you describe the rig in two layers: the exact path you used, and the role each piece plays — "the clock master," "the mono synth source," "the sampler." The role survives a gear change; the exact model does not.

Describe each role so a stranger can substitute their own gear
Clock masterAbleton — sends clock + transport. Role: the one device everything else follows.
Mono synth sourceMicroFreak → M4 in 1. Role: monophonic raw material, recorded as static audio.
Sampler / drum sourceDigitakt II → M4 in 3/4. Role: clocked drums and resampling, performed live.
Recorder / arrangerAbleton Arrangement. Role: where audio is captured, edited, and automated.
MonitoringDT 990 Pro. Role: a known reference — and a known liar at 7–10 kHz, which the note must warn about.

The teaching-artifact template

Template

The seven blocks of a teachable lab note

The fixed order
Write them in this sequence — the order is the teaching
1 — ThesisOne sentence: the felt result the method produces. The reader's target.
2 — Reader profileOne sentence: who the stranger is, what they may assume, what they may not.
3 — RefusalsWhat the method does not do. Stated as plainly as the instructions.
4 — Rig & rolesThe exact path, then the role of each piece so it can be substituted.
5 — MechanismThe core reproducible move, with ranges, orders, and the clock named.
6 — Steps + decision checksEach step ends with a check the reader answers without you.
7 — What is lostAn honest note of what the document cannot carry.
Why this order, not another
  • Thesis before gear because "how" is meaningless before "why." A reader who does not know the target cannot judge their own result.
  • Refusals before steps because what to avoid is as much the method as what to do. "Do not add a riser" is an instruction, not a footnote.
  • What is lost, last because the reader needs the whole method in hand before they can understand which part of it is theirs to supply.

How to capture a method as you run it

Capture

Document live, because memory rewrites the method

The capture discipline
  • Run your method once with a notebook or a text file open beside the DAW. Every time your hand moves, write what it did and the value — sidechain release 120 ms, not "set the release." The instinct you do not record is the instinct the stranger will miss.
  • Capture the order as it happens, even when it surprises you. If you EQ before reverb without thinking, that ordering is a step.
  • Mark every moment you "judged by ear." Those are the spots that need a listening test in the final note, or an honest flag that the reader must supply their own judgement.
  • Screen-record or photograph the routing and the device chain. A picture of the signal path settles a dozen questions a stranger would otherwise ask.
Two capture files, kept separate
What goes where
method_raw.txtThe unedited live log — every move, value, and order, written as it happened. Messy on purpose.
method_note.htmlThe clean teaching artifact, written from the raw log into the seven-block template.
  • Keep the raw log. When the stranger's rebuild breaks, you compare their result against the raw log to find which instinct never made it into the clean note.
Decision check
  • Have I described my rig as both an exact path and a set of roles a stranger can substitute?
  • Do I have the seven-block template ready, in order, before I start writing?
  • Did I capture the method live — every value, every order, every by-ear moment — rather than from memory?
  • Are my raw log and my clean note separate files, so I can trace any break back to its source?
