The template, and the difference between good and bad instructions

Here is the lab-note template filled in once, end to end, so you can see what each block looks like when it works. Then a gallery of instructions rewritten from bad to good — because the failure of most teaching notes is not missing steps but vague ones. The vocabulary of a teachable instruction is small; this page is that vocabulary.

The template, filled in

A worked example using the breathing-bass sidechain method. Read it as a shape to pour your own method into — not as content to copy.

Worked example

"The breathing bass" as a teaching note

1 — Thesis
  • The bass should swell back between kicks like a held breath, so the low end pulses without anyone hearing a compressor work.
2 — Reader profile
  • You can arm a track, drop a device, and read a gain-reduction meter. You do not share my kick, my room, or my ear. Wherever I say "by ear," you supply your own.
3 — Refusals
  • No audible pumping. If you can hear it duck, it is wrong.
  • No fader war — the kick and bass coexist by timing, not by one being louder.
  • No multiband, no fancy tools. One compressor, one sidechain.
4 — Rig & roles
  • Exact: Ableton Compressor on the bass track, sidechain input from the kick track, kick taken Pre-FX.
  • Roles: a compressor that can be triggered by another track, on the bass; a clean kick source as the trigger. Any DAW with a sidechain compressor substitutes directly.
5 — Mechanism
  • The compressor on the bass listens to the kick, not to the bass. Each kick presses the bass down; between kicks the bass rises back. Ratio 4:1, attack 1 ms, release 120 ms as a starting point. Clock master is the DAW; both tracks run on the same transport so the trigger lands on the beat.
6 — Steps + decision checks
  • Drop the compressor on the bass; enable sidechain; set source to the kick, Pre-FX.
  • Lower the threshold until the gain-reduction meter shows about 3 dB per kick.
  • Tune the release by ear until the swell-back is audible but the pump is not.
Decision check
  • Does the bass swell back between kicks, or does it stay flat? (Flat = release too long.)
  • Can you hear the compressor working? (Yes = too much reduction or release too short.)
  • Does the meter dip steadily about 3 dB on every kick? (Wild dips = the source has bleed; use a clean kick.)
7 — What is lost
  • The exact release that feels like breathing depends on your kick's tail and your tempo. I gave you 120 ms and the listening test; the final number is a judgement only your ear can make. That judgement is yours, and it is the part of this I cannot hand over.

Good vs bad instructions

Same intent, two ways. The bad version is not wrong — it is unreproducible. Read the right-hand column as the thing you are training yourself to write.

Vagueness → reproducibility
"Open the filter as it builds.""Automate the low-pass cutoff from 600 Hz to 3.5 kHz over bars 94–140, as an S-curve."
"Add some movement to the drone.""Route LFO → Timbre at depth ~25, sine, one cycle per 10–20 s, unsynced."
"Sidechain the bass so it pumps nicely.""Sidechain bass to kick, 4:1, release 120 ms, lower threshold to ~3 dB reduction per kick, then tune release by ear until the swell is audible and the pump is not."
"EQ the drone so it doesn't clash.""High-pass the drone at 120 Hz, 24 dB/oct, before the reverb — the bass owns everything below."
"Make the intro atmospheric.""Drone only from bar 1, atmosphere underneath at -22 dB, nothing rhythmic until bar 32."
"Keep it from getting muddy.""Kick owns 40–80 Hz, bass owns 80–180 Hz, nothing else below 200 Hz. Check by soloing the pair."

Why the bad ones fail

  • They assume the reader's ear matches yours
  • "Nicely," "some," "atmospheric" are your taste, unstated
  • No number means no way to confirm the result
  • The reader can perform them and still be wrong

Why the good ones work

  • A range or value the reader can dial exactly
  • An order, where order changes the result
  • A checkable observation — what to hear or see
  • An honest "by ear" flag where judgement is required

The four ingredients of a teachable instruction

  • A value or range. "600 Hz to 3.5 kHz," not "open it." A number can be reproduced; a verb cannot.
  • An order, when it matters. "EQ before reverb." If swapping two steps changes the result, the order is part of the instruction.
  • A checkable result. "Until the meter shows 3 dB per kick." The reader confirms they hit the intention, not just that they acted.
  • An honest by-ear flag. "Tune by ear until the swell is audible." Where judgement is unavoidable, name it and give the listening test — do not disguise it as a fixed number.
Restraint, applied to writing

The same discipline as the music: do less, but make every instruction load-bearing. A teaching note with twenty vague steps transfers less than one with seven exact ones. Cut any step the reader could not get wrong, and sharpen any step they could.
