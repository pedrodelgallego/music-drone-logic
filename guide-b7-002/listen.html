Live Resampling · 02 — Listen

Hear the loop before you build it

Three performers who feed their own output back into the set. Listen for the moment a captured phrase returns transformed — and for the discipline that keeps it from swamping the source. These are reference lessons in the Block 7 repertoire; study them, then make something that is yours.

Three reference performances

  • Transforms applied slowly; the source survives the return
  • Listen for what re-enters versus what is genuinely new

What to listen for

In each set, find the instant a captured phrase comes back. Ask: is it reversed, pitched, filtered, or chopped? Does it sit under the source or replace it? Could you tell it was the set's own output if you hadn't watched it being captured?

A focused listening pass

  • One section, three times. Pick a single 60–90 second passage from one set. First pass: follow the live source. Second pass: ignore the source and track only what re-enters. Third pass: watch the levels — does the return stay under the source?
  • Name the transform. For every returning layer, write one word: reverse, pitch, chop, freeze, filter. If you can't name it, the performer hid the seam well — that is the goal.
  • Find the gain ceiling. Listen for the moment a loop could have run away and didn't. Something held it down — a fader pull, a filter close, a limiter. That restraint is the lesson, not the capture.
  • Count the bars. Tap along and estimate the capture length. Almost always it is a clean musical count — 1, 2, 4, or 8 bars — never a ragged hand-timed snippet.
Originality constraint

Borrow the discipline, never the material. Do not capture or recreate these artists' phrases. Study how they time, transform, and tame the loop — then build a section out of your own sound, captured live from your own rig.
