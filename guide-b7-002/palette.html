Lab Note B7-002
Live Resampling · 05 — Capture palette

Capture and transform recipes

A vocabulary of transforms that make a capture read as new material, not as a doubled mix. Each recipe is one move — stutter, pitch, reverse, freeze. The rule that governs all of them: one transform per capture. Stacking them just makes mud.

The test that decides everything

If the replay sounds like the source with a delay on it, you have not transformed it — you have just doubled the mix. Reverse it, pitch it, chop it, or freeze it until it reads as a new voice.

Recipe — Reverse

  • What it does. The captured envelope swells toward the beat instead of decaying away from it — anticipation, for free, from material already in key.
  • Ableton. Double-click the captured clip → Reverse in Clip view. Or load it in Simpler and engage Reverse.
  • Digitakt II. Set the sample's playback to reverse on the track's source page.
  • Role. Best as a bed or a transition swell. High-passed and reverbed, a reversed capture becomes a riser that came from the room itself.

Recipe — Pitch / octave

Pitch moves and what they buy you
Down one octave (-12 st)A sub layer under the source. Low-pass it; let it reinforce, not muddy.
Up one octave (+12 st)A ghostly high counter-line. High-pass and reverb so it floats above the mix.
Up a fifth (+7 st)A harmonic shimmer that stays consonant because the source already defined the key.
Down a fifth (-7 st)A darker shadow of the phrase; pairs with a long tail for depth.
  • Ableton. Transpose in Simpler, or set the clip's Pitch in semitones. Warp on, Mode Complex Pro for clean pitch shifts of full-mix captures.
  • Digitakt II. Tune the sample on the track's SRC page; pitch is sample-rate based, so extreme shifts also change character — use that.
  • Discipline. A pitched capture is the most exposed role — a hook. Use it once, as a peak event, never as a constant bed.

Recipe — Sample-start chop / stutter

  • What it does. Move the start point so the loop falls on an offbeat — same audio, new rhythm. Retrigger short slices and the phrase becomes a stutter that is in key by construction.
  • Ableton. In Simpler, drag the sample start marker; map it to a control to chop live. Beat Repeat after RS RETURN for grid-locked stutters (e.g. 1/16 repeats, Chance 30%).
  • Digitakt II. P-lock sample start per trig, and use retrigs (1/16 or 1/32) on chosen steps. The Digitakt's per-trig control is built for exactly this.
  • Role. A counter-rhythm — the highest clash risk. Quantise the launch to the bar and keep it under the source.

Recipe — Freeze

  • What it does. Holds one slice of the capture into a sustained pad or drone — the rhythm dissolves into texture. A captured bar becomes the room the next section lives in.
  • Ableton. Grain Delay on the capture (Freq low, Spray 60 ms, Feedback high) freezes a grain cloud. Or a long-tail reverb with high Dry/Wet on a held slice.
  • Digitakt II / Octatrack. Loop a tiny region of the buffer with a long amp release, or set a Flex slice to loop and ride the filter.
  • Role. A bed. Filter and reverb until it names nothing — the safest return, almost impossible to clash.

Which transform for which role

Match the transform to the layer you decided on in page 03
BedReverse, or Freeze — heavily filtered, reverbed, low in the mix
Counter-rhythmSample-start chop / stutter — quantised, under the source
HookPitch up or down — once, exposed, as a peak event
Sub reinforcementPitch down an octave, low-passed — reinforce, don't muddy
One move, then commit

Pick the transform that serves the role and stop. A reversed, pitched, chopped, frozen capture is no longer a capture — it is noise that happens to be in key. The restraint is the recipe.
