Lab Note B7-002
Live Resampling · 03 — Decisions

The resampling loop, decided in advance

Resampling on stage is the move where the system feeds itself: capture a few bars of what you are already playing, then replay that capture as new material — pitched, reversed, chopped, frozen. The theory below exists so that nothing is decided under pressure. Settle every choice here; the build page only executes them.

The loop, in one diagram

Live mix
→
Capture (4–8 bars)
→

→
Replay as new layer
→

The output becomes the input. The danger and the magic are the same thing: a captured layer played back over the source it came from compounds — which is why timing and gain discipline matter more here than anywhere else in the set.

Why it is worth the risk

A resampled layer is, by definition, perfectly in key and in tempo with the set — it is the set. It gives you a new element that could never clash, captured live, with the room's energy already baked in. No preset, no preloaded loop, can match that.

Decision 1 — What kind of layer do you want back?

A capture can return in three roles. Decide which one your section needs before you reach for the record button — the role determines the transform, the level, and where it sits in the spectrum.

A bed

  • Filtered and reverbed until it reads as atmosphere
  • Sits low, fills the room, names nothing
  • The safest first capture — hard to make it clash

A counter-rhythm

  • Sample-start chopped so it lands off the source's grid
  • Adds groove without a new instrument
  • Highest clash risk — must be timed to the bar

A hook

  • Pitched up or down into a clear melodic counter-line
  • The most exposed; demands the cleanest capture
  • Use once, as a peak event, not as a constant

Decision 2 — One capture engine, chosen now

Pick one rig path and rehearse it — switching mid-set is a routing change under pressure
Ableton resampling trackAn audio track with Audio From → Resampling, armed. Captures the full internal mix post-master-fader. Replay via Simpler or a clip slot. Most flexible transforms.
Digitakt II samplingSample the incoming MicroFreak or the Digitakt's own output via the sampling page. Lands on a track you can p-lock and sequence immediately. Tightest clock.
Octatrack MKII (optional)The purpose-built tool: a record buffer captures continuously, a Flex machine plays it back, all clocked. If you own one, this is the resampling instrument the others approximate.
One capture engine per set

Decide before the set which box does the resampling. Switching capture paths mid-performance is exactly the kind of move that goes wrong in front of people. Pick one, rehearse it, commit. The setup page wires the one you choose.

Decision 3 — Capture timing

  • Arm early. Set the buffer length and arm it before the section you want to catch begins. Arming late is the classic failure: you decide to capture, then fumble for record while the moment passes.
  • Quantise the start. Set capture start to launch quantised to 1 bar. You press at any time in the bar; the recorder waits for the boundary. The capture is on-grid even when your hand is not.
  • Choose a musical length. 4 or 8 bars, never a ragged hand-timed snippet. A clean bar count loops without a seam; an odd length fights the grid forever.
  • Capture the densest bar. The moment the perc and a long echo coincide is more useful than a sparse intro bar. You want the capture to carry character, because you are about to strip it down with a transform.

Decision 4 — Latency and clock, understood

Round-trip latency

  • Output → converter → capture → replay adds delay at every hop
  • A capture replayed over its source can land a few ms late and smear the transient
  • Measure the round-trip once in rehearsal and nudge the replay early to compensate

Clock alignment

  • Capture must be clocked to the same master as everything else
  • A buffer recorded against free-running audio drifts and never loops clean
  • Quantise both the capture start and the replay launch to the bar

These two problems compound. If the clock drifts, no fixed latency offset can save the loop — it will be early on one bar and late on the next. Lock the clock first (one master, everything slaved), then measure and correct the constant round-trip offset. Order matters: alignment before compensation.

Decision 5 — The gain ceiling

Gain ceiling on the loop

When a capture is replayed back into the mix it came from, levels accumulate. Put a limiter on the resample return and bring the replay in at least -6 dB below the source. A resampling loop that gains stage by stage is a feedback system in disguise. Decide the ceiling now, set it in setup, and never raise it live.

  • Return at -6 dB or lower. The capture already contains the full mix; it does not need to compete with it.
  • Limiter as a hard backstop, not a sound. Ceiling at -1 dBFS on the return, so a runaway loop is caught before it reaches the master, not after.
  • One capture live at a time. Capturing a mix that already contains a previous capture is how a single resample becomes a wall in four bars. Retire the old layer before the new one returns.
Decision summary

By the end of this page you should be able to state, in one sentence: "I will capture N bars on the bar with [engine], transform it by [one move] into a [bed / counter-rhythm / hook], return it at -6 dB through a limiter, nudged X ms early." If any blank is still open, fill it before you touch the gear.
