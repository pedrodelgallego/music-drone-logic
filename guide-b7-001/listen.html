Lab Note B7-001
Parameter Riding · 02 — Listen

Three performers who ride, not press play

Before you map a single control, listen to people whose tracks change because their hands change them. Each of these is a workshop replication lesson — open it, study what they touch, then steal the discipline and leave the set. Listen for the move you cannot see: the filter that opens over four minutes, the send that blooms once, the mute that lands exactly on the bar.

What to listen for

The listening question

For every change you hear, ask one thing: was that a hand reacting to the room, or a curve that fires the same way every night? The answer tells you whether it belongs under your fingers or inside the machine.

The three lessons

Listen 01

Jeff Mills — Exhibitionist

Why it teaches riding
  • Mills performs three machines at once and never stops moving — but the moves are small. Watch how much of the energy comes from muting and unmuting drum tracks, not from new sounds arriving.
  • The 909 ride is the lesson: he is constantly adjusting level, tuning, and decay on individual voices in real time. Notice that no single move is dramatic — the density is the sum of many tiny rides.
  • Steal the economy of attention: his hands are always busy, but each gesture is to one thing. Watch the replication lesson for the exact technique to rehearse.
Carry into your map
  • Mutes are arrangement performed live. Decide which of your Digitakt tracks you will drop and bring back, and quantise them.
  • One ride at a time. If both hands are on continuous controls, you have nothing left to recover with.
Listen 02

Surgeon — live at Boiler Room x Dekmantel, Amsterdam 2014

Why it teaches riding
  • This is a study in filter and feedback as the main instrument. Long sections change almost entirely because Surgeon is opening a filter or pushing a delay toward the edge of runaway, then pulling it back before it tips.
  • Listen to the transitions: the tension builds because feedback piles for a handful of bars, then resolves as the next element lands. That is the exact gesture you will map to your echo encoder.
  • Notice the restraint at the top of the range — he flirts with self-oscillation and never quite lets it loose. The replication lesson breaks the technique down.
Carry into your map
  • Your feedback encoder needs a hard ceiling below runaway. This set is why you set the Max to ~70 %, not 100 %.
  • A transition can be a single continuous ride — no new clip required. Rehearse the four-to-eight-bar feedback push as one gesture.
Listen 03

Richie Hawtin — DE9: Closer to the Edit

Why it teaches riding
  • DE9 is the opposite extreme: an enormous number of tiny fragments, ridden and re-combined. It teaches the upper limit — how much a performer can hold under their hands before the performance becomes a juggling act.
  • Listen for how loops are filtered and faded in and out as continuous gestures, not hard cuts. The edit is the ride; nothing arrives abruptly.
  • Use it as a warning as much as a model: this is virtuoso multi-layer control built over years. The replication lesson shows what to attempt and what to leave for later.
Carry into your map
  • Start with four controls, not forty. DE9 is where you arrive after a year of riding four well.
  • Crossfades and filter fades are rides too — a level ridden up over eight bars reads as an element surfacing, not appearing.

Listening notes — fill these in

  • For each set, name the one parameter that carries the most change. Could you reproduce that move with a single mapped control?
  • Mark a transition you liked. Was it a ride, a mute, or a new clip? Plan to perform it the same way.
  • Find a moment where nothing new enters but the energy still rises. That is the move worth learning before any other.
  • Write down one thing each performer does that you will not attempt yet. Knowing your ceiling is part of the map.
  • Originality constraint: these are references for technique, not material. Do not copy a build, a drum pattern, or a signature gesture. Learn how a hand shapes a system, then shape your own.
