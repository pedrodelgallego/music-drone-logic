Lab Note B7-001
Parameter Riding · 07 — Reflection

Fault, carry-forward, principle

Most of the learning happens here, listening back to a take you cannot edit your way out of. A performance is honest — it shows you exactly where your attention ran out. Answer slowly. Write the answers on the ride sheet.

How you know it worked

Success signals
  • The take sounds intentional even with your hands off it.
  • No single move is audible as a discrete "event."
  • The peak arrives because things opened, not because things were added.
  • You rode four controls and never reached for a fifth.
  • The feedback encoder visited the edge and always came home.
  • Every mute landed on the bar.
  • You could play this set again tomorrow from the ride sheet alone.
  • The master never went near the danger zone, and your hands never went near the master.
  • The take returns to its opening state — it loops.
Failure signals
  • You can hear individual knob moves as events — the rides were too fast.
  • Your attention ran out and a control sat frozen for a section.
  • The feedback encoder got parked high and the echo ran toward a wall.
  • Mutes lurched mid-bar because launch quantise was off.
  • The energy came from new elements arriving, not from things opening.
  • You wished you had a fifth or sixth control — a sign the map, not the take, was wrong.
  • The take cannot loop because nothing returned to its opening state.
  • A hand drifted toward the master or the kick.

The fault

Name your single biggest fault

Almost every first ride take fails the same way: too fast, too busy, or too many controls. Pick the one that hurt yours most. A fault you can name is a fault you can rehearse away — a vague "it felt off" is one you will repeat.

  1. Which of the four controls did I ride best, and which did I neglect?
  2. Where did my attention run out — what section went static because I was busy elsewhere?
  3. Was any move audible as an event? Which one, and how much slower should it have been?
  4. Did the feedback encoder ever scare me? Did I always bring it home?
  5. Did the peak come from opening, or did I cheat and add something?
  6. Were four controls too few, exactly right, or secretly too many?
  7. Did any mute land off the bar? Why — quantise, or my timing?
  8. Could a stranger play my set from the ride sheet, or does the map only live in my head?

The carry-forward

What this note hands to the rest of the workshop
The four-control disciplineAny live set you build later starts by choosing the few controls that matter, not by mapping everything
Min/Max as safetyRange-clamping a mapping so a careless turn stays musical transfers to every performance patch you ever map
Hands vs. curvesThe habit of sorting every move into "ride it" or "print it" is the core of all the performance-systems work ahead
The ride sheetA reproducible plan on paper is what turns a one-off jam into a set you can play again
One change, then re-ride

Wait a day. Change one thing on the ride sheet — usually "slow down the move that rushed." Record v2. Compare. The discipline is the same as the studio note: one deliberate change beats ten panicked ones.

Final principle

Control is not reaching for everything. It is mapping few things well, knowing where each one ends, and riding them slower than feels natural. Four controls you can watch and recover beat twelve that ride you.
