Lab Note B7-001
Parameter Riding · 06 — The build

Build a performable arrangement and record a ride take

Now you perform. This page builds a set you can play with four controls, rehearses the gestures until they are slow and reliable, and records a live ride take into Arrangement that you could repeat in front of a room. Keep the ride map and setup page open — every step refers to the exact mappings and ranges you built there. Each step is small, each has a decision checkpoint, and every gesture is spelled out.

Step 01

Prepare the project for performance

Start from a finished track, not a blank set
  • Open the Lab Note B1-001 project — or any project with a bass take, a drone take, drums, and an atmosphere layer already recorded. You are performing existing material, not designing sounds.
  • File → Save a Copy as lab_b7_001_ride.als in a dedicated folder. Never perform on your master arrangement; the ride session is a throwaway you re-record freely.
  • Tab to confirm you are in Arrangement view. The ride is recorded here.
Lay the takes out as loopable clips
  • The performance plays against looping material, not a fixed arrangement. In Session view, drag each recorded take into a clip slot: DT DRUMS, MF BASS, MF DRONE, ATMOS.
  • Set each clip to loop. Confirm the loop brace is exactly on bar boundaries so nothing drifts over a long set.
  • If your drum take is a single consolidated clip, split it into separate kick, hat, perc, texture clips (or use the Digitakt's own per-track mutes). Ride 04 needs independent mutes.
Set the global launch quantise
  • Top-left of Live, set the global launch quantisation to 1 Bar. Every clip launch and mute now lands on the next bar line, not the instant you press.
  • Set the metronome count-in to 1 Bar so your recorded take starts clean.
  • Raise the buffer to 256 samples — low enough that encoder moves feel instant, stable enough for a long recording. If a turn feels laggy, drop to 128; latency on a continuous ride is felt as the hand and the sound disagreeing.
Why perform existing audio rather than the live hardware
  • The hardware takes were captured once, clean, in the studio note. Performing against the recorded clips means the sound design is frozen and only the movement is live — which is exactly the skill this note isolates.
  • It also makes the take repeatable. A live MicroFreak patch drifts; a recorded clip does not. The only variable left is your hands, so a fault you hear is a fault in the riding, not in the source.
  • Later, once the four-control discipline is muscle memory, you can swap a recorded clip for the live box and ride its front-panel knobs too. Walk before that.
Decision check
  • Do all four clips loop seamlessly when launched? Loop each for two minutes and listen for a click at the seam.
  • Can each drum element mute independently? Tap each mute and confirm only that element drops.
  • Is this a copy, not my master? Confirm the title bar reads lab_b7_001_ride.
Step 02

Confirm the four maps and their ranges

Re-verify each mapping before you trust it
  • ⌘ M into MIDI map mode and open the MIDI Mappings browser. You should see exactly four rows — three encoders and the mute pads. If there are more, delete the strays.
  • Confirm each Min/Max against the ride map: 600 Hz / 3.5 kHz on bass cutoff, 35 % / 70 % on echo feedback, -12 dB / -3 dB on drone send.
  • ⌘ M out. Push each encoder to both physical stops and watch its parameter clamp at the range edges — never beyond.
Set the opening state
Where every control sits at bar 1
BASS FLT600 Hz — closed, underwater
FEEDBK35 % — floor, tension valve shut
VERB-12 dB — dry, intimate
MUTESDrone + atmos only. Kick, hat, perc all muted.
  • The set starts as a quiet drone in a small room. Everything you do for the next eight minutes opens out from this single state.
Bar 1: drone only → ride → filter opens, kick in → ride → peak: bright + wet → ride → strip back → close to drone
Decision check
  • Exactly four mappings, each clamped to its musical range?
  • Does the opening state sound like a small, dark room — drone only, dry, closed?
  • Is the master off every surface? Wiggle every control; the master fader must not move.
Step 03

Rehearse ride 01 — the bass filter, alone

Isolate the gesture
  • Launch the bass and drone clips. Mute everything else. You want to learn the filter gesture with nothing competing for your attention.
  • Hand on encoder 1 only. Start at 600 Hz. Over a slow count of 16 bars, open to about half travel (~1.2 kHz). Stop. Listen for a full minute at that value.
  • Then continue, over another 16 bars, to the top (~3.5 kHz). Hold. Then bring it all the way home over 16 bars.
Find the S-curve in your hand
  • Notice your hand naturally wants to ease in and ease out — slow at the start, slow at the target. Lean into that; it is the S-curve that reads as intention.
  • If you can hear the filter move as an event, you moved too fast. Halve the speed and try the same 16 bars again.
  • Do this for a full five minutes. The gesture has to become muscle memory, because during the take your attention will be split across four things.
Decision check
  • Can I open from 600 Hz to 3.5 kHz over 16 bars without the room hearing a single "moment of change"?
  • Does the bass stay musical at every point in the travel? (It should — the range guarantees it. If anything sounds wrong, re-check the Min/Max.)
  • Can I return to 600 Hz cleanly, so the take could loop?
Step 04

Rehearse ride 02 — feedback as a transition

The pile-and-resolve gesture
  • With drums and bass looping, route a little of the perc to the echo (a send, or the Digitakt p-lock you already programmed). Hand on encoder 2.
  • From the 35 % floor, push feedback up over 4 bars toward 65 %. The echoes pile and the tension climbs. Hold one bar at the top.
  • As the next section is about to land — a mute change, a filter open — pull feedback back to 35 % over 2 bars. The pile resolves into the new section.
Learn where the edge is
  • Carefully push to the 70 % ceiling and hold. Hear how close it is to runaway — the echoes nearly self-sustain. This is the edge Surgeon flirts with. Memorise how it feels so you never reach it by accident.
  • Then practise the recovery: from the ceiling, get back to 35 % fast but smoothly. This is your panic path if a pile gets away from you.
  • Never end this rehearsal with feedback parked high. Always leave it at the floor.
Decision check
  • Can I build a pile over 4 bars and resolve it into the next section, on the bar?
  • Do I know exactly how the 70 % ceiling sounds and feels — and can I get back from it in two bars?
  • Did I leave the encoder at 35 %? It must never rest high.
Step 05

Rehearse ride 03 — pair the reverb with the filter

Two hands, one swell
  • Hand 1 on encoder 1 (bass filter), hand 2 on encoder 3 (drone reverb send). Start both at their floors: 600 Hz and -12 dB.
  • Over 32 bars, open both together — filter toward 3.5 kHz, send toward -3 dB. They reach their peaks at the same moment.
  • The room should feel one large swell — brighter and bigger at once — not two separate knob moves. If you can pick out which is which, your speeds are mismatched; sync them.
The return
  • Bring both home over 16 bars. The track shrinks and darkens together — the peak deflates as one gesture.
  • Practise leading slightly with the filter and trailing with the reverb, so the brightness arrives a touch before the space. This tiny offset reads as anticipation.
Decision check
  • Do filter and send read as a single swell, not two moves?
  • Can I run both hands for 48 bars without one stalling while I think about the other?
  • Does the return deflate cleanly back to the dark, dry state?
Step 06

Rehearse ride 04 — the mutes as structure

Prepare, then release on the bar
  • With launch quantise at 1 bar, a mute you trigger anywhere in a bar lands at the next bar line. Use that: prepare the change a beat early, let the quantise place it.
  • Rehearse the core arrangement moves in order: bring the kick in, then hat, then perc; later drop perc, thin the hat; finally strip back to kick + drone.
  • Never drop the kick mid-floor without a plan. The room feels a missing kick instantly — keep it on unless a deliberate breakdown is the point.
Mutes plus rides together
  • Combine: as you bring the kick in, the bass filter is already climbing. As you drop the perc for a strip-back, the reverb send recedes. Mutes mark the sections; the encoders shape the energy inside them.
  • This is the whole performance in miniature. Run the full sequence — drone only → kick → bass-open → full → peak → strip → close — once, slowly, before you ever hit record.
Decision check
  • Do all mutes land on the bar, never mid-bar?
  • Can I run a section change (mute) and an energy ride (encoder) at the same moment without fumbling either?
  • Is the kick always on except where I deliberately want a breakdown?
Step 07

Write the ride sheet — the eight-minute plan

One page, on paper, beside the rig
  • A performance is rehearsed, not improvised from nothing. Write a ride sheet: time, section, and the one move that defines it. You will glance at this, not the screen.
The ride sheet — your map of the eight minutes
0:00 — INTRODrone only, dry, closed. Hands off. Let the patch's own LFOs breathe.
1:00 — KICK INUnmute kick on the bar. Begin opening BASS FLT off the floor.
2:00 — BASS / HAT INUnmute hat. Bass clip surfacing; filter climbing toward 1.2 kHz.
3:00 — FULLUnmute perc. Both hands begin the paired filter+reverb swell.
4:30 — PEAKBASS FLT + VERB at their tops. One short FEEDBK pile into the moment. Nothing new enters.
5:30 — STRIPDrop perc, thin hat. Filter and reverb recede together.
6:30 — OUTRODrop bass, then kick. Close filter to 600 Hz. Drone + atmos carry the exit.
8:00 — ENDEverything back at the opening state — the take loops.
  • Note the thesis of the genre in the PEAK row: the biggest moment arrives because things opened, not because things were added. That is the move this whole note exists to teach.
Decision check
  • Is the ride sheet on paper, beside the rig, readable in dim light?
  • Does each section have exactly one defining move — not a list of things to juggle?
  • Does the peak come from opening, not from new elements arriving?
Step 08

Record the ride take

Arm automation recording
  • In Arrangement, enable the Automation Arm button (the red switch near transport) so encoder and mute moves are written as automation envelopes onto the timeline.
  • Confirm all four controls are at their opening-state values (Step 02). Position the playhead at bar 1.
  • F9 for Arrangement Record. Play the full eight minutes and ride the four controls against your sheet — slower than feels natural, the whole way.
Perform the whole arc in one pass
  • Do not stop to fix a fumble. A take is a performance — finish it. Mistakes are information about what to rehearse, not reasons to abort.
  • Keep the FEEDBK encoder honest: visit the pile, resolve it, return to 35 %. Never let it sit high while your hands are elsewhere.
  • Watch the master meter stays under -3 dBFS through the peak's reverb bloom. If it spikes, the VERB ride is too wet — note it for the next take, do not fix it live.
Where most first takes break
  • The lull at 5:00. After the peak, attention drops and a control freezes. The strip-back is not a rest — it is an active recede on filter and reverb. Keep both hands moving through it.
  • Rushing the open. The intro feels long while you wait, so the hand opens the filter early. Trust the sheet: nothing happens for the first minute, on purpose.
  • Forgetting the feedback floor. A pile resolves but the encoder is left at 50 %, and the next pile starts from a danger position. Always return it to 35 % the moment a transition lands.
  • Muting on the press, not the bar. If a mute lurches, your finger is fighting the quantise — prepare the change a beat early and let Live place it.
Two takes, keep the calmer one
  • Record the eight minutes twice. The second take is almost always calmer — the first burns off nervous energy, and only on the second does your hand trust the slow speed.
  • Listen to both back to back. Keep the one where you cannot hear the moves as events. Calm beats busy every time; a performance that draws attention to the performer has failed at hypnosis.
  • Do not comp the two together. A ride is a single continuous gesture across eight minutes — splicing two performances re-introduces exactly the seams and lurches the slow riding was meant to avoid. Pick one whole take.
  • After: right-click each automation lane → Simplify Envelope to thin breakpoint clutter while keeping the shape your hand drew. This removes the micro-jitter of an encoder without flattening the intentional curve.
Decision check
  • Did the peak arrive because things opened, not because things were added? Play 4:00–5:00 blind and check.
  • Can I hear any move as a discrete "event"? If yes, that gesture was too fast — rehearse it slower and re-record.
  • Did the take return to the opening state so it loops? Play the seam between 8:00 and 0:00.
  • Was the master ever in danger? Confirm peaks stayed under -3 dBFS across the whole take.
Step 09

Review, refine, and capture the keeper

Audition the recorded ride
  • Play the kept take from the top with your hands off. This is the test: a good ride sounds intentional even when no one is touching anything.
  • Find the weakest 16 bars. Usually it is a gesture that rushed or a mute that landed without a ride to support it. Note it on the ride sheet.
  • If one section is wrong, you can punch in: loop that region, arm automation, and re-ride only those bars over the existing take. Surgery after performance — same discipline as the studio note.

Decision check
  • Does the take sound intentional with my hands off it?
  • Could I sit at the rig and play this set again from the ride sheet alone?
  • Is there one move I now know I must rehearse before the next take? Name it.
