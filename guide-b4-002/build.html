Lab Note B4-002
Saturation · 06 — The build

Build a track one nonlinear stage at a time

This is the deep page. We assemble a full original track where the structure is staged saturation: grit baked at the source, density built on the bus, the whole intensity arc driven by pushing harder into curves rather than adding parts. Every step has exact dB values, a decision checkpoint, and a stated commitment point. Keep the setup page open — its gain ledger is the contract this page assumes.

Step 01

Set up the project for honest saturation

New set, clean headroom
  • ⌘ N, Tab to Arrangement. Tempo 130, 4/4 — a touch faster than the hypnotic build; this track leans industrial.
  • ⌘ , → Audio: MOTU M4, 48000 Hz, buffer 256. Inputs as on the setup page.
  • Right-click the ruler → Time Ruler Format → Time so positions read m:ss.
  • Create audio tracks: DT KICK, DT HATS/PERC, MF BASS, MF DRONE, ATMOS. Two returns: A — REVERB, B — ECHO.
The honesty tool — A/B at equal loudness
  • Saturation flatters. Before you build anything, decide how you will judge it: every nonlinear stage gets its output trimmed to match bypass, and you A/B with that trim in place.
  • Put a Utility at the very end of the master, gain at 0 dB, as a manual loudness-match when comparing whole-track versions.
  • Keep a notepad open. Every stage you commit gets one line: DT KICK Saturator, Analog Clip, +4 dB, out -4 dB. The ledger is the deliverable as much as the audio.
Hygiene
  • ⌘ S → lab_b4-002_sat.als in a dedicated folder. File → Collect All and Save at every session end.
  • Confirm the clock: press play, both boxes start, screens read 130. A take with baked grit recorded against a bad clock is unrecoverable.
Step 02

Bake the kick — stage A at the source

Choose and tune the sample
  • On Digitakt II, project SAT LAB, pattern A01. T1 KICK: a short, dark kick with controlled sub.
  • SRC — Tune the fundamental to A or E. A detuned kick under a tonal bass smears the moment you saturate either.
  • Trigs on 1, 5, 9, 13. Keep it dry — the bite will come from drive, not reverb.
Drive at the source — the first commitment
Digitakt T1 amp page
DriveStart 0 %. Raise slowly while the loop plays. At ~ 40 % the kick gains a clipped corner and an aggressive odd-harmonic bite.
Sample levelStrong but not clipping the box's output — let the Drive be the only clipper. Watch the Digitakt's own meter.
DecayShort enough that the tail is gone before the next bass note.
  • This is irreversible. The grit is now welded into the rendered audio. That is the point — it grounds the whole track in a flavour no plugin can wobble.
Record a dedicated kick pass
  • Mute T2–T4. Arm DT KICK, monitor Auto. Record 4 minutes of kick-only.
  • Confirm the clip peaks -12 to -6 dBFS — headroom for stage A in Ableton.
  • A separate kick pass also gives a clean sidechain source later, with no hat bleed.
Stage A in Ableton — the channel curve
DT KICK device chain, in this order
1 — EQ Eight HP28 Hz, 24 dB/oct — before the Saturator, so sub doesn't hog the curve
2 — Utility (input gain)Set so the kick hits the Saturator at ~ -8 dBFS avg
3 — SaturatorType Analog Clip, Soft Clip on, Drive +4 dB
4 — Saturator OutputTrim -4 dB until on/off match by ear
  • Bypass the Saturator at matched loudness. The driven version should sound denser and more forward, not just louder. If it only sounds louder, your output trim is wrong — fix it before moving on.
Decision check
  • At equal loudness, does the saturated kick beat the clean one? If not, you were chasing level.
  • Does the transient still punch, or did the drive flatten the attack? If flattened, lower Drive or engage Soft Clip harder.
  • Have I written the source Drive % down? I cannot recover it later — it must live in the ledger.
Step 03

Hats and perc — parallel grit

Program and drive at source
  • T2 HAT on 3, 7, 11, 15; T3 PERC one hit on step 8 with a 25–50 % probability condition.
  • Digitakt Drive on T2/T3 at 20–30 % — present, never fizzy.
  • Add 52 % swing and nudge hats a hair late for roll. Record 4 minutes into DT HATS/PERC (kick muted).
Stage A — parallel so the transient survives
DT HATS/PERC chain
1 — EQ HP500 Hz before the curve — no low end to distort
2 — SaturatorType Soft Sine, Drive +3 dB, Dry/Wet 60 %
3 — OutputTrim -2 dB to match
  • Parallel (Dry/Wet 60 %) keeps the dry transient clean while the wet path adds odd-harmonic body underneath — bite without the tick going brittle.
  • On the DT 990 this will sound sharp at 7–10 kHz. Resist cutting; confirm on a second source first.
Decision check
  • Does the hat sit around the kick, or fight it for the same forward space?
  • Is the parallel mix preserving the attack? Flip Dry/Wet to 100 % for a second — if it gets brittle, you'd have killed the tick at full wet.
  • When the probability perc lands, is it a surprise? If predictable, make it rarer.
Step 04

Bass — even body, then rising odd grit

Stage 0 — analog grit at the MicroFreak
  • Shift + Type to init a patch. Oscillator Wavetable, Paraphonic off, root A1.
  • Drive the filter: oscillator level high, Resonance ~ 11 o'clock, Cutoff low. Push until the filter adds an analog, even-leaning grit — this is a nonlinear stage in the hardware.
  • Program 3–5 notes over 16 steps, root on step 1, gaps left for the kick. Save the patch as SAT BASS.
  • Arm MF BASS, monitor Auto, peaks -12 to -6 dBFS. Record 4 minutes, hands off the knobs — the grit is already in the take.
Stage A — the staged plugin chain (order is the recipe)
MF BASS chain, strictly in this order
1 — EQ HP40 Hz, 24 dB/oct — protect the sub before any curve
2 — Dynamic TubeEven-harmonic bias, Drive low — body and warmth, no bite
3 — Utility (input)Set bass to ~ -8 dBFS into the next stage
4 — SaturatorType Medium (rising odd), Drive +2 dB at the start
5 — Saturator OutputTrim to match bypass at +2 dB drive
6 — Compressor (sidechain)After the saturation; keyed to DT KICK, Pre FX
Prove the order matters — do this once
  • Print the chain as is. Then duplicate the track, move the HP to after the Saturator, print again. A/B.
  • The second version has audible sub distortion — the curve was allowed to chew the low end. Keep the first. You have just proven non-commutativity on your own bass.
  • Now move the sidechain compressor before the Saturator on a third copy. The grit no longer ducks against the kick — it masks it. Keep the original. Write the finding in the ledger.
Sidechain settings
Compressor on MF BASS, keyed to DT KICK
Ratio4:1
Attack / Release1 ms / 120 ms
Gain reductionThreshold down until ~ -3 dB per kick
  • Because the comp is after the saturation, the harmonics duck with the fundamental. The grit breathes with the kick instead of fighting it.
Decision check
  • Did the order swap audibly change the sound? If not, one stage is doing nothing — find out which.
  • At matched loudness, does the staged bass sound fuller than the raw take, or just louder?
  • Is the sub clean? Solo below 80 Hz — there should be a tone, not fizz.
Step 05

Drone — the protected, near-linear voice

Capture it clean
  • Shift + Type to init. Oscillator Harmo or Wavetable, Paraphonic on, long attack/release, a slow unsynced LFO → Timbre.
  • Hold, play root A1 + fifth E2. Keep the MicroFreak filter clean here — no overdrive. The drone is the contrast voice.
  • Record 4 minutes. Save patch as SAT DRONE.
One gentle even stage, then protect it
MF DRONE chain
1 — EQ HP120 Hz — drone owns mids, never sub
2 — Dynamic TubeDrive low, even bias — body and a hint of air only
3 — Reverb sendTo Return A, after and fully clean. Never saturate into a long tail.
  • Do not automate any drive up on the drone. Its job is to stay still while the kick and bass grow — the still element is what makes the others' growth read.
Decision check
  • Can I hear grit on the drone? If yes, the Tube is too high — back it off until it is body only.
  • Does the drone create the room the driven elements live in?
  • Flip the reverb to check: is the tail clean, or is it amplifying distortion I added upstream?
Step 06

Stage B — drive on the drum bus

Group and feed it the right level
  • Select DT KICK + DT HATS/PERC → ⌘ G → DRUMS.
  • The summed group arrives hotter than either channel. Pull the group fader until the bus stage sees ~ -10 dBFS — set the input with the fader, not by re-driving the channels.
Roar on the group
Stage B — shared density
ModeSingle band first. Once it works, split: drive 150 Hz – 4 kHz only, leave the sub band clean.
Amount / Drive15 % static — the floor of the build
Tone / ColorSlightly dark, so the added top doesn't stack harshly with the hats' stage A
OutputLevel-match to bypass; confirm the kick transient still pokes through
  • Now there are two saturation stages on the kick: the baked source grit (A) and this bus density (B). They compound — which is exactly why each was kept modest. Two gentle stages beat one extreme one for control.
Decision check
  • Does the bus stage glue the kit, or smear the kick attack? If smeared, lower the drive or protect the transient (Drum Buss Transients +, or split Roar to spare the sub).
  • Bypass A and B together at matched loudness — the dry kit should sound thin but intact. That confirms saturation is shaping, not propping.
Step 07

Build the intensity arc from drive alone

The thesis: automate curves, not parts
  • The peak of this track adds no new element. Energy comes from pushing the existing voices harder into their curves. This step draws those drive automations.
  • A for automation lanes, pencil B for drawing. Bend ramps to S-curves. No ramp shorter than 8 bars; most are 16–48.
The drive automation map
Times → bars at 130 BPM (≈ 14.8 bars/min)
Bass Saturator Drive @ 0:00+2 dB — body only, almost clean
Bass Saturator Drive @ 3:00+6 dB — odd harmonics emerging
Bass Saturator Drive @ 4:30 (peak)+10 dB — full grit, the bass's loudest, densest moment
Bass Saturator Drive @ 6:00+4 dB — recedes for the close
Drum-bus Roar Amount @ 0:00 → 3:0015 % → 25 %
Drum-bus Roar Amount @ 4:3035 % — the kit thickens at the peak
DT KICK Saturator Drive @ 4:30+4 → +9 dB over 16 bars, then back
Drone Tube DriveFlat — never automated. The still voice that makes the growth audible.
Keep it honest while it grows
  • Drive automation also raises level. Either automate each Saturator's Output down in step with the Drive up, or trust the master and check peaks stay ≤ -3 dBFS.
  • The peak must arrive because things got denser and harder, not merely louder. Play 4:00–5:00 with the master Utility pulling level back to intro-loudness — if the peak still reads as a peak, it is harmonic intensity, not gain.
Perform it on the Launchpad
  • ⌘ M, map the bass Saturator Drive and the drum-bus Roar Amount to two pads. Set each mapping's Min/Max to the dB range above so a full pad sweep covers exactly the musical range.
  • F9 Arrangement Record, ride the two drive lanes slower than feels natural. Two takes, keep the calmer. Simplify Envelope after.
Decision check
  • Play the peak blind: does it feel bigger because of harmonics, or only because of level? Master-Utility test settles it.
  • If I freeze the arrangement and remove the drive automation, does the track go flat? It should — that proves the arc is built from saturation.
  • Did I leave the drone flat? If I automated it too, I lost the still reference.
Step 08

Arrange — sections defined by drive, not parts

Locators and the map
Section → time → bar at 130 BPM
INTRO — 0:00Bar 1 — drone clean, bass at +2 dB drive, no drums
KICK IN — 1:00Bar 15 — driven kick enters, source grit doing the work
BASS IN — 2:00Bar 30 — staged bass surfaces, sidechain breathing
FULL — 3:00Bar 44 — hats + perc, bus drive climbing
PEAK — 4:30Bar 67 — no new element; every drive lane at maximum
STRIP — 5:30Bar 81 — pull bus drive back to 20 %, perc out; the strip makes the peak retroactively bigger
OUTRO — 6:30Bar 96 — bass drive to +4 dB, kick out, clean drone closes
END — 7:30Bar 111
Editing craft
  • Cuts on kick downbeats only. ⌘ E, grid snap on. Overlap take seams by one bar with 4-beat crossfades — hard joins click, and a clicked join into a saturated bus distorts the click.
  • When a section is final, ⌘ J consolidate. One clean clip per section.
  • The PEAK section contains the same clips as FULL. The only difference between them is the drive automation. That contrast is the whole lesson, placed on the timeline.
Decision check
  • Does PEAK differ from FULL by drive alone? If I had to add a part to make PEAK land, the saturation arc wasn't strong enough — go back to Step 07.
  • Does STRIP make the prior fullness feel bigger in hindsight?
Step 09

Stage C — master glue, and the mix pass

Mix to feed the master stage cleanly
Starting balance (peaks)
DRUMS group-9 dB
MF Bass-12 dB
MF Drone-16 dB
Atmosphere-20 dB
Mix sum into master≤ -6 dBFS — headroom for Stage C
  • Low-end pecking order, enforced by HP filters already placed: kick owns 40–80 Hz, bass owns 80–180 Hz. Saturation makes violations worse — fix them here.
The master glue — cohesion only
Master chain
1 — SaturatorType Soft Sine, Soft Clip on, Drive +1 to +2 dB
2 — UtilityFinal trim, peak ≤ -3 dBFS
3 — SpectrumMetering only. No limiter — loudness is a distribution problem this track doesn't have.
  • Toggle Stage C at equal loudness. If you can barely tell, it is doing its job — seating the three earlier stages into one fabric, not creating a sound.
Verification passes
  • Cumulative-saturation check: bypass A, then B, then C in turn and listen. Each removal should thin the track a little. If removing one does nothing, it was redundant — delete it.
  • Sidechain check: bass comp shows steady ~3 dB dips locked to the kick through FULL.
  • Mono check: flip master to mono — the sub must survive; saturation can collapse a wide low end.
  • Quiet check: listen once at low volume. The saturation's real balance shows; if the grit dominates quietly, it is too much.
  • Take the headphones off every 15 minutes — the DT 990's lift fatigues the ear exactly where saturation lives.
Decision check
  • Does each of the three stages earn its place on the bypass test?
  • Is the master stage glue, or am I using it to fix a sound that should have been fixed at the source?

Archive the irreversible decisions
  • File → Collect All and Save. Save the Digitakt project and both MicroFreak patches.
  • Write the final saturation ledger next to the WAV: every stage, its curve, drive, and output trim. The baked source grit (Digitakt Drive %, MicroFreak filter setting) is the part you can never recover from audio — it must be in the ledger or it is gone.
  • Listen elsewhere — phone, laptop speakers. Laptop speakers are brutally honest about over-saturated mids. Wait a day, change one stage, render v2, compare at equal loudness.
  • What you built: a track whose structure is three staged nonlinear curves — baked source grit, automated bus density, a hair of master glue — with the entire intensity arc driven by pushing into those curves rather than adding parts. The drone, kept linear, is the still axis the growth rotates around.
