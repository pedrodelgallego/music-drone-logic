Lab Note B4-002
Saturation · 04 — Setup & signal path

The rig, and where each curve sits

Saturation lives at specific points in the chain, and a nonlinear stage only sounds right if it is hit at the level it was set for. This page locks the rig, lays out a gain ledger every later step refers to, and maps where Ableton's Saturator, Roar, and the per-device Drive sit — at the source, on the bus, and at the end.

The rig

Ableton Live 12 Suite

  • Hosts every plugin nonlinear stage: Saturator, Roar, Drum Buss, Amp, Dynamic Tube
  • Records the hardware grit as audio; automates drive over the arrangement
  • Master MIDI clock for both hardware boxes

Elektron Digitakt II

  • Per-track Drive in the amp page — saturation baked at the source
  • The level you sample at sets how hard a sample clips, permanently
  • Master overdrive on the FX track for whole-kit grit

Arturia MicroFreak

  • Overdrive the analog-style filter for grit before any plugin sees it
  • High oscillator level + resonance pushes the filter into nonlinearity
  • Source of the bass and drone that get staged through Ableton

MOTU M4

  • 4-in / 4-out interface; the converter is itself a (clean) stage to protect
  • Input 1: MicroFreak mono, with a gain knob you set for headroom
  • Inputs 3/4: Digitakt stereo, fixed line level — the box's volume is the record level

Launchpad Pro MK3

  • Maps to drive knobs for live, hand-ridden saturation moves
  • One pad column = one drive lane across the whole arrangement
  • Performs the intensity arc that the build page draws by hand

Beyerdynamic DT 990 Pro

  • Open-back, 250 Ω — honest, but a 7–10 kHz lift
  • That lift exaggerates exactly the harmonics saturation adds
  • Cut less high-end than the headphones beg for; check on a second source
The headphone trap, stated plainly

The DT 990's upper-mid bump sits right where odd harmonics pile up. Saturation that sounds harsh on these headphones is often fine elsewhere — and saturation that sounds perfect here may be too bright in the room. Decide drive amounts by meter and A/B, then confirm on a second source before committing.

Where saturation sits — the staged signal path

Saturation is not one box at the end. It is up to three nonlinear stages, each at a different point. Knowing which stage is which keeps you from stacking grit by accident and from undoing one stage with the next.

hardware grit → recorded audio → channel Saturator → bus Roar / Drum Buss → master glue
The three plugin stages and what each is for
Stage A — source / channelSaturator on the individual track. Defines each voice's core flavour. Set once, automate the drive for the arc.
Stage B — busRoar (multiband / multi-mode) or Drum Buss on the group. Glues elements and adds shared harmonic density. Drive automated for the build.
Stage C — masterA whisper of soft clip / Saturator on the master, for cohesion only. Never the place to create a sound — only to seat one.
The plugins, and the curve each gives you
SaturatorThe workhorse. Type chooses the curve: Analog Clip hard, Soft Sine / Medium gentle, Digital Clip harsh. Drive is the input into the curve.
RoarMultiband, multi-stage saturation. Run it as one band to start; split into bands later to drive only the mids and leave the sub clean.
Drum BussDrum-specific: Drive + Crunch add grit, Boom adds a tuned sub. The Transients control protects the kick's attack from the drive.
Dynamic TubeAsymmetric, even-harmonic tube colour. Use on bass and drone for body, not bite.
Amp / CabinetExtreme, character-rich nonlinearity. Reserve for an atmosphere layer or a one-off stab, not the core voices.

The gain ledger

Because a nonlinear stage responds to level, every stage needs a written input and a written output. This ledger is the contract: fill it once, hit each stage at the level it expects, and the saturation behaves the same every session. The build page assumes these exact numbers.

Out of channel SaturatorTrim output to match bypassSo you judge timbre, not loudness. Honesty control.
Into bus stage (Stage B)~ -10 dBFS on the groupSum of channels arrives a little hotter; the bus drive is calibrated for this. Pull the group fader, not the channels, to hit it.
Into master glue (Stage C)Mix peaks ≤ -6 dBFSThe master stage adds only a hair of harmonics; it needs a clean, headroomed input to do that gently.
Final master peak≤ -3 dBFSNo limiter in this exercise. The headroom is the deliverable.
The golden rule, restated for saturation

Set the level going into each nonlinear stage with a gain stage (Utility or fader), not by riding the Drive knob. Drive shapes the curve; the input gain decides how far up the curve you climb. Mixing the two is how a "warm" idea turns into mud you can't trace.

Hardware nonlinearity — bake it or skip it

Grit at the source — decided when you record, not after
MicroFreak — overdrive the filterPush oscillator level + resonance into the filter input for analog grit. Even-leaning, musical. Baked into the recorded take.
Digitakt II — per-track DriveThe amp-page Drive saturates each sample at source. Hard, odd, immediate. The drum bite of this track lives here.
Digitakt II — sample hotRecording a source loud into the box bakes in clipping no later plugin can undo. Decide this when sampling, not in the mix.
Resample through a plugin stagePrint a saturated result back to audio to freeze stage A, so you can stack stage B cleanly on top.
Setup decision check
  • Is every recorded clip peaking -12 to -6 dBFS, leaving room for stage A to push?
  • Does each nonlinear stage have a written input level and a level-matched output?
  • Have I decided, before recording, which grit is baked in hardware (irreversible) and which stays in plugins (revisable)?
  • Is the sub protected — no element below ~60 Hz routed into a hard stage?
  • One clock, one truth: Ableton is the only MIDI clock master; both boxes receive. A drifting clock ruins a take, and a take with baked-in grit recorded against a bad clock cannot be salvaged — the saturation is welded to a wrong timing.
