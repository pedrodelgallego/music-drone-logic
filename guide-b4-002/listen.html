Lab Note B4-002
Saturation · 02 — Listen

Hear the curve before you reach for it

Before any theory, train the ear. These three records live or die on nonlinearity — the grit is not on top of the music, it is the music. Listen for where the distortion sits in the chain, whether it is even or odd, and what stays clean. Then read the linked replicate lessons in the workshop. Study the craft; your track stays original.

What to listen for in each

Four ears-only questions, every track
Where is the saturation?At the source, on a bus, on the master — or all three? Density that survives a quiet drum hit is staged early; density that only appears when everything plays is bus or master.
Odd or even?Hollow, reedy, aggressive grit reads as odd harmonics. Fuller, sweeter, "fatter" warmth reads as even. Most industrial techno leans hard and odd.
Soft or hard?Does it growl gradually as the track builds (soft), or do peaks sound sliced and buzzy (hard clip)? Listen to the kick's leading edge.
What stays clean?The most driven records still protect something — usually the sub or the reverb tail. Find the element that was deliberately kept linear.

The three references

Ancient Methods — The Jericho Records

  • EBM-flavoured techno where every voice is pushed into hard clip
  • The kick is the clearest lesson: a clipped corner, density baked in at the source
  • Notice what is not saturated — the space around the drums stays open

Regis — Gymnastics

  • Birmingham minimalism: a loop driven until it breathes on its own
  • Saturation is the texture and the groove — strip it and nothing is left

British Murder Boys — Learn Your Lesson

  • Surrey/Regis collaboration: brutal, staged distortion across the whole chain
  • Listen for layered drive — source grit, then bus crush, then master glue
  • The intensity arc is built almost entirely from pushing harder, not adding

How to use these

Listen first with no plugin open. Name where the saturation sits before you read anything. Then open the replicate lesson and check your guess against the breakdown. The goal is a trained ear that can locate a nonlinear stage by sound alone — not a copied arrangement.

A structured listening pass

Method

Three passes per track

Pass 1 — locate
  • Play the loudest section. Ask only: how many distinct saturation stages can I hear, and on what?
  • Write a one-line guess: e.g. kick hard-clipped at source, whole bus soft-driven, master glued.
Pass 2 — flavour
  • Play the quietest section. With less masking, the harmonic flavour is easier to read.
  • Mark each stage odd or even, soft or hard. Note any element that stayed clean.
Pass 3 — arc
  • Play the whole track. Track how intensity grows: is it more drive, or more parts?
  • In all three references the answer is mostly drive. That is the lesson the build page operationalises.
Decision check
  • Could I rebuild the intensity arc with the same parts and only a drive automation? If yes, I understood the record.
  • Which stage is irreversible — where has a peak clearly been sliced off for good?
  • What did the producer protect from saturation, and why would that matter in my own track?
  • Originality constraint: these are ear-training references, not templates. Borrow the discipline — staged drive, protected sub, level-matched decisions — never the loop, the tempo signature, or the arrangement.
