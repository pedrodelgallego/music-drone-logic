Saturation · 03 — Decisions

The theory you decide with

Saturation is the first transformation where the level you feed in is the sound design. A linear stage treats every level the same; a nonlinear stage changes character with input. This page builds the mental model — linear vs nonlinear, what bending a wave produces, soft vs hard, and why the order of the chain stops being cosmetic the moment a stage goes nonlinear.

Linear vs nonlinear — the distinction everything rests on

Linear stage

  • Output is a straight-line scaling of the input
  • Double the input, double the output — no new frequencies
  • Gain, faders, EQ (in level terms), panning, delay, reverb mix
  • Order between two linear stages rarely changes the result

Nonlinear stage

  • The transfer curve bends — louder inputs are treated differently
  • Bending the waveform generates new harmonics that weren't in the source
  • Saturation, clipping, compression, tape, valves, transformers
  • Input level chooses how much of the curve you use, so level = timbre
The core fact

Because a nonlinear stage responds to level, the gain before it is a sound-design control, not a housekeeping one. Two people with the same Saturator and different input gains get two different sounds. Decide the input level on purpose, write it down, and keep it.

A useful picture: the transfer curve is a graph of output-vs-input. A linear stage is a straight diagonal line. A nonlinear stage is that line bent into an S or a flat-topped plateau. A quiet signal only travels along the near-straight middle of the curve and barely distorts; a loud signal climbs into the bent ends, where the bending rewrites its harmonic content.

Harmonic generation — what bending the wave produces

Curve shape → harmonic signature
Symmetric soft clipMostly odd harmonics (3rd, 5th). Hollow, reedy, "warm" overdrive. Both halves of the wave clip the same way.
Asymmetric / tubeAdds even harmonics (2nd, 4th). Fuller, sweeter, more "analog". One half of the wave clips more than the other.
Hard clipStrong high-order odd harmonics. Buzzy, aggressive, digital. The transfer curve has a sharp corner.
WavefoldingFolds the peak back on itself — dense, metallic, inharmonic clusters. Far beyond simple clipping.

Even harmonics — sweetness

  • 2nd, 4th, 6th — octaves and octave-fifths above the fundamental
  • Musically consonant; reads as "fuller", "rounder", "fatter"
  • Comes from asymmetric curves — tube, transformer, tape
  • Use on bass and drone where you want body without grit

Odd harmonics — grit

  • 3rd, 5th, 7th — fifths and beyond, increasingly dissonant
  • Reads as "hollow", "reedy", "aggressive", "square-wave-ish"
  • Comes from symmetric clipping — most digital saturators by default
  • Use on drums and stabs where you want bite and presence

Soft clip

  • The curve bends gradually toward a ceiling — no sharp corner
  • Harmonics rise smoothly as you push harder; forgiving and musical
  • Adds density and "glue" before it ever sounds distorted
  • Reversible-feeling: back the drive off and it returns to clean

Hard clip

  • The curve hits a wall — peaks are sliced flat
  • High-order harmonics appear suddenly and harshly
  • Aliasing risk on digital sources; can sound brittle
  • Once you clip a peak off, that peak is gone — irreversible
Reversibility note

Soft saturation at modest drive is effectively reversible by ear — pull the drive back and the timbre returns. Hard clipping is cumulative and one-way: the energy above the ceiling is discarded and cannot be recovered. Treat clipping like cutting, not like seasoning.

Level-in / level-out discipline

A nonlinear stage has three controls that interact: input gain (how hard you hit the curve), the curve itself (drive/amount), and output gain (matching level back). Confusing the three is the most common saturation mistake.

The three gains and what each does
Input gainChooses how much of the nonlinear curve the signal reaches. This is the real timbre control. More input = more harmonics, regardless of the output knob.
Drive / amountSteepens or scales the curve itself. Often interacts with input — read the manual for which one your plugin uses.
Output gainLinear make-up after the curve. Use it to level-match so you judge timbre, not loudness.
Loudness deception

Saturation almost always makes things louder, and louder almost always sounds better for the first few seconds. Always trim the output to match the bypassed level before deciding. If it still sounds better at equal loudness, the saturation earned its place. If not, you were fooled by level.

Why order matters — A→B is not B→A

source → EQ boost → Saturator vs. Saturator → EQ boost

EQ before saturation

  • The boosted band is now the loudest part hitting the curve
  • So that band drives the harmonic generation hardest
  • Boost the low-mids first → a fat, harmonically thick saturation
  • You are choosing what distorts by choosing what is loudest going in

EQ after saturation

  • The curve sees the flat source; harmonics are generated evenly
  • The EQ then sculpts the already-distorted tone
  • Boost the highs after → you lift the harmonics, not redirect them
  • Same two devices, audibly different result — that is non-commutativity
The rule of thumb

Anything you do before a nonlinear stage changes what the stage distorts. Anything you do after only shapes the distortion that already happened. Filter-then-saturate sounds different from saturate-then-filter — and a resonant filter peak placed before a Saturator drives the saturation hardest at the resonant pitch.

The order-of-chain decisions you will actually make

Common slots and what each one does to the sound
HP filter → SaturatorRemove sub before the curve so the bass doesn't dominate the harmonic generation. The single most useful pre-saturation move for a clean low end.
Saturator → HP filterLet the full-range signal distort, then clean up the rumble the distortion added. Fatter, but riskier for mono sub.
Compressor → SaturatorEven out dynamics first, so the curve is hit at a consistent level — a controlled, predictable grit.
Saturator → CompressorGenerate harmonics first, then glue them. The compressor reacts to the new high-end energy.
Saturator → SaturatorStaging: a gentle even-harmonic stage feeding a harder odd stage stacks two flavours. The order decides which flavour dominates.
Sidechain after saturationPump the already-distorted bass against the kick, so the harmonics duck too — keeps the grit from masking the kick.
