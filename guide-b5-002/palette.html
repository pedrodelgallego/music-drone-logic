Lab Note B5-002
Reverb · 05 — Palette

Four rooms, dialled to exact values

Four reverb recipes — room, plate, hall, and freeze — each with the pre-delay, decay, size, and EQ you can type straight into Hybrid Reverb. Build all four once, audition each against the same dry drone, and you will hear the place-vs-blur spectrum in ten minutes. These are starting points to internalise, then move away from.

The four-room spectrum

Room → Plate

  • Pure placement: the sound stays itself, just situated
  • Short tails, audible only as "air" and depth
  • Used on drums and percussive sources

Hall → Freeze

  • Toward blur: the source dissolves into the room
  • Long tails, heard as a layer in their own right
  • Used on the drone and the atmosphere

Recipe 1 — Room (placement)

Hybrid Reverb — "NEAR ROOM"
AlgorithmRoom (algorithmic) or small Convolution IR
Pre-delay15 ms
Decay0.9 s
Size~25 % (small)
Diffusion / densityHigh — early reflections close together
EQ before reverbHP 300 Hz, LP shelf -3 dB @ 9 kHz
Dry/Wet (return)100 %
  • What to listen for: with the send low, you should not hear a "reverb" at all — only that the drums have a body and sit in a space. Mute the send and they go flat and pasted-on. That absence test is how you know placement is working.

Recipe 2 — Plate (bright placement with a tail)

Hybrid Reverb — "PLATE"
AlgorithmPlate
Pre-delay25 ms
Decay2.2 s
Sizen/a (plates have a fixed character) — set tone/damping instead
Damping / toneMid-bright; let the top ring a little
EQ before reverbHP 400 Hz, LP shelf -2 dB @ 10 kHz
Dry/Wet (return)100 %
  • What to listen for: a plate has no real "room shape" — it is a dense, bright sheet of reflections. It flatters metallic perc and rim hits, giving a sustained shimmer without reading as a large space. Pre-delay keeps the hit's attack intact in front of the sheen.

Recipe 3 — Hall (blur)

Hybrid Reverb — "FAR HALL"
AlgorithmHall (large algorithmic or Convolution Hall)
Pre-delay40 ms
Decay7 s
Size~80 % (large)
Diffusion / densityHigh, smooth — no fluttery early reflections
EQ before reverbHP 350 Hz, LP shelf -4 dB @ 7.5 kHz
After reverbCompressor, kick-sidechained, ~3 dB reduction
Dry/Wet (return)100 %
  • What to listen for: at a healthy send, drone notes should start to overlap — the tail of one note still ringing when the next arrives. That overlap is blur. If the source vanishes entirely, that is fine for the drone; if it vanishes on the drums, pull their send down.

Recipe 4 — Freeze (the room becomes a pad)

Hybrid Reverb — "FREEZE"
AlgorithmLarge algorithm with a Freeze control
Pre-delay0 ms
Decay12 s (held indefinitely once frozen)
SizeMaximum
FreezeEngage while a single note/chord rings — then it sustains forever
EQ before reverbHP 250 Hz only — let it stay wide and dark
Dry/Wet (return)100 %
  • What to listen for: freeze a single drone note and you have an instant pad with no oscillator. Freeze a snare tail and you have a metallic drone. Capture it to audio (Setup, return C) and it becomes the atmosphere layer — material, not a live effect you're afraid to turn off.

The whole palette at a glance

Pre-delay at 124 BPM — synced values

Note value → milliseconds (for a tail that lands on the grid)
1/32~60 ms
1/16~121 ms
1/16 triplet~81 ms
1/8~242 ms
  • Use a synced pre-delay when the reverb fights the groove. A 1/16 (121 ms) pre-delay on the near room makes each kick's room-bloom re-attack a sixteenth later, so the space pulses with the beat instead of washing across it. For the far hall, keep pre-delay free (40 ms) — its job is size, not rhythm.
Decision check
  • Audition all four against the same dry drone: can you hear the spectrum from "placed" to "dissolved" as one continuous move?
  • On the Room and Plate, does muting the send make the source flatten? (Placement working.)
  • On the Hall, do drone notes overlap into a wash without burying the drums?
  • Did the Freeze produce something you could keep as a layer, or is it still a live effect?
