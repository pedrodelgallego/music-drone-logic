Lab Note B5-002
Reverb · 06 — The build

Building a track where the room is the form

A complete reverb-defined piece, from empty project to render. We record everything dry, build three rooms, place each source at a chosen depth, and then let the space itself — growing, freezing, and stripping back — carry the whole arc. Each step is small, has a decision checkpoint, and spells out every value. Keep the setup page open in another tab; it is the wiring this page assumes.

Step 01

Create the project and the three rooms

Open the set
  • ⌘ N for a new Live Set, Tab to Arrangement view. Tempo 124, 4/4.
  • Right-click the timeline ruler → Time Ruler Format → Time so positions read as m:ss.
  • ⌘ T four times for audio tracks: DT DRUMS, MF BASS, MF DRONE, ATMOS.
Create the three return tracks
  • ⌘ ⌥ T three times → rename A — NEAR ROOM, B — FAR HALL, C — FREEZE.
  • Build each one exactly as the setup page specifies — Room 0.9 s, Hall 7 s, Freeze 12 s, all at 100 % wet, all high-passed before the reverb.
  • On B — FAR HALL, add the kick-sidechained Compressor after the reverb now, even though there is no kick yet. Wiring it early means you won't forget it later.
The wet/dry check, done once and for all
  • Click each return's Hybrid Reverb and confirm Dry/Wet reads 100 %. Write it on a sticky note if you have to. Every muddy reverb mix in history started with a return left at 50 %.
  • ⌘ S → save as reverb_lab_b5_002.als in a dedicated project folder.
Decision check
  • Are all three returns at 100 % wet?
  • Is each return high-passed before its reverb?
  • Does the project read tempo on both hardware boxes once you enable sync (see Step 02)?
Step 02

Record every source dry

Sync and gain-stage the hardware
  • Enable MIDI clock + transport to the Digitakt II and MicroFreak (Ableton is the only master). Press play; confirm both boxes show 124.
  • MicroFreak Out → M4 In 1 (mono); Digitakt II Main L/R → M4 In 3/4 (stereo). Peaks -12 to -6 dBFS.
The drone — the source the big room acts on
  • MicroFreak: Shift + press Type to init, choose Wavetable or Harmo. Paraphonic on. Long attack and release. Filter dark (10 o'clock).
  • Two slow, unsynced modulations: LFO → Timbre ~25, Cycling Env (loop, slow) → Wave ~15.
  • Press Hold, play root A1 + fifth E2. Arm MF DRONE, Monitor Auto, record 4 minutes totally dry. Save the patch as LAB DRONE.
Bass, drums, and a single perc hit for the room
  • MF BASS: monophonic, dark, short envelope, 3–5 notes in A minor, root on step 1. Record 4 minutes dry. Save patch LAB BASS.
  • DT DRUMS: kick on 1, 5, 9, 13; tight hat on offbeats; one rim or metallic perc hit on step 8. Tune the kick to A or E. Keep the kick's Digitakt reverb send at zero — the kick must stay dry and near.
  • Record a separate muted-kick pass into a DT KICK track for clean sidechaining of the far hall and bass.
Decision check
  • Is every take genuinely dry — no Digitakt reverb baked in?
  • Is there exactly one percussive hit (the perc on step 8) chosen to show off the room later?
  • Are the kick and bass tuned to agree, so the dry low end stays clean under all that space?
Step 03

Place each source by depth

Assign depths before touching a send
  • Decide the depth ranking out loud: bass = dead front, dry; kick = near; perc = mid; drone = far; atmosphere = the room itself. Write it down. Every send setting below serves this ranking.
Set the sends, source by source
Send levels — start here, then trust the depth test
MF BASS → A, Boff / off — bass stays dry and front
DT DRUMS → A (near room)-18 dB — body and place, no tail
DT DRUMS → B (far hall)off — drums must not blur
Perc (via p-lock send on Digitakt) → A-10 dB — the one hit allowed to show the room
MF DRONE → A (near room)-16 dB — a little definition
MF DRONE → B (far hall)-6 dB — most of the drone dissolves into the hall
  • The drone going to both returns is the place-and-blur trick from the listen page: its body sits in the near room, its tail walks far back into the hall.
  • Resist the urge to send the bass anywhere. Every reverberant low frequency you add now is a decision you will have to undo in the mix, and the dry bass is precisely what makes the vast room above it legible — there has to be one solid object in front for the depth behind it to register. A track where everything is reverberant has no near, and a mix with no near has no far either; the two only exist in contrast.
  • Set the perc send via a parameter-lock on the Digitakt rather than a fixed track send, so only the chosen hit on step 8 wakes the room. A constant perc send would keep the near room ringing under every offbeat and quickly turn placement into wash.
The depth test
  • Close your eyes and play 16 bars. Point with your finger at where each sound sits, front to back. If two sounds occupy the same depth and shouldn't, change a send — not a fader.
  • Flip every return to mono for ten seconds. If the drone collapses or the room hisses, pull the offending send or revisit the return EQ.
Decision check
  • Can you point to a clear near / mid / far for the four main sounds?
  • Is the bass still dry and unmistakably in front?
  • Does muting send A make the drums flatten (placement working), and muting send B make the drone shrink (blur working)?
Step 04

Freeze a tail and print the atmosphere

Capture the cloud
  • Temporarily raise MF DRONE → C (FREEZE) send to 0 dB. Let one sustained chord ring into the freeze return.
  • Click Freeze on the FREEZE return's Hybrid Reverb at a moment the chord is full and rich. The tail now holds forever.
  • New audio track, Audio From → Resampling, Monitor Off. Arm it. ⌘ ⇧ R, record 32 bars of the frozen cloud. Un-freeze, and drop the FREEZE send back to off.
Turn the cloud into a layer
  • Drag the resampled clip to ATMOS. Double-click → Reverse so it swells toward events instead of away — free anticipation, in key, from material you already had.
  • Auto Filter HP @ 300 Hz, gentle resonance. Send ATMOS → B at -9 dB so the printed tail itself sits in the far hall.
  • The throughline in practice: the music happened in the return path, and you committed it. The atmosphere is now reverb made solid — it keeps playing even when you later automate the live reverb down to nothing.
Decision check
  • Is the FREEZE send back to off, so it isn't quietly bleeding into the mix?
  • Does the printed atmosphere read as material you could solo, not as a live effect?
  • Reversed, does it create lift into each section rather than a tail dragging behind?
Step 05

Automate the room — the space is the arrangement

What moves, and what doesn't
  • In a reverb-led track, the arrangement is mostly drawn on three lanes: the drone's send to B, the far hall's decay, and the far hall's pre-delay. Almost nothing else needs to change. This is the discipline the whole Lab Note is built around: instead of fading instruments in and out, you grow and shrink the space they sit in, and the listener experiences that as the music breathing rather than as parts being switched.
  • Treat the three lanes as one gesture, not three. They should peak together at the same moment, because a room that is simultaneously louder, longer, and larger reads as a single physical event — the walls moving outward — rather than as three plug-in parameters drifting independently. If they peak at different times the effect smears and the ear stops believing it is a real space.
  • A shows automation lanes, B is the pencil. No ramp shorter than 16 bars — the room should grow so slowly the listener never catches it changing. The single most common mistake here is impatience: a reverb send that climbs over four bars is heard as someone turning a knob, while the same climb over thirty-two bars is heard as the architecture itself opening up.
The space-growth map

  • Bend every ramp into an S-curve (Curve on the segment) — straight lines read as a ruler, S-curves read as intention.
Perform it on hardware (optional but better)
  • ⌘ M for MIDI map. Map the drone's B-send and the far hall's Decay to two Launchpad controls. Set each mapping's Min/Max to the musical range above so a full pad sweep covers exactly that span.
  • F9 Arrangement Record, play the whole 8 minutes, and ride the two controls slower than feels natural. Two takes, keep the calmer one. Afterwards, Simplify Envelope on each lane.
Decision check
  • Play 4:00 – 5:00 blind: does the peak arrive because the room opened, not because anything was added? That is the thesis of this Lab Note.
  • If you can hear the reverb change as an event, the ramp is too fast — stretch it.
  • Can you remove one automation lane without weakening the arc? If yes, remove it.
Step 06

Arrange clips around the moving room

Mark the map — sections are room-states
  • Right-click the scrub area → Add Locator at each boundary. Name them for the space, not the instruments: SMALL, PULSE, OPENING, VAST, RECEDING, TAIL.
Section → room state → time/bar at 124 BPM
SMALL — 0:00 / bar 1Dry drone in a modest room; no beat. The listener settles into the space.
PULSE — 1:30 / bar 47Kick enters into the near room; the room gets a heartbeat.
OPENING — 3:00 / bar 94Bass + perc; drone send to the hall begins climbing.
VAST — 4:30 / bar 140Peak. Nothing new enters — the room is at its largest (decay 9 s, send 0 dB).
RECEDING — 5:30 / bar 171Space shrinks back; the dry sources re-emerge from the wash.
TAIL — 7:00 / bar 218Beat out, drone out; only the printed atmosphere and the last live tail.
END — 8:00 / bar 249Silence after the fade.
Clip placement, section by section
  • SMALL — Drone from bar 1, printed atmosphere underneath at -22 dB. No drums.
  • PULSE — DT drums (kick-only portion) from bar 47. ⌘ E-split the take if it opened busy and use only the kick here.
  • OPENING — Bass from bar 94 with a 4-bar fade-in drawn on the clip corner; perc audible shortly after. The hall-send automation is already climbing through here.
  • VAST — No new clips. The peak is entirely the room automation from Step 05.
  • RECEDING — Drum clip gain down 2 dB; the hall send retreats. The strip-back makes the earlier vastness feel retroactively bigger.
  • TAIL — Cut bass and drums; let the printed atmosphere and the decaying live tail carry the exit under the master fade.
Editing craft
  • Make every cut on a kick downbeat: ⌘ E at the playhead with grid snap on.
  • Where two takes meet, overlap by one bar and use a 4-beat crossfade — hard butt-joins click, and a click is fatal inside a quiet reverberant passage.
  • When a section's edits are final, select its clips and ⌘ J to consolidate.
Decision check
  • Does each section feel like a different size of room rather than a different set of instruments?
  • Is the VAST section genuinely empty of new clips, carried only by space?
  • Do any edits click against the quiet tail? Loop each seam (⌘ L) and listen three times.
Step 07

Mix the space, then export

Balance with the room in mind
Starting balance
DT Drums-10 dB (peaks ~ -6 dBFS)
Bass-12 dB, dry
Drone-16 dB
Atmosphere (printed tail)-22 dB
A — Near Room-9 dB
B — Far Hall-8 dB (automated)
Master peak≤ -3 dBFS, including the VAST bloom
  • Pull all faders to -inf, then raise one at a time, 30 s apart: kick, bass, drone, atmosphere, then the returns. Each return earns its level against a mix that already works dry.
Verification passes for a reverberant mix
  • Low-end check: confirm the kick owns 40–80 Hz and the bass 80–180 Hz with nothing reverberant below 250 Hz. If the low end is woolly, the return high-pass slipped — raise it.
  • Mono check: flip the master to mono. The reverb should narrow but survive; if the drone vanishes, the far-hall image is over-wide.
  • Sidechain check: watch the far-hall Compressor's gain reduction through the VAST section — it should breathe open between kicks, ~3 dB dips, not pump.
  • Quiet check: listen once at low volume. The depth ranking — near vs. far — should still be obvious. If everything flattens to one distance, the sends need spreading.
