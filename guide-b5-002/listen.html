Lab Note B5-002
Reverb · 02 — Listen

Hearing the room as the instrument

Before you reach for a single knob, train the ear. In these three recordings reverb is not decoration — it is the place the music happens and, in two of them, the thing that actually changes over time. Don't copy them; listen for the space: how big it reads, where each sound sits in it, and whether the tail is a layer or a smear. Each links to a full replication lesson in Block 5.

Three studies in space as form

Stars of the Lid — "Don't Bother They're Here"

  • Reverb so long the source dissolves — pure blur, the room is the music
  • Listen for tails that overlap into a single moving cloud with no attacks
  • Form carried entirely by swells of space, never by a beat or a hook

Replication lesson →

Biosphere — "Kobresia"

  • A cold, vast room where dry pulses are placed precisely inside huge reverb
  • Listen for place and blur together: near elements stay defined, far ones wash
  • Pre-delay keeping each event readable against an enormous decay

Replication lesson →

Thomas Köner — "Teimo"

  • Sub-bass and reverberant noise treated as a single tectonic space
  • Listen for how slowly the room evolves — change measured in minutes
  • Depth used as the whole content; almost no melodic or rhythmic event

A structured way to listen

Pass 01

Measure the room

How big is the space
  • Play the opening twice. On the second listen, find one transient — a pulse, a pluck, a note onset — and time how long its tail takes to fall to silence. That is the decay, and it tells you the size of the room.
  • Ask whether you can still hear the dry sound inside the wash, or whether the source has dissolved entirely. The first is a placed sound; the second is a blurred one.
  • In Stars of the Lid the answer is "dissolved"; in Biosphere it is "both, on different elements". Naming this for each track is the first decision page 03 makes you make.
Pass 02

Place each sound by depth

Near, mid, far
  • Pick three elements and rank them front to back. The dry, defined ones are near; the soft, blurred ones are far. That depth ranking is the mix's real architecture.
  • Listen for the gap between an attack and its tail. A clear gap (pre-delay) keeps a sound up front even when the room is huge. No gap pushes it back.
  • Count how few sources there really are. In all three references it is a handful — the apparent richness comes from the space, not from more parts.
Pass 03

Hear the space move

The room as the arrangement
  • Listen across a whole minute, not a bar. Does the room grow — decay lengthening, send rising, more of the mix dissolving into tail? Does it ever shrink back?
  • Mark the moment of largest space. In a reverb-led piece the climax is where the room blooms, not where an element is added.
  • Write down, in plain words, the rule you'd need to reproduce 30 seconds: how big the room is, what sits near, what sits far, and whether the space is opening or closing. That sentence is your brief for the build.
  • The originality constraint: these recordings are ear-training, not templates. Take the spatial idea — a coherent room, sources placed by depth, the space itself as the moving part — and build something entirely yours. The track you make in this Lab Note must not reproduce any reference's melody, sample, or arrangement.
