Reverb · 03 — Decisions

Place or blur — the decision that sets every knob

Reverb answers one question before any parameter: do you want to place this sound somewhere, or blur it into the background? Placement and blur pull the controls in opposite directions. Decide the intent, then the settings follow — and the most common reverb mistakes all come from confusing the two, or from misreading what "dry/wet" means.

Place vs blur — two jobs, one device

Reverb to place

  • Short, controlled — the sound is still clearly itself, just in a room
  • Pre-delay keeps the dry attack distinct from the tail
  • Decay short enough that the next hit isn't smeared
  • Goal: depth and position without losing definition

Reverb to blur

  • Long, large — the source dissolves into atmosphere
  • Little or no pre-delay; the wet swallows the dry
  • Heavy high-passing so the cloud doesn't drown the mix
  • Goal: texture and scale, not a locatable object
The principle

Placement reverb you should barely notice; you'd notice its absence. Blur reverb is meant to be heard as a layer. Knowing which you're after stops you from "fixing" one with the other's settings.

What a reverb is actually made of

Every reverb, hardware or software, is three things happening in sequence. Hearing them separately is what lets you place a sound: you can keep one part defined while the rest dissolves.

The three phases of a reverb tail
Direct soundThe dry signal itself, arriving first. On a send this is the source track; on an insert it is the dry-through. Its clarity is what "near" is made of.
Early reflectionsThe first few bounces off near surfaces, arriving 5–80 ms after the direct sound. These carry the size and shape of the room — the ear reads distance from them before the tail even starts.
Late reflections (the tail)The dense, diffuse wash that follows, decaying over the RT60 time. This is the part that blurs. Long late reflections = a large, vague space.
  • Why early reflections matter most for placement: the gap and pattern between the direct sound and the first reflections is what your ear uses to judge distance. A sound with strong, slightly-delayed early reflections and a modest tail reads as "in a real room, up front". A sound with no early reflections and a huge tail reads as "lost in fog, far away".

The four parameters that actually matter

Hybrid Reverb — what each control does to the space
Pre-delayGap between the dry sound and the onset of the tail. Longer = bigger perceived room and a clearer dry transient. The single most underused control.
Decay (RT60)How long the tail takes to fall 60 dB into silence. Sets the size of the gesture in time. Short places; long blurs.
SizeThe geometry of the space — early-reflection spread and density. Big size + short decay = a large room you can still hear through.
Dry/Wet vs send levelOn an insert, Dry/Wet is the blend. On a return, lock it to 100 % wet and let the send be the blend (see below).

Pre-delay — the control that buys you depth without mud

Pre-delay times and what they do
0 msTail starts the instant the sound does. The wet smears the attack — sounds distant and indistinct. Use only when you want blur.
10–25 msA small gap. The transient cuts through, then the room blooms behind it. The sweet spot for keeping drums and plucks defined inside a big space.
40–80 msAn audible gap that reads as a genuinely large room. The dry sound is unmistakably near; the tail is unmistakably far.
Synced (1/16, 1/8)Set pre-delay to a note value at the tempo and the tail re-attacks on the grid — the reverb stops fighting the groove and joins it.
  • Pre-delay is rhythmic, too: at 124 BPM a 1/16 note is ~121 ms and a 1/32 is ~60 ms. Setting pre-delay to one of these lands the tail's onset on a subdivision, so the room pulses with the track instead of washing over it.

Decay — sizing the gesture in time

Decay ranges by intent
0.3–0.8 sA tight room. Adds body and "air" to a percussive sound without any audible tail. Pure placement.
1.5–3 sA plate or small hall. The tail is heard as space but clears before the next bar. Place with a hint of blur.
4–8 sA large hall. Tails overlap across notes; the source still recognisable but the room dominates. The drone's home in this build.
10 s+ / FreezeFull blur. Individual events merge into one evolving cloud. This is where reverb becomes a pad, not an effect.

The wet/dry ambiguity — the trap that makes mixes muddy

"Dry/Wet" means two completely different things depending on where the device sits. Confusing them is the most common reverb mistake, and it always sounds the same: a washed-out, distant mix that no amount of EQ rescues.

On an insert

  • Dry/Wet blends dry-through with wet. 30 % means 70 % dry passes the device.
  • Correct place to set the balance for one sound.
  • Raise it and the dry shrinks — the source recedes.

On a return

  • The dry already exists on the source track. The return carries only wet.
  • Set Dry/Wet to 100 % and never touch it. Any dry leaking through the return doubles and smears the source.
  • The send knob is the blend — per track, with one shared space.
One-line diagnosis

If a return is set to anything other than 100 % wet, you are summing two copies of the dry signal at different phases. That is the muddy mix. Fix the knob before you fix anything else.

Send vs insert — same reverb, different role

DT drums → send A → Return A: Hybrid Reverb 100% wet + MF drone send → master
When to use which
One shared room (send)Drums, bass, drone all sit in the same space. Cohesive, efficient, printable. The default for this guide.
A private space (insert)One sound needs its own reverb character no other track should share — e.g. a gated room only on the snare, or a freeze on a single drone note.
Two sends for two depthsReturn A = short "near" room, Return B = long "far" wash. Send a track to both to place its body up front and its tail far back.
  • Why two depths is the whole trick: with a near return and a far return on separate sends, depth becomes a single knob you can automate. Push a track from the near send to the far send over sixteen bars and it walks backward into the room — no fader move, no EQ, just space.

Independent vs shared — does every sound want the same room?

One shared room

  • Cohesive: everything sounds like it is in the same place
  • Efficient: one reverb to tune, one tail to EQ and duck
  • The realistic default — real rooms hold every sound at once

Separate spaces

  • Surreal: a near, dry beat inside a vast, slow drone-room
  • The Biosphere effect — defined pulses against an enormous wash
  • Costs an extra return and the risk of an incoherent mix if overused

Keep the room out of the way

EQ and dynamics on the reverb path — before and after
High-pass the send or return250–400 Hz — low frequencies carry no useful spatial information and just thicken the low end
Tame the topLP shelf around 8 kHz, -3 dB — keeps the tail from hissing and sitting on top of the hats
EQ before the reverbFilter the source going in so you reverberate a clean signal, not rumble you then can't remove
Duck the reverb to the kickSidechain the return to the kick so the space breathes open between hits instead of masking the groove
Decision check
  • For each source: are you placing it or blurring it? Do the pre-delay and decay agree with that intent?
  • Every reverb return: is it exactly 100 % wet? Check now, because a leaked dry signal is invisible until the mix is mud.
  • Is the low end high-passed out of the reverb, so the room adds depth without weight?
  • Do all your sounds want the same room, or does one need its own? Don't pay for a second return unless the answer is clearly yes.
