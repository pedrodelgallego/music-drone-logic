Reverb · 07 — Reflection

Fault-finding, carry-forward, principle

Most of the learning happens here, after the render, not during the build. Answer slowly and write the answers down. The aim is to leave with one habit you keep and one fault you can name on sight.

How you know the space worked

Success signals
  • The track sounds like one coherent room, not separate sounds with reverb on them.
  • You can point to a clear near, mid, and far for the main sources.
  • The peak arrives because the room opened, not because anything was added.
  • The bass and kick stay clean and defined under all the space above them.
  • Every reverb return is at 100 % wet and the low end is high-passed out of the room.
  • A frozen tail became real material on the atmosphere track.
  • The reverb changes are felt, never heard as events.
  • The mix survives a mono check without the room collapsing.
  • You can explain why each source got the depth it did.
Failure signals
  • The mix is washed-out and muddy, and no EQ fixes it — usually a return left below 100 % wet.
  • The low end is woolly because reverb is reaching below 250 Hz.
  • Everything sits at the same distance; there is no near or far.
  • The reverb was added at the end to "glue" a mix that was never spaced.
  • The room never moves, so the eight minutes feel static.
  • Reverb changes are abrupt enough to hear as events.
  • The frozen tail is still a live effect you're afraid to turn off.
  • The drums smear because the near room had no pre-delay.

Carry-forward — what to keep for the next track

  1. Before any knob: were you placing this sound or blurring it? Did the pre-delay and decay agree?
  2. Which return — near room or far hall — did more of the work, and why?
  3. Did pre-delay buy you depth without mud, or did you forget to use it?
  4. Which source most needed to stay dry, and did keeping it dry hold the mix together?
  5. Did the moving room carry the arrangement, or did you fall back on adding elements?
  6. What did freezing and printing a tail give you that a live reverb could not?
  7. Where did the space sound largest, and was that the right place for the peak?
  8. Did the mix survive mono? If not, where did the width come from?
  9. Could one return have done the job of two? Or did you genuinely need both depths?
  10. What would you do differently with the room next session?
Final principle

Reverb is not an amount you add at the end. It is a place you decide first, and a place that can move. Choose to place or to blur, build one coherent room, and let its size become the form.
