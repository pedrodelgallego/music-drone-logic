Lab Note B2-001
Synthesis from Zero · 06 — Build

Build the all-synthesized piece, step by step

Now you assemble the recipes into one finished 4–6 minute piece in which every sound is synthesized — drums included. Each step is small, each has a decision checkpoint, and every device setting and click is spelled out. Keep the Setup page and Palette page open in other tabs — this page assumes them. Keep Spectrum on the master the entire time.

project → kick → hat + clap → bass (FM) → lead (FM) → pad (additive) → drone (west-coast) → arrange → mix + print
Step 01

Create the project and the analyzer

Open a new set
  • ⌘ N for a new Live Set. Tab to Arrangement view — the whole build lives here.
  • Tempo 122, time signature 4/4. Right-click the ruler → Time Ruler Format → Time so positions read as m:ss.
  • ⌘ S → save as synth_from_zero_b2_001.als in its own project folder.
Put the analyzer on the master — and leave it
  • Select the Master track. Drag Spectrum onto it. Set Range to −80 dB, Block size large enough to resolve low harmonics (8192).
  • This is the device that proves the Lab Note's promise: evenly spaced spikes = harmonic, uneven spikes = inharmonic, a continuous band = noise. Verify every prediction here.
  • Add a Utility after Spectrum for the mono-check later. No limiter — headroom is the sound.
Create the tracks and groups
  • ⌘ ⇧ T for MIDI tracks: KICK, HAT, CLAP, BASS, LEAD, PAD, DRONE.
  • Select KICK, HAT, CLAP → ⌘ G → name the group SYNTH KIT.
  • ⌘ ⌥ T twice for returns: A — REVERB (Reverb, decay 1.8 s, Dry/Wet 100%, used for the clap glue) and B — ECHO (Echo, 3/16 ping-pong, Feedback 35%, HP at 250 Hz).
  • Color drums one family, tonal synths another. ⌘ R to rename. Save.
Decision check
  • Is Spectrum reading on the master right now? If not, fix it before anything else — you cannot verify a prediction you cannot see.
  • Are there zero samples loaded anywhere? Confirm the only audio in the set will be synthesis you bounce yourself.
Step 02

Build the kick — a falling sine plus a click

The body
  • On KICK, add Operator. Enable oscillator A only (mute B, C, D for now), waveform Sine, Coarse 1, level full.
  • Amp envelope on A: Attack 0 ms, Decay 250 ms, Sustain 0, Release 10 ms. This is the body length.
  • Open the Pitch tab, enable Envelope. Set Initial high and Decay very fast: the oscillator starts ≈ 150 Hz and falls to the resting note. Pitch env time ≈ 5 ms.
  • Play a note so the resting frequency lands ≈ 50 Hz (F1). Tune this to the track root — write the root down now, because the bass must agree with it.
The click
  • Enable oscillator B as Operator's Noise waveform (or a high sine, Coarse set high). Amp env: Decay 3 ms, level low.
  • Route B in parallel (algorithm where B reaches output directly), not as a modulator of A. It mixes with the body as the front edge.
  • Solo the kick, watch Spectrum: a brief broadband flash (the click) over a low spike that settles (the body).
Kick — final values (adjust between takes)
Osc ASine, Coarse 1, level full
Amp env AA 0 ms · D 250 ms · S 0 · R 10 ms
Pitch envstart ~150 Hz → rest ~50 Hz, time ~5 ms
Osc B (click)Noise, parallel, D 3 ms, low level
Solo peak≈ −12 dBFS
Decision check
  • Can I hum the body but not the click? If I can hum the click, it is too long — shorten osc B's decay.
  • Does the kick punch, or boop? If it boops, the pitch-env time is too slow — bring it under 8 ms.
  • Is the resting note tuned to the track root? A detuned kick + a tonal bass = permanent low-end blur.
Step 03

Build the hat and clap from noise

Hat — gated high-passed noise
  • On HAT, add Operator, oscillator A waveform Noise. Add an Auto Filter, High-pass, cutoff ≈ 7.5 kHz, mild resonance.
  • Amp env: Attack 0 ms, Decay 40 ms, Sustain 0 — that short decay is the closed hat.
  • Duplicate the track to HAT OPEN, lengthen Decay to 300 ms. Program the closed hat to choke the open one. (If you keep only one hat, that is fine — note it.)
  • Spectrum: a band of energy above ~7 kHz, no low spike. No pitch to sing — correct.
Clap — staggered bursts plus a tail
  • On CLAP, add Operator, oscillator A Noise. Amp env: A 0 ms, D 15 ms, S 0 — one short burst.
  • Add an Auto Filter, Band-pass, centre ≈ 1.2 kHz, moderate resonance — the "hand" colour.
  • In the clip, place 3–4 MIDI notes 10–20 ms apart (zoom in; nudge them off-grid). Make the last burst slightly louder and longer.
  • Send the clap to A — REVERB at −12 dB: a ~200 ms tail glues the bursts into one clap. Without it they read as separate ticks.
Decision check
  • Hat: solo the 8th-note pattern — do the hats close before the next kick, or smear into a wash? A wash means decay too long.
  • Clap: in slow motion, can I hear the front-edge flutter of several bursts? A single dry snap means the stagger collapsed.
  • Mono-check the clap (Utility on master to 0% width): does the tail survive? If it vanishes, pull the reverb width back.
Step 04

Program the drum pattern

A 16-step foundation
  • On KICK, draw a clip: hits on steps 1, 5, 9, 13 (four on the floor).
  • HAT: off-beats 3, 7, 11, 15. Alternate velocity so the hats breathe instead of ticking.
  • CLAP: steps 5 and 13 (beats 2 and 4) — its staggered bursts sit inside those steps.
  • Add 52% groove (swing) to the kit, on the edge of perception. Loop and listen for two minutes before adding anything.
Kit bus
  • On the SYNTH KIT group: EQ Eight HP at 25 Hz; light Glue Compressor for cohesion (Ratio 2:1, slow-ish, ~1–2 dB).
  • Group peaks ≤ −6 dBFS. The kit is now one drawable bus of pure synthesis.
Decision check
  • Does the groove move without thinking? If a hit feels decorative, mute it and listen for a minute — if nothing is lost, it stays muted.
  • Could a stranger believe these were sampled drums? If they sound stiff and identical, vary velocity and clap stagger — the realism is in the micro-variation you control.
Step 05

Build the bass — harmonic FM

Two operators, integer ratio
  • On BASS, add Operator. Choose the algorithm where B sits on top of A (B modulates A; A is the only carrier reaching output). Turn C and D off.
  • Set A and B Coarse = 1 (ratio 1:1). Carrier A full; modulator B level at zero to start.
  • Hold a low note and raise B's level (the index). Watch Spectrum: evenly spaced harmonics appear above the fundamental — 2×, 3×, 4× — and the pitch never moves. Stop at a brightness that sits under the kick, not over it.
Envelope the index for attack bite
  • Give modulator B its own amp envelope: Attack 0 ms, Decay 120 ms, Sustain low. The bass is bright at the attack and mellow in the body — exactly the e-piano "tine" mechanism applied to a bass.
  • Carrier A amp env: A 0 ms, short Decay/Release so notes leave silence between them. Tune the played note to agree with the kick's resting note.
  • Gain-compensate: trim A's output as you raised B's level so the brightness change is not just a loudness change. A/B at matched level.
A short sequence
  • Write a 16-step clip, 3–5 notes, root on step 1, gaps where the kick lives. A minor key keeps it dark.
  • Sidechain: Compressor on BASS, Sidechain from SYNTH KIT (Pre FX), Ratio 4:1, Attack 1 ms, Release 120 ms, ~3 dB gain reduction per kick.
Decision check
  • Can I play a melody and stay in tune? If yes, the ratio is integer and the bass is harmonic — confirm on Spectrum (even spikes).
  • Is the brightness change really brightness, or did I just turn it up? Gain-match and re-judge.
  • Does the bass own 80–180 Hz while the kick owns 40–80 Hz? Two tonal sources fighting below 200 Hz is the most common low-end fault.
Step 06

Build the lead — FM with an inharmonic attack

Harmonic body, metallic tine
  • Duplicate the bass Operator onto LEAD, or build fresh: B → A, ratio 1 : 1.
  • Steepen the index envelope on B: Attack 0 ms, Decay 150 ms, Sustain low. The brief high index at the attack is an inharmonic "tine" that fades into a harmonic body — the DX-Rhodes structure you heard in listening.
  • Add a touch of operator feedback on B for edge — seasoning only.
  • Carrier A amp env: A 0 ms, sustained, medium release for a struck-but-singing feel.
An inharmonic variant for colour
  • Duplicate to a parked clip and move modulator B to ≈ 1.41 (Coarse 1 + Fine, or Coarse 3.5). Spectrum: spikes now unevenly spaced — a bell/mallet. Try to sing the pitch; it resists. Use this sparingly as a perc accent, not the main lead.
Decision check
  • Hold a long note: does the spectrum animate (bright → mellow), or sit still? A static index sounds dead — keep the envelope.
  • On high notes at high index, does it turn harsh and detuned? That is aliasing — lower the index or play lower, or keep it as deliberate dirt and note the choice.
Step 07

Build the pad — additive-style wavetable

A morphing bed
  • On PAD, add Wavetable. Oscillator 1: a harmonically rich table (a "Basics" saw-to-sine sweep works).
  • Assign a slow LFO to the wavetable position: rate ≈ one cycle per 10–20 s, depth small. As the position moves, partials redistribute — additive-style movement with no new notes. Watch Spectrum: spikes rise and fall in level while the fundamental holds.
  • Filter: gentle low-pass, cutoff mid — let the table do the work, not the filter. Amp env: A 300–800 ms, long Sustain, long Release.
  • Hold root + fifth. Width via Unison/Utility — keep it mono-compatible.
Place it under everything
  • EQ HP at 150 Hz so the pad owns the mids, not the low end the kick and bass own.
  • Reverb send −10 dB. The pad is the room the track lives in.
Decision check
  • Is the pad evolving from the wavetable sweep, or sitting still? If still, raise the LFO depth a touch — but slower than feels natural.
  • Solo pad + bass: is 80–200 Hz muddy? Raise the pad's HP before reaching for anything else.
Step 08

Build the drone — west-coast fold + low-pass gate

Fold a sine (VCV path)
  • Open VCV Rack. Add a VCO; take its sine output — harmonically poorest on purpose, so every new harmonic comes from the fold.
  • Add a wavefolder (Bogaudio FOLD / Befaco / Surge XT). Patch sine → folder input. Raise the fold knob: a pure tone sprouts harmonics, brighter as it is driven harder. Confirm on a scope.
  • Add an AD/function envelope → folder fold-depth CV (attenuated). Now brightness tracks dynamics — folding is amplitude-driven.
Gate it, don't filter it
  • Add a low-pass gate (Bogaudio LPG, or Veils in LPG / "both" mode). Patch folder out → LPG audio in → audio interface → Live's DRONE track.
  • Add a slow trigger/clock → the LPG's ping/excite input. Each ping = a pluck that opens bright+loud and closes along the vactrol curve, darkening as it fades.
  • The one move: drive fold depth (or ping strength) from a slow LFO/random. Harder ping = brighter + louder + longer — confirm by ear.
  • Record a 60–120 s pass into DRONE. (No VCV? Use the Wavetable noise drone in the Palette, or the MicroFreak folder + a tight envelope as the documented approximation.)
Decision check
  • Does the pluck darken as it decays (LPG), or just get quieter (a plain VCA)? Darkening is the west-coast signature — set the LPG to "both".
  • Freeze the one expressive control: does the part die? If it stays interesting, the expression was coming from elsewhere — strip the other movement.
  • Did I accidentally reach for a resonant filter with a "wah"? That is east-coast, and the prescribed fault — replace it with the LPG.
Step 09

Arrange the timeline

Mark the map
  • Right-click the scrub area → Add Locator at each boundary. Name: INTRO, KIT IN, BASS IN, FULL, PEAK, STRIP, OUTRO.
Section → time → bar at 122 BPM
INTRO — 0:00Bar 1 — drone + pad only
KIT IN — 0:45Bar 23 — kick, then hat/clap
BASS IN — 1:30Bar 46 — FM bass surfaces
FULL — 2:15Bar 69 — lead enters sparsely
PEAK — 3:15Bar 99 — nothing new; the automation peaks
STRIP — 4:00Bar 122 — lead and clap out
OUTRO — 4:45Bar 145 — drone + pad carry the exit
Place the clips
  • Loop the MIDI clips across the sections; draw fade-ins on the clip corners where parts surface rather than arrive.
  • PEAK: nothing new enters. The energy comes from automation drawn in Step 10 — that is the thesis of the genre, and of this Lab Note's restraint.
  • Make cuts on kick downbeats (⌘ E); consolidate finished sections with ⌘ J.
Decision check
  • Does the piece work with fewer than eight elements? Count them. If you added a ninth to fill a gap, find the real fix instead.
  • Does the arrangement feel like a journey, or a loop pasted for five minutes?
Step 10

Draw the automation — synthesis parameters, not just faders

Automate the synthesis itself
  • The cheapest movement comes from enveloping the parameter unique to each method. Automate these slowly across the arc:
Automation map (no ramp shorter than 8 bars)
Bass FM index (Operator B level) @ INTRO→PEAKlow → high → low — the bass brightens into the peak without a filter
Pad wavetable position @ FULL→PEAKa slow drift to a brighter table region
Drone fold depth @ across the piecethe one expressive control, riding slowly
Lead reverb send @ PEAK−12 → −4 dB — space blooms at the top
Echo feedback @ PEAK35% → 55% → 35% — echoes briefly self-regenerate
  • A shows automation lanes; B is the pencil. Bend ramps into S-curves so they read as intention, not a ruler.
  • Gain-compensate index automation so "brighter" never becomes "louder" by accident.
Decision check
  • Play PEAK blind: does the climax arrive because things opened (index, fold, send), not because things were added? That is the test of the whole exercise.
  • If you can hear an automation move as an event, it is too fast. Slow it.
Step 11

Mix pass and the sample A/B

Balance and separate
  • Pull all faders to −inf. Bring up one at a time, 30 s apart: kick, then bass against it, then hat/clap, then pad, then lead, then drone.
  • Frequency pecking order below 200 Hz: kick owns 40–80 Hz, bass owns 80–180 Hz, nothing else allowed in. HP everything else above 150 Hz.
  • Target peaks: kit bus ≈ −6 dBFS, bass −12 dB, pad −16 dB, lead −14 dB, drone −18 dB, master ≤ −3 dBFS.
The honesty test — A/B against a sampled kit
  • Park a factory sampled drum kit on a muted track, trimmed to the same perceived loudness as your SYNTH KIT (a louder kit always "wins").
  • Play your synthesized kit, then the sampled one. Write three things you gained by synthesizing (e.g. "the kick is tuned to the bass", "every clap stagger is mine", "the hat decay locks to tempo") and two things you lost (e.g. "the sampled kit has recorded-room character").
  • This A/B is the proof you understand when to synthesize — not a contest your kit must win.
Decision check
  • Listen once quietly: does the bass vanish or the hat dominate? Fix the balance at low volume before anything else.
  • Mono-check: flip Utility width to 0% — anything disappearing means widening is overdone (usual suspects: drone, clap reverb).
Step 12

Print and archive your synthesis

Render and document
  • Print each drum voice as a one-shot WAV (now allowed — it is your synthesis) plus the full mix. File → Export Audio / Video, WAV, 48 kHz, 24-bit, Normalize OFF.
  • Save every device preset: KICK, HAT, CLAP, BASS, LEAD, PAD; save the VCV patch and a screenshot of its cabling.
  • Draw each voice's signal flow by hand with every envelope time labelled. File → Collect All and Save.
Decision check
  • Can I point to a specific number for every part of every sound and say what changing it would do? If any answer is just an adjective, that voice is not finished.
  • Is the printed track assembled from my own patches, with no borrowed recording anywhere? The replica study stays in the logbook.
