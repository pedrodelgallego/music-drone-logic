Lab Note B2-001
Synthesis from Zero · 07 — Reflection

Criteria, faults & carry-forward

The track proves you can build sound from zero; this page proves you understand what you built. Answer slowly, in mechanism not adjective, and write the answers down. The required fault below is not optional — recognising it by ear is the skill this Lab Note certifies.

How you know it worked

Success signals
  • Every sound in the track is synthesized — drums included, no samples anywhere.
  • You can draw each voice's signal flow from memory: source → shaper(s) → output.
  • You predicted harmonic vs inharmonic from the patch and Spectrum confirmed it.
  • The kick has a defendable front edge: you can name where the click ends and the body begins.
  • The hat closes before the next kick; the clap reads as one event, not separate ticks.
  • The FM bass and lead brighten by index, not by getting louder — gain-compensated.
  • The pad evolves from a wavetable sweep, not from new notes.
  • One control carries the drone; freezing it kills the part.
  • The peak arrives because parameters opened, not because elements were added.
  • You can explain why each synthesis method was chosen for each sound.
Failure signals
  • You reached for a sampled drum "just to fill it out."
  • The kick boops — a slow downward glide with no click and no punch.
  • The hats smear into a continuous wash and the groove disappears.
  • The clap sounds like one dry snap — the multi-burst stagger collapsed.
  • An FM "brightness" change was really just a volume change.
  • The FM lead is a static buzzer — the index never moves.
  • The "west-coast" drone has a resonant filter "wah" — that is east-coast.
  • Any sound you can only describe with an adjective, never a number.

The required fault — induce it, hear it, diagnose it

Required

You must create at least one of these faults on purpose, hear it clearly, and write the diagnosis. Recognising these by ear later — when they happen by accident — is the certification of this Lab Note.

Fault

The transient-less kick (and its siblings)

Induce it
  • On the kick, slow the pitch-envelope time from ~5 ms to 80–150 ms and mute the click oscillator.
  • Play it: the kick is now a "boooo" — a slow glide with no punch that will not cut through the hat and clap.
  • Restore the fast pitch envelope and the click. Compare at the same volume and write the rule.
Diagnosis — symptom → cause → fix
SymptomCauseFix
Kick boops, no clickPitch env too slow / no separate clickPitch env time 3–8 ms; add a 2–5 ms click in parallel
Hats smear into a washDecay too long / sustain above 0Sustain 0, closed-hat decay ~40 ms
Clap is one dry snapStagger collapsed to a single burstRestore 3–4 bursts ~12 ms apart + a glue tail
FM tone lifelessStatic, un-enveloped indexAdd a decay envelope to the modulator level
"Brighter" is just louderIndex raised without gain-compensatingTrim carrier/output so loudness stays matched

As a method, not a preset

  • Rebuild any voice on the MicroFreak by translating the envelopes and filters
  • Design a target sound by naming its method and ratio first, then dialing
  • Treat the sample library as a fallback, not the menu

Reflection questions

  1. At what pitch-envelope time did the kick change from "boop" to "punch"? Name the number.
  2. Which method gave you the most control, and which gave you the most surprise?
  3. At which FM ratio did the tone stop being singable? Was the boundary as sharp as you expected?
  4. What did enveloping the index (or fold depth) give you that raising it statically could not?
  5. How did the low-pass gate's decay differ from an ADSR-closed VCA on the same pluck?
  6. Which single control carried the drone, and did freezing it really kill the part?
  7. After the A/B against samples, when would you choose a sampled kit — and what is the mechanical reason?
  8. Did the peak arrive because parameters opened, or because you secretly added an element?
  9. Which sound is still described by an adjective in your notes, and what number is missing?
  10. What would you build entirely differently next session, now that you can predict the spectrum?
Final principle

Sound is built, not picked. A drum is an envelope around a moving frequency; a bell is a ratio; a pad is a stack of partials you chose. When you can predict the spectrum from the patch, the sample library becomes a fallback — and the cheapest way to sound like yourself is to manufacture your sounds.
