Lab Note B2-001
Synthesis from Zero · 02 — Listen

Listen for the machine, not the mood

Before you patch anything, train your ear to hear synthesis as mechanism. The records below are landmarks of sound built from zero. Run each one through the same six questions — you are not listening for whether you like it, you are reverse-engineering how it was made.

The six-question frame

Ask these six questions of every sound you study. They map directly onto the synthesis decisions you will make on the Decisions and Build pages.

#QuestionWhat it tells you
1Can I sing its pitch?A clear pitch means a harmonic spectrum (integer ratios, low partials). A smeared or absent pitch means inharmonic content or noise.
2Where do the harmonics come from?Subtraction (filter carving a rich source), addition (FM/additive building partials up), or folding (a sine made complex by amplitude). Listen for which.
3How does the timbre change over one note?If brightness moves while pitch holds, something is enveloped — a filter cutoff, an FM index, a fold depth. A static timbre sounds dead.
4What shapes the volume?Fast attack + short decay = percussive. Slow attack = a pad swell. Does the brightness fall with the volume (a low-pass gate) or independently (a VCA)?
5Is there noise, and is it shaped?Hats and claps are filtered noise. Ask whether the noise is high-passed (airy hat), band-passed (clap/snare body), or sweeping (riser).
6What single move carries the part?The most hypnotic synthesized parts evolve from one parameter moving slowly — find it. That is the move you will steal.

How to use each reference

Listening protocol

Find one sound, run the six questions, write one hypothesis

Isolate, don't admire
  • Pick a single repeated sound in the record — a kick, a pluck, a held tone. Loop a few seconds of it in your head.
  • Run the six questions in order. Write the answers as mechanism, not adjective: "pitch falls then settles, ~5 ms" — not "punchy".
  • If an answer is just a feeling, you have not heard the machine yet. Keep listening to that one sound.
Name the method out loud
  • Carlos: "harmonic, subtractive — a saw carved by a filter, with a separate envelope on cutoff."
  • Subotnick: "a folded tone whose brightness tracks loudness, articulated by a gate that darkens as it fades."
  • Ciani: "a sequence kept alive because fold depth changes per step — freeze it and the part dies."

The one stolen principle

Steal this — and only this

From Subotnick and Ciani, steal the discipline of one moving control: a single synthesis parameter, enveloped or sequenced, can carry an entire part for minutes. From Carlos, steal the opposite rigour — every partial placed on purpose. Your finished piece should let a stranger freeze one knob and hear the part collapse, proving the expression really lived in that one move.

  • Steal a principle, not a sound. You may copy the idea "brightness rises with loudness" — you may not copy a Subotnick phrase. The replica stays in your logbook as a study; the printed track is yours.
  • The six questions are the bridge. Every answer you write here becomes a decision on the next page. "Inharmonic, bell-like" → FM with a non-integer ratio. "Airy noise, no pitch" → high-passed white noise. Listening is the spec.
