Lab Note B2-001
Synthesis from Zero · 01 — Overview

Build a finished piece where every sound is synthesized from zero

No samples anywhere — not even the drums. Oscillators, noise, and envelopes are the only raw material. By the end you will be able to look at a patch and predict its spectrum before you hear it, and to defend every millisecond of every envelope. This is a deep-reference companion to Block 2 · Sources.

The one principle

Principle

Sound is built, not picked. A sample is a decision someone else already made and froze. A synthesized sound is a decision you keep making — adjustable to the millisecond, tunable to the bassline, predictable from the patch.

A drum is not a recording. It is a volume shape wrapped around a frequency that moves. A bell is not a found object — it is a ratio between two oscillators. A pad is not a preset — it is a stack of partials you chose. Once you internalise that every sound is a small machine you assemble, the sample library stops being the menu and becomes a fallback.

What you'll make

A 4–6 minute all-synthesized piece

  • Kick, hat, clap built from oscillators and noise — no sampled drums
  • Bass, lead, pad, and a drone, each from a named method
  • Five synthesis methods covered: subtractive, FM, additive, noise shaping, west-coast

A spectrum you can predict

  • Integer ratio → harmonic · non-integer → inharmonic
  • White noise + high-pass → hat · band-pass → clap
  • Fold a sine → harmonics that bloom with level; gate it → bongo decay
Core objective

Understand each synthesis method well enough to choose it on purpose — and to draw the signal flow of any sound in the finished track from memory.

Why it matters

The sample-library instinct

  • Browse for "a better kick" for an hour, audition nothing in context
  • Accept whatever attack, pitch, and tail the file came with
  • Fight the result with EQ instead of fixing it at the source

The synthesis discipline

  • Decide where the click ends and the body begins
  • Tune the kick's fundamental to the bass so the low end is one decision
  • Make a part evolve by enveloping a synthesis parameter, not by adding tracks

The skill transfers everywhere — techno, ambient, sound design, scoring, generative systems. You are not learning presets. You are learning that every sound is a system with inputs you control, and that the cheapest way to make a track sound like yours is to manufacture its sounds instead of borrowing them.

Originality constraint

Replicas stay in the logbook. You may study any reference and steal a single principle from it, but the piece you print must be assembled from your own patches — not a clone of a record.
