Synthesis from Zero · 03 — Decisions

Five methods, and when each is the right one

Every synthesis method is a different answer to the same question: where do the harmonics come from? Subtraction removes them, FM and additive build them up, noise has all of them at once, and west-coast folds them out of a sine. Choosing the method first, from the sound you want, is the whole discipline — it is the difference between designing a sound and stumbling onto one.

The decision that sits above all others

Harmonic or inharmonic?

Before you pick a method, decide whether the sound has a singable pitch. Harmonic spectra (partials at integer multiples of a fundamental) read as pitched — basses, leads, pads. Inharmonic spectra (partials at non-integer ratios) read as bells, metal, wood, glass — material rather than melody. This single answer eliminates half the methods immediately.

The five methods

Subtractive

  • Start rich (saw/square), remove harmonics with a resonant filter, shape with ADSR
  • Brightness and loudness are separate controls
  • Best for: basses, classic leads, anything you want to filter-sweep
  • The default; what most synths and the MicroFreak filter do

FM / phase modulation

  • One oscillator bends another's pitch thousands of times a second, adding sidebands
  • Ratio decides harmonic vs inharmonic; index decides brightness; pitch never moves with either
  • Best for: electric pianos, metallic bells, aggressive bass, glassy perc
  • Ableton Operator is a true four-operator PM synth

Additive

  • Build the spectrum partial by partial; each harmonic has its own level and envelope
  • Total control, total cost — many parameters to manage
  • Best for: evolving pads, organ tones, sounds where partials move independently
  • Ableton Wavetable and the MicroFreak Harmo engine approximate it

Noise shaping

  • White noise has every frequency at once; a filter chooses the colour, an envelope the length
  • High-pass → hat · band-pass → clap/snare body · sweeping band-pass → riser
  • Best for: all unpitched percussion, air, texture, transitions
  • No oscillator pitch to tune — only filter and envelope

West-coast (wavefolding + low-pass gate)

  • Start with a harmonically poor sine/triangle; a wavefolder adds harmonics as input level rises
  • A low-pass gate (vactrol VCA+VCF) ties brightness, loudness, and decay length into one gesture
  • Best for: organic plucks, generative sequences, anything where one control should carry the part
  • Built in VCV Rack; the MicroFreak has a folder but no true LPG

The choices and their tradeoffs

Tradeoff 01

Predictability vs. surprise

The spectrum
  • Subtractive is the most predictable: you can hear what a filter sweep will do before you do it.
  • FM is predictable once you know the model — ratio sets the spectrum's shape, index its strength. Its "unpredictable" reputation comes from twiddling without that model.
  • Additive is fully deterministic but expensive — every partial is a decision.
  • Choose subtractive when you want to feel the result in your hands; FM when you want a specific spectral colour (bell, e-piano) that subtraction cannot reach.
Tradeoff 02

Coupled vs. independent controls

How expression is wired
  • East-coast (subtractive) keeps brightness, loudness, and length as three separate knobs — flexible, but expression must be programmed across all three.
  • West-coast (LPG) fuses them: hit it harder and it is louder, brighter, and longer at once — organic, but you give up independent control.
  • Choose the LPG when you want one expressive gesture to carry a part (the Subotnick/Ciani move). Choose subtractive when you need a deliberate filter sweep that loudness must not affect.
Tradeoff 03

Static spectrum vs. movement over the note

What keeps a sound alive
  • A fixed spectrum is interesting for one second, then inert. Every method needs something enveloped: filter cutoff (subtractive), modulation index (FM), partial levels (additive), fold depth (west-coast).
  • The cheapest aliveness comes from enveloping the one parameter unique to each method — that is what makes a patch read as an instrument rather than a buzzer.
  • This is the link to the Listen page: "how does the timbre change over one note?" is answered by which parameter you envelope.

The drum decision

A drum is an envelope shaping a frequency

The kit is not one method — it is three small machines. The kick is a falling sine (pitch envelope) plus a click (a 2–5 ms second generator). The hat is gated high-passed noise — almost all decay, no attack. The clap is three or four band-passed noise bursts staggered ~12 ms apart, glued by a short reverb tail. Drawing each as a signal flow is the certification this Lab Note demands.

Decision check — before you touch a synth
  • For every sound in the piece, can I name the method and say why, in one sentence, before I open the device?
  • For each pitched sound: harmonic or inharmonic — and which knob makes it so?
  • For each sound: which single parameter will I envelope to keep it alive over the note?
  • Is any sound a sample I reached for out of habit? If so, name the method that would build it instead.
