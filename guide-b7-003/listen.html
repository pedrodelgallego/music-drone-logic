Lab Note B7-003
Feedback Safety · 02 — Listen

Listen before you patch a single cable

A feedback instrument is not learned from a diagram — it is learned from the ear of someone who has lived inside the loop for years. These three players define the practice: the no-input mixer as a solo voice, feedback as an architectural space, and the high sine as a held edge. Listen to each as the rep lesson directs, then come back. The whole point of this note is to make sounds like these without copying any of them.

Three players, three uses of the loop

Toshimaru Nakamura

  • Patience: long tones, micro-moves, the mixer's own character

David Tudor

  • Rainforest IV — feedback through resonant physical objects
  • The loop as a space the audience walks inside, not a single tone

What to listen for in each

Nakamura — No-Input Mixing Board
The instrumentA mixer's outputs patched back to its own inputs. No microphone, no synth, no sample — the circuit's own noise floor, amplified into a tone.
Listen forHow slowly he moves. A single fader nudge changes the pitch of the whole system. Nothing is fast, because in this instrument fast means runaway.
Steal thisThe discipline of treating one knob as the whole performance. You will cap that knob's range on page 04 so the slowness is structural, not just willpower.
Tudor — Rainforest IV
The instrumentAudio driven into physical objects (sheet metal, barrels, springs); their resonance fed back through contact pickups into the loop.
Listen forThat the feedback has a body — it rings at the object's pitch, not an abstract sine. The room and the object are part of the circuit.
Steal thisThe idea that an EQ or a resonant filter in your loop is the "object" — it decides which frequency oscillates. You tune the instability instead of fighting it.
Sachiko M — Sine Wave Solo
The instrumentA sampler's test-tone sine waves, held and shifted with almost no other material.
Listen forThe nerve it takes to commit to one high tone and let it sit. The tension is entirely in what does not change.
Steal thisRestraint as content. A held feedback tone at the brink is a musical event by itself — you do not need to "do" anything to it.
Safety note while listening

Even these recordings are best auditioned on speakers at a moderate level. Sine and feedback content sits in narrow bands your ear fatigues to quickly — and you are about to spend a session making more of it. Protect your hearing now; you only get the one pair.

The originality line

  • Copy nothing. These players are references for an approach, not source material. Your loop, your mixer, your room — the tone will be yours because the circuit is yours.
  • Take the discipline, not the lick. What carries over is the slowness, the tuned instability, the commitment to a held edge — never a specific gesture or pitch.
  • Name what you took. In the reflection you will write down which of these three taught you the most. Knowing the lineage is how you stay original on purpose.
