Lab Note B7-003
Feedback Safety · 01 — Overview

Build, tune, and safely perform a feedback instrument

The no-input mixing board and the self-oscillating filter are the most alive instruments in the rig — they make sound from nothing but their own returning signal. They are also the only instruments that can hurt you. This Lab Note treats the feedback system as a live performance: how to build the loop, find and hold its playable states, and stay one move from silence the whole time. Safety is not a warning at the bottom — it is the instrument's first design decision.

The goal

A performed feedback take

  • A 4–6 minute take of a no-input mixer or self-oscillating instrument played live
  • Built loop, tuned tone, swells ridden to the brink and pulled back — on purpose
  • Recorded into the workshop set as raw material, fully original to your rig

Fundamentals you'll practice

  • Closing a feedback loop deliberately; reading where unity gain sits
  • In-circuit limiting, capped encoder ranges, speaker-only monitoring
  • A rehearsed panic path; tuning the loop to a pitch; performing the swell
Core objective

Make an unstable system playable — stable enough to perform, alive enough to be worth performing — without ever being more than one move from killing it.

The angle of this note

There are two ways feedback shows up in this workshop, and they are different instruments with different disciplines. Do not confuse them.

This note — the loop as instrument

  • Feedback is the source — no sample, no synth note under it
  • A no-input mixer, a self-oscillating filter, an amplified object
  • Played live and on the edge of runaway; the danger is the point

Block 5 — dub feedback (separate)

  • Delay/echo feedback applied to an existing sound
  • There is always a dry source to fall back to
  • A flavour, not an instrument — covered in its own note
Why it belongs in Block 7

Block 7 is about performance systems — riding parameters, live resampling, set structure. A feedback instrument is the purest test of all of it: there is no undo, no dry signal, and no second chance if the panic path fails. Master it here and every other live move feels safe by comparison.

Why this exercise matters

The beginner instinct

  • Crank the feedback to hear it scream, then panic-grab a knob
  • Set it up in headphones because the room is loud
  • Treat safety as something to bolt on once the sound is found

The opposite discipline

  • Build the ceiling — limiter, capped ranges, panic path — before the first loud sound
  • Live at the brink as a musical event, never over it
  • Tune the instability to a pitch so it sings instead of shrieks

The discipline transfers. A musician who can hold a runaway system at the edge and recover it blind can perform anything: modular patches, dub mixers, live electronics, generative rigs. You are not learning a trick — you are learning to perform a system that does not want to be controlled.

Principle

The feedback path has no ceiling of its own. The ceiling is the one you build — and you build it first.
