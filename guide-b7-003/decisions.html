Feedback Safety · 03 — Decisions

What a feedback instrument is, and why it is dangerous

Before any cable is patched, you decide three things: what the instrument actually is, how the safety discipline is built into it from the start, and what makes an unstable system playable rather than merely loud. Every choice on this page is made on purpose, in the studio, while nothing is at risk — because once the loop is live, there is no time to think.

What a feedback instrument is

No-input / self-oscillating

  • A mixer routed back into itself; an output patched to its own input
  • A filter or echo pushed past unity so it sustains on its own energy
  • The instrument is the loop — there is no source sample to fall back on

Why it is unstable

  • Gain above unity grows without bound — exponentially, fast
  • A small knob move can flip it from a hum to a scream
  • Room temperature, cable, and EQ all shift the threshold mid-set

The theory, in just enough depth

You do not need control-systems mathematics to play this instrument, but you need the intuition behind one number: loop gain. It is the total amount the signal is multiplied by as it travels once around the loop and back to where it started.

Loop gain — the one concept that governs everything
Below unity (gain < 1)Each pass is quieter than the last. Any sound dies away. The loop is silent and stable — useless, but safe.
At unity (gain = 1)Each pass returns at the same level. A tone, once started, sustains forever. This is the playable region — the held edge.
Above unity (gain > 1)Each pass is louder than the last. The level doubles, then doubles again, in milliseconds. This is runaway — the scream.
  • The playable edge is a knife, not a plateau. The interesting region between "dies away" and "runs away" can be a sliver of one knob's travel. Performing this instrument is keeping the loop balanced on that sliver.
  • The threshold drifts. Loop gain depends on cable length, the room's reflections, the EQ curve, even temperature warming the gear. The edge you found in soundcheck is not exactly the edge two hours into the set.
  • Frequency is selective. The loop does not oscillate at all frequencies equally — it screams loudest where the loop gain is highest, which is wherever your EQ or filter has the most boost. That is the lever you use to tune the tone (page 05).
The central question

When the system becomes unstable, does the performer understand it well enough to recover musically? A feedback rig only belongs in a set if the answer is yes — which means the recovery is built before the sound is.

The safety discipline — built before the sound

These three rules are not advice. They are the design of the instrument. Decide them now, build them on page 04, and never relax them live. They are listed as decisions because they constrain everything that follows.

Rule 01

A limiter before the loop closes

A hard ceiling that lives on the path
  • Place a limiter on the feedback return, in-circuit, with the ceiling at -3 dBFS. The loop can want to run away; the limiter decides it cannot.
  • This is not the master limiter. It sits on the feedback path specifically, so the runaway is caught before it ever reaches the master bus or the PA.
  • Set the ceiling in rehearsal at the loudest the loop will ever be allowed to get, and never raise it live. Raising it live is the single most common way performers hurt a room.
  • A limiter does not prevent the squeal — it caps how loud the squeal can be. That is the difference between an unpleasant moment and an injury.
Rule 02

Gain ceilings on every encoder

Map the danger out of reach
  • Any encoder that controls feedback amount (echo feedback, the no-input send, the resonance knob) gets its Min/Max range set so the maximum is below runaway, not at it.
  • The encoder physically cannot reach the value that screams. The limit is structural, not a thing you remember to respect under pressure.
  • From the workshop's earlier performance notes, echo feedback was already capped at 70 % for this reason. A self-oscillating instrument deserves a tighter cap still — find the runaway point in rehearsal and set the cap a clear margin below it.
  • The cost of a cap that is slightly too low is a tone you cannot quite push to the edge. The cost of no cap is a room full of people with their hands over their ears. Choose the cheap mistake.
Rule 03

Never on headphones

Protect your hearing first
  • A feedback path that jumps to its ceiling in headphones can injure your ears before your hand reaches the kill. Monitor feedback experiments on speakers, at a level you could comfortably leave the room from.
  • On stage, the feedback loop reaches the PA. Know where the house engineer's master is and that they can pull it — your safety net has a second pair of hands.
  • This is the one rule with no musical exception. There is no take worth the risk. If the only monitoring you have is headphones, you do not build a feedback instrument today.

The panic path — decided now, rehearsed later

A panic path is a single, rehearsed move that returns the system to a safe, silent-enough state regardless of what it was doing. It is the most important thing on the whole surface. You decide where it lives here; you make it reflex on page 06.

The panic path — one move, always in the same place
What it isOne dedicated control — a mapped mute, a kill scene, a single mixer channel — that cuts the feedback path to silence instantly.
Where it livesThe same physical spot every set. Taped, labeled, never remapped. You must be able to find it without looking.
What it doesDrops the feedback return to zero and mutes the loop's source. Not a fade — an instant cut. Musicality comes after safety, never before.
How you keep itRehearse hitting it blind, mid-build, until it is reflex. A panic path you have to think about is a panic path you will miss.
The principle

You are never more than one move from silence. If that is not true of your feedback rig, the rig is not ready to leave the studio.

What makes an unstable system playable

A loud feedback loop is not an instrument — it is a fault. What turns the fault into something you can perform is a small set of choices that put musical movement between dead silence and runaway.

A wide, slow approach to the edge

  • The control that crosses unity should do so over a long, gentle travel
  • If unity is reached in a hair of knob movement, you cannot ride it — you can only flip it
  • Page 04 spreads that travel across the encoder's whole range

A frequency you chose

  • An EQ or filter in the loop decides where it oscillates
  • Tune that frequency to a note in your set's key and the instability sings
  • Without it, the loop screams wherever the gear happens to peak

A held middle state

  • A region where a tone sustains steadily without growing
  • This is the "playable" plateau you return to between gestures
  • Find it once in rehearsal and learn exactly where the knob sits

An instant exit

  • The panic path, so any approach to the edge is reversible
  • Knowing the exit is one move away is what lets you push toward the edge at all
  • Confidence comes from the kill, not from the gain

The choices to make before page 04

Decisions you carry into the build
Which instrument?No-input mixer (most control, needs a small mixer), self-oscillating filter in the DAW or hardware (easiest to cap and limit), or amplified object (most character, least predictable). Beginners: start with the self-oscillating filter — every safety rule is trivial to enforce in software.
Where is the limiter?On the feedback return, in-circuit, ceiling -3 dBFS. Decide the exact insert point now.
Which control crosses unity?The one encoder you will ride. Everything else stays parked. Name it now so page 04 can cap only it.
Where is the panic path?One fixed, labeled control. Pick the physical spot and commit — it never moves again.
What key / pitch?The note you will tune the loop toward, so it agrees with the rest of the set.
Monitoring?Speakers, named and confirmed. If the answer is "headphones", stop here.
Restraint, as always

One feedback voice, one control to ride, one tuned frequency. The instrument is dangerous in direct proportion to how much of it is moving at once. Do less, and make the one thing you do reversible.
