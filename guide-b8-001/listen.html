Lab Note B8-001
Spec & Voice · 02 — Listen

Three authors who wrote the system down

Before you write a spec, study people who treated the rules as part of the work — who published constraints, refusals, and theses, not just finished tracks. Listen and read for the document behind the output: the deck of provocations, the manifesto, the voice that survives a changing palette.

Listen for the spec, not the sound

Each of these is a rep lesson in the Block 8 workshop. Open them in order. For each, do not ask "do I like this?" Ask "what rule produced it, and would I sign that rule?"

Listen 01

Brian Eno & Peter Schmidt — Oblique Strategies

A constraint system you can hold in your hand
  • Open the rep lesson. The "work" here is a deck of cards, each a single instruction — a constraint extracted from intuition and made repeatable.
  • Notice the form: every card is short, actionable, and can be obeyed or refused on the spot. "Honour thy error as a hidden intention." "Use an old idea." This is exactly the texture a good constraint has — a rule with teeth, not a mood.
  • The deck is tool-agnostic. It would survive a change of studio, decade, or genre. That portability is the mark of a System Spec, not an engineering note.
What to take into your own spec
  • A spec can be a set of small independent rules, not one paragraph. Each rule earns its place by changing a decision.
  • The cards externalise taste so it can be argued with. Your spec should do the same — written so a non-musician could catch you breaking it.
Listen 02

Matthew Herbert — the P.C.C.O.M. manifesto

A refusal list published as law
  • Open the rep lesson. Herbert's Personal Contract for the Composition of Music is a numbered set of bans: no drum machines, no presets, no sounds sampled from other recordings, no audio created the day before the edit.
  • This is a spec that is almost entirely refusals. Read how much character emerges from the prohibitions alone — you can hear the music in the rules before you hear a note.
  • The constraints are generative: banning the sample library forces him to record the world, which becomes the signature. The limit is the engine, exactly as page 03 argues.
What to take into your own spec
  • Write the refusal list first. It costs you something, which is why it is harder to fake than a list of aspirations.
  • A constraint should make a specific kind of work inevitable, not merely forbidden. Test each ban: what does it force you toward?
Listen 03

Laurel Halo — Dust

One voice across a deliberately unstable palette
  • Open the rep lesson. Dust changes instrumentation, collaborators, and language track to track, yet it is unmistakably one author's record.
  • This is the proof of the central claim: the voice is not a sound. If it were, swapping the palette would erase it. Instead the system reappears — the handling of space, the refusal to resolve, the way a piece declines to announce its structure.
  • Listen for the recurring move that survives every change of material. That survivor is the signature; everything else is wardrobe.
What to take into your own spec
  • If your voice depends on one synth patch, it is a sound, not a voice. Write the spec so it would still stand on a borrowed rig.
  • The thing that recurs against your intention is the raw material. Page 06 is where you go find it in your own archive.

What the three have in common

The rules are part of the art

  • The document is authored as deliberately as the output
  • Constraints are published, not hidden
  • The rules outlive any single piece

The refusals carry the weight

  • Each names what it will not do, in plain terms
  • The bans cost the author a tool others rely on
  • What is closed makes the open doors matter
  • Influences are not a voice. All three could list their influences; none of their voices reduce to a list. The voice is the system underneath, which is what you are about to write down.
  • Read the rules as falsifiable. Herbert's manifesto can be broken, the Strategies can be ignored, Halo's restraint can be abandoned — and you would notice. A rule you cannot break is decoration.
