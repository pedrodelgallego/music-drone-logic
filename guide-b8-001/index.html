Lab Note B8-001
Spec & Voice · 01 — Overview

Author the document your work obeys

For seven blocks you followed specs someone else wrote. This Lab Note reverses the arrow: you write the System Spec and the Engineering Spec yourself, design a constraint system from nothing, name what you refuse, and compress it all into one thesis sentence. A spec is not a description written after the fact — it is a contract you sign before you start, and the part of you that signs it is your voice.

The goal

Two documents and one sentence

  • A System Spec — what must be true of the result, tool-agnostic
  • An Engineering Spec — how the rig produces it, reproducible
  • A thesis sentence a stranger could use to recognise your work blind

Skills you'll practise

  • Auditing your own archive for the move you cannot stop making
  • Writing constraints as a budget — numbers and refusals, never vibes
  • Building one short piece that obeys a spec you authored, not borrowed
Core objective

Make the voice legible in a document, so the work is recognisably yours before a single note is played.

Why this matters

The instinct without a spec

  • Start from the gear and a mood, hope a voice arrives
  • Decide the rules silently, then quietly break them at 2 a.m.
  • Describe the work after it exists, never before

The discipline of authoring

  • Name the felt result, then the rules that make it inevitable
  • Write constraints you can be caught breaking
  • Let the refusals — not the influences — define the edges

A spec is portable in a way a track is not. The track is one output; the spec is the generator behind every future output. Hand a stranger your spec and watch them make something recognisably yours — that is the proof the voice lives in the system, not in your hands.

Principle

What you refuse defines you more sharply than what you allow.

The seven pages

  • 02 Listen — three authors who wrote their systems down: Eno & Schmidt's deck of refusals, Herbert's manifesto-as-spec, Halo's voice across a changing palette.
  • 03 Decisions — what a spec is, the two altitudes, constraint design, refusal theory, and how a spec encodes a voice.
  • 04 Setup — how the spec maps to a session template on the workshop rig: clock master, track list, return logic.
  • 05 Palette — a fill-in spec template and a voice-audit worksheet, both with worked examples.
  • 06 The build — the deep page: audit your archive, write the spec, design the constraints, draft the thesis, and build a piece to your own spec.
  • 07 Reflection — the fault, the carry-forward, the principle, and the return to the workshop.
