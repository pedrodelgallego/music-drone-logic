Lab Note B8-002
The Release · 03 — Decisions

The theory you decide before you meter

Mastering goes wrong when "how loud is it?" is answered by ear at one volume. There are four honest answers — peak, RMS, LUFS, true-peak — and only some of them describe loudness at all. This page is the theory: what each meter measures, why streaming normalisation makes the loudness war unwinnable, when and how to dither, and how to sequence an EP as one statement. Decide all of it here, so the build is execution, not improvisation.

The four ways to answer "how loud?"

"Make it loud" is not a target — it is a wish. A target is a number with a meter behind it: −14 LUFS integrated, true-peak under −1 dBTP, delivered 16-bit dithered. Once you can read those numbers, loudness stops being a vibe and becomes a decision you defend.

The metering vocabulary
Peak (sample peak)The single highest sample value in the file. Tells you about clipping headroom, almost nothing about loudness — a quiet click and a wall of sound can share the same peak.
RMSThe average energy over a window — closer to "how loud does it feel" than peak, but it ignores how the ear weights frequencies. A useful old tool; LUFS is its modern, ear-matched replacement.
LUFSLoudness Units relative to Full Scale — RMS filtered to match human hearing, and the unit every streaming platform uses. Three windows: momentary (≈400 ms, the instantaneous feel), short-term (≈3 s, the section feel), and integrated (the whole piece averaged — the one number that defines a master's loudness).
True-peak (dBTP)The real peak between samples, after the file is reconstructed into a continuous wave on playback or conversion. A file can read 0.0 dB on a sample-peak meter and still overshoot to +0.8 dBTP when decoded — inter-sample clipping a sample-peak meter never showed you.
Correlation / phaseA meter from −1 to +1 showing how the left and right channels relate. +1 is mono-summable; 0 is wide but safe; −1 means the channels cancel and the signal disappears in mono. Watch it the way you watch true-peak.
Why integrated LUFS is the one number

Momentary and short-term tell you about moments; integrated tells you about the master. When a platform normalises, it acts on the integrated value — so that is the number you target, and the short-term meter is how you watch a loud section not run away from it.

Loudness normalisation — why the war is unwinnable

The squashed master

  • Mastered to −6 LUFS to "win" loudness
  • The platform turns it down to its target — the loudness is gone
  • What remains is the cost: crushed transients, no dynamic contrast, pre-distortion on lossy encode
  • It arrives no louder than anyone else, and worse

The dynamic master

  • Mastered to ≈ −14 LUFS, true-peak under −1 dBTP
  • The platform leaves it near where you set it
  • It keeps its transients, its quiet, its room to move
  • It plays back at the same loudness as the squashed one — but intact
The trade, in one sentence

Every dB of integrated LUFS you gain above the mix's natural level costs dynamic range and risks pumping. Streaming normalisation then gives the loudness back to everyone equally — so the dB you sacrificed bought nothing. Master for how the piece wants to move, not to win a fight that no longer exists.

  • Streaming wants ≈ −14 LUFS. Above it the platform only turns you down, keeping the dynamics you crushed for nothing. At or below it, you keep your dynamics and lose no loudness.
  • Club and long-form can sit quieter and more dynamic. A hypnotic piece built on a slow rise should keep its range — do not crush it to match a single. The mastering target follows the listening context, not a default.
  • True-peak is the real ceiling. Set −1.0 dBTP so lossy encoders never clip on conversion. Sample-peak at 0.0 dB is not safe; the inter-sample overshoot is the part that distorts.

Dither — apply once, last, only at bit-depth reduction

Theory

What dither is, and the rules

Why it exists
  • Reducing from 24-bit to 16-bit throws away the lowest bits. Without dither, the rounding errors correlate with the signal and become audible quantisation distortion in quiet tails — the worst place for it.
  • Dither adds a tiny, carefully shaped noise before the truncation, decorrelating the error. You trade a barely-audible noise floor for the removal of an ugly distortion. It is the right trade, every time you reduce bit depth.
The rules
Dither discipline
Apply onceExactly one dither stage, the very last thing in the chain, on the bit-depth-reducing export. Dithering twice stacks two noise floors for no benefit.
Only on reductionDither only when going to 16-bit. Never dither a 24-bit master — there is no truncation to mask.
Keep the source un-ditheredThe 24-bit / 48 kHz master is the source of truth. Export every delivery format from it; dither happens inside each 16-bit export, not in the master.
Noise shapingUse the encoder's standard noise-shaped dither for material a listener will sit with. It pushes the dither noise into frequencies the ear is least sensitive to.

Reference-matching — match balance, not loudness

What to match

  • Broad spectral balance — the relative weight of sub, low-mid, mid, and top
  • The relationship of bass to top, the thing that stays constant across systems
  • How dynamic the reference is — whether it breathes or sits flat
  • How it translates on a phone, a car, and in mono

What not to match

  • Its exact loudness — level-match the reference down to your master to compare honestly
  • Its arrangement, its sounds, its signature gestures — that breaks originality
  • Its peak loudness as a goal — loud references will pull you into the unwinnable war
  • A single moment — match the whole-piece balance, not one drop
Level-match before you judge

The louder of two tracks always sounds "better" for the first few seconds — more weight, more excitement. Before A/B-ing against a reference, turn the reference down so its loudness matches yours. Only then are you comparing tone and dynamics instead of being fooled by level.

Sequencing the EP as one statement

A release is not a folder of tracks. It is one arc made of several pieces — the same long-form discipline from a single track, applied across the running order.

Sequencing decisions
OpenerStates the thesis plainly. It is the EP's intro — it sets the room and earns the rest.
Signature piecePlace it where its weight lands — often second or penultimate, rarely first. The opener earns it.
Keys & tempoSequence so adjacent tracks share or neighbour a key, and tempo steps feel intentional. Abrupt jumps read as a compilation, not a statement.
GapsDecide the silence between tracks. A long-form release often wants near-gapless flow; the space is part of the composition, not a default.
CloserResolves or deliberately refuses to — the same tension-and-release choice you made inside one track, now at EP scale.
Inter-track loudnessMaster the EP to a consistent integrated LUFS across tracks so the listener never reaches for the volume. A quiet interlude is a choice; a track 4 LUFS quieter by accident is a fault.
  • Listen to the whole sequence in one sitting, start to finish, more than once. The running order is wrong until it stops feeling like a list.
  • The artist statement is the thesis sentence expanded — it names the intention and the refusals, not the gear. The positioning names who it is for and who it is not for. The archive stores the master, session, statement, and patches together so the release can be defended twice.
Decisions to lock before the build
  • What is my integrated-LUFS target, and why — does this music want impact-on-first-listen or dynamic contrast?
  • What is my true-peak ceiling? (≤ −1.0 dBTP recommended.)
  • Which one delivery context am I mastering for first — streaming, file, or club?
  • What is my running order, and does it read as one statement when played whole?
  • Have I chosen one reference track in the same idiom, in lossless form, to match balance against?
