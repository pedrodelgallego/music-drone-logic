Lab Note B8-002
The Release · 06 — The build

Mix to master to sequence to deliver — one EP, one target

Take three finished pieces to a delivered EP at a named loudness target. Each step is small, has a decision checkpoint, and names exact dB and LUFS values. Keep the delivery targets page open in another tab — it is the recipe this build assumes — and keep the rig page open for the chain order. We master to −14 LUFS integrated, true-peak −1.0 dBTP, for streaming; the method transfers to any other target by changing one number.

Step 01

Finalise the mix and bounce a clean source

Confirm the mix is actually finished
  • Open the mix set for track 1. The master bus carries Spectrum and Utility only — no limiter, no master EQ. Mastering happens in a separate set; mixing and mastering must not blur into each other.
  • Peaks across the whole piece sit ≤ −3 dBFS with headroom for the loudest section's reverb bloom. If anything touches 0 dBFS, the mix is too hot — pull the master fader, do not reach for a limiter here.
  • Do the four mix-verification passes one last time: sidechain steady, mono-compatible, solo-pairs resolved, and a quiet-volume listen where the balance still holds.
  • Take headphones off for five minutes before deciding the mix is done. A fatigued ear over-brightens.

Decision check
  • Is the mix-bus genuinely free of dynamics processing, so the master starts from honest material?
  • Do all three mixes peak ≤ −3 dBFS with room left for tails?
  • Could I hand these three WAVs to a stranger and call them finished mixes?
Step 02

Build the mastering set and read the starting point

Set up the session
  • ⌘ N for a new set. Save as EP-MASTER.als in a dedicated release folder. Buffer 512, sample rate 48 kHz.
  • Drag ep_track1_mix_v1.wav onto an audio track. Clip view → Warp off. Never time-stretch a finished mix.
  • On the master chain, build the recipe from the setup page in order: EQ Eight (corrective) → Glue Compressor → EQ Eight (tonal) → Limiter → LUFS meter. Bypass the Glue, tonal EQ, and Limiter for now.
Read the unprocessed loudness first
  • Reset the LUFS meter (integrated → reset). Play the whole track start to finish, untouched, and let the integrated reading settle.
  • Log it. A typical dynamic mix lands somewhere around −18 to −16 LUFS integrated with true-peak near −3 dBTP. That gap to −14 is the work the limiter will do.
  • If the mix already reads near or above −14 LUFS, it is too compressed at the mix stage — you have no room to master. Go back and restore dynamics before continuing.
Open the metering log

Keep a plain text file, ep_master_log.txt, beside the set. One line per reading. This is the engineering half of being able to say why — the same habit Visconti models on the listen page.

Metering log — starting state (example)
track 1 · mix, unprocessed−17.2 LUFS-I · −2.9 dBTP · corr +0.7
track 2 · mix, unprocessed−16.1 LUFS-I · −2.4 dBTP · corr +0.8

Decision check
  • Did I read the integrated LUFS over the whole piece, not a loud section?
  • Is there at least 2–3 LU of room between the unprocessed mix and the −14 target?
  • Is the starting state written in the log before I changed anything?
Step 03

Reference-match the tonal balance

Set up the A/B
  • Drop your chosen lossless reference on a second track, routed so it bypasses the master chain (or open it in a parallel set you toggle to). You must hear the reference raw, not through your processing.
  • Pull the reference track's gain down until its loudness matches your master on the LUFS meter and by ear. Removing the louder-sounds-better bias is the whole point of this step.
Match broad balance, fractions of a dB
  • Engage the tonal EQ Eight. A/B in short bursts on the same section type — sub against sub, top against top. Listen to broad balance, never detail.
  • If the reference has more low weight, add a low shelf of +0.5 to +1.5 dB below ~120 Hz. If it has more air, a high shelf of +0.5 to +1.5 dB above ~10 kHz — but trust the phone and car here, not the DT 990's lift.
  • Hard ceiling: if any move wants more than ±2 dB, stop. That is a mix problem — re-open the mix set and fix it, then re-bounce. Mastering EQ flavours; it does not rescue.
Glue, gently
  • Engage the Glue Compressor: ratio 2:1, attack 30 ms, release Auto. Bring the threshold down until the loudest section shows ≤ 1–2 dB gain reduction.
  • Bypass and compare at matched output: the compressed version should feel slightly more cohesive, never more squashed. If you hear pumping, you have gone too far — back off.
Decision check
  • Did I level-match the reference before judging tone?
  • Is every EQ move under ±2 dB — and could I name why each one is there?
  • Does the glue compression add cohesion without audible pumping at matched level?
Step 04

Reach the loudness target with the limiter

Set the ceiling before the gain
  • Engage the Limiter. True-peak limiting ON. Ceiling −1.0 dBTP. Set this first — the ceiling is the law, the loudness is what you negotiate underneath it.
  • Reset the LUFS integrated reading. Now raise the limiter's input gain slowly, playing the loudest section, until the short-term LUFS hovers around your target during the peak.
Confirm over the whole piece
  • Reset integrated again, play the entire track, and read the settled integrated value. Nudge input gain so it lands at −14.0 LUFS ± 0.5.
  • Watch the gain-reduction meter. Brief catches on transients are fine; sustained 3–4 dB+ reduction means you are pushing too hard — accept a slightly lower loudness instead of crushing the dynamics you protected all the way here.
  • Listen for the cost: if the kick stops punching or the low end pumps, you have hit the music's limit. The target is a ceiling on loudness, not a quota you must fill.
Log the result
Metering log — after master chain (example)
track 1 · mastered−14.1 LUFS-I · −1.0 dBTP · corr +0.7 · GR peak 2.3 dB
track 2 · mastered−13.9 LUFS-I · −1.0 dBTP · corr +0.8 · GR peak 1.8 dB
track 3 · mastered−14.2 LUFS-I · −1.0 dBTP · corr +0.6 · GR peak 2.6 dB
  • Three tracks within ±0.3 LU of each other is the goal — the EP must feel level across its length, with no track sending the listener for the volume.

Decision check
  • Is integrated LUFS at −14.0 ± 0.5 and true-peak holding at −1.0 dBTP?
  • Is gain reduction modest — transient catches, not sustained crushing?
  • Did reaching the number cost the punch or the dynamics? If yes, back off and accept a quieter, more honest master.
Step 05

Verify translation across four systems

Bounce a check file, listen everywhere
  • Quick-export the mastered track to a temporary 24-bit WAV. Copy it to your phone.
  • Audition in this order: monitors or DT 990 → phone speaker → car or earbuds → mono fold-down (Utility Width 0 % on the master, briefly).
What each system must confirm
Monitors / DT 990Detailed picture holds; you are not over-cutting the 7–10 kHz region the headphones exaggerate
Phone speakerGroove and lead survive with no sub and no air — the harshest honest test
Car / earbudsLow end present but not boomy; nothing fatiguing over a full play
MonoNothing essential cancels; correlation stays positive; the wide drone survives the fold-down
Fix at the right stage
  • If the phone exposes a thin low-mid, that is usually a mix fix, not a master EQ boost — re-open the mix before piling dB on the master.
  • If mono cancels part of the drone, the width was overdone in the mix's Utility — fix it there and re-bounce. Mastering cannot un-cancel phase.
  • Re-check the reference on the same four systems and note the one thing it keeps everywhere. Your master must clear that same bar.
Decision check
  • Does the master hold its shape on all four systems, quiet and loud?
  • Did anything vanish in mono? If yes, the fault is phase/width in the mix, not the master.
  • Does it clear the same translation bar the reference clears?
Step 06

Sequence the EP as one statement

Decide the running order
  • Lay all three mastered tracks end to end on the timeline of a fresh sequencing set. Treat the order like a single track's arrangement.
  • Opener states the thesis plainly. Signature piece lands where its weight pays off — often penultimate, rarely first. Closer resolves or deliberately refuses to.
  • Order so adjacent tracks share or neighbour a key and the tempo steps feel chosen. An abrupt jump reads as a compilation; smooth it or make the jump deliberate.
Decide the gaps
Silence between tracks
Standard gap1–2 s — a clean breath between distinct pieces
Near-gapless0–0.5 s — a long-form release where the flow is the composition
CrossfadeOverlap tails by a bar where two tracks share a drone or key — the EP becomes one continuous arc
  • Listen to the whole sequence in one sitting, start to finish, more than once. The order is wrong until it stops feeling like a list.
  • Confirm inter-track loudness: with all three at −14 ± 0.3 LUFS, the listener never reaches for the volume. A deliberately quiet interlude is fine; an accidental 3-LU drop is a fault to fix in step 04.
Decision check
  • Does the order read as one statement, not a folder of singles?
  • Are the key and tempo steps between tracks intentional?
  • Is the silence between each pair a decision, not a default?
Step 07

Export the deliverables — dither exactly once

The two exports per track
Export from the mastering set, per track
1 — Source masterWAV · 48 kHz · 24-bit · Normalize OFF · Dither OFF → ep_track1_master_24b_v1.wav
2 — Delivery (16-bit)WAV · 44.1 kHz · 16-bit · Dither ON → ep_track1_16b_v1.wav
LossyEncode MP3/AAC from the source with the platform encoder — do not re-dither
  • Dither is applied only on the 16-bit export, because that is the only place bit depth drops. The 24-bit source is never dithered. Two exports, exactly one dither stage.
  • Verify the 16-bit file once on the meter: integrated LUFS and true-peak should match the 24-bit master within rounding. If true-peak crept above −1.0 dBTP, the limiter ceiling was set wrong — fix and re-export.

Decision check
  • Is there a 24-bit un-dithered source and a 16-bit dithered delivery for every track?
  • Was dither applied exactly once per delivery file, and never to the source?
  • Do the delivered files still read at target LUFS and under the true-peak ceiling?
Step 08

Statement, positioning, and the archive

Write the artist statement
  • One short paragraph a stranger can hold: the thesis sentence expanded. It names the intention and the refusals — not the gear list.
  • Write it so it would still be true of your next release. Honest enough to be wrong; specific enough to be useful.
State the positioning
  • Who this is for — and, just as important, who it is not for. Where it belongs: a label, a context, a kind of listening.
  • Positioning is a refusal applied to the market, not a marketing trick. Distribution serves the work; never let it edit the work.
Assemble the archive
  • Masters: the un-dithered 24-bit sources plus each delivered format, every file dated and version-tagged.
  • Sessions: the collected-and-saved mix project per track, plus the EP-MASTER.als mastering set — samples, patches, automation intact (File → Collect All and Save).
  • Documents: the artist statement, the running-order notes, and the ep_master_log.txt metering log.
  • Provenance: a short note on what changed from the original spec and why — the honest gap between intention and result.
Decision check
  • Does the master translate on a phone, in a car, and in mono?
  • Did I dither exactly once, only at the bit-depth reduction?
  • Does the running order read as one statement played whole?
  • Could a stranger reopen this release in three years and understand every decision?
  • Step away before you ship. Wait a day. Re-read the metering log, re-listen to the sequence on the phone, change at most one thing, and re-export as v2. An un-archived, un-rested release is a statement you can defend only once.
