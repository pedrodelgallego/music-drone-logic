Lab Note B8-002
The Release · 01 — Overview

Taking the work to delivery — mix, master, sequence, ship

A deep reference for the back half of production: turning finished takes into a master that translates, sequencing an EP as one statement, and delivering files at a named loudness target that survive every conversion downstream. Mastering is not making it louder — it is making it sound like itself everywhere, and making the decisions defensible. Inspired by the discipline of careful release-craft, not by any one record. Your release must be your own.

The goal

A delivered EP, not a folder of WAVs

  • The track from Lab Note B1-001 and two siblings, mastered to one named LUFS target
  • A 24-bit / 48 kHz un-dithered master kept as the source of truth, delivery formats exported from it
  • A running order that reads as one arc, an artist statement, and an archive a stranger could reopen

Skills you'll practice

  • Reading LUFS (momentary / short-term / integrated), true-peak (dBTP), and stereo correlation
  • The loudness-versus-dynamics trade, and why streaming normalisation makes the loudness war unwinnable
  • Reference-matching by spectral balance, translation across four systems, dither applied exactly once
  • Sequencing an EP by key, tempo, and gap; the artist statement; positioning; the archive
Core objective

Understand that a release is the final draft of the work, written in deliverables — and that loudness is a number with a meter behind it, not a vibe you chase by ear.

Why this exercise matters

The beginner instinct

  • Slam a limiter on the master and chase maximum loudness
  • Master by ear on one pair of headphones at one volume
  • Treat the EP as a folder to upload, and the release as finished when the files leave

The opposite discipline

  • Pick a named target — ≈ −14 LUFS integrated, true-peak under −1 dBTP — and meter your way to it
  • Check translation on purpose: monitors, headphones, phone, car, and mono
  • Sequence the EP as one statement and archive the release so it can be defended twice

The skills transfer. The same instincts apply to ambient albums, film cues, generative releases, sound-design packs, and any work that must leave the studio and survive contact with the world's playback chain. You are not learning a genre — you are learning how to finish.

Principle

A master is finished when it holds its shape quiet and loud, on a phone and in a car, in stereo and in mono. If it only works at one volume on one system, it is not mastered yet.
