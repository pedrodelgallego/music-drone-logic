Lab Note B1-003
Lock · 01 — Overview

Clock, latency & the monitoring path, taught from zero

A single-topic deep reference. Before a track can sound good it has to be in time with itself — every box agreeing on the beat, and every sound reaching your ears exactly once. This Lab Note treats that timing integrity as one thing, called lock, and shows you how to build it before you make a single creative decision.

The one principle

Principle

Lock is two promises kept at once: one clock that every device follows, and one monitoring path that lets you hear an input exactly once. Break either promise and the rig drifts or doubles — and no amount of mixing later can repair a take that was never in time with itself.

Everything in this Lab Note is downstream of that sentence. A clock master, a buffer size, a monitor source — each is just a way of deciding, on purpose, how time and sound move through your rig. Decide them once, name them, and the rig stops fighting you. This is the deep companion to Block 1 · Lab 002 — Clock, Latency & Monitoring.

What you'll build

A rig that is provably in lock

  • Digitakt II and Ableton Live following one clock, starting and stopping together with no drift
  • A live input monitored exactly once — no doubling, no audible delay
  • Three numbers you can name: clock master, buffer size, monitor path

The skills you'll practice

  • Choosing one clock master and making every other device follow it
  • Measuring round-trip latency and entering the correction in Live
  • Picking exactly one monitor path and proving what gets recorded
  • Hearing drift, flam, and doubled monitoring before they ruin a take

Why lock before everything else

The beginner instinct

  • Plug everything in, press play, and hope it lines up
  • Leave two boxes free-running and call the flam "character"
  • Switch on every monitor path at once because more sound feels safer

The opposite discipline

  • Name one clock master before any device makes a sound
  • Measure latency once, write the number down, compensate
  • Choose one monitor path per task and prove what it prints

The skill transfers. A rig that locks cleanly is the foundation under everything else in the course — groove, sources, space, performance. You cannot hear a deliberate 4-tick lean if an unknown system delay is already smearing the take. Lock first, then trust your ears. This is the same do-less discipline the whole course teaches: keep the timing path simple and known, so every later decision is one you actually made.

How to read this Lab Note

Work the pages in order. Listen trains your ear on what drift, flam, and latency sound like. Decisions is the theory — clock master, buffer, monitor path. Setup wires the rig for lock. Palette hands you the concrete moves and numbers. The build is the long, exact, step-by-step. Reflection turns the result into a carry-forward rule. The originating workshop lab is Lab 002; the full course build starts at Lab Note B1-001.
