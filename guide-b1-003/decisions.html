Lab Note B1-003
Lock · 03 — Decisions

The theory of lock, and the three choices it forces

Lock is built from three decisions made before a single cable is plugged in: who is the clock master, what buffer size you work at, and which single path you monitor through. This page teaches each from zero — what it is, why it matters, and what to choose — so that on the build page you are committing decisions, not guessing.

Three clocks in the room

  • The audio clock. The interface's internal timer, ticking 48,000 times a second, placing every recorded sample precisely. This is the fine ruler.
  • MIDI clock. A coarser timing signal sent over USB. Good enough to keep the Digitakt at the right tempo, not precise enough to place a single hit exactly on the beat.
  • Your hands. They adapt to whatever they hear. If they hear late, they play late, and the take keeps that drag. This is why the monitor path is a timing decision, not just a volume one.

These three clocks do not automatically agree. Lock is the work of making them agree on purpose. The three decisions below are how you do it.

What "lock" means — clock + monitoring

Clock lock

  • Every device follows one tempo source
  • Start and stop happen together, on the same bar one
  • The offset between devices stays fixed — it never grows

Monitoring lock

  • A live input reaches your ears by exactly one path
  • No doubling, so no comb filtering or flam
  • You know whether what you hear is what gets printed
Principle

Lock is two promises: one clock everyone follows, and one monitoring path that lets you hear an input exactly once. The first stops drift; the second stops doubling. Both must be true at the same time, or the rig is not locked.

Decision 1 — who is the clock master

Concept 01

One device sets tempo; everything else follows

The mechanism
  • A clock master generates the tempo and the transport (start/stop). A slave receives both and runs at the master's tempo. MIDI clock is the signal that carries this over USB — a steady stream of ticks, plus start, stop, and continue messages.
  • Exactly one device may be master. If two devices both generate clock, you have two free-running tempos and the rig drifts — the gap between them grows, and no setting fixes it. This is the drift you learned to hear on the Listen page.
  • The master decides the tempo; the slave's own tempo display becomes irrelevant — it simply shows whatever the master is sending.
The two real choices for this rig
Digitakt as master vs Live as master
Live as master (recommended)Live sends MIDI clock + transport to the Digitakt over USB. Live's tempo rules; the Digitakt follows. Tempo automation, arrangement, and Link all live in Live — one place to drive everything.
Digitakt as masterThe Digitakt sends clock to Live; Live's tempo follows the box. Useful if you compose hands-on at the hardware and want the box to lead. Live must be set to receive sync, which makes its own transport a follower.
  • The decision: for this course, make Live the only clock master. It keeps the timeline, automation, and recording in one authority, and the Digitakt starts cleanly on Live's bar one.
  • Whichever you choose, the rule is the same: name the master out loud, set every other device to receive, and confirm only one device is sending clock.
One clock to rule them all

Exactly one device generates tempo; everything else listens. If the Digitakt's screen shows a tempo different from Live's, sync is broken — stop and fix it before recording, because a take recorded against a drifting clock cannot be trusted and cannot be repaired.

Decision 2 — your buffer size

Concept 02

The tradeoff between delay and CPU

What latency is, from zero
  • When sound passes through the computer it arrives a little late. The interface collects a chunk of audio — the buffer — the computer processes it, and the result comes back out. Every stage adds delay.
  • The buffer size is how many samples the computer gathers before processing. A bigger buffer is more stable — the CPU has more time — but adds more delay. A smaller buffer is faster to respond but strains the CPU, and if it can't keep up you hear crackles and dropouts.
  • Round-trip latency is the full journey: into the interface, through the computer, back to your ears. The minimum is simple: round trip ≈ 2 × buffer ÷ sample rate. One buffer fills on the way in, one on the way out. Converters and the driver add a little more on top.
The numbers at 48 kHz
Buffer size — pick per task
128 samples~ 2.7 ms each way. Use when software-monitoring through Live's effects feels sluggish under your hands.
256 samples~ 5.3 ms each way, ~ 12–15 ms round trip. The course default — stable and low enough for recording and playing.
512+ samplesMix and export only. Latency no longer matters; CPU headroom does.
  • Live's latency compensation. Live reports its own input + output latency, but the figure is usually a little low because parts of the path are invisible to it. The shortfall is your driver error, entered in Driver Error Compensation so a recorded transient lands on the grid line, not after it.
  • Recording vs monitoring latency. Compensation pulls the recorded audio back onto the grid. It does not change the delay you feel while playing — that is set by the buffer alone. Two different problems: one fixed by a number you enter, one fixed by a smaller buffer or a direct monitor path.
The decision
  • Work at 256 samples for programming and recording. Drop to 128 only if software monitoring feels laggy. Raise to 512+ once the takes are printed and you are mixing.
  • Whatever you pick, measure the round trip once at that buffer and enter the driver-error value. A compensation number is only valid for the configuration it was measured on — change the buffer and you re-measure.

Decision 3 — your monitor path

Concept 03

Hardware direct vs software — pick exactly one

The two ways to hear yourself while recording
  • Direct (hardware) monitoring. The M4 routes your input straight to your headphones inside the interface, before the computer. Near-zero delay, but dry — none of Live's effects. The M4's monitor mix knob sets how much input you hear against the computer playback.
  • Software monitoring. You hear the signal after it passes through Live — with effects — but late by the full round-trip delay. Set with the track's Monitor switch (In / Auto).
The doubled-monitoring trap

Switch on both at once and the same input reaches your ears twice, a few milliseconds apart. The two copies cancel at some frequencies — the hollow, phasey comb filtering from the Listen page. It is never useful. Pick one path and turn the other off, every time.

How to pick, per task
TaskMonitor throughWhy
Designing sounds, jamming, sequencing hardwareM4 direct (monitor mix knob toward input), Live track Monitor OffZero latency — knob moves feel instant
Recording a take with Live effectsLive Monitor Auto, M4 direct off for that channelYou hear exactly what is being printed, including FX
Recording the Digitakt's analog outsM4 direct on inputs 3/4, Live Monitor OffYou hear the box itself; the USB copy records silently
Editing, arranging, mixingLive playback onlyThe hardware is silent; latency is irrelevant
  • The decision: default to M4 direct monitoring while designing and jamming, and switch to Live Auto only when an effect is part of the sound you're tracking. Never both — that is the whole rule.
  • Cue vs main. The M4's monitor mix lets you blend direct input against computer playback. Keep it deliberate: too far toward input and you can't hear the track; too far toward playback and you lose the instant feel direct monitoring is for.

Edge cases — when devices won't lock

  • Jitter. Tiny variation in the timing of MIDI-clock ticks. On a direct USB port it is well under a millisecond and inaudible for this music. Through a cheap unpowered hub it can grow — so plug hardware straight into the computer.
  • Transport sync, not just clock. Clock keeps tempo; transport (start/stop/continue) keeps position. A device can follow tempo but still start on the wrong bar if transport receive is off. Both must be enabled on the follower.
  • It won't lock at all. If the Digitakt ignores Live's play, the usual causes are: sync not enabled on the right MIDI port, clock/transport receive off on the box, two devices both sending clock, or a flaky hub. Work through them in that order.
  • Drift after a clean start. If devices start together but slide apart, stopping and restarting Live's transport realigns the follower to bar one. Make it a rule: restart the transport before any take that matters.
Decisions locked before setup
  • I can name my clock master and say why exactly one device is allowed to be it.
  • I know my buffer size and the round-trip number it implies.
  • I understand that compensation fixes the recording, not the feel.
  • I have chosen a default monitor path and know when I switch it.
  • I can describe the doubled-monitoring trap and how to avoid it.
  • I know the difference between a fixed offset (latency) and a growing one (drift).
