Lab Note B3-003
Polymeter & Phase · 06 — The build

Step-by-step: a track built from disagreement

Now we touch the gear. The plan: a fixed 16-step kick, a 15-step perc drift and a 14-step texture drift on the Digitakt, and a MicroFreak figure phased over 32 bars in Ableton. Each step is small, each has a decision checkpoint, and the arrangement turns land exactly on the realignment bars you computed. Keep the rig page and palette open — this page assumes both.

Step 01

Project, clock, and the realignment grid

Open the set
  • ⌘ N for a new Live Set, Tab to Arrangement view. Tempo 124, signature 4/4.
  • Right-click the timeline ruler → Time Ruler Format → Bars:Beats. For this study you think in bars, not minutes — the whole form is pinned to realignment bar counts.
  • Establish the master clock as on the rig page: Ableton sends MIDI clock + transport to Digitakt II and MicroFreak; nothing else sends clock. Press play and confirm both boxes follow and show 124.
Draw the realignment grid as locators
  • Our headline polymeter is 16 vs 15 → realigns every 15 bars. Our phase cycle is 32 bars. The form lives on multiples of these.
  • Right-click the scrub area → Add Locator at bars that matter: 1, 16 (first 15-bar realignment +1), 31, 33 (phase cycle 1 ends), 61, 91, 121. Name them REALIGN-1, PHASE-1, and so on.
  • These locators turn the abstract math into physical targets. Every section change you make later snaps to one of them.
The structural grid for this build (124 BPM)
Bar 1Start — kick + 15-perc launched in alignment
Bar 16First polymeter realignment (perc snaps back under the kick)
Bar 33First phase cycle complete (MicroFreak copy back to unison)
Bar 31 / 46 / 61Successive 15-bar polymeter realignments
Bar 65 / 97Successive 32-bar phase cycles
Create the tracks
  • ⌘ T for audio tracks: DT DRUMS, MF FIGURE, MF DRONE.
  • ⌘ ⌥ T for returns: A — REVERB, B — ECHO. Reverb decay 5 s, Echo synced 3/16, 100% wet, high-passed at 250 Hz.
  • Don't make the phase-copy track yet — it is a duplicate created after the figure is recorded (Step 06).
  • ⌘ S → save as labnote_b3_003.als in a dedicated folder.
Step 02

The fixed clock — kick + hat on 16

Set up the Digitakt project
  • On Digitakt II, create a project named B3-003, pattern A01. Confirm it follows Ableton's clock.
  • Switch the SCALE menu to PER TRACK mode now, before programming — see the rig page. This is the one switch the whole build depends on.
  • Assign four tracks: T1 KICK, T2 HAT, T3 PERC, T4 TEXTURE.
Program the steady backbone
  • Set T1 KICK to LEN 16. Trigs on 1, 5, 9, 13 — a four-on-the-floor anchor that never moves. This is the reference everything else disagrees with.
  • Set T2 HAT to LEN 16. Trigs on the offbeats 3, 7, 11, 15. Parameter-lock alternating velocity so it breathes.
  • Tune the kick's fundamental to your track's root (A or E) so it agrees tonally with the drone later.

Decision check
  • Does the kick sit dead steady? It must — its job is to be the thing that does not move.
  • Is the kick tail short enough that the perc drift won't be smeared by it later?
  • Is the hat supporting, not competing? At LEN 16 it should feel locked to the kick, a calm backbone against the coming drift.
Step 03

The drift — perc on 15, texture on 14

Set the unequal lengths
  • Select T3 PERC. On the SCALE page (PER TRACK), set LEN 15. The track now has only fifteen cells; on every bar it wraps one step before the kick, walking leftward.
  • Select T4 TEXTURE, set LEN 14. Leave SPD at 1× on both — speed changes turn this into polyrhythm.
  • Leave CHNG long/off so the short tracks loop within the pattern instead of advancing it.
Program the drifting figures sparsely
  • On T3 PERC (15 steps) place just 3 hits — a rim or click. Sparse is essential: the interest comes from where the few hits land relative to the kick, not from density.
  • On T4 TEXTURE (14 steps) place 2 hits of a resampled fragment or noise tick. Give one a 25–50% trig probability so the drift also breathes irregularly.
  • Resist filling the odd tracks. Every extra hit makes the rotation harder to hear; the ear needs space to track a single voice walking across the bar.

Decision check
  • Watching the LEDs: does T3 visibly wrap at step 15, walking its loop point left of the kick each bar? If it wraps at 16, you are still in PER PATTERN mode.
  • Listen for 30 seconds — can you hear a single perc hit travel across the bar relative to the kick? If it's a blur, you have too many hits.
  • At bar 16, does the perc audibly snap back under the kick? That is the realignment; mark it.
Step 04

Gain-stage and record the polymeter pass

Connect and stage
  • Digitakt II Main L/R → M4 inputs 3/4 (TRS). DT DRUMS Audio From → Ext. In 3/4, Monitor Auto.
  • Digitakt main volume so Ableton peaks just below -6 dBFS. Inputs 3/4 have no gain knobs; the box's own volume is the record level.
Record long — the cycle needs room
  • This is the key discipline of the whole study: record at least 4 minutes. The 15-bar realignment happens roughly every 29 seconds; the full interplay of the 15 and 14 drifts only resolves over many bars. A short take captures none of it.
  • Arm DT DRUMS, position playhead at bar 1, ⌘ ⇧ R. Let it run hands-off for the first 2 minutes — the polymeter is the performance; you don't need to play anything.
  • In the back half, perform sparse mutes: drop the texture for 16 bars, bring it back, mute the hat briefly so the bare kick-vs-perc drift is exposed. These become arrangement options later.
Decision check
  • Play back: can you hear the perc walk across the bar and resolve at the 15-bar points? Find one realignment on the waveform and confirm it lands where the math says (bar 16, 31, 46…).
  • Is the drift legible — anchored by the steady kick — or does it sound like random mistimed hits? If random, thin the odd tracks further.
  • Is the take long enough to contain at least three full realignments? If not, re-record longer.
Step 05

Record the MicroFreak figure

Design a phaseable figure
  • Shift + press Type to init a patch. Oscillator Wavetable or KarplusStr — a clear, percussive attack phases best; sustained pads smear into mush.
  • Short envelope: Attack 0, Decay ~25, low Sustain, short Release. Each note must speak distinctly so two copies crossing produce audible emergent patterns, exactly like Reich's pianos.
  • Program a tight 1-bar (16-step) figure of 5–7 notes in your scale. Same length as the kick — phase needs identical patterns; the difference will be tempo, not length.
Record the anchor copy
  • MicroFreak Out → M4 In 1. MF FIGURE Audio From → Ext. In 1, Monitor Auto.
  • Confirm the MicroFreak is clocked to Ableton (Shift + Tempo → USB). Record a clean 4-minute pass of the looping figure, hands off the knobs — this is the static raw material both phase copies derive from.
  • Save the patch as B3 FIGURE.
Decision check
  • Is the figure short and distinct enough that you'll be able to hear it crossing itself?
  • Does each note have a clear transient? Wash-y notes ruin phase legibility.
  • Is it warped to the grid as a clean 1-bar loop, so the duplicate starts in perfect unison?
Step 06

Build the phase — duplicate and drift

Make the drifting copy
  • Select the MF FIGURE track, ⌘ D to duplicate it whole → rename the copy MF FIGURE (phase). Both now hold the same audio in perfect unison.
  • On the copy's clip, choose your drift method from the rig page. Recommended: set the clip BPM field to 120.1 (from the palette table) for a 32-bar phase cycle against the 124 project. The copy now slides ahead one bar-length every 32 bars and rejoins unison at bar 33, 65, 97.
  • High-pass the copy at ~200 Hz and pull it down ~3 dB so it sits behind the anchor rather than doubling its weight.
Verify the cycle lands
  • Play from bar 1: the two should sound like one. By bar ~17 the copy is half a cycle out — emergent melodies from the two crossing should be clearly audible. At bar 33 they snap back to unison.
  • If they don't rejoin exactly on bar 33, your BPM offset is slightly off. Use the precise figure from the palette's reveal (124 × 32/33) or nudge the clip BPM in hundredths until the unison lands on the bar.
MF FIGURE (anchor, 124) unison → drift → half cycle emergent pattern → bar 33 unison again
Decision check
  • Is the drift slow enough to be felt, not heard happening? If you can perceive "it's speeding up", widen the cycle (lower offset).
  • Do new melodies emerge mid-cycle that neither copy plays alone? That emergence is the entire point.
  • Does the unison return at bar 33 feel like a clearing — a small structural event? Mark it; the arrangement will use it.
Step 07

The drone bed that makes drift legible

Why the drone matters here
  • In Drumming, a steady pulse underneath is what lets the ear track the phasing voice. Your drone is that bed: a held, slow-moving tone that gives the rotating parts something stable to be measured against.
  • Init a fresh MicroFreak patch. Oscillator Harmo or Wavetable, Paraphonic on, long attack/release, dark filter. Hold the root + fifth, record a 4-minute pass with only the internal LFO moving.
  • MF DRONE chain: EQ HP at 120 Hz, Hybrid Reverb 6 s at 35% wet, Utility width 130%. Save the patch as B3 DRONE.
Decision check
  • With the drone in, is the polymeter drift easier to follow? It should be — the steady bed is the ruler.
  • Is the drone staying out of the kick's and figure's way (HP at 120 Hz, sitting around -16 dB)?
Step 08

Arrange on the realignment grid

Let the math write the form
  • This is the thesis of the study made concrete: every section turn lands on a realignment bar, not on an arbitrary 16-bar count. The drift cycles are the form.
  • Make every clip edit on a kick downbeat (⌘ E at the playhead, grid snap on). Overlap takes by a bar with 4-beat crossfades so seams never click.
Section → bar → what carries it
INTRO — bar 1–15Drone alone, then kick enters. One full polymeter cycle before anything else, so the realignment at bar 16 is the first "arrival".
DRIFT IN — bar 16–32Perc (15) and texture (14) enter at the realignment. The phase figure (anchor only) joins quietly. The ear learns the rotating parts.
PHASE — bar 33–64The drifting figure copy comes up at the bar-33 unison. Two full phase cycles. Polymeter and phase now run together — the densest interplay.
PEAK — bar 65–96Nothing new enters. The bar-65 phase unison and a near-coincident polymeter realignment stack into the biggest clearing. Energy from automation only.
STRIP — bar 97–120Pull the phase copy out at the bar-97 unison. Bare kick-vs-perc drift exposed — the strip makes the earlier density retroactively bigger.
OUTRO — bar 121+Drift parts fall away one realignment at a time; drone carries the exit. Final fade over the last ~30 s.
Editing craft
  • When a section's edits are final, select its clips and ⌘ J consolidate — one clean clip per section keeps the timeline legible.
  • Loop a suspicious seam with ⌘ L and listen three times before accepting it.
  • Resist adding a new instrument to "lift" a section. The lift is supposed to come from a realignment arriving, not from a new sound. If a section sags, move its boundary onto a realignment bar instead.
Decision check
  • Does every section boundary sit on a realignment or phase-unison bar? If one doesn't, ask why and usually move it.
  • Play 64→65 blind: does the peak arrive because the clocks agreed, not because something was added?
  • Can you remove one drifting part without collapsing the form? If yes, it was decoration — cut it.
Step 09

Automation that serves the clocks

Light touch — the drift is the movement
  • The polymeter and phase already supply nearly all the motion. Automation here is seasoning: it should emphasise the realignment events, never compete with them.
  • A for automation lanes, pencil (B) to draw. No ramp shorter than 8 bars; most span a full drift cycle.
The few moves worth drawing
Reverb send on MF FIGUREBloom up into each phase unison (bar 33, 65) then recede — the clearing gets a touch of space exactly as it arrives.
Auto Filter on DT DRUMSSlowly open across the DRIFT and PHASE sections, brightest at the bar-65 peak, closing through STRIP.
Phase-copy volumeFade in at bar 33, out at bar 97 — its entrances and exits land on unison bars so they feel inevitable.
Drone volume-12 dB intro, -16 dB through the busy middle, back to -10 dB for the outro to close the circle.
Decision check
  • If you can hear the automation move as an event, it's too fast — slow it.
  • Mute all automation for a section: does the drift still carry it? It should. Automation is the garnish, the clocks are the meal.
Step 10

Mix, export, and step away

Quick mix pass
  • Pull faders to a starting balance: drums -10, figure pair -12 (anchor a touch above copy), drone -16. The low end belongs to kick (40–80 Hz) and drone (above 120 Hz); keep the phasing figures out of the sub.
  • Mono check: flip the master to mono periodically. Two phasing copies can comb-filter badly in mono — pan the anchor and copy slightly apart and high-pass the copy so the cancellation is harmless.
  • No limiter for this exercise. Peaks ≤ -3 dBFS across the whole length, including the bar-65 bloom.
Export
  • Select bar 1 to the end. File → Export Audio / Video. WAV, 48 kHz, 24-bit, Normalize OFF.
  • Filename labnote_b3_003_v1.wav. File → Collect All and Save. Save the Digitakt project and both MicroFreak patches.
  • Listen elsewhere, wait a day, change one thing, render v2. The thing you change is almost always a length or a drift rate — not a new sound.
Decision check
  • Does the track stay alive for its full length with no new elements after the PHASE section?
  • Can a listener feel the realignments arrive without being able to say what changed?
  • Is it unmistakably your own figures — the engine studied, not a reference copied?
