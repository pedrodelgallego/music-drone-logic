Polymeter & Phase · 01 — Overview

Clocks that disagree on purpose

A single-topic deep reference on the engine behind the most hypnotic repetitive music: patterns of unequal length, and identical patterns drifting in tempo. Polymeter and phase make a handful of unchanging notes feel like they evolve for ten minutes. This is a Block 3 study — fully original output, no reference track copied.

The one idea

Polymeter — unequal lengths

  • Two loops share one 16th-note pulse but have different step counts
  • A 16-step kick against a 15-step perc figure slides one step per bar
  • Nothing new is written — the alignment does all the composing

Phase — drifting tempo

  • Two copies of one pattern, one creeping imperceptibly ahead
  • Reich's gradual process: a slow journey through every misalignment
  • The realignment back into unison is a structural event you didn't write
Core objective

Build a 6–8 minute original where time itself is the material — the form is drawn by clocks falling out of, then back into, alignment.

Why this is the deep end of repetition

The beginner instinct

  • Add a new part whenever the loop gets boring
  • Keep every pattern the same length so it always lines up
  • Quantise everything hard so nothing ever drifts

The opposite discipline

  • Set one part to a different length and let it rotate
  • Let the realignment cycle write your arrangement turns
  • Compute when the loops agree and place form on those bars

The skills transfer well beyond techno. The same instinct drives minimalist composition, generative patches, polymetric drum machines, and any music that wants to feel alive while standing still. You are not learning a trick — you are learning to compose with time.

Principle

Polymeter changes length; phase changes tempo. Pick one engine and let it run. Two at once and no listener can hear either.
