Polymeter & Phase · 05 — Length palette

Length pairs & drift rates

A working sound palette for this study is a set of numbers, not timbres. These tables give you length pairings with their realignment cycles, the character each produces, and the phase drift rates that land cleanly back in unison. Choose from here and you skip the trial-and-error.

Polymeter length pairs

All against a fixed 16-step kick on a 16th-note grid. Realignment in bars = LCM(16, n) ÷ 16. Pick the cycle length that matches how patient your track wants to be.

Second track length vs the 16-step kick
n = 12Realigns every 3 bars. Tight 3-against-4 lean. The most danceable, least wandering — a foundation, not a headline.
n = 14Realigns every 7 bars. A gentle periodic sway; lands on a musically friendly odd bar count.
n = 13Realigns every 13 bars. Coprime and prime — restless, the parts almost never agree until the big return.
n = 15Realigns every 15 bars. The signature setting: near-static, slowly rotating, hypnotic. The recommended headline drift.
n = 11Realigns every 11 bars. Short odd loop, busier and more insistent than 15.
n = 9Realigns every 9 bars. Quick odd rotation; good for a hat or click that should feel jittery.
n = 7Realigns every 7 bars (LCM 112). Very short cycle, very fast rotation — almost a polyrhythmic flicker.
How to read character

Coprime-with-16 lengths (15, 13, 11, 9, 7) give the longest, most "rotating" drift. Lengths sharing a factor with 16 (12, 14) realign sooner and feel more periodic. Longer cycle = more hypnotic; shorter = more groove.

Stacking three lengths

When more than two parts disagree, the full realignment is the LCM of all the lengths together — and it grows fast. Use this to engineer a track whose every element only agrees once, near the end.

Three-way realignment (everything realigns at the listed bar)
16 · 12 · 8All share factors → LCM 48 steps = 3 bars. Tight, locked; the three feel like one groove with internal motion.
16 · 15 · 14LCM 1680 steps = 105 bars (~3:23 at 124). Practically never fully agrees — a whole-track-length super-cycle.
16 · 12 · 15LCM 240 steps = 15 bars. The 12 realigns often; the 15 sets the headline cycle. A two-speed drift.
16 · 14 · 7LCM 112 steps = 7 bars. 7 is a factor of 14, so they collapse onto the 14's cycle.
  • Pairwise vs full realignment. Even when all three only agree at bar 105, each pair agrees much sooner (16&15 at 15, 16&14 at 7). Those pairwise meetings are smaller structural events you can also use — partial clearings between the big return.

Phase drift rates

For phase, choose how long the drifting copy takes to travel one full pattern-length ahead and return to unison. That span is your structural unit. The BPM offset below is the copy's clip tempo against a 124 BPM project for a one-cycle-per-span drift on a 1-bar (16-step) figure.

Drift span → required tempo offset (1-bar figure, 124 BPM project)
16-bar cycleCopy ≈ 116.5 BPM (−6.25%). Fast, clearly audible motion — for a single dramatic phase event.
32-bar cycleCopy ≈ 120.1 BPM (−3.1%). The recommended default — felt, not heard happening.
48-bar cycleCopy ≈ 121.4 BPM (−2.1%). Slow, meditative; one cycle spans a whole section.
64-bar cycleCopy ≈ 122.1 BPM (−1.5%). The most Reich-like — the drift is barely perceptible as motion.
The math behind the offset

One full phase cycle means the copy gains exactly one pattern-length (1 bar) over the chosen span. To gain 1 bar over N bars, the copy must play N+1 bars in the time the original plays N — so its tempo is the project tempo × N ÷ (N+1). For a 32-bar cycle: 124 × 32/33 ≈ 120.2 BPM. Use the precise figure and the copy lands back in unison on the bar, giving you a clean structural turn.

Drift direction

Slowing the copy (lower BPM) makes it fall behind; speeding it makes it run ahead. Either works — Reich's player sped up. Behind tends to read as "the echo lagging", ahead as "anticipation". Pick by feel; keep it consistent within a section.

Before you move on
  • I chose my length pair (or trio) and wrote down its realignment bar count.
  • If using phase, I chose a drift span and the exact BPM offset that lands it in unison.
  • My longest cycle is at least 7 bars — long enough to feel, not a quick flicker.
  • The kick is the one fixed reference; nothing else stays put.
  • The numbers are mine; I'm not transcribing a reference track's pattern.
