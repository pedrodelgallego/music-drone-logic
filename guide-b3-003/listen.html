Lab Note B3-003
Polymeter & Phase · 02 — Listen first

Where the idea was invented

Before you build, hear the engine running at full strength in the work that defined it. Each of these is a single short idea stretched into a long form by misalignment alone — exactly what you are about to attempt. Open the linked rep lessons in the Block 3 workshop and listen the way they ask you to: for what moves, not for what is added.

The phase canon

Listen 01

Steve Reich — Piano Phase (1967)

What to hear
  • Two pianists play the same 12-note figure. One holds tempo; the other speeds up so slightly that you cannot catch the moment it happens — sliding one note ahead, then two, then a full cycle, until they lock back into unison.
  • The material never changes. Only the alignment does. Every interim position sounds like a new pattern, yet nothing was written but the original twelve notes.
  • This is the purest statement of the whole Lab Note: time is the material. Listen for the resulting melodies that emerge from two identical lines crossing — patterns no one composed.
Why it matters for your build

Piano Phase proves that a gradual process can be both the structure and the entire interest of a piece. Your phasing automation in Step 07 of the build is a direct descendant — keep the drift slow enough that, like Reich's, it can only be felt, never heard happening.

  • Rep lesson: Steve Reich — Piano Phase
Listen 02

Steve Reich — Drumming, Part I (1971)

What to hear
  • Tuned bongos build a 12-beat cycle one stroke at a time, then phase it. Reich adds the technique of the player who is not phasing imitating the resulting combined pattern — making the emergent melody audible as a part in its own right.
  • The cycle is long and the phasing is patient. Listen for how a steady pulse underneath makes the drifting voice legible: without the anchor, drift is just chaos; with it, drift is movement.
  • This is the bridge from concert phase music to a club kick drum — the steady reference is the kick you will keep fixed while everything else rotates around it.
  • Rep lesson: Steve Reich — Drumming, Part I

Polymeter in practice

Listen 03

King Crimson — Discipline (1981)

What to hear
  • Two interlocking guitar lines of different lengths — famously a figure in 5 against a figure in 7, or similar — sharing one pulse and slowly rotating against each other. The title is exact: the discipline is in not deviating, so the math can do the work.
  • Listen for the moment the two lines briefly agree and the texture seems to "click" — then they part again. That click is the realignment you will engineer with LCM math on the next page.
  • It is rock instrumentation running a minimalist engine. Proof that polymeter is a structural choice, not a genre.
  • Rep lesson: King Crimson — Discipline

Going further in the Block 3 canon

These are not required for the build, but each isolates one facet of misaligned time. Sample as your ears want.

Adjacent listening in the workshop
Reich — Clapping MusicPhase reduced to its bones: one clapped pattern shifted a beat at a time. The whole mechanism, no instruments to hide behind.
Philip Glass — Two PagesAdditive process — lengths grow and shrink by a note. The sibling technique to phase: the pattern's length is what changes.
Terry Riley — A Rainbow in Curved AirTape-delay and looped figures of uneven length stacking into a shimmering, never-quite-repeating texture.
Ewe Drumming from GhanaThe living source: bell timelines and drums of different cycle lengths. Polymeter as a centuries-old practice, not a studio trick.
Before you move on
  • I can hear, in Piano Phase, that no new notes arrive — only the alignment moves.
  • I noticed at least one emergent melody created purely by two lines crossing.
  • I located the "click" of realignment in Discipline.
  • I understand the steady pulse is what makes drift legible rather than chaotic.
  • I am building something original — I am studying the engine, not copying a piece.
