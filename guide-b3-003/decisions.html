Polymeter & Phase · 03 — Decisions

Choose your disagreement

Three techniques get confused constantly, and choosing the wrong one — or blending them — is the single most common way this track fails. This page draws the distinctions sharply, works the realignment math you will use to build the form, and lays out the decisions to settle before any cable is plugged in.

Three words people confuse

Polymeter

  • Same pulse, different bar lengths
  • A 16-step part against a 15-step part, both in 16ths
  • The steps line up; the loops don't

Polyrhythm

  • Same span, different subdivisions
  • 3 evenly-spaced hits against 2, inside one beat
  • The classic 3:2 cross-rhythm

Phase

  • Identical patterns, drifting tempo
  • One copy creeps ahead of the other
  • Reich's gradual process — the pattern shifts against itself
The distinction, in one line

Polymeter changes length, polyrhythm changes division, phase changes tempo. This Lab Note is about the first and the third — length and tempo. Polyrhythm is here only as the thing they are not.

Polymeter vs polyrhythm vs phase — worked out

Concept 01

Polymeter — when the loops are different lengths

How it works
  • Keep the kick on a 16-step track. Put a percussion figure on a track set to 15 steps. Both run at the same 16th-note pulse, but the perc pattern restarts one step earlier each bar.
  • Every cycle the perc lands one step further left relative to the kick. After 15 of the perc's cycles the two realign — a long super-pattern that never wrote a single new note.
  • Crucially the pulse is shared. Each step is still a 16th note; what differs is how many steps before the loop wraps. That is why polymeter stays danceable while feeling like it's rotating.

Concept 02

Polyrhythm — the thing this is not

Why we set it aside
  • Polyrhythm fits a different number of hits into the same span — 3 against 2 inside one beat. The loops are the same length; the subdivisions disagree. That is a vertical tension, felt in the body, that resolves on every downbeat.
  • Polymeter and phase are horizontal — the disagreement plays out over many bars, not within one beat. That long timescale is what this Lab Note is built around.
  • You can use a polyrhythmic figure as one of your polymetric parts (e.g. a 12-step triplet feel on a track that also has an odd length), but don't make polyrhythm the engine. It resolves too fast to carry an 8-minute form.
Concept 03

Phase — the gradual process

How it differs from polymeter
  • In polymeter the patterns are locked to the same clock — they only disagree because their lengths differ, and the realignment is exact and predictable.
  • In phase the patterns are the same length but one runs at a fractionally different tempo, so it slides continuously rather than in step-sized jumps. The drift is analog and smooth; there is no grid to it.
  • Reich's Piano Phase: two copies of one figure, one speeding up imperceptibly, sliding one note ahead, then a full cycle, then back to unison. Every interim position sounds composed; nothing was.
  • The realignment is the event. In both polymeter and phase, the moment the parts snap back into unison is a structural turn you didn't have to write — the texture simplifies and the room seems to open. Place your section boundaries on these points and the form draws itself.

The LCM realignment math

For polymeter, the realignment is pure arithmetic. Two patterns of length a and b steps return to their starting alignment after the least common multiple of a and b steps. Divide by 16 to get the bar count.

Math 01

Computing when the clocks agree

The formula
  • LCM(a, b) = (a × b) ÷ GCD(a, b), where GCD is the greatest common divisor.
  • When a and b share no common factor (they are coprime), the GCD is 1 and the LCM is simply a × b — the longest possible cycle. That is why 16 vs 15 takes a full 240 steps to realign: 16 and 15 are coprime.
  • When they share a factor, the cycle collapses. 16 and 12 share a factor of 4, so LCM = (16 × 12) ÷ 4 = 48 steps — only 3 bars.

Reading the table for arrangement
  • A long realignment (16 vs 15 = 15 bars) gives you a slow, hypnotic super-pattern and one big structural event you can build a whole section turn around.
  • A short realignment (16 vs 12 = 3 bars) gives a tight repeating groove that never sounds static but also never wanders far — good as a foundation, less so as the headline.
  • Stacking three lengths multiplies the cycle. 16 + 15 + 14 only all realign at LCM(16,15,14) = 1680 steps = 105 bars — practically the length of the track. Use this deliberately, not by accident.
Phase has no clean LCM — here is its equivalent

Because phase drift is continuous, you don't get an integer step count. Instead you choose a drift rate: how long the slipping copy takes to travel one full pattern-length ahead and return to unison. Pick that duration directly — e.g. "one full phase cycle over 32 bars" — and that span becomes your structural unit, the way the LCM bar count is for polymeter. The decisions to settle now

Decision check
  • Engine: polymeter (predictable, grid-locked, danceable) or phase (smooth, analog, meditative)? Pick one. If unsure, choose polymeter for a first build — it is easier to control and to hear.
  • The fixed clock: which element stays steady? Almost always the kick. Everything else disagrees with it; it never disagrees with anything.
  • The lengths: which pair from the table? Long realignment (15 bars) for a hypnotic headline, short (3 bars) for a foundation groove.
  • The realignment bar: compute it now and write it down. Your section turns will land on it.
  • Originality: the figures are yours. Studying Discipline teaches the mechanism; copying its intervals does not make a Block 3 track.
