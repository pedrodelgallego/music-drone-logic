Lab Note B3-001
Groove · 06 — The build

Build one groove-driven track, step by step

Now we touch the gear. Every step is small, every step has a decision checkpoint, every tick value is spelled out. We build a track whose entire interest is feel — a kick that never moves, a hat that leans, a bass dragged into the same pocket by a groove you extract from your own hands. Keep the setup page and the palette open in other tabs.

Step 01

Create the project and lock the clock

New set
  • ⌘ N, then Tab to Arrangement view. Tempo 124, signature 4/4.
  • ⌘ , → Audio: device MOTU M4, sample rate 48000 Hz, buffer 256 samples.
  • Input Config: enable mono input 1 (MicroFreak) and stereo 3/4 (Digitakt). Rename them in plain language.
  • Save as groove_b3_001.als in a dedicated project folder.
Sync the boxes — exactly one clock master
  • ⌘ , → Link, Tempo & MIDI. On the Digitakt II output row enable Sync (clock + transport out). On its input row leave clock send off.
  • Digitakt II: Settings → MIDI Config → Sync — clock receive ON, transport receive ON, clock send OFF.
  • MicroFreak: Shift + Tempo → source USB.
  • Press play in Ableton. The Digitakt must start instantly and read 124. If not, fix it now — a groove recorded against a drifting clock is worthless.
Measure system latency once
  • Record 8 bars of a dead-straight Digitakt kick on every downbeat. Zoom into bar 1.
  • If the transient sits a few ms after the grid line, enter that value in ⌘ , → Audio → Driver Error Compensation, or drag clips left to the grid afterwards.
  • This is non-negotiable for groove work. Until the system offset is zeroed, you cannot tell a +4-tick lean from converter delay.
Decision check
  • Does the Digitakt's screen read exactly 124 when Ableton plays?
  • Does a recorded straight kick now land on the grid line, not after it?
  • Is exactly one device generating clock?
Step 02

Lay the reference clock — the locked kick

Create the Digitakt project
  • On Digitakt II load or create a project named GROOVE LAB, pattern A01, length 16 steps.
  • Assign four tracks: T1 KICK, T2 HAT, T3 PERC, T4 GHOST. Leave the rest empty.
Program the kick — and only the kick
  • On T1 KICK, place trigs on steps 1, 5, 9, 13. Velocity full and constant.
  • Set micro-timing to 0 on every kick trig. The kick is the reference clock; it never moves and its level never wavers. Everything else in the track leans against this.
  • Tune the kick's fundamental to A or E so it later agrees with the bass — but tuning is the only thing you touch. No swing reaches the kick (swing only affects even steps; the kick sits on odd steps anyway, but confirm the track's swing is at 50 %).
Listen to the bare clock
  • Loop the kick alone for one minute. It should feel metronomic and slightly cold — that is correct. A reference clock is not supposed to groove; it is supposed to be the thing the groove is measured against.
  • Record an 8-bar pass to a track named DT KICK (mute T2–T4). This separate kick pass is both your sidechain source and your Extract-Groove anchor later.
Decision check
  • Is every kick trig at micro-timing 0 and constant velocity?
  • Does the kick land on the grid in the recorded clip?
  • Have I resisted adding anything else "to make it interesting"? The interest comes next, from the lean — not from the clock.
Step 03

Build the lean — hats with intentional offset

Place the offbeat hats
  • On T2 HAT, load a tight closed hat. High-pass it around 500 Hz on the FLTR page — hats carry no useful low end.
  • Place trigs on offbeat steps 3, 7, 11, 15 — the positions between the kicks, where the groove lives.
Choose your lean mechanism — pick one
Two ways to drag the hats — never both at once
Per-trig micro-timingHold each hat trig, turn the micro-timing knob to +4 (late). Most control; you can vary individual hits.
Per-track swingSet swing to 55 % on the hat track only. Faster to dial; affects all even steps uniformly.
  • For Template A, use per-trig +4 ticks — it is the more deliberate, more describable choice and it lets you later drag one hat further than the others.
  • Do not stack both. Swing at 55 % plus a +4 nudge double-counts the lean and pushes you out of the pocket without your noticing.
Couple velocity to timing
  • P-lock alternating AMP volume: steps 3 and 11 at velocity 100, steps 7 and 15 at 80. The quieter hats read as ghosts, the louder as accents.
  • The dragged-late hats are now also varied in level — late + soft on the soft ones, which is exactly how a human hat player sits behind a beat.

Decision check
  • Loop kick + hat for two minutes. Does it roll, or does it merely sound late? If it sounds wrong rather than deep, reduce to +3.
  • Can I state the groove in one sentence with three numbers?
  • Did I avoid stacking swing and micro-timing?
Step 04

Add the push — percussion ahead of the beat

Program the perc accent
  • On T3 PERC, load one rim, click, or conga. Place hits on steps 4 and 12 — the lead-ins to beats 2 and 4.
  • Hold each perc trig and set micro-timing to −3 (early). Against the dragged hats, this early flick reads as forward urgency: the track leans back and leans forward at the same time, which is what "pocket" means.
  • Velocity ~90. On step 12, add a trig condition of 50 % so the second accent only plays half the time — the loop stops being a loop.
Add a probabilistic ghost
  • On T4 GHOST, place one quiet hit on step 14, micro-timing +6 (well behind), velocity 55.
  • Set its trig condition to 1:4 — it fires on one pattern cycle in four, a slow heartbeat of variation that never disrupts the pocket.
  • This is the one place a tiny touch of unpredictability is sanctioned, and even here it is a rule (1 in 4), not random jitter.
Decision check
  • Does the early perc create tension against the late hats, or do they cancel into mush?
  • When the 50 % accent drops out, does the bar breathe?
  • Is every offset still describable as a number? No "I just nudged it til it felt right".
Step 05

Record the groove and audition the pocket

Record the full drum pass
  • Unmute T1–T4 on the Digitakt. Arm DT DRUMS in Ableton (Ext. In 3/4), monitor Auto.
  • Set the Digitakt main volume so DT DRUMS peaks just below −6 dBFS. ⌘ ⇧ R, record 4 minutes of the loop untouched.
  • Then record a second 8-bar pass of DT KICK alone (T2–T4 muted) if you didn't in Step 02 — you need a clean kick for both sidechain and groove extraction.
The three-minute test
  • Loop the recorded drums and listen for a full three minutes. Set a timer. A pocket that bores you at 30 seconds and locks you in at 3 minutes is the right one.
  • Flip the hat micro-timing to 0 on the Digitakt for ten seconds, then back to +4, while the recording loops alongside the live box. The difference between dead and rolling should be obvious — that is the proof your groove is doing the work.
Decision check
  • Does the loop move without thinking?
  • Does the kick still read as the metronomic truth while everything leans around it?
  • Did the recorded clip preserve the offsets, or did the system latency smear them? (Re-check against the grid.)
Step 06

Extract the groove into a portable template

Get one bar of drums to MIDI
  • Select the recorded DT DRUMS clip. Crop to one clean bar (⌘ E at the bar lines, keep the middle).
  • Right-click the clip → Slice to New MIDI Track, or transcribe by hand onto a MIDI track named GROOVE REF. The point is to capture the transient positions — your +4 hats and −3 perc — as MIDI note timing.
Extract Groove
  • Right-click the GROOVE REF clip → Extract Groove. Ableton reads your hand-nudged micro-timing and writes it into the Groove Pool as a reusable file. Name it B3-001 POCKET.
  • Open the Groove Pool (wave icon, bottom-left). Set Base to 1/16, Quantize 0 %, Timing 100 %, Random 0 %.
  • You now own your pocket as an object you can drop onto any clip in the project.
Hand-nudged DT pattern → record → DT DRUMS audio → slice → GROOVE REF MIDI → Extract Groove → B3-001 POCKET → apply → Bass + tonal parts
Decision check
  • Does the extracted groove's offset list roughly match my stated numbers (+4 on offbeats, −3 on the perc steps)?
  • Is there exactly one groove file, or did I create competing duplicates?
Step 07

Bend the bass into the same pocket

Record a deliberately straight bass
  • On the MicroFreak, init a patch: Wavetable, Wave ~30 %, Cutoff 11 o'clock, Attack 0, short Decay. Root A1, A natural minor, 3–5 notes, root on step 1.
  • Program the sequence dead straight — no swing, no glide gymnastics. You want a clean, unswung bass so that all of its feel comes from the extracted groove, not from a second, slightly-different lean.
  • Arm MF BASS (Ext. In 1, mono), monitor Auto, record 4 minutes. Then transcribe or warp the take so its notes live as MIDI you can groove, or record the MicroFreak sequence as MIDI directly.
Apply the groove
  • Select the bass clip. In its clip view, set the Groove chooser to B3-001 POCKET. The bass notes are pulled toward the exact lean of the drums.
  • In the Groove Pool: Timing 100 % (full shared offset), Velocity 0–30 % (keep the bass's own dynamics), Random 0 %.
  • Solo bass + drums. The two now share one pocket instead of two slightly different feels. If the bass feels over-dragged, drop Timing to 75 %.
  • When certain, hit Commit Groove on the bass clip to bake the offsets into the note positions — only once you're sure, because it's permanent.
Sidechain to the locked kick
  • Compressor on MF BASS, Sidechain on, source DT KICK (the clean pass), Pre FX.
  • Ratio 4:1, Attack 1 ms, Release 120 ms, threshold down to ~−3 dB gain reduction per kick.
  • The pump now locks to the reference clock, reinforcing the kick-as-truth relationship the whole groove depends on.
Decision check
  • Do bass and drums feel like one performance, not two machines?
  • Is the sidechain locked to the kick (steady ~3 dB dips), reinforcing the anchor?
  • If I A/B the bass with groove on vs off, is the "on" version clearly deeper?
Step 08

Evolve the groove over the arrangement

Make the same loop feel different — by feel, not by addition
  • This is the thesis of the genre and of this Lab Note: the loop barely changes, but the feel moves. Use Digitakt pattern copies to stage the groove arc.
  • Copy A01 to A02 and A03. Keep notes identical; change only the lean.
Three patterns, one set of notes, three feels
A01 — intro / verseHat micro-timing +4, perc −3. Template A, the home pocket.
A02 — deepeningHat +6, ghost (T4) condition raised to 1:2. The track sinks back without a single new sound — Template B territory.
A03 — peakPerc −5, off-accent on step 7 added at −6, hat back to +2. The pocket pushes forward — Template C — and the section feels urgent though nothing was layered on.
Record the arc as one performance
  • Rehearse switching A01 → A02 → A03 on bar lines using the Digitakt pattern chain or manual pattern change. Prepare the change before releasing it so it lands cleanly.
  • Arm DT DRUMS and record a single 8-minute pass while you move through the patterns. The groove evolution is now printed as audio.
  • If a transition feels abrupt, the fix is a slower hand on the pattern change, not a crossfade — the listener should feel the pocket shift, never hear a seam.
Decision check
  • Play 0:30 against 4:00 blind. Do they feel different even though the notes are identical?
  • Is the change of feel the only thing carrying the section, or did I sneak in a new element?
  • Does the kick stay locked through every pattern? If the anchor ever moved, the whole illusion collapses.
Step 09

Place clips and verify the pocket survives the mix

Edit on kick downbeats only
  • Make every cut on a kick downbeat — the one position guaranteed to be on the grid. ⌘ E at the playhead, grid snap on.
  • Where two takes meet, overlap by one bar with a 4-beat crossfade. A hard butt-join mid-groove clicks and lurches.
  • When a section is final, ⌘ J consolidate to one clean clip per section.
Mix without flattening the feel
Processing that respects the groove
Drum bus compressorLight glue only, Attack 10 ms+ so it never clamps the transients that carry the timing. Fast attack here kills the pocket.
DT DRUMS EQHP 25–30 Hz; small dip 7–10 kHz if hats are harsh on the DT 990 — cut less than your ears ask for.
Saturator on drums+1 to +3 dB Soft Sine. Adds density without touching timing.
MasterSpectrum + Utility only. No limiter — a brick-wall limiter pumps on the transients and can drag the perceived timing.
Verification passes
  • Transient check: bypass the drum-bus compressor for ten seconds. If the groove suddenly feels deeper, your attack time is too fast — raise it.
  • Quiet check: listen once at low volume. The pocket should still pull; if it vanishes, a fast compressor or limiter is flattening it.
  • Mono check: the timing must survive in mono — flip Utility width to 0 % and confirm the lean is intact.
Decision check
  • Does any dynamics processor flatten the pocket when engaged? Slow its attack or remove it.
  • Are all edits on kick downbeats, so no cut introduced a phantom timing artifact?
  • Does the groove still read at low volume and in mono?
Step 10

Export and listen away from the grid

Render
  • Select 0:00 to 8:00. File → Export Audio / Video. WAV, 48000 Hz, 24-bit, Normalize OFF. Filename groove_b3_001_v1.wav.
  • File → Collect All and Save. Save the Digitakt project, the MicroFreak patch, and the B3-001 POCKET groove file — your feel now exists as a reusable object across projects.
Listen away from the visual grid
  • Copy to phone. Listen walking, in a car, on laptop speakers — anywhere you cannot see the timeline. The grid is the very thing that lies to you about feel; remove it and judge with the body.
  • groove_b3_001_notes.txt, max 10 lines, timestamped: 2:10 hats feel too straight here, not "mix feels off".
  • Wait a day. Change one offset. Render v2. Compare. The discipline of changing one tick at a time is how you learn what a tick is worth.
Final decision check
  • Played away from the screen, does the track breathe like a body rather than tick like a clock?
  • Can I still state the whole groove as a handful of numbers?
  • Is the feel doing the work that a beginner would have done with extra parts?
