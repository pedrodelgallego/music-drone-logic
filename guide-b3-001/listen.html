Lab Note B3-001
Groove · 02 — Listen

Listen before you build

You cannot program a feel you have not located by ear. Before any gear, listen to four records that live or die by their pocket and run each through the same six-question frame. The goal is not to copy a groove — it is to name the mechanism, so you can rebuild that mechanism with your own material.

The six-question frame

Loop one clear 30–60 second section. Answer all six in one short sentence each before you read the references' own answers. Name behavior, never mood.

The frame

Six questions, one sentence each

Ask, in order
  1. What is the reference clock? Which part is locked to the grid and reads as "the truth" against which everything else leans?
    Why it matters

    A groove needs a metronomic anchor — usually the kick or a quarter-note pulse. If everything swings, nothing swings; the ear has nothing to measure the lean against.

  2. Which parts pull (land late) and which push (land early)?
    Why it matters

    Most deep grooves do both at once: a dragging hat, a hurrying accent. Separating pull from push tells you what carries weight and what carries urgency.

  3. Is the lean systematic (swing, the same on every pair) or per-hit (one chosen note dragged further)?
    Why it matters

    Systematic lean is a swing setting. Per-hit lean is micro-timing. They are different controls on the Digitakt II and you need to know which one you're hearing.

  4. What stays boring so the groove stays audible?
    Why it matters

    Pocket is contrast. Plain notes and a steady clock let the deviation be felt. Busy material buries the very thing you're listening for.

  5. Where does velocity sit relative to timing — do the late hits also get quieter?
    Why it matters

    Human feel couples timing and dynamics. A ghost note is usually both late and soft. Groove templates store both, which is why velocity is half the pocket.

  6. If you stripped the sounds and kept only the timing, would it still read as this record?
    Why it matters

    This is the test of whether you copied the system or the style. The groove should survive a change of samples. If it doesn't, you heard the sound, not the feel.

Four grooves to study

Each links a full replication lesson in the Block 3 workshop. Run the six-question frame first, then open the lesson and build the mechanism with your own material.

RecordThe mechanism to stealReplication lesson
Theo Parrish — "Solitary Flight" Hand-swing at machine tempo. Drum-machine spine, but the off-steps lean so far they are almost falling over — and that lean is held, bar after bar, like a rule rather than a slip. rep-theo-parrish-solitary-flight.html
Moodymann — "I Can't Kick This Feeling When It Hits" Loop-as-pocket. A sampled phrase looped so its internal timing — already humanly uneven — becomes the grid. The "off-grid" is the source material's own feel, kept whole instead of quantized away. rep-moodymann-i-cant-kick-this-feeling-when-it-hits.html
Tony Allen — "Jealousy" Drummer's micro-timing as composition. The hi-hat and snare sit a hair behind a dead-straight pulse, with velocity and placement coupled — the textbook source of "behind the beat" that you can extract as a groove template. rep-tony-allen-jealousy.html
Burnt Friedman & Jaki Liebezeit — "Secret Rhythms" Repeating offset as hypnosis. A small, fixed timing displacement looped for minutes until the body stops hearing it as "wrong" and starts leaning into it. The deviation is so consistent it becomes the meter. rep-burnt-friedman-and-jaki-liebezeit-secret-rhythms.html
  • Listen on the rig you'll build on. The DT 990 Pro's wide stage makes timing offsets easier to place than laptop speakers. Loop, don't scrub — the pocket only appears across repetitions.
  • Write the answers down. Six sentences per record. A groove you can't write in words is one you can't program in ticks.
  • Resist the sound. Theo Parrish's drums and Tony Allen's kit are seductive. You are not stealing the kick — you are stealing the millisecond it does or doesn't land on.

The one stolen principle

Carry this into the build

Across all four records, the same thing is true: the groove is a fixed deviation, repeated long enough to become a meter of its own. None of them is random; none of them is freshly performed each bar. They lean the same way every cycle. That is the only principle you are stealing — repeat a chosen offset until the body predicts it.

Before you move on
  • I can name the reference clock in each of the four records.
  • I can say, per record, what pulls and what pushes.
  • I know which records use systematic swing and which use per-hit timing.
  • I have one written sentence: the groove I want to build leans like ___.
  • I am stealing a mechanism (a repeated offset), not a sound.
