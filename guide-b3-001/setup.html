Groove · 04 — Setup

The rig, wired for feel

Groove work is unforgiving about clock and latency: a few stray milliseconds will masquerade as a timing decision you never made. This page wires the rig so that every offset you hear is one you chose — and explains the one thing that ruins micro-timing if you skip it.

Ableton Live 12 Suite

  • The only clock master for both hardware boxes
  • Groove Pool, Extract Groove, automation
  • Records the Digitakt's hand-nudged feel as audio + MIDI

Elektron Digitakt II

  • Per-track swing and per-trig micro-timing in ticks
  • The groove engine — kick locked, everything else leaning
  • Trig conditions and per-track length for slow drift

Arturia MicroFreak

  • Bass and tonal stabs that must share the drums' pocket
  • Synced LFO follows Ableton's clock
  • Recorded into Ableton, then bent to the extracted groove

MOTU M4

  • 4-in / 4-out USB-C interface — the single audio device
  • MicroFreak mono into input 1; Digitakt stereo into 3/4
  • Low, stable round-trip latency at 256 samples

Launchpad Pro MK3

  • Mutes for rehearsing the groove arc by hand
  • Live fader rides captured as automation
  • Not a clock — purely a control surface here

Beyerdynamic DT 990 Pro

  • Open-back, 250 Ω — wide stage makes timing legible
  • Honest about whether a hat drags or merely sounds late
  • Watch the 7–10 kHz bump on hat brightness, not on timing

Why clock discipline is the whole game

For a drone, a few milliseconds of slop is inaudible. For groove, it is the entire subject. If the clock jitters or the recorded take drifts, you cannot tell your +4-tick hat from a converter delay. Lock the clock first, then trust your ears.

One clock to rule them all

Exactly one device generates tempo; everything else listens. If the Digitakt's screen shows a tempo different from Ableton's, sync is broken — stop and fix it before recording, because a groove recorded against a drifting clock cannot be trusted and cannot be repaired.

  • No unpowered hubs for the clock. USB clock jitter is inaudible on a direct port and can become audible through a cheap hub. For groove work specifically, plug the Digitakt and MicroFreak straight into the computer.
  • Why mono for the MicroFreak. The bass must share one pocket with the drums. A mono file forces you to create width deliberately later, and keeps the timing reference unambiguous.

The latency measurement you cannot skip

Measure the offset once, before any groove work
  • Set Ableton to 124 BPM, buffer 256 samples at 48 kHz. Record 8 bars of the Digitakt playing a dead-straight kick on every downbeat, monitor on Auto.
  • Zoom all the way into bar 1. The kick transient will sit a few milliseconds after the grid line — that is clock-start lag plus converter latency, not a groove.
  • If the offset is consistent, fix it: enter the measured value in ⌘ , → Audio → Driver Error Compensation (global), or drag each recorded clip left so transients sit on the grid (per-take).
  • Only after this is your +4-tick hat actually a +4-tick hat and not a +4-tick hat plus an unknown system delay. Measure once, now.
Buffer size at 48 kHz — pick per task
128 samples~ 2.7 ms each way. Use if hand-nudging feels laggy while you audition micro-timing.
256 samples~ 5.3 ms each way. The default for programming and recording the groove.
512+ samplesMix and export only. Latency no longer matters; the take is already printed.

Tempo & feel

Recommended tempo: 122–126 BPM. Start at 124 BPM — one 16th step ≈ 121 ms, one tick ≈ 2.5 ms.
