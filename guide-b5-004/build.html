Lab Note B5-004
Translation & Subtraction · 06 — The build

Build a track that survives every system, by taking away

A complete, ordered build for one short original piece, mixed for translation and arranged by removal. Every step has a subtraction in it and a translation check at the end. Keep the setup page open — it is the metering reference this page assumes — and keep the master mono button under one finger throughout.

Step 01

Set the project up to judge translation

Project basics
  • ⌘ N for a new Live Set, Tab to Arrangement view. Tempo 124, 4/4.
  • Right-click the timeline ruler → Time Ruler Format → Time so the arrangement reads in m:ss.
  • ⌘ , → Audio: MOTU M4 in and out, 48000 Hz, buffer 512 — this is a mixing session, latency is irrelevant and CPU headroom is free.
Build the master metering chain first
  • On the Master: Utility then Spectrum, in that order. No limiter, no master EQ — this exercise judges translation, not loudness, and a limiter would hide the faults you are trying to find.
  • Leave the Utility's Gain at 0 dB and Width at 100 %. It is a monitoring tool here, not a processor; the only control you will touch is its Mono switch.
  • Map the Utility Mono button to a single Launchpad pad (⌘ M, click Mono, tap pad, exit). This is the most important control in the whole session — if checking mono takes more than one finger, you will stop doing it.
  • Enable the correlation display so you can see it while mixing. Park the meter where your eyes already are, on the master track or a docked view.
  • ⌘ S → save as b5_004_translation.als in a dedicated project folder. File → Collect All and Save at the end of every session so the project is self-contained.
What the meters mean before you trust them
Reading the master chain
Correlation near +1Effectively mono — bulletproof but narrow. Fine for the foundation, dull for everything.
Correlation +0.3 to +0.8A healthy wide mix. Brief dips on tails are normal; sustained dips are not.
Correlation below 0 (sustained)Phase cancellation waiting to bite in mono. Investigate, do not ship.
Spectrum after UtilityShows exactly what leaves the master, including whatever the mono switch does. Watch the sub for build-up.
Decision check
  • One button collapses the whole mix to mono without taking your hands off the faders?
  • The master is monitoring-only — nothing is processing the sound?
  • Correlation and spectrum are both visible at a glance?
Step 02

Lay the mono-safe foundation

Build the core dead centre
  • Create tracks KICK, BASS, STAB, PAD, PERC, plus returns A — REVERB and B — ECHO. Record or program your own material — the patterns must be original.
  • Pan KICK and BASS hard centre and leave them there. The kick owns 40–80 Hz, the bass owns 80–180 Hz, and nothing else is allowed below 180 Hz.
  • Keep the STAB — the single chord that will carry the track — centred and dry at this stage. Its width comes later, from the reverb return, never from the source.
Lock the low end to mono now
  • Group KICK + BASS → ⌘ G → LOW. Add a Utility on the group and enable Bass Mono with the crossover at 120 Hz.
  • This is insurance, not mixing: it removes the possibility of low-frequency phase cancellation before you can introduce one by accident. Everything below 120 Hz is now forced to centre; everything above keeps whatever image it has.
  • KICK: EQ Eight high-pass at 28 Hz, 24 dB/oct, to strip sub-rumble that only steals headroom.
  • BASS: high-pass at 35 Hz; a narrow bell cut at 200–300 Hz if it is woolly. Cut, don't boost.
The low-end pecking order
One owner per band, below 180 Hz
Sub — below 40 HzHigh-pass everything here except the kick body. Inaudible rumble eats headroom and smears the fold.
Kick band — 40–80 HzThe kick owns this. Tune its fundamental to the root so it agrees with the bass.
Bass band — 80–180 HzThe bass owns this. If the kick and bass fight, sidechain the bass to the kick rather than fader-war them.
Above 180 HzOpen to other parts — but force the whole region below 120 Hz mono so nothing leaks width into it.
  • Every low-end translation problem traces back to a violation of this table. Fix the ownership before you touch a fader.
Kick (centre) + Bass (centre) → LOW group → Utility Bass Mono → Mono-safe foundation
Decision check
  • Hit mono: do kick and bass hold their level exactly? They must be unchanged by the fold.
  • Is exactly one element in the kick band and one in the bass band — no third party below 180 Hz?
  • Is the stab still centred and dry, with nothing widening it yet?
Step 03

Earn width honestly

Width from a real source — the reverb return
  • Return A: Hybrid Reverb, Plate or Hall, decay 4–6 s, Dry/Wet 100 % (returns are always fully wet; the send is the mix control).
  • Send the mono, centred stab into Return A. The stereo decay of the reverb is now the width — a real source, not a phase trick. The dry stab stays in the Mid; the wet tail lives in the Side.
  • EQ the return before it returns: high-pass the reverb at 250 Hz so the tail never adds wide low-frequency content. Width belongs above the mud zone.
M/S EQ to widen only where it is safe
EQ Eight in M/S mode on the STAB or its group
Side — low shelf cutPull side content below 200 Hz to zero. The width should not exist down there.
Side — high shelf, gentle boost+1 to +2 dB above 6 kHz — air and width in the band where phase problems are inaudible.
Mid — narrow cutCarve any masking band out of the centred core; leave the Side untouched.
The honest-width test
  • Solo the stab + its reverb. Watch correlation: it should sit positive, dipping only on the tail.
  • Hit mono. The dry stab must stay solid; the reverb tail will narrow — that is correct, the tail is meant to be optional.
  • If the whole thing thins out in mono, your "width" was a phase trick. Pull any Utility Width back below 120 % and re-earn it from the reverb.
Decision check
  • Is every wide thing wide because of a reverb, two takes, or side-only high EQ — never an extreme widener?
  • In mono, does the dry core survive while only the tails narrow?
  • Is there any width below 200 Hz? There should be none.
Step 04

Mix by removal — find and cut the maskers

Set a static balance first
  • Pull all faders to -inf. Bring up one at a time, 30 s apart: kick, then bass against it, then stab, then pad, then perc, then returns. Each element earns its place against what is already there.
  • Starting balance: kick -9 dB, bass -12 dB, stab -12 dB, pad -18 dB, perc -16 dB, reverb return -9 dB, echo return -12 dB. Master peaks ≤ -3 dBFS.
  • If a fader wants to drift more than 4 dB from here, the real problem is masking or width, not level. Fix the cause, not the symptom.
The masking hunt — cut, never boost
  • Masking, not level, is the usual reason a part feels weak. When two parts occupy the same band, one buries the other no matter where the faders sit.
  • The technique: on the part that is masking, sweep a narrow EQ Eight boost until the clutter jumps out, then flip that boost into a cut. The masked part returns with no energy added to the mix and no headroom spent.
Find the masker, carve a narrow dip in it
Stab feels buriedFind the pad or bass tail sharing its band (often 300–600 Hz); cut a narrow dip there. The stab returns with no added level.
Bass feels woollyCut 200–300 Hz on the pad, not a boost on the bass. The mud zone clears for free.
Mix sounds clutteredSweep a narrow boost to find the offending band, then turn it into a cut on the part that owns it least.
Highs feel harshDip 7–10 kHz — but less than the DT 990 begs for. Confirm on the phone before committing.
  • Cuts are cleaner than boosts and add no level to fight the master ceiling later. A mix built from cuts arrives at the master with headroom to spare — itself a translation advantage.
Remove the duplicate
  • Pad and stab both filling the mids? Mute the pad for two minutes. If the track still breathes, it stays muted — fewer parts always translate better.
  • Perc and hat hits competing in the same range? Keep the one with more rhythmic purpose, mute the other, and let the space it leaves be part of the groove.
  • Write down what you removed. A pass where you added nothing and removed something is a pass in the right direction.
Decision check
  • For every part that felt weak, did you cut a masker or mute a duplicate rather than boost it?
  • Listen once quietly: does the balance hold? Low-volume listening reveals the real balance and the masking you missed.
  • What did you remove this pass? "Nothing, I added" means you mixed the wrong direction.
Step 05

Arrange subtractively — build the peak by removing

Mark the map
  • Add locators at each boundary: INTRO, GROOVE, FULL, STRIP, PEAK, OUTRO.
  • Counter-intuitively, PEAK comes after STRIP. The peak is felt because something was removed just before it, not because something was added at it.
Section → time → bar at 124 BPM
INTRO — 0:00Bar 1 — stab + reverb only, the room before the rhythm
GROOVE — 1:00Bar 32 — kick and perc enter, bass surfaces
FULL — 2:00Bar 63 — every element present, the densest the track gets
STRIP — 3:00Bar 94 — remove the pad and perc; only kick, bass, stab remain
PEAK — 3:45Bar 117 — the stripped groove plus a single reverb bloom; the past now reads as huge
OUTRO — 4:30Bar 140 — cut bass, then kick; stab + reverb carry the exit and fade
The subtractive drop
  • At STRIP, remove parts on a kick downbeat — ⌘ E to split, then delete the pad and perc clips. The sudden space is the event.
  • At PEAK, add nothing new. Bloom Return A's send up by 6 dB for a few bars so the existing stab fills the room the removal just opened. Density without a single new element.
  • Make every cut on a downbeat; crossfade any seam between clips by a beat (drag the clip fade handles) so nothing clicks.
Drawn movement, not new material
Automation that opens space — times at 124 BPM
Reverb send @ STRIP→PEAK-9 dB → -3 dB over 8 bars — the room blooms exactly as parts leave
Stab filter @ FULL→PEAKSlowly open a low-pass so the one surviving part brightens; no ramp shorter than 8 bars
Echo feedback @ OUTRO40 % → 60 % → 40 % — the tails self-regenerate briefly, then recede
Master volume @ final 30 sA slow fade — the only place the master is touched
  • If you can hear an automation move as an "event", it is too fast. The listener should notice things are different, never that they changed. Right-click a lane → Simplify Envelope to thin breakpoint clutter while keeping the shape.
Editing craft
  • Every cut lands on a kick downbeat. ⌘ E at the playhead with grid snap on.
  • Where two clips of one part meet, overlap by a beat and crossfade the seam — hard butt-joins click and lurch, and headphones will expose them even when the room hides them.
  • Loop a suspicious seam with ⌘ L and listen three times before deciding it passes.
  • When a section's edits are final, ⌘ J to consolidate — one clean clip per section keeps the timeline legible.
Decision check
  • Play FULL → STRIP → PEAK blind: does the peak land because things opened, not because things were added?
  • Did the drop remove a part rather than stack one?
  • Can you remove one more element across the whole arrangement without weakening it? If yes, remove it.
Step 06

The mono pass — fold the whole mix down

Live in mono for a while
  • Hit the mono button and leave it on. Mix a full pass entirely in mono. If the groove works folded, the width you add back later is pure bonus.
  • Walk the correlation meter through the whole arrangement. Any sustained dip below 0 on a load-bearing part is a fault to chase, not a flavour to keep.
Hunt the disappearing parts
Toggle mono on/off and watch what changes
Reverb tail vanishesExpected and fine — the tail is meant to be optional Side content.
Bass loses level/punchLow-frequency width leaked in. Re-check Bass Mono crossover and any stereo effect on the bass.
Stab thins outYou widened the source instead of the return. Narrow the source; widen only the wet path.
Whole mix drops in levelPhase cancellation across multiple parts. Pull every Utility Width back to 100 % and re-earn width one part at a time.
Decision check
  • Toggling mono on and off: does the energy hold, or does a load-bearing part vanish?
  • Does the correlation needle stay positive on the foundation through every section?
  • Could you ship the mono fold as-is and still have a groove? That is the bar.
Step 07

The cross-system pass — three judges

Render and listen everywhere before changing anything
  • Export: WAV, 48 kHz, 24-bit, Normalize OFF, Convert to Mono OFF, no limiter. Filename b5_004_v1.wav.
  • Get it onto the phone, into a second room, and onto the headphones. A/B against a commercial reference you already trust on each system.
  • Mix for the worst of the three. If the phone fails, fix that first — a phone-proof groove is a finished groove.
What each judge tells you
Translate the complaints into fixes
Phone: groove goneYou leaned on lows and width. Re-balance so the centred core drives, then re-test.
Headphones: clicks / edits audibleCrossfade the seams; the room and phone hid them, headphones won't.
Room: bass too big or too smallTrust the phone and headphones for the relative bass; the room lies about absolute low-end level.
All three agree something is wrongThat is a real fault, not a system quirk. Fix it.
Run it as a fixed routine
  • Set one comfortable listening level on the M4 phones knob and leave it. Translation judgements drift with volume — the only variable you change between systems must be the system.
  • Listen to the same 60-second span on each judge, in the same order, every time. A repeatable routine catches regressions a casual listen misses.
  • Keep a commercial reference cued on all three. You are checking your track against records that already translate, not against silence.
  • Note the fault in one concrete line per system before touching anything. Then fix the worst-system fault first.
Decision check
  • Does the track read the same on the phone, the headphones, and the room?
  • Did you make it work on the worst of the three rather than the best?
  • Is there any system on which it needs to sound good and currently doesn't? Then it is not done — it is hiding.
Step 08

Final removal pass, then export and step away

One last subtraction
  • Take everything you learned from the three judges and make it a removal: cut a masking band, mute a part that only worked in the room, narrow a width that only flattered the headphones.
  • Confirm the master still peaks ≤ -3 dBFS across the whole piece, including the PEAK bloom. Cuts will have given you headroom back — keep it.
  • Re-run the mono toggle one final time. Nothing load-bearing should move.
Export and archive
  • Render the full span as b5_004_v2.wav with the same settings. File → Collect All and Save.
  • Write b5_004_notes.txt next to the WAV — max 10 lines, timestamped and concrete: 3:00 strip needs another beat of space, not peak feels off.
  • Wait a day. Listen on all three systems. Change one thing — ideally a removal — render v3, compare.
Decision check
  • Was your last move a removal? If it was an addition, ask whether the addition is really needed.
  • Does the final render survive the fold and all three systems with nothing hiding?
  • Could the track lose one more element and still be itself? If so, you know what v3 starts with.
