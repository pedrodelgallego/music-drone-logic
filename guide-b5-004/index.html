Translation & Subtraction · 01 — Overview

Make a mix that survives every system, built by taking away

A single-topic deep reference on translation and subtraction. Translation is the discipline of making your track read the same in a club, on headphones, and out of a phone speaker. Subtraction is the method that gets you there — because most of what wrecks translation is content you should have removed. Inspired by the dub-techno lineage of restraint; the track you make from it must be fully original.

The goal

A mix that translates

  • Reads the same on a room, headphones, and a phone
  • Folds to mono with no part vanishing and no level lost
  • Built on centred, in-phase foundations — width earned, never faked

A track built by removal

  • EQ by cutting the masker, not boosting the masked
  • Peaks built by stripping parts away, not piling them on
  • Every element interrogated: mute it, and keep it muted if nothing is lost
Core objective

Decide what to remove before you decide what to add. A mix that needs one particular system to sound good is not finished — it is hiding.

Why this matters

The beginner instinct

  • Widen everything until it sounds "big" on headphones
  • Boost the part that feels weak instead of cutting what masks it
  • Add a layer to fill a gap, then add another to fix the first
  • Mix only in the room, then ship it and hope

The opposite discipline

  • Foundation centred and mono-safe; width only where it survives the fold
  • Carve the muddy band out of the masker so the masked part is freed
  • Three judges — room, headphones, phone — and you mix for the worst of them
  • The drop is a subtraction: take a part out and the past reads as bigger

The skills transfer. Mono compatibility, correlation, and mid/side thinking are the same in ambient, dub, sound design for film, generative installation work, and any record meant to leave the room it was made in. You are not learning a mastering trick — you are learning to build sound that survives.

Principle

Subtract until it nearly breaks, then translate everywhere.
