Translation & Subtraction · 02 — Listen

Listen for what isn't there

Before you touch a meter, train your ears on records that translate. The dub-techno lineage was built for systems it could not control — pressed to vinyl, played on warehouse PAs, dubbed onto cassette — and it survives because of what was left out. Listen first for the empty space, then for how few elements carry the weight, then for the mono-safe core under all the width.

Reference lessons in this block

Open each rep lesson and listen on two systems at least — your good headphones and your phone speaker. The point is not the genre. The point is to hear how a record stays itself when the playback system strips it down.

Maurizio — M5

  • A whole track from a chord stab, a kick, and reverb tail
  • Width lives entirely in the wet return — the dry core is mono
  • Listen on phone: the groove survives because the foundation is centred

Rhythm & Sound — Carrier

  • Subtraction as the entire arrangement language
  • Elements drop out and the room they leave becomes the event
  • Almost nothing happens in the sides that matters in mono

Porter Ricks — Biokinetics 1

  • Depth built from filtered, mono-safe sources and long tails
  • Sub stays dead centre while texture breathes around it
  • A masterclass in width that never threatens the low end

How to run a translation listen

Listen 01

Three systems, one record

The pass
  • Play the same 60 seconds of a reference on headphones, then a phone speaker, then the best room you have access to.
  • On headphones, note where the width sits — is it the reverb, the percussion, the pad? Name the source of every wide thing.
  • On the phone, note what survives. The kick, the bass, the central stab should still drive. If they don't on the reference, they won't on yours.
  • In the room, note the low end and the depth. This is where you judge balance — and where you learn to distrust your own room's bass.
What to write down
  • One line per system: what dominated, what disappeared, what stayed put.
  • The element that is identical on all three is the foundation. That is the lesson.
  • The element that vanishes on the phone is built from width or lows alone — note how the artist kept it inessential.
Decision check
  • Can you name, for each reference, the single mono-safe element the whole groove rests on?
  • Did anything you loved on headphones turn out to be inessential on the phone? That is the trap you are learning to avoid.
  • Where did the artist remove rather than add to make a moment land?
  • More to study in this block: Basic Channel — Phylyps Trak, Pole — Modul, Fluxion — Lark, and DeepChord presents Echospace — The Coldest Season. All survive the fold; all say less than you expect.
  • Run the full block index for the rest: Block 5 reference lessons.
Listening rule

You are not listening for what the producer added. You are listening for what they had the nerve to leave out — and for the centred core that lets the rest be optional.
