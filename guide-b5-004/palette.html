Translation & Subtraction · 05 — Palette

The translation checklist and the subtraction moves

This is the working vocabulary for the build — a checklist you run on every part, and a set of named moves you reach for instead of adding. Treat it as a palette of removals. Each move makes the mix smaller and more certain, never larger and more hopeful.

The translation checklist

Run this on every element as you place it, and again on the whole mix before render. It is the same checklist whether the part is a kick, a pad, or a delay return.

Per-element translation pass
  • In mono, does this part hold its level, or does it thin out / disappear?
  • Is its correlation positive, or does it park below zero when soloed against the mix?
  • If it lives below ~120 Hz, is it mono? If it is wide down there, narrow it.
  • Is its width built from a real source (mono + reverb, two takes) or from a phase trick (extreme Utility, all-pass widener)?
  • On the phone speaker, can you still tell what it is doing — or only on headphones?
  • Does it occupy a band something else needs? If so, who yields, and have you cut rather than boosted?
  • If you mute it for two minutes, is anything actually lost?
Translation failure signals
  • Energy drops the moment you hit mono — phase cancellation somewhere wide.
  • The bass loses punch in mono — low-frequency width that should be centred.
  • The groove vanishes on the phone but feels great on headphones — you mixed for width and lows you can't rely on.
  • The mix only "opens up" after a boost — you are filling a masking hole the long way round.
  • The correlation meter lives in the negative — you widened with phase, not with sources.

The subtraction moves

When a part feels weak, reach for one of these before reaching for a boost or a new layer. Each one is a removal that solves the problem by clearing space, not by adding energy.

Cut the masker

  • Find the part occupying the weak part's band
  • Cut a narrow dip there, don't boost the weak part
  • The weak part returns with zero added level

Mute the duplicate

  • Two parts doing one job is one part too many
  • Mute the weaker for two minutes; keep it muted if nothing is lost
  • Fewer parts always translate better

Narrow then earn

  • Pull width back until the mix nearly collapses
  • Add back only what the part genuinely needs
  • Width earned this way survives the fold

Drop to lift

  • Build the peak by removing on the way out
  • Strip a part; the past reads as bigger in hindsight
  • The drop is a subtraction, never a pile-up

Removal targets, band by band

Where the clutter usually hides — cut, don't boost
Sub — below 40 HzHigh-pass everything that is not the kick or bass. Inaudible rumble eats headroom and muddies the fold.
Low — 40–120 HzOne element owns the kick band, one owns the bass band. Force this region mono. Cut, don't stack.
Low-mid — 120–350 HzThe mud zone. Where pads, drone, and bass tails pile up. Narrow cuts here clean the whole mix.
Mid — 350 Hz–2 kHzWhere masking is most audible. Carve the masker so the lead or stab is heard, not louder.
High — 2 kHz+The safe place to widen (side EQ). Cut harshness, don't reach for it to add "presence."
  • The DT 990 Pro lifts 7–10 kHz. If your headphones beg you to cut the highs, cut less than your ears ask for, and confirm the decision on the phone and in the room before committing.
  • Every cut is a translation gain. Removing a masking band, a duplicate layer, or wide low-end content makes the mix more certain on every system at once. There is no system where less clutter sounds worse.
  • Width is a high-frequency luxury. The honest places to widen are the air band and the reverb return. The dishonest places are the bass and anything load-bearing.
Decision check
  • For each weak part, did you reach for a cut or a mute before a boost or a layer?
  • Is the entire low end below ~120 Hz mono and owned by exactly one element per band?
  • Can you name the one mono-safe foundation the whole track rests on?
  • Is every wide thing wide because of a real source, not a phase trick?
