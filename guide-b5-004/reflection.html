Translation & Subtraction · 07 — Reflection

Fault, carry-forward, principle

Most of the learning happens here, not while mixing. Find the one fault that matters, name the one thing you carry into the next note, and hold onto the principle. Answer slowly. Write the answers down next to the render.

How you know it worked

Success signals
  • The mix folds to mono with no part vanishing and no level lost on the foundation.
  • The groove reads the same on a phone, headphones, and a room.
  • The correlation meter lives positive on every load-bearing part.
  • Everything below ~120 Hz is mono and owned by one element per band.
  • Every wide thing is wide because of a real source, not a phase trick.
  • The peak lands because something was removed, not added.
  • The clarity came from cuts and mutes, not from boosts and layers.
  • You can name the single mono-safe element the whole track rests on.
Failure signals
  • The energy drops the moment you hit mono.
  • The bass loses punch in the fold.
  • The groove vanishes on the phone but feels great on headphones.
  • The mix only opened up after a boost or a new layer.
  • The correlation meter parks in the negative on a load-bearing part.
  • The peak is a pile-up of added elements rather than a removal.
  • The track needs one particular system to sound good.

Find the fault

Every mix that doesn't translate fails in one identifiable way. Name yours in a single sentence — it is the most useful thing you will write today.

  1. When you hit mono, what was the first thing to disappear or weaken?
  2. Was that part load-bearing, or was it decoration you can afford to lose?
  3. If it was load-bearing, where did its width or low-end leak come from?
  4. Which system exposed the fault first — phone, headphones, or room?
  5. Did you fix the fault by removing something, or did you patch it by adding?

Carry forward

  1. Which single subtraction improved the mix the most?
  2. Which boost or layer did you reach for first, before catching yourself — and what removal replaced it?
  3. Which element could still be removed without weakening the track?
  4. Did the subtractive drop land harder than an additive build would have?
  5. What will you mono-check earlier next time?
  6. What is the one habit from this note you want automatic in the next session?
Final principle

A mix that survives every system is built by removal, not addition. Subtract until it nearly breaks, mono-check constantly, and earn width only where it survives the fold. The clearest track wins on every speaker — the loudest only wins on one.
