Lab Note B6-005
The Score · 01 — Overview

Write a score — a rule-set that produces sound — and realize it twice

A single-topic deep reference for instruction-based composition. A score is not a transcription of music you already heard; it is a system — a small set of rules that, when obeyed, produces music. You will write one score, realize it once, realize the same score again differently, and read the gap between the two takes. That gap is the lesson. The piece must be fully original: your rules, your realizations, no copied reference.

The goal

A score and two realizations

  • One page of rules in a chosen idiom — text, graphic, event-list, or open-form
  • Two printed takes from that one page: R1 and R2, each 2–5 minutes
  • Fully original: you author the rules; the recordings are evidence of how they read

Fundamentals you'll practice

  • Notation as design — deciding what each rule fixes and what it hands to the realizer
  • Indeterminacy with boundaries — chance that lives inside a written fence
  • Reading a delta — tracing every sameness and difference back to the rule behind it
Core objective

Author the smallest rule-set that still leaves the music somewhere to go — then prove it by realizing it twice and naming exactly which rule fixed what stayed the same and which rule freed what changed.

Why this exercise matters

The beginner instinct

  • Write down everything — turn the score into a transcription nothing can vary
  • Or write down nothing — call "do whatever" a score and trust the vibe
  • Treat the recording as the work, and the instructions as scaffolding to discard

The opposite discipline

  • Treat the rule-set as the work — the recording is one output among many
  • Decide, line by line, what is fixed (determinate) and what is free (indeterminate)
  • Tune the freedom until two realizations are recognizably the same piece, audibly different

The skill transfers far past experimental music. Once you can encode an intention as rules and hear what survives realization, you can write generative patches, prompt systems, live-coding sets, and conducted improvisations — anything that has to run, or be read, without you holding its hand.

Principle

A score is a system. Every line either fixes an outcome or hands it to the realizer — and you should know which, before you hear a note.
