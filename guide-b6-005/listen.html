Lab Note B6-005
The Score · 02 — Listen

Six scores, not six recordings

Each piece below exists primarily as a set of instructions. The famous recordings are realizations — one possible output among many. Listen the way you will soon be writing: in two columns. What must every realization share (the rule the score fixed)? What changes from performance to performance (the freedom the score granted)? Reading scores this way is the same act as reading your own delta in the build.

How to listen this week

For each piece, find two recorded realizations if you can. Name one feature they share — that is the score working — and one feature where they diverge wildly — that is the indeterminacy working. You are training the exact ear the build demands: the ability to hear a rule and a freedom as two separable things.

Rules that react — Christian Wolff

Wolff fixes relationships, not notes: "play this only after you hear that," "respond within a held silence," "enter on a cue you cannot predict." The system is the web of contingencies between players; the actual sounds are left almost entirely open. A rule about listening produces structure without naming a single pitch.

  • Listen for the fixed part — the timing logic, the call-and-response shape — versus the free part: every actual pitch and timbre.
  • Carry into the build: a response rule ("react to the previous event") makes the piece depend on its own unfolding, so it can never be the same twice even with one player.
  • Replicate it: Christian Wolff — For 1, 2 or 3 People →

Graphic, maximal freedom — Earle Brown

December 1952 is a single page of floating horizontal and vertical lines — no notes, no clef, no time. The performer reads the rectangles as events in two dimensions (often register × duration) and decides everything else. Two realizations can sound like different pieces entirely, yet both are correct. This is your purest model for a graphic idiom: a page that constrains shape and density while leaving instrument, pitch, and dynamics open.

  • Listen for what the graphic does constrain — relative thickness, density, the broad up/down of the page over time — and how much it leaves open.
  • Carry into the build: a graphic score makes the determinate/indeterminate split visual — thickness might be fixed while exact pitch floats.
  • Replicate it: Earle Brown — December 1952 →

The other graphic pole — Morton Feldman

Projection 1 for solo cello is graphic too, but tighter: the grid fixes time and register-band (high/middle/low) and articulation (harmonic, pizzicato, arco) while leaving exact pitch within a band free. It is the proof that "graphic" does not mean "loose" — Feldman fixes a great deal and frees a little. Hold it against Brown to feel the whole span a graphic page can cover.

  • Listen for how much is pinned — the rhythm and density are nearly fixed — against the one thing left free: which pitch inside a band.
  • Carry into the build: you can fix the architecture completely and free a single dimension. A narrow freedom, well chosen, is still a real freedom.
  • Replicate it: Morton Feldman — Projection 1 →

The rule is fixed; chance ran once — John Cage

Music of Changes is the opposite pole. Cage used chance operations (the I Ching) to compose the score — but once written, it is fully fixed, notated to the millimetre. The indeterminacy lived in the generating system, not the realization. It is the essential warning for this Lab Note: a rule-set can be so determined that two realizations are identical. That is valid composition — but it is not what the build is testing.

  • Listen for how precisely fixed the result is — every duration and dynamic specified, despite being chance-derived.
  • Carry into the build: decide where your indeterminacy lives — in how you generate the score, or in how it is read. The build requires the second, so R1 and R2 can differ at all.
  • Replicate it: John Cage — Music of Changes →

A process anyone can run — Terry Riley

In C is one page: 53 short melodic fragments, played in order, each repeated as long as the player likes, everyone over a steady pulse. The rules are tiny and almost entirely about when to move on. The result is unmistakably the same piece in any performance — and never the same twice. This is the cleanest demonstration of the band you are hunting: a score recognizable across all its realizations precisely because so little is fixed and that little is fixed hard.

  • Listen for what is rigid (the pulse, the fragment order) versus what is free (how long each fragment lasts, who is where).
  • Carry into the build: a process score — "do X, then advance when a condition is met" — is realizable solo on the rig by one player wearing many hats.
  • Replicate it: Terry Riley — In C →

Open-form — Witold Lutosławski

Jeux vénitiens uses aleatoric counterpoint: blocks of fully-notated material whose internal coordination is left loose, and whose order or repetition the conductor cues in the moment. The macro-form is open; the micro-material is fixed. It models the fourth idiom you can choose — open-form, where you compose modules and leave their sequence or recurrence to a decision made at realization time.

  • Listen for the seam between fixed blocks and free assembly — the material is composed, the joinery is decided live.
  • Carry into the build: open-form lets you write rich fixed modules and still guarantee R1 and R2 differ, because the order or the cue is the free zone.
  • Replicate it: Witold Lutosławski — Jeux vénitiens →
The throughline of the tradition

Wolff frees almost everything but the relationships. Cage frees almost nothing once the page is set. Feldman, Brown, Riley, and Lutosławski each sit somewhere between — fixing some dimensions hard and opening others wide. Cardew (Treatise) and Oliveros (Sonic Meditations, Deep Listening) push further: Cardew into a 193-page graphic with no instructions at all, Oliveros into prose scores that instruct attention itself. The whole tradition is one long argument about where the freedom goes. Your build is your entry in that argument.

Before you leave this page, write one sentence in the logbook: the claim you intend to test. The target is something like — a score is a system whose every rule either fixes an outcome or hands it to the realizer, and I can predict which before I hear a note.
