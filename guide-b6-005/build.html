Lab Note B6-005
The Score · 06 — The build

Write a score, realize it twice, read the delta, defend the rule

This is the deep page. You will write one original score from the primitives on page 05, realize it once as R1, realize the same score again differently as R2, lay the two takes side by side, and trace every sameness and difference back to the rule responsible. The gap between R1 and R2 is the entire lesson — it is the audible proof of what your rules fixed and what they freed. Keep the setup page open; it is the rig reference this build assumes.

write the score → predict the delta → realize R1 → choice log → realize R2 → delta map → defend the rule
Step 01

Write the score — one page, one idiom

The score is written away from the rig. You are designing a system, not a sound. Choose an idiom from page 03, pull primitives from page 05, and decide line by line what is fixed and what is free. We will build a worked example alongside you — an event-list score — and you will write your own in parallel.

Hold one discipline above all others while writing: keep the score readable by a stranger. The single fastest way to fool yourself here is to write rules you understand only because you already know what you meant. A rule that depends on your intent is not a rule — it is a private note. Every line should survive being read cold by someone who has never spoken to you, because in Step 02 and again in Step 03 you become that stranger: in R2 you must obey the page, not your memory of the performance. So write as if handing the score to a person across the world. If a line needs a footnote to be obeyed correctly, fold the footnote into the line. If two readers could obey it differently and you did not intend that freedom, the line is ambiguous and the build's required fault is already baked in. The discipline is not pedantry; it is the only way the delta between R1 and R2 can mean anything. A delta produced by a score only you can read measures your memory, not your rules.

Choose the idiom and commit
  • For the worked example we choose event-list: an ordered table, one row per move, with an explicit free? column. It is the most tractable to realize solo on the rig and it maps row-for-row onto the delta map you fill in Step 04.
  • Your score may instead be text, graphic, or open-form. Whichever you pick, the discipline below is identical: tag every line D or I, and guarantee at least one hard D and one wide I.
The worked score — "Three Answers"

A score in four fixed rules and one event-list. It pairs a boundary D (the identity) with an open-source I (the freedom) and a relational primitive (the engine). Read it as something a stranger could realize without asking a question.

Three Answers — the fixed rules (D)
R-a · frameD — Begins and ends in silence. Total length 3:00–3:30.
R-b · countD — Exactly three sound events, in sequence, no overlap.
R-c · orderD — Each event answers the one before: opposite register (if the last was low, go high) and opposite length (if the last was long, go short). Event 1 has nothing to answer, so it is free.
R-d · separationD — Between events, a silence of at least one unhurried breath, no more than ten seconds.

Write your own — the rules
  • Write 4–8 lines. Tag each D or I in the margin. Pull from page 05: one boundary primitive for identity, one choice or attention primitive for freedom, and optionally one relational or process primitive for the engine.
  • Stress-test each I: does it have genuine range, or is it a fake freedom ("C or C#") that will make R1 and R2 clones?
  • Stress-test each D: would a stranger read it the same way twice? "Begins in silence" passes; "start gently" fails — it is ambiguous, and ambiguity is the fault this Lab Note hunts.
Predict the delta — in writing, before you record
  • Write one sentence naming what R1 and R2 will share (your D rules) and where they will diverge (your I zones). For the worked score: "Both will be three answered events in silence, the same up-down-up contour and short-long pacing; they will differ only in what the three sounds are."
  • This prediction is the hypothesis. The two realizations confirm or break it. A broken prediction is not failure — it is the most valuable result this page produces.
Decision check
  • Could a stranger realize this with no questions? Hand it to someone and watch where they hesitate.
  • Is there exactly one idiom, one hard D, and one wide I minimum?
  • Does my indeterminacy live in the reading, not just in how I generated the score? (If it's all Cage-style fixed, R1 and R2 cannot differ.)
  • Have I written the share/diverge prediction down where I can check it later?
Step 02

Realize R1 — perform the score once, print it

A realization is a performance, not an edit. Obey every rule honestly — including resisting the urge to fix "mistakes" that are actually legal outcomes of the rules. Print to one stereo track and render without surgery.

Set the rig to match the score
  • Open the arrangement named R1. Because Three Answers frees timing (durations are "long/short," not bars), work gridless: Time Ruler Format → Time, metronome off, ruler in m:ss. If your own score fixes timing, work on the grid instead.
  • Confirm the gain target: peaks ≈ -12 dBFS. Write the M4 Input 1 gain setting on a sticky note — R2 must match it exactly.
  • Stage the sources the free zone allows so none requires browsing mid-take: a MicroFreak patch, two or three Digitakt samples (one of them a field recording or a struck object), all reachable in a beat.
Perform the score, obeying each rule
  • Arm the print track, monitor Auto. ⌘ ⇧ R. Start in silence (R-a).
  • Event 1 — free register, free length, free source. Suppose you choose a low, long MicroFreak drone. Note it: low / long.
  • Silence — one unhurried breath (R-d).
  • Event 2 — the answer rule (R-c) forces high and short. Source still free: a brief metallic Digitakt ping. The register and length were decided by the score; only the timbre was yours.
  • Silence, then Event 3 — answer of event 2, so low and long again. Free source: a field-recorded rumble. End in silence (R-a). Stop the take inside the 3:00–3:30 window.
  • Keep your hands off anything the score didn't delegate. If a sound rings longer than you meant, that is a legal outcome — keep it.
Render and log the choices
  • File → Export Audio/Video. 24-bit WAV named R1.wav.
  • Write a one-line choice log — for each I zone, what you chose this time:
R1 choice log — the free zones only
Event 1 sourceMicroFreak low drone
Event 2 sourceDigitakt metallic ping
Event 3 sourcefield-recorded rumble
Pacing within the breath-silences~4 s, ~6 s
  • The choice log is what makes R2 honest. In R2 you must choose differently in these same zones — not re-run this performance.
Decision check
  • Did I obey every D rule, including the answer-contour and the silence frame?
  • Did I leave the I zones genuinely chosen, not defaulted to habit?
  • Is the take one continuous performance, not a comp of fragments?
  • Is the choice log complete enough that I could deliberately contradict it tomorrow?
Step 03

Realize R2 — same score, different choices

The same page, read again, with deliberately different decisions in every free zone — and, where the rule allows, entirely different sources. The D rules are the controls you hold constant; the I zones are the only thing you change.

Re-read the score cold
  • Open the arrangement named R2. Do not play the R1 take while realizing — consult only the R1 choice log, so you can be sure to choose otherwise.
  • Same gridless setting, same gain target, same monitoring level. Everything except the free zones matches R1, or the delta lies.
Make genuinely different free-zone choices
  • Event 1 — free register/length again. Choose differently: a high, short bowed-glass sample. (Note: this flips the whole contour, since R-c answers relative to whatever event 1 is — and that is fine, the relationship is fixed even though the absolute registers differ.)
  • Event 2 — answer forces low and long. Free source: a sustained MicroFreak Speech-engine vowel.
  • Event 3 — answer forces high and short. Free source: a single struck metal object miked into Input 1.
  • Obey R-a, R-b, R-d exactly as in R1. Three events, silence frame, breath-length gaps, 3:00–3:30.
Render and log
  • 24-bit WAV named R2.wav. Write its own choice log next to R1's.

Decision check
  • Did every free zone get a genuinely different choice from R1?
  • Did I hold every D rule constant — same count, same frame, same answer-logic?
  • Did the gain and monitoring match R1, so a loudness difference would be a real choice and not an accident?
  • Can I hear, already, that this is recognizably the same piece — and audibly a different take?
Step 04

Lay R1 and R2 side by side — read the delta

This table is the lab's product. Map every audible sameness back to the D rule that fixed it, and every difference back to the I zone that freed it. Reading the delta backwards is reading your own rule-set.

Set up the comparison
  • Drop R1 and R2 on two tracks, time-aligned at their starts. A/B them at the marked phones level — do not touch the volume knob between them.
  • Listen three times: once for what they share, once for where they part, once with the score in front of you matching each observation to a line.

Decision checkpoint
  • Can I point to at least one clear sameness with a D rule behind it, and at least one clear difference with an I zone behind it?
  • If either column is empty, the score failed — under-specified (no sameness) or over-specified (no difference). Fix the rule and re-realize before claiming a pass.
  • Did the result match my Step 01 prediction? Where it didn't, that is the lesson — a rule read differently than I intended.
Step 05

Tune the score — taste both failures on purpose

You only know you've found the band between transcription and noise by stepping over both edges. Mis-tune the score deliberately, in both directions, and listen to it break.

loosen a D rule → re-realize ×2 → hear the collapse → pin an I zone → hear the clone → restore & state the band
Under-specify — delete your firmest D
  • Replace R-c (the answer rule) with "do whatever you like." Realize twice, quickly. The two takes now share almost nothing — the score has become a permission slip, not a system. This is the ambiguity / under-specification fault, and you have just heard it.
Over-specify — pin your widest I
  • Replace "any unheard source" with "exactly this MicroFreak preset at A1 for 8 seconds." Realize twice. The takes are now identical — you have written a transcription, and indeterminacy is dead.
Restore and state the band
  • Return to the tuned score. In one sentence, name the band you found: how much freedom keeps the piece recognizable while still letting it vary. For Three Answers: "Fix the count, the frame, and the answer-contour; free the source and the pacing — that is enough identity and enough room."
Step 06

Defend the rule — the pass gate

A pass is not "they sound different." A pass is naming exactly which rule produced the difference between R1 and R2, and which rule produced the sameness — and being right when someone checks against the takes.

Write the defence
  • One paragraph, the form: "The 'any unheard source' rule made R1's events drone/ping/rumble and R2's glass/vowel/metal — that is the difference. The 'three answered events in silence' rules made both feel like the same gesture — that is the sameness."
  • Add the system-diff sentence: "I thought the score would fix X and free Y; the realizations showed it fixed ___ and freed ___." If your prediction held perfectly, say so — but most don't, and the surprise is the point.
The report bundle
  • The score — one page, one idiom, every line tagged D/I, one deliberate wide I zone.
  • R1.wav + its choice log.
  • R2.wav + its choice log, with genuinely different free-zone choices.
  • The delta map — every sameness and difference traced to a named rule.
  • The fault entry from Step 05 — the under- and over-specified re-realizations and the band statement.
  • The written defence + the system-diff sentence.
Pass standard

Minimum: the bundle exists; one sameness and one difference are each traced to a named rule. Solid: every line of the delta map maps to a rule, and the band statement is specific and defensible. Advanced: you can hand the score to another person, predict the delta of their realization in advance, and be right about which features hold.

Final decision check
  • Can I say, in one sentence each, the rule behind the sameness and the rule behind the difference?
  • Is the score the work here — would I be content if someone realized it a third way I never heard?
  • Did I keep the originality constraint: my rules, my realizations, nothing copied from the reference scores?
  • What did the delta teach me that I did not predict in Step 01?
