Lab Note B6-005
The Score · 03 — Decisions

Four idioms, and how much to fix versus leave free

Every choice on this page happens before you touch a sound. First: which idiom does the score take — text, graphic, event-list, or open-form? Second, and harder: for each dimension of the music, do you fix it (determinate) or free it (indeterminate)? Get the idiom and the fix/free split right and the build is almost mechanical. Get them wrong and the two realizations will either be clones or strangers.

The core idea: determinacy is a dial, not a switch

Think of every musical dimension — pitch, register, duration, density, order, timbre, dynamics, timing — as having its own dial that runs from fully fixed to fully free. A score is not "determinate" or "indeterminate"; it sets each dial somewhere. Cage's Music of Changes pins every dial to fixed. Wolff frees almost all of them and fixes only the relationships between events. Most useful scores fix a few dials hard and open a few wide — and the art is choosing which.

The one rule that governs the build

At least one dial must be fixed hard enough to stamp an identity on every realization, and at least one dial must be opened wide enough that two honest realizations genuinely diverge. Fixed dials become the sameness in your delta; free dials become the difference. A score with no fixed dial is a permission slip; a score with no free dial is a transcription.

The four idioms

The idiom is the form your rules take on the page. It shapes what is easy to fix and easy to leave open — choose it to match where you want the freedom to live.

Text / verbal

  • Prose or a numbered rule list — Oliveros, Wolff, much of Fluxus
  • Best when the freedom is conceptual: attention, response, choice of source
  • Easy to fix logic ("after X, do Y"); hard to fix precise pitch or rhythm

Graphic

  • Shapes read as events — Brown, Feldman, Cardew's Treatise
  • Best when the freedom is dimensional: register × time × density laid out spatially
  • You decide the key; the page can fix shape while freeing exact pitch

Event-list

  • An ordered table — like a recipe: order · what · how long · how loud · which free
  • Best when sequence matters and you want the fix/free split explicit per row
  • Maps cleanly onto a DAW arrangement and onto the delta map you fill later

Open-form

  • Fixed modules, free assembly — Lutosławski, Brown's Available Forms, Riley's In C
  • Best when you want rich composed material and guaranteed divergence
  • The order, recurrence, or cueing is the free zone; the blocks themselves are fixed

Choosing the idiom — a decision table

Where do you want the freedom to live?
In how performers respond to each other or to chanceText / verbal — encode the contingency as a sentence
In which pitch or timbre fills a fixed shapeGraphic — fix the contour and band, free the exact content
In a single named dimension, with everything else pinnedEvent-list — one column flagged free, the rest determinate
In the order or recurrence of fully composed chunksOpen-form — fix the modules, free the sequence
You are realizing solo on the rig and want it tractableEvent-list or open-form — both perform cleanly without a second player

The fix/free decision, dimension by dimension

This is the real work. For your chosen idiom, walk each dimension and set its dial. Write D (determinate) or I (indeterminate) against each. The right-hand column is what that choice buys you, and what it costs.

Dimensions and what fixing vs freeing each one does
Pitch / harmonyFix it and the piece has a clear tonal identity across realizations; free it and timbre and gesture carry the recognition instead.
Register / bandA cheap, strong fix — "lowest sound enters last" or "stays in the high band" stamps an identity while leaving exact pitch free.
Duration / timingFix it for a tight, comparable shape; free it (gridless) when you want each realization to breathe differently.
Order / sequenceThe open-form lever. Fix it and you have through-composition; free it and assembly becomes the variable.
Density / count"Exactly three events" is one of the most reliable identity-fixers — easy to obey, audible across takes.
Timbre / source"Choose any source not yet heard" is the classic wide-open I zone — it makes R1 metallic and R2 vocal from one rule.
DynamicsOften best left free unless the piece is about loudness; fixing it can over-constrain without adding identity.
Response logic"React to the previous event" is meta — it doesn't fix or free a dimension, it makes the piece depend on its own unfolding.
  • A real I has range. "Choose any unheard source" is a genuine freedom. "Choose C or C#" is barely one — two realizations will sound the same and you'll mistake an over-specified score for a working one.
  • A firm D survives a stranger. "Begins and ends in silence" reads identically to anyone. "Start quietly" does not — it is ambiguous, and ambiguity is the fault this Lab Note hunts.
  • Fix the cheap dimensions, free the expensive ones. Register, count, and boundary are cheap to fix and stamp identity hard. Timbre and exact pitch are expensive to fix and usually better left free.

How a score encodes a voice

Two composers given the same instruction-writing assignment produce unmistakably different scores — and not because of the sounds, which they never chose. The voice is in the rule-set: in which dimensions they trust the realizer with, in how tightly they pin the rest, in whether they fix relationships (Wolff) or contours (Brown) or attention (Oliveros). When you decide what to fix and what to free, you are not just engineering a piece — you are declaring what you believe music is for. The build will surface this whether you intend it or not; the reflection asks you to read it back.

Indeterminacy is not randomness

A randomizer removes a decision; indeterminacy relocates one — from the composer to the realizer, or to a chance procedure the composer chose. Cage's chance was rigorously designed before a coin was tossed. Wolff's freedoms are precisely bounded by listening rules. The freedom in a good score is fenced; that is what separates it from noise.

Decision summary — fill this before you write

Decision check
  • Which idiom? (text / graphic / event-list / open-form) — and is the freedom you want naturally expressible in it?
  • Which dimension is your hardest D — the one that stamps identity on every realization?
  • Which dimension is your widest I — the one with real range, that R1 and R2 will diverge on?
  • Where does your indeterminacy live: in generating the score (Cage) or in reading it (Wolff, Brown)? It must be the second.
  • Can you predict, in one written sentence, what the two realizations will share and where they will differ? If not, the score is not yet decided.
