The Score · 05 — Score primitives

A vocabulary of instructions you can assemble into a score

A score is built from primitives — single instructions, each of which fixes one thing or frees one thing. This page is the parts bin. Every primitive comes with an example you could paste straight into a score, a note on whether it tends to read as D or I, and what it costs. Build your score by choosing a handful and arranging them; the build page shows the assembly.

How to read this page

Each primitive is a candidate line for your score. The mark D means it usually fixes an outcome (it will show as sameness in your delta); I means it usually frees one (it will show as difference). Some are D/I — they fix one dimension and free another in the same breath. Aim for a score of 4–8 lines with at least one hard D and one wide I.

Boundary primitives — they fix the edges

Cheap, strong identity-fixers · usually D
Begin / end state"Begins and ends in silence." Every realization shares its frame. The single most reliable identity rule.
Event count"Exactly three sound events, no more." Audible across takes, trivial to obey, impossible to misread.
Total duration"The whole piece lasts between 3:00 and 3:30." Fixes scale while leaving internal timing free.
Register band"All sound stays below 200 Hz." Stamps a sonic identity without naming a pitch.
Entrance order"The lowest sound enters last." Fixes architecture; exact sounds stay free.

Choice primitives — they open a real range

Wide-open freedoms · usually I · give them genuine range
Open source"Choose any sound source not yet heard in the piece." The classic wide I — makes R1 metallic, R2 vocal, from one line.
Free pitch in band"Any pitch within the chosen band; it may change or not." Frees content while a boundary rule fixes the band.
Free duration"Hold each sound as long as one comfortable breath, or longer." Riley/Oliveros flavour — the realizer's body sets the clock.
Performer's discretion"Repeat any gesture as many times as feels necessary, then move on." Frees length and recurrence at once.
Open dynamics"Any dynamic, but never twice the same in succession." A freedom with a small constraint riding on it.

Relational primitives — the rule is between events

These are Wolff's territory: they fix nothing about any single sound, only how sounds relate. They produce structure that survives realization while leaving the material wide open. On the rig, realized solo, you obey them by treating each prior recorded event as the cue for the next.

Contingency rules · D in logic, I in content
Response"Each new sound answers the previous one — higher if it was low, longer if it was short." Makes the piece depend on its own unfolding.
Cue / wait"Enter only after a silence you did not plan." Fixes the trigger, frees the timing.
Mirror / contrast"The second half undoes the first, in reverse order." Fixes a relationship across the whole form.
Density coupling"When one layer thickens, another must thin." A conservation law for texture.

Process primitives — do X until a condition, then advance

Riley's territory: a tiny rule that, run long enough, generates a whole piece. Solo-realizable on the rig by stepping the process yourself or by mapping it onto a sequence.

Process rules · D in mechanism, I in when
Ordered advance"Move through these 6 cells in order; repeat each as long as you like before advancing." The In C mechanism, scaled down.
Accumulate"Each pass adds one new layer and never removes one." A one-directional build.
Decay"Each repetition is quieter and shorter than the last until nothing remains." A process with a built-in ending.
Phase"Two identical figures at slightly different lengths, allowed to drift apart and realign." Reich's mechanism as a rule.

Graphic primitives — shapes as instructions

For a graphic idiom, the "lines" of your score are marks on a page plus a key that says how to read them. The key is itself a set of D/I decisions — it declares which spatial dimension maps to which musical one, and what is left unsaid.

Reading conventions you declare in the key
Horizontal = timeLeft-to-right is the clock. Usually D in order, often I in exact rate.
Vertical = registerHigher on the page = higher in pitch/band. Fixes contour, frees exact pitch.
Thickness = dynamic or densityThick mark = loud or many. A clear visual D.
Mark type = articulation / sourceA dot vs a line vs a smear maps to attack, sustain, texture — your choice, stated in the key.
Empty spaceSilence, or freedom — say which. Undeclared empty space is the commonest graphic ambiguity.
  • Cardew's Treatise has no key at all — 193 pages of graphics and not one instruction. That is a legitimate extreme, but for this build you must declare your key, because an unread key is the under-specification fault waiting to happen.

Attention primitives — the Oliveros move

Pauline Oliveros's Sonic Meditations instruct attention and intention rather than action: "listen to the lowest sound you can hear and tune toward it." On the rig you can encode these as rules about what you respond to. They produce the most original results and the hardest-to-read deltas — use one if you want the freedom to live in perception itself.

Attention rules · I in nearly every dimension
Tune toward"Find the lowest pitch present and add a sound a fifth above it." The pitch is determined by what's already there.
Fill the gap"Add sound only where you hear silence." The density self-regulates against the existing recording.
Match and leave"Match a timbre already present, hold it, then remove the original." A handover the realizer hears in real time.

Worked mini-examples — three two-line scores

Text, tight

  • D "Exactly three events; begins and ends in silence."
  • I "Each event is any source not yet heard."
  • Delta: same shape and frame, totally different sounds.

Process, drifting

  • D "Step through 5 register-bands, lowest to highest, in order."
  • I "Hold each band as long as one breath; choose its sound freely."
  • Delta: same ascent, different pacing and timbre.

Relational, alive

  • D "Every sound answers the last: opposite register, opposite length."
  • I "Stop when you have made a sound that answers nothing."
  • Delta: same logic, different conversation and length.
Assembling a score from primitives

Pick one boundary D (the identity), one choice or attention I (the freedom), and optionally one relational or process primitive (the engine that makes it move). Three good lines beat eight muddled ones. The build page takes exactly this kit and writes a full score with it.
