Lab Note B1-004
Metering · 02 — Listen

Pair what you see with what you hear

A meter is useless until its reading means something in your body. Before you measure anything of your own, train the link both ways: make a deliberate fault, look at how it reads, and listen to what that reading sounds like. Do this once for each meter and a number stops being abstract — it becomes a sound you recognise.

The four pairings

Each exercise creates a known fault on purpose, then asks you to connect the meter's behaviour to the sound. Use a finished reference track you trust and one simple test source — a synth pad or a one-bar loop. Loop, don't scrub; readings settle across repetitions.

Pairing 01

A clipped peak — see the red, hear the crackle

Make the fault, then read it
  • Play your loop and raise a track's gain with a Utility until the channel meter's tip lights red and holds at the top.
  • That red is the peak indicator saying you crossed 0 dBFS — the digital ceiling. There is no headroom above it; the signal is being squared off.
  • Now listen. The sound gains a brittle crackle or a hard edge on the loudest hits. That edge is the clip you can see — distortion born the moment the meter went red.
What you learned
  • Red on the channel meter is not "a bit hot" — it is a fault you can hear. Pull the gain back until the tip never reaches the top and the crackle is gone.
  • The fix is provable: the red disappears, the crackle disappears, both at the same level. The meter led; your ear confirmed.
Pairing 02

A 100 Hz boost — see the hump, hear the mud

Make the fault, then read it
  • Put Live's Spectrum on the master. Play the loop and learn its resting shape — a gentle downward tilt, lows on the left, highs on the right.
  • Add an EQ Eight and push a wide +6 dB bell at 100 Hz (or sweep up to 250 Hz for low-mid mud). Watch a broad hump rise on Spectrum exactly where you boosted.
  • Listen: the loop thickens, gets boomy or muddy, loses clarity. The hump you see and the thickness you hear are the same event.
What you learned
  • "Sounds muddy" now has an address. You can point at the frequency on Spectrum, not just feel it.
  • Sweep a narrow boost slowly and a resonant ring will leap out as a tall thin spike — the same trick finds harshness up at 2–4 kHz. The spike is the sound that hurts; cut there and it eases.
Pairing 03

A phase-cancelled fold — see the dip, hear it vanish

Make the fault, then read it
  • On a stereo pad, push a heavy stereo widener hard, or duplicate it, detune the copy a few cents, and pan the pair hard left and right. Watch the correlation needle plunge below 0 toward −1.
  • Negative correlation means left and right are turning into mirror-opposites — content that cancels when summed.
  • Collapse to mono with a Utility at Width 0 %. The widened element thins or disappears, and Spectrum drops into regular comb-shaped dips. The needle warned you; the mono fold proved it.
What you learned
  • This is the one fault headphones hide completely: huge and wide in stereo, gone on a phone or a club's mono sub. Only the meter and the mono switch catch it.
  • Fix it — remove the widener, mono the low end — and the needle climbs back above 0, the element survives the fold. Recovery on the meter is the proof, not "it sounds fine to me".
Pairing 04

A drifting drone — see the cents, hear the beating

Make the fault, then read it
  • Play a sustained MicroFreak drone through Live's Tuner. Detune it slowly until the tuner reads −15 cents flat of its target note.
  • Stack a second voice in tune at the same note. Listen for a slow wah-wah-wah pulse — beating — as the two pitches fight.
  • The tuner shows the gap in cents; the beating is that gap made audible. Pull the drift back to 0 cents and the beating slows, then stops.
What you learned
  • Tuning is a measurement too. A drone that "feels uneasy" is often just a few cents out, and the tuner names it instantly.
  • Beating speed and the cents reading move together. Once you've paired them, you can tune two oscillators by ear and confirm on the tuner — or the reverse.
  • Listen on the rig you'll measure on. The DT 990 Pro is honest about harshness and width, but headphones exaggerate stereo. When the needle and your ears disagree about width, trust the needle.
  • Write each pairing down. One line: the reading, then the sound it made. A meter you can't connect to a sound is a meter you can't act on.
  • Match loudness when you compare. Louder always sounds and looks better. An unmatched A/B lies on every meter and every ear.

The one stolen principle

Carry this into the build

Across all four pairings the same thing is true: the meter saw the fault before — or more reliably than — your ears. A red tip, a hump, a needle below zero, a cents reading. None of them is a matter of taste; each is a fact you can act on and then confirm. That is the only principle you are stealing — read the instrument first, then let your ears agree.

Before you move on
  • I can recognise a clipped peak both as red on the meter and as crackle in the sound.
  • I can point at a low-mid hump on Spectrum and name it as mud I hear.
  • I have watched the correlation needle predict a mono collapse my ears missed.
  • I can read a drone's drift in cents and hear it as beating against an in-tune voice.
  • I have one written sentence: the reading I trust most over my ears is ___.
