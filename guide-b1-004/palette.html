Metering · 05 — Palette

Every meter, and the move it informs

The complete bench, one meter at a time. For each: what it measures, the healthy reading to aim for, and the exact corrective move a bad reading should trigger. Read a meter, find its row, make the move. No guessing.

The whole bench at a glance

Each meter, what it tells you, and the move it informs
Channel / peak meterThe highest sample level in dBFS. Reads clipping risk. Move: if the tip hits red / 0, pull gain down until it never reaches the top.
True-peak (dBTP)The real waveform's peak between samples. Move: if above −1 dBTP, lower output or add a true-peak limiter ceiling at −1.
RMS / averageShort-window energy — how big it feels. Move: use it to balance two parts by body, not by their spiky peaks.
LUFS — integratedOverall perceived loudness of the whole piece. Move: adjust master gain until it hits your target (−14 LUFS).
LUFS — short-termLoudness over the last 3 s. Move: watch it to keep sections even — a chorus shouldn't jump 4 LU over a verse unless you meant it.
LUFS — momentaryLoudness over the last 400 ms. Move: spot a single too-loud hit and tame it.
SpectrumFrequency balance, tilt, resonant peaks. Move: a hump → EQ cut at that frequency; a spike → narrow notch; a thin top → gentle high shelf.
Correlation / phaseMono safety, +1…−1. Move: below 0 → reduce width, mono the low end, find the offending element.
TunerPitch in note + cents. Move: off by more than a few cents → retune the oscillator / drone to 0.
Gain-reduction meterHow many dB a compressor is removing. Move: read it, don't set it blind — too much GR (>6 dB steady) → raise threshold or lower ratio.
M4 input meter + clip LEDHardware level at the converter. Move: clip LED lights → lower the M4 input gain, never the level in Live.

Level meters — the three numbers in detail

Peak vs RMS vs LUFS — when to read which
Peak (dBFS)Reads one instant. Use it only to answer "am I clipping?" Never use it to judge loudness — a sharp drum can peak high yet feel quiet.
True peak (dBTP)Reads the reconstructed waveform. Use it on the master before export. Ceiling −1 dBTP.
RMSReads average energy. Use it to match the perceived size of two sustained parts when balancing a mix.
Integrated LUFSReads the whole piece, ear-weighted. Use it as your one loudness target. The number that matters at export.
Sign convention used on this page

All level readings are negative below the ceiling. 0 dBFS is the top; −14 LUFS is quieter than −9 LUFS. "Hotter" means closer to zero. Your true-peak ceiling is −1 dBTP no matter how loud the LUFS target.

Spectrum — what each shape means

Reading Live's Spectrum
Gentle downward tiltHealthy. Strong lows, falling highs. The target shape for most finished music.
Broad hump at 200–500 HzMud. Everything sounds thick and unclear. Cut a wide few dB there.
Tall narrow spikeA resonance — one frequency ringing. Narrow notch at the spike's frequency.
Hump at 2–5 kHzHarshness / fatigue. A wide gentle cut eases it.
Flat or rising tiltThin, brittle. Often too little low end or too much top.
Steep downward tiltDull, muffled. Lacking air and presence up top.
  • Read the broad shape, not the dancing detail. Spectrum flickers constantly; judge the standing features and the overall tilt, not a single instantaneous frame. Use a slower averaging so the display settles.
  • FFT in one plain line: Spectrum chops the sound into frequency bins and measures the energy in each. More bins (higher FFT size) means finer frequency detail but a slower, laggier display. The default is fine; you are reading shape, not surgical detail.

Correlation — where the needle should live

Reading the phase / correlation meter
+1Mono. Left and right identical. Perfectly safe; where the low end belongs.
+0.3 … +0.9The healthy working band. Width with safety.
0Fully decorrelated. Maximum safe width. Still survives mono.
below 0Warning. Channels turning into opposites; partial cancellation in mono.
−1Total cancellation. Disappears entirely when summed to mono.

The tuner — pitch as a number

Reading Live's Tuner
Note name + 0 centsIn tune. The needle centred. The target for every drone and oscillator.
±5 centsClose enough for most material; the ear rarely flags it alone.
±10–20 centsAudibly off, especially in a sustained drone or a stacked unison. Retune.
Slow beating against a referenceTwo pitches a few cents apart. The beating speed is the size of the gap. Tune until it slows to a stop.
  • Set your reference first. Confirm the tuner is at A = 440 Hz (or whatever your project uses). Everything is measured against that anchor; tune the kick, bass, and drone to one shared root so the low end agrees.

Gain reduction — reading, not setting

  • What the GR meter shows. On a compressor, a small meter that falls as the compressor turns the signal down. A dip of −3 dB means it removed 3 dB at that instant. At rest it reads 0.
  • Read it to confirm intent. Gentle glue is 1–3 dB on the loudest moments. Obvious pumping is 4–6 dB+. If the meter is pinned down constantly, you're crushing — raise the threshold or lower the ratio.
  • Light touch only here. This Lab Note teaches you to read the GR meter, not to dial a compressor. The full treatment of compression lives elsewhere in Block 4 — here you only confirm a number matches your intent.
The palette rule

For any fault, name it on the meter first, then make the single move that row prescribes — and re-read to confirm the move worked. One reading, one move, one confirmation. Chasing a sound by ear across three plugins is the indecision this Lab Note exists to cure.

Before the build
  • I can say what each meter measures and the move a bad reading triggers.
  • I know peak is for clipping and LUFS is for loudness — never swapped.
  • I can name the healthy Spectrum shape and the three faults it reveals.
  • I know the correlation needle should live above 0.
  • I can read the tuner in cents and the GR meter in dB removed.
