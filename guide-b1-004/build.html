Lab Note B1-004
Metering · 06 — The build

Measure one loop, end to end

Now we read the instruments. We take a single eight-bar loop and measure everything about it — its input level, its peak, its loudness, its spectral balance, its phase, its tuning — and we act on each reading with one corrective move, then confirm the move on the meter. Every step ends in a decision check. Keep the setup page and the palette open in other tabs.

Step 01

Create the project and stand up the meters

New set
  • ⌘ N. Tempo 124, signature 4/4. ⌘ , → Audio: device MOTU M4, sample rate 48000 Hz.
  • If you saved the measurement template from the setup page, it is already here. If not, add to the master, in order: Spectrum, a loudness meter (Live's or Youlean), a correlation meter, and a Utility last.
  • Save as meter_b1_004.als.
Confirm the chain reads, doesn't shape
  • Play any sound. Spectrum and the loudness meter should move; the Utility at Width 100 % changes nothing yet.
  • Confirm the correlation meter shows a +1 … 0 … −1 scale and the master strip shows peak in dBFS.
Decision check
  • Are all four meters last in the master chain, after everything else?
  • Is the Utility the very last device — my mono switch?
  • Does nothing in the chain alter the sound until I toggle the Utility?
Step 02

Read the first meter — the M4 input

Set the level at the door
  • Play your eight-bar Digitakt loop. Watch the M4's input meters on channels 3/4, not anything in Live yet.
  • The ladder should ride healthily on the loudest hits and the red clip LED must stay dark. If it flickers, lower the M4 input gain.
  • Record the loop into DT DRUMS, monitor Auto. A clean converter level is the honest start every later reading depends on.
Write the first number
  • Note the loop's input behaviour in your logbook: "M4 in 3/4 peaked mid-ladder, clip LED never lit." That is your first measured fact.
Decision check
  • Did the M4 clip LED stay dark through the loudest bar?
  • Is the recorded clip clean, with headroom, not slammed to the top?
Step 03

Measure the peak — true peak too

Read the channel and master peak
  • Loop DT DRUMS. Read the channel meter's peak hold. Note the highest value in dBFS — e.g. −3.2 dBFS.
  • On the loudness meter, find the true-peak (dBTP) reading. Note it. It will often sit a touch higher than the sample peak — that is the inter-sample overshoot the meter predicts.
Act on the reading
  • If true peak is above −1 dBTP, that's your first corrective move: drop the channel gain (or master output) until it sits at or below −1 dBTP.
  • Re-read. The move is proven only when the meter confirms it: "true peak went from +0.3 to −1.0 dBTP." Write both numbers down.
Decision check
  • Do I have a sample-peak and a true-peak number written down?
  • Is true peak now at or under −1 dBTP?
  • Did I confirm the fix on the meter, not just assume it worked?
Step 04

Measure loudness against your target

Read integrated LUFS
  • Reset the loudness meter and let the loop play for a full pass so the integrated reading settles. Note it — e.g. −18.4 LUFS.
  • Compare to your target from the decisions page: −14 LUFS. This loop is quieter than target by about 4 LU.
Act on the reading — carefully
  • Raise master gain to close the gap, watching that true peak does not cross −1 dBTP. If loudness and peak fight, that tension is the job of a later mastering stage — for now, get the loudness honest and keep peak safe.
  • Glance at short-term LUFS as it plays: it should stay roughly even across the loop, with no single bar leaping out. A momentary spike points at one too-loud hit.
Momentary (400 ms) → widens to → Short-term (3 s) → widens to → Integrated (whole) = your target
Decision check
  • Do I have an integrated-LUFS number, and how far is it from −14?
  • When I corrected loudness, did true peak stay under −1 dBTP?
  • Is short-term loudness even across the loop, or is one bar jumping?
Step 05

Read the spectral balance

Characterize the tilt
  • On Spectrum, watch the broad shape with a slower averaging so it settles. Write down the tilt: downward (healthy), flat (thin), or steep (dull).
  • Look for standing features: a broad 200–500 Hz hump (mud), a tall narrow spike (resonance), a 2–5 kHz rise (harshness).
Act on one reading
What you see → the single move it triggers
Broad hump at ~300 HzWide EQ cut of a few dB at that frequency. Re-read: the hump flattens.
Spike at one frequencyNarrow notch at the spike. Re-read: the spike drops to its neighbours' height.
Thin, rising tiltGentle high-shelf down or low-shelf up. Re-read: the tilt slopes downward again.
  • Make one move, justified by the reading, and confirm it on Spectrum. Write it as "cut 3 dB at 300 Hz; hump gone." That sentence is the proof.
Decision check
  • Can I name the loop's tilt in one word?
  • Did I name a fault as a frequency, then cut there, then confirm it changed?
  • Did I resist cutting by ear without a reading to point at?
Step 06

Read phase, then fold to mono

Read the correlation needle
  • Watch the correlation meter across the loop. Note where the needle lives and its lowest dip — e.g. "lives at +0.6, dips to +0.2."
  • If it ever drops below 0, an element is going out of phase. Solo tracks to find which one — usually a widened pad or a stereo effect.
Prove it with the mono switch
  • Toggle the Utility to Width 0 %. Watch Spectrum and listen. If anything thins, drops out, or shows comb-shaped dips, that element was relying on phase that doesn't survive the fold.
  • Act: reduce that element's width, or mono everything below 120 Hz, until the needle climbs above 0 and nothing vanishes in the fold. Re-read in mono to confirm recovery.
Read needle → Fold to mono → See what vanishes → Narrow / mono low end → Confirm recovery
Decision check
  • Do I have a correlation number — where it lives and its lowest dip?
  • Did the mono fold lose anything? If so, did I fix it and re-confirm?
  • Is the low end mono and the needle above 0?
Step 07

Tune the drone against the reference

Measure the pitch
  • Solo the MicroFreak drone through Live's Tuner. Confirm the tuner's reference is A = 440 Hz.
  • Read the note name and the cents offset. If it reads, say, A −12 cents, the drone is flat of its target by 12 cents.
Act and confirm by ear too
  • Retune the oscillator until the tuner reads 0 cents. Then stack it under the kick's tuned fundamental: the slow beating you may have heard should slow and stop as the cents close to zero.
  • Tune the kick, bass, and drone to one shared root so the low end agrees rather than fights. Pitch is just another reading you act on.
Decision check
  • Does the tuner read within a few cents of 0 on the drone?
  • Did the beating against the in-tune voice slow as the reading closed to zero?
  • Do the low-end parts share one root?
Step 08

Read gain reduction on a compressor

Watch the GR meter, don't dial blind
  • Put a Compressor on the drum bus for gentle glue. Lower the threshold until the gain-reduction meter dips 1–3 dB on the loudest hits and returns to 0 between them.
  • Read the number, don't guess it: "GR dipping to −2 dB on the kick." If the meter is pinned down at −6 dB+ constantly, you're crushing — raise the threshold or lower the ratio until it breathes.
Light touch only
  • This Lab Note is about reading the meter, not designing the compression. A dip you can see and name is the goal; the deep craft of compression is Block 4's job. Confirm the GR matches your intent and move on.
Decision check
  • Can I state the gain reduction as a number of dB removed?
  • Does the GR meter return to 0 between hits, or is it pinned?
  • Does the reading match what I intended — glue, not crush?

Decision check
  • Is every row filled with a real number, not a vibe?
  • Does every fix have a before and an after reading?
  • Could someone else reproduce my corrections from this sheet alone?
Step 10

Make one corrective move and prove it

Pick the worst reading and fix it for real
  • From the sheet, choose the single reading furthest out of bounds — the true peak above −1, the 300 Hz mud, the needle below 0, or the drone 12 cents flat.
  • Make exactly one move to correct it. State the move as a sentence with both numbers: "Cut 3 dB at 300 Hz; the hump on Spectrum flattened and the loop reads clearer."
Confirm everywhere
  • Re-read the affected meter to prove the move worked. Then glance at the others to confirm you didn't break a second reading fixing the first — e.g. a loudness boost that pushed true peak over the line.
  • Listen at your fixed monitoring level. Your ears should now agree with the meter — the proof that measurement led and hearing followed.
Final decision check
  • Did I justify the move with a reading, not a feeling?
  • Can I state the before and after number for the thing I changed?
  • Did the fix hold without knocking another meter out of bounds?
