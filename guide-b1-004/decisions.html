Metering · 03 — Decisions

The theory of metering, and the choices it forces

A meter only helps if you know what it measures and which one to trust for a given job. This page teaches each meter from zero, one at a time, and ends in three decisions you make before you measure: your loudness target, your mono-check policy, and which reading wins when two disagree.

Why three different level meters exist

Peak — the highest sample

  • The single loudest instant in the signal
  • Tells you about clipping, not loudness
  • Measured in dBFS, ceiling at 0

RMS — the average

  • Energy averaged over a short window
  • Tracks body and sustain, ignores spikes
  • Closer to "how big does this feel"

LUFS — perceived loudness

  • Average weighted for how the ear hears
  • The number streaming platforms judge you by
  • Your real loudness target
Principle

Peak protects you from clipping. RMS and LUFS tell you how loud it actually feels. A track can peak at −0.1 dBFS and still feel quiet, because peak measures one instant and loudness measures the whole. You need both meters because they answer different questions.

The unit you measure in: dBFS, dBTP, and LUFS

  • dBFS — decibels full scale. The digital scale. 0 dBFS is the hard ceiling; everything below is negative. −6 dBFS is half the ceiling's voltage, −12 a comfortable working level. You never want a sustained signal kissing 0.
  • dBTP — true peak. A sample meter reads the dots; the real analogue waveform between the dots can rise higher when reconstructed. A true-peak meter predicts that overshoot. A file that reads −0.1 dBFS on samples can be +0.4 dBTP in the real world — and clip a converter or a lossy encoder. Aim for −1 dBTP as a safe ceiling.
  • LUFS — loudness units full scale. A loudness average designed to match human hearing. Momentary covers the last 400 ms, short-term the last 3 s, and integrated the whole measured span. Integrated LUFS is the one number that says how loud your finished piece is overall.

Decision 1 — your loudness target

Concept 01

Choosing a number to build toward

The mechanism
  • Streaming platforms normalise everything to roughly −14 LUFS integrated. Push louder than that and they simply turn you down — so the extra crushing buys nothing but lost dynamics.
  • For a working mixdown before any mastering, aim lower: around −16 to −14 LUFS integrated with true peak under −1 dBTP. That leaves headroom for a mastering stage to do its job.
  • Club and DJ-pool techno often sits hotter and more dynamic, but the discipline is identical: pick a number first, then mix toward it, rather than turning up until it feels loud.
Loudness targets and what they're for
−16 LUFSDynamic working mix. Plenty of room for mastering.
−14 LUFSThe streaming reference. A sensible finished-track target.
−9 to −7 LUFSLoud club master. Hot, dense, far less dynamic range.
True peak−1 dBTP ceiling regardless of loudness target.
The decision
  • Write your integrated-LUFS target down before you touch a fader. For this Lab Note: −14 LUFS integrated, −1 dBTP ceiling.
  • A target you wrote down is a destination. "As loud as it'll go" is not a target — it is the absence of one.

Decision 2 — your mono-check policy

Correlation near +1

  • Left and right are nearly identical
  • Mono-safe — survives any fold
  • Where the low end must always live

Correlation below 0

  • Left and right turning into opposites
  • Cancels on mono sum — disappears on a phone
  • A fault to find before it ships, not after
  • The correlation meter in one line: a needle scoring how alike the two channels are. +1 identical and mono-safe; 0 unrelated and still safe; −1 mirror-opposites that cancel to silence in mono.
  • The policy to decide now: check mono on every wide element as you place it, not at the end. Keep everything below roughly 120 Hz mono. The needle should live above 0 and never park below it.
  • Why this is non-negotiable for techno: club PAs sum the sub to mono. A bass spread wide in headphones can lose half its power on the floor. The correlation meter is the only thing that warns you in advance.

Decision 3 — which reading wins when two disagree

The distinction that decides everything

Your ears, a peak meter, a loudness meter, and a spectrum can all point at the same moment and say different things. Decide in advance who has the final word for each job — and it is almost never your ears, because your ears are the thing the meters exist to correct.

Trust your ears for

  • Whether a sound is musically right
  • Arrangement, groove, emotional weight
  • The final yes / no on taste

Trust the meter for

  • Level, clipping, and loudness — always
  • Mono safety and phase — always
  • Tuning and spectral balance — when in doubt
  • The rule: measurement leads, hearing verifies. Read the instrument, form a hypothesis, then listen to confirm. When the two clash on anything technical — level, phase, tuning — the meter wins and you go re-train your ear against it.
  • The one trap: matched loudness. An A/B at different volumes makes the louder side win on every meter and every ear. Level-match first, then judge — otherwise both your ears and your meters are lying to you. See Lab Note B1-002 — Unity / gain for the gain discipline this rests on.
Decisions locked before setup
  • I know peak, RMS, and LUFS measure three different things.
  • My integrated-loudness target is written down: −14 LUFS, −1 dBTP ceiling.
  • My mono-check policy is "every wide element as I place it; low end stays mono".
  • I have decided the meter wins over my ears on level, phase, and tuning.
  • I will only ever compare at matched loudness.
