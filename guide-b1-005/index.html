Lab Note B1-005
Subtractive · 01 — Overview

One oscillator, one filter, one voice — built from an init patch

A single-topic deep reference. One raw tone, one filter that takes pieces away, one shape over time — and then the same voice left to run with no clock under it. This Lab Note teaches subtractive synthesis the way you learn an instrument: one control at a time, hearing only that control, until a single voice can hold a room on its own.

The one principle

Principle

Start from a raw oscillator and take things away. Subtractive synthesis is not building a sound up from parts — it is carving a sound out of a buzzing tone by removing what you do not want. Learn each tool that does the removing, one at a time, and you can build any voice you can name.

Everything in this Lab Note is downstream of that sentence. The init patch, the waveform, the cutoff, the resonance, the envelope, the LFO — each is just a way of stating, precisely, what a single voice is doing. Name every control doing the work, and a sound you stumbled onto becomes a sound you can rebuild from zero.

What you'll build

One self-evolving voice

  • One synth voice — one oscillator, one filter, one amp — that changes for two minutes with nothing added
  • Made on the Arturia MicroFreak, with Ableton's Analog or Operator as a software fallback
  • Fully original — no preset, no borrowed patch; the voice is yours, built from an init patch you can name in full

The skills you'll practice

  • Reaching an init patch — one raw oscillator, everything else neutral
  • Isolating cutoff, resonance, and filter type and hearing each alone
  • The amp envelope (A·D·S·R) and routing an envelope to the filter
  • One slow LFO for life, and removing the clock to make a free-running drone

Why one voice, one control at a time

The beginner instinct

  • Load a preset, turn ten knobs at once, and hope something good falls out
  • Add a second oscillator, a sub, a chord, an effect — reach for more when a sound feels thin
  • Treat the synth as a mystery box of happy accidents you cannot reproduce

The opposite discipline

  • Start from an init patch where you can hear the raw oscillator and nothing else
  • Move one control, listen only to that control, then move the next — never two at once
  • Make the one voice evolve from the inside; reach for the mod matrix before you reach for a second sound

The skill transfers everywhere. Every analog, virtual-analog, and most digital synths share this signal flow — oscillator into filter into amp, with envelopes and an LFO moving them. Learn it on one voice and you can sit at any synth, init it, and build the sound in your head one control at a time.

The thesis of this note

Do less, but make the one element evolve. This is the companion to Block 1's One Oscillator, One Filter and One Voice, No Clock. There the rule was "when you can't add anything new, find the variation inside the sound itself." Here we slow down and learn the controls that variation lives in — and then we take the clock away entirely.

How to read this Lab Note

Work the pages in order. Listen trains your ear on what each isolated control does, and on the difference between a static voice and an evolving one. Decisions is the discipline of choosing few — which one waveform, which one filter mode, which one modulator. Setup wires the MicroFreak from init into a clean, monitored signal. Palette hands you the small set of moves on one voice. The build is the long, exact, step-by-step. Reflection turns the result into one carry-forward rule and one proof gate.
