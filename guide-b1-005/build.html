Lab Note B1-005
Subtractive · 06 — The build

Build one evolving voice, then remove the clock

Now we touch the gear. Every step is small, every step moves one control at a time, every step has a decision checkpoint. We start from a true init patch, add the oscillator, then the filter, then the envelope, then one modulator — hearing each alone — until a single voice evolves on its own. Then we take the clock away and make it a self-sustaining drone. Keep the setup page and the palette open in other tabs.

Step 01

Reach a true init patch

Clear the bench and the patch
  • On the bench: MicroFreak, M4, headphones, Live — nothing else. Physically set aside any second synth or drum machine. The constraint starts at the wall.
  • New Live set saved as B1-005-subtractive. One audio track, Audio From → Ext. In → 1, mono. No effects on the track.
  • On the MicroFreak, follow the init checklist from Setup: one engine, filter wide open, neutral envelope, no modulation, no glide, paraphonic off.
Confirm you hear the raw oscillator alone
  • Hold one low note. You should hear a steady, unshaped buzzing tone — no movement, no brightness sweep, no shaping.
  • If anything moves on its own, a routing is still live. Clear the Matrix and zero the LFO and Cycling Envelope until the tone is dead still.
  • Set the M4 gain on this steady tone by your B1-002 / B1-003 method. Peak target −15 dBFS or lower, read on the body of the tone.
Decision check
  • Do I hear only a raw, still oscillator — nothing moving, nothing shaping?
  • Is the Matrix empty and every modulator at zero?
  • Is the gain set on the settled tone, with headroom to spare?
Step 02

The oscillator — choose one raw tone

Pick one engine and commit
  • Audition a few engines briefly, then choose one and write its name in your log. After this step the engine knob does not move again.
  • Choose by how much there is to carve: a saw or bright virtual-analog engine if you want the filter to have work to do; a triangle or soft engine if you want a gentle tone the filter barely touches.
  • Set Wave and Timbre to a plain mid position. You want a clean raw tone to start carving — not an already-shaped one.
Hear pitch and register
  • Play the same note an octave low, then mid, then high. This one tone must work as a bass low and as a lead high. If it only works in one register, adjust Wave until both read.
  • Do not touch the filter yet. You are judging the raw oscillator alone — the rest of the build is what you remove from it.
Decision check
  • Have I committed to one engine and logged its name?
  • Does the raw tone read as bass low and lead high?
  • Is the filter still wide open — am I truly hearing the oscillator alone?
Step 03

The filter — cutoff, then resonance, then type

Cutoff alone — hear the subtraction
  • Hold one note. Sweep only the cutoff from fully open down to nearly closed and back. Hear the top harmonics being removed and returned. Say out loud "I am subtracting the highs."
  • Park the cutoff where the tone is warm but not muffled — around 11 o'clock for Patch A. This is your resting timbre.
  • Move nothing else. If a second thing changed, your hand drifted — reset the cutoff and sweep again.
Resonance alone — add the ring
  • Cutoff parked, raise only resonance from zero. A whistle appears at the cutoff and grows. Stop where it adds character without screaming — around 15 % for a pad.
  • For curiosity, push resonance near maximum and sweep the cutoff: hear it sing its own note. Then return to your chosen low value. You now know both ends.
Filter type alone — choose the survivor
Switch the mode and hold each — pick one
Low-pass (LP)Keeps the lows. Warm, the default. Choose this unless you have a reason not to.
Band-pass (BP)Keeps a middle slice. Small, focused, telephone-like.
High-pass (HP)Keeps the highs. Thin and airy; removes weight.
  • Switch mode, hold a note in each, and commit to one. For this note, LP unless you are deliberately building an airy voice. Once chosen, do not switch mid-voice — that is changing instruments.
Decision check
  • Can I sweep cutoff and hear only the highs leaving and returning?
  • Is resonance set for character, not for a scream I can't sit with for two minutes?
  • Have I committed to one filter mode?
Step 04

The amp envelope — shape the note in time

Attack alone, then release alone
  • Play short repeated notes. Lengthen only the attack: the note goes from a sharp start to a gradual fade-in. Set it where the start suits your voice — fast for a pluck, slow for a pad.
  • Now lengthen only the release: after you let go, the note fades instead of cutting off. Set it so the note leaves cleanly with no click.
  • Each of attack and release changes one edge of the note. Hear them separately so you own the difference between a stab and a drone.
Decay and sustain — the body
  • For a sustained pad: Sustain high (~70 %), Decay medium — the note holds while you hold the key.
  • For a stab: Sustain 0, Decay short — the note falls away even while held. This is Patch B.
  • Decide now which this voice is. The rest of the build assumes a sustained voice heading toward a drone; if you are building the stab, keep that envelope and follow along.
Decision check
  • Does the note arrive the way I want — sharp or gradual — from attack alone?
  • Does it leave cleanly with no click, from release alone?
  • Have I decided stab or sustained, and set decay/sustain to match?
Step 05

Route the envelope to the filter — a moving timbre

The single most useful routing
  • Lower the resting cutoff a little. In the Matrix, route the filter envelope to Cutoff and raise the amount.
  • Now each note opens the filter on its attack and closes it on its decay — the note is bright at its start and darker as it falls. The timbre moves with every note, from one envelope, with no second sound.
  • Sweep only the envelope amount and hear the depth of the bloom change. This is the core of the plucked-stab voice and a powerful move on a pad too.
Oscillator → Filter (cutoff low) → Amp ▲ envelope opens cutoff ▲ Filter envelope
Decision check
  • Does each note bloom bright then close, from the filter envelope alone?
  • Can I change the depth of that bloom with one knob — the envelope amount?
  • Is this still one voice, with no second oscillator added?
Step 06

One LFO — give the voice life with no hands

Add one slow LFO to cutoff
  • In the Matrix, route one LFO to Cutoff. Set the depth small at first.
  • Sweep only the LFO rate: slow makes a long drift, fast makes a shimmer. For this note, set it very slow — one cycle over several seconds — so it reads as the tone breathing, not as a tremolo.
  • Sweep only the LFO depth: this sets how far the cutoff travels. Wide enough to be felt, never so wide it pumps.
Hold the two-minute test
  • Hold one note for two full minutes with the LFO running. Set a timer. The voice should stay alive the whole time because the cutoff is always drifting — something is always changing.
  • Zero the LFO depth for ten seconds and hold the note: it dies, the ear stops tracking it. Return the depth. The difference between dead and alive is your one modulator doing the work.
  • If you used the filter envelope as your modulator in Step 5, an LFO here is optional — but for the drone half you will want the LFO, because the envelope only fires when a note is struck.
Decision check
  • Does the held note stay interesting for two minutes with the LFO on?
  • Did it clearly go dead when I zeroed the LFO depth?
  • Is the rate slow enough to read as breathing, not tremolo?
Step 07

Record the evolving voice in three roles

Print the voice low, mid, high
  • Arm the audio track, monitor Auto. Confirm the gain still peaks at −15 dBFS or lower on the body of the tone.
  • Record three passes of the same voice: one low (bass role), one mid, one high (lead role). Same patch every time — only the register changes.
  • Let the LFO run untouched through each pass. The point is that the same voice plays more than one role because register and the moving cutoff changed — not because anything was added.
  • Rename the clips PASS-LOW, PASS-MID, PASS-HIGH.
Find the percussion in the same patch (optional)
  • Raise resonance to ~85 %, set Attack 0, Decay very short, Sustain 0. Play staccato low notes — clicks and thumps from the same voice.
  • Record a short pass of this if you want rhythm in the sequenced half. Log both settings groups so you can switch back to the pad quickly.
Decision check
  • Do the three passes sound like one voice playing three roles?
  • Did the gain hold — no clipping as the LFO pushed the level up?
  • If I made the percussion, can I switch the patch to it and back from memory?
Step 08

Remove the clock — slow every edge

Turn the voice into a drone
  • Stop the transport. There is no sequencer and no MIDI clip from here on — notes are held by hand or by the MicroFreak's Hold button.
  • Lengthen the attack to 2 s or more, sustain to full, release long. The note must fade in gradually and never click on or off.
  • Free the LFO: set it unsynced, one cycle per 15–20 seconds, small depth. With the clock gone, this slow drift is the only motion the tone has — it must keep moving on its own.
  • Hold a note for two minutes and confirm the tone keeps evolving with your hands off. If it sits still, widen the LFO depth or slow it further until the drift is felt.
The thesis, made literal

Do less, but make the one element evolve. With the clock gone there is nowhere to hide. If the voice does not move on its own — beating, slow drift, a swell — the piece is silence with a tone in it. Every minute of interest now comes from inside the patch.

Decision check
  • Does the note fade in with no click and never cut off sharply?
  • Is the LFO free-running and slow enough to drift over tens of seconds?
  • Does the tone keep evolving for two minutes with my hands off?
Step 09

Build the rhythm from beating — no grid

Detune a second held pass
  • Record a held drone pass at your root note. Then play it back through the monitors and record a second held pass a few cents detuned from the first.
  • Nudge the MicroFreak's fine-tune until you hear a slow beating pulse — two near-identical pitches pushing in and out of phase. Aim for roughly one pulse per 1–3 seconds.
  • Write the beat rate down in cycles per second. The detune amount is the rate: tones 1 Hz apart beat once per second. This pulse is the drone's rhythm, made with no clock at all.
Set the gain on the body, again
  • Re-check the gain on the settled sustain, not the swell-in. Two held tones together are hotter than one — confirm the pair still peaks at −15 dBFS or lower.
  • Take your headphones off for a moment. Sustained tones tire your ears fast; judge the beating with fresh ears, not after twenty minutes of staring at it.
Decision check
  • Do the two detuned passes beat at a slow, musical rate I can name?
  • Is the gain set on the body of the combined tone, with headroom?
  • Is the rhythm coming from beating, not from any hidden grid?
Step 10

Compose two minutes with level alone

Plan the volume story on paper first
  • With no clock and one voice, structure comes from raising and lowering level — no cuts, no mutes, no new sounds. Write the arc before you ride a single fader.
  • A starting shape: low drone alone → low + mid detuned, beating surfaces → high pass blooms over the top → back down to the low alone. Every move spans at least 30 seconds.
  • Ride or draw the fades in Live. Nothing faster than thirty seconds — a fast fade reads as an edit, and there are no edits in a drone.
A two-minute drone shape — a starting point, not a rule
0:00–0:30Low drone alone, fading in. The LFO drifts the cutoff.
0:30–1:00Mid detuned pass rises under it — the beating pulse appears.
1:00–1:30High pass blooms over the top; the voice is at its fullest.
1:30–2:00Level falls away one pass at a time, back to the low alone, then out.
Decision check
  • Is every change a level move, with no cut and no new element?
  • Does each fade span at least thirty seconds?
  • Do the three things carrying time — beating, slow drift, level — all read?
Step 11

Render and listen away from the screen

Render both versions
  • If you built the sequenced evolving voice too, render it as B1-005-sequenced.wav. Render the drone as B1-005-drone.wav. WAV, 24-bit, Normalize OFF.
  • File → Collect All and Save. Save the MicroFreak patch and your log of every control position — the voice now exists as a patch you can recall and rebuild.
A/B the two kinds of time
  • Play the sequenced version and the drone back to back at matched loudness. The difference between them is exactly what the clock had been doing for you.
  • Write three sentences: what the grid gave the sequenced version, and what beating, drift, and level had to replace it in the drone.
  • Listen to the drone away from the timeline — on a phone, walking, anywhere you cannot see the screen. A held tone reveals its faults when you stop watching the waveform.
Final decision check
  • Does the drone hold two minutes with one voice and no clock?
  • Can I name every control doing the work, from the engine to the LFO rate?
  • Did I make the one element evolve, instead of reaching for a second?
