Lab Note B1-005
Subtractive · 05 — Palette

The small set of moves on one voice

Three init-patch starting points, the exact control positions for each, and the handful of moves that make one voice evolve — cutoff, resonance, envelope amount, LFO rate and depth. Steal a starting patch, then move one control at a time. Start from real positions, not from "it sounded good".

Reading the positions

Convention used on this page

Knob positions are given as clock positions or percentages — 9 o'clock is low, 12 o'clock is middle, 3 o'clock is high. These are starting points to hear, not laws. The discipline is to set them, then move one at a time and listen to only that one.

The five moves — the whole vocabulary on one voice

Every move you make in this note, and what each one does
CutoffOpen or close the filter. Brighter ↔ darker. The most-used move; subtraction itself.
ResonanceAdd or remove ring at the cutoff. Character ↔ restraint; at the extreme, a click.
Envelope amount (→ filter)How far each note's envelope opens the cutoff. A still timbre ↔ a moving one.
LFO rateHow fast the automatic wobble cycles. Slow drift ↔ fast shimmer.
LFO depthHow far the wobble travels. Barely-there ↔ wide sweep.
  • That is the entire palette. Five moves on three blocks. Everything in the build — bass, lead, drone, the percussion hiding in the patch — is some combination of these five, plus the choice of note and octave. If you ever want a sixth move, the answer is almost always a deeper use of one of these five.

Patch A — The evolving pad (the default)

A warm tone with a slow filter LFO for life. The all-purpose starting voice — works as bass low, lead high, and holds two minutes on its own. Start here.

Patch A — control positions from init
Engine / waveSaw or a bright virtual-analog engine · Wave ~30 %
FilterLP · Cutoff 11 o'clock · Resonance 15 %
Amp envelopeAttack fast · Decay medium · Sustain ~70 % · Release medium
ModulatorOne LFO → Cutoff · rate very slow (one cycle per several seconds) · depth small
Glide / Paraphonic0 · OFF
  • One sentence: "Saw into a half-open low-pass, a touch of resonance, with one slow LFO breathing the cutoff." If you can say that, it is a patch you own, not one you found.

Patch B — The plucked stab

The same blocks, shaped to a short, bright attack that darkens. The filter envelope does the work — each note blooms open and closes. Good as a sequenced bass or a rhythmic lead.

Patch B — control positions from init
Engine / waveSaw or square · Wave to taste
FilterLP · Cutoff 9 o'clock (low at rest) · Resonance 30 %
Amp envelopeAttack 0 · Decay short · Sustain 0 · Release short — a stab
ModulatorFilter envelope → Cutoff · amount high · each note opens bright then closes
MovementOptional one LFO → Cutoff at low depth for slow drift between stabs
  • The move that defines it: the filter envelope amount. With the cutoff low at rest and the envelope opening it on each note, every stab is bright at its start and dark by its end — a moving timbre from one envelope, no second sound.

Patch C — The free-running drone (no clock)

The same voice with every edge removed and the clock gone. A slow swell that never clicks on or off, kept alive by a free LFO and by beating against a second held note. This is the Lab 004b voice.

Patch C — control positions from init
Engine / waveTriangle or soft saw · gentle raw tone
FilterLP · Cutoff 12 o'clock · Resonance 10 % — restraint, no scream over two minutes
Amp envelopeAttack 2 s+ · Sustain full · Release long — fades in, never clicks off
ModulatorOne free (unsynced) LFO → Cutoff or Timbre · one cycle per 15–20 s · small depth
BeatingRecord a second held pass tuned a few cents away · slow pulse at roughly one per 1–3 seconds
Held byHold button or hand — no sequencer, no grid
The drone's three replacements for the clock

With no grid, three things carry time: beating (two detuned tones pulsing — the rhythm), slow movement (the free LFO drifting the timbre), and volume (the swell and any fader ride). If the drone bores you, one of these three is missing. Name which.

The percussion hiding in the same patch

One voice, turned into a click or thump
Resonance~85 % — the filter rings hard at the cutoff
Amp envelopeAttack 0 · Decay very short · Sustain 0 — a single tick of sound
CutoffBecomes the drum's pitch — tune it to taste
How to play itStaccato low notes give thumps; higher notes give clicks
  • This is not an exception — it is proof. The same three blocks that make a pad make a percussion hit, with only resonance and the envelope changed. If you can switch your voice between Patch A and this in a few moves, you have proven you understand what each control does.

The move-by-move cheat-sheet

What to reach for when the voice needs something
Feels dullOpen the cutoff a little, or raise the filter envelope amount. Do not add an oscillator.
Feels lifeless / staticAdd or widen the LFO depth, or slow the LFO rate so the drift is felt. Movement, not a new part.
Feels harsh / whistlingLower the resonance. The ring was screaming, not singing.
Feels too sharp at the startLengthen the attack. The note was clicking on.
Feels like it stops deadLengthen the release. The note was clicking off.
Needs rhythm with no clockDetune a second held tone for beating. Rhythm without a grid.
The palette rule

Pick one patch and commit. Mutating it — Patch A into Patch C by slowing the envelope and freeing the LFO — is composition. Hopping between patches because none "feels right" is the indecision this Lab Note exists to cure. One voice, five moves, two minutes.

Before the build
  • I have chosen one starting patch (A, B, or C).
  • I have written the position of every control from init.
  • I know my five moves and that everything comes from them.
  • I can name my one modulation source — filter envelope or one LFO.
  • I know how to turn the same patch into a percussion hit, and back.
