Lab Note B2-002
Concrète · 04 — Setup

Capture rig & editing session

Two halves of one workflow: a capture rig that gets clean material into files, and an Ableton session built to dissect those files into sound-objects. Every gain stage and every track is spelled out — get this right and the build page is pure listening.

Handheld field recorder

  • Stereo built-in mics for ambiences and field captures
  • Records 24-bit / 48 kHz WAV to card — your portable rig
  • Line/mic input for the contact mic when away from the desk

Contact (piezo) mic

  • Hears vibration through solid objects, not air
  • Turns any object into a sound-source — metal, wood, glass
  • High output impedance; needs a preamp or recorder line in

Ableton Live 12 Suite

  • The editing brain — imports, slices, classifies, montages
  • Warp off; this is sample-accurate audio surgery
  • Granular, spectral, and convolution tools for transformation

MOTU M4

  • 4-in / 4-out USB-C interface for desk captures
  • Input 1: front combo preamp with gain — the contact mic lands here
  • 48 V phantom available for a condenser if you have one

Beyerdynamic DT 990 Pro

  • Open-back, 250 Ω — reveals grain and room tone
  • Honest about detail; the 7–10 kHz lift exaggerates hiss
  • Your editing monitor — check decisions on a phone speaker too

Wind protection + a quiet hour

  • A foam or fur windshield — wind ruins more field takes than anything
  • A genuinely quiet recording window for low-level objects
  • Headphones on the recorder so you monitor while capturing

The capture signal path

Two capture routes, one destination: clean 24-bit WAVs in a project folder. Field captures go through the recorder; desk captures go through the M4. Decide which route a sound needs before you raise a level.

Route A — field / portable

Source → air → Recorder stereo mics → SD card (WAV) → copy → Ableton project

Route B — desk / contact mic

Object → vibration → Contact mic → TS → M4 In 1 → USB-C → Ableton (record)
Recommended approach

Capture everything to disk first, transform later. Never process a sound on the way in — no recorder EQ, no reverb, no "scene" presets. A flat, clean, slightly-too-quiet recording is infinitely more useful than a coloured one. You can always add character; you can't remove it.

The golden rule of capture

Record quiet, in 24-bit, with the mic close. Headroom is free; a re-shoot of a once-only sound is not.

  • Contact mics hear only what touches them. They reject the room almost entirely — which is why a quiet, isolated object recorded by contact can be cut clean with no background to fight. Press, tap, scrape, bow; every contact is a different object.
  • Handling noise is the enemy. The cable transmits every bump straight into the element. Tape the cable down and keep your hands off it during a take.
  • Mind the input type. A bare piezo into a low-impedance mic input sounds thin and harsh — use a Hi-Z / instrument input or a buffer pedal so the low end survives.

The editing session

Build the Ableton set once, before importing anything. This session is a workbench, not a song — it is organised by the role a sound plays, not by instrument.

Ableton preferences for concrète editing
Sample Rate48000 Hz — match your recorder so nothing resamples on import
Bit Depth (record)24-bit WAV
Buffer Size256 for editing; 512 for the mix pass
Warp defaultOFF — Auto-Warp would time-stretch objects and smear their attacks
Auto-Warp Long SamplesOFF — a sound-object's real duration is part of its identity
Time Ruler FormatTime (m:ss) — the piece is shaped in seconds, not bars
TrackHoldsWhy it's separate

Before you import a single file
  • Is Warp off by default? Confirm — one warped object will undo the sample-accuracy the whole method depends on.
  • Is the project saved into a dedicated folder, with Collect All and Save planned for session end? Concrète projects scatter dozens of source files; lose one and the montage breaks.
  • Is every track named by role, not by source? You are organising by what a sound does, never by what made it.

Capture targets & texture

Aim to come back with 15–25 short objects and 3–5 longer textures. More is fine; fewer than that starves the montage.
