Lab Note B2-002
Concrète · 06 — The build

Step-by-step: from capture to finished acousmatic piece

The full method, in order: capture, classify, transform, montage, gesture, mix, finish. Each step is small and ends in a decision checkpoint. Keep the setup page open for the rig and the session, and the palette open for your catalogue — this page assumes both.

Step 01

Capture the field — ambiences and scenes

Plan the hunt before you leave the desk
  • Write the spine sentence from the Decisions page on a card. Every recording you make should help travel that axis — e.g. "noisy → pitched" sends you hunting for both broadband fields and resonant objects.
  • Target 3–5 textures on this pass: a broadband ambience, a low rumble, a moving water/granular bed, and one quiet "room tone" you can hide under everything.
  • Fit the windshield. Wind is the single biggest killer of usable field takes, and it is unfixable after the fact.
Record long, record still
  • Set the recorder to 24-bit / 48 kHz, gain so peaks sit near -12 dBFS. Monitor on headphones — you cannot fix what you didn't hear at the source.
  • Record each texture for at least 90 seconds unbroken. A texture needs length so you can later find the calmest, most loopable 8 seconds inside it.
  • Hold the recorder dead still, or rest it on a stable surface. Handling noise is low-frequency rumble that contaminates the whole take.
  • Slate each take: say the location and intent quietly at the head. "Vent drone, pipe, take one." Future-you will not remember.
Decision check
  • Does each texture survive being played in isolation for 20 seconds without a distracting event (a car horn, a voice)? If not, re-record or earmark the clean section.
  • Do your textures cover both ends of the spine — at least one noisy, at least one pitched/resonant?
  • Is the noise floor low enough that you can raise the file 12 dB without hiss taking over? Concrète is often quiet; the floor matters.
Step 02

Capture objects — the contact-mic session

Set up Route B
  • Contact mic → M4 Input 1 (Hi-Z / instrument input if available), one short TS cable. Tape the cable down so handling noise stays out.
  • In Ableton, arm a track set to Ext. In 1 (mono), Monitor In while auditioning, Auto when recording. Buffer 256.
  • Raise M4 Input 1 until taps peak into amber. Piezos are low-output — this knob goes higher than for a normal mic.
Play every object three ways
  • For each object, capture it struck (tap), scraped (drag), and sustained (bow, rub, or excite continuously). One object becomes three morphologies.
  • Press the mic against metal, wood, glass, a stretched membrane, a wire. Each surface resonates differently — the contact mic is hearing the body, not the air.
  • Record one continuous take per object family, leaving a clear gap of silence between hits. Gaps are where you'll cut.
  • Target 15–25 short objects. Quantity now buys freedom in the montage.
Capture targets
Object families and what each buys you
Struck metalPitched gestures with long ringing tails — bridges toward the pitched end of the spine
Wood / box knocksDry, mid-axis impulses — clean punctuation with no tail
Scrapes & frictionRising/falling moving gestures — your transition material
Glass / ceramicBright ticks and pure-ish sustained tones — the pitched anchor
Granular surfaces (gravel, paper)Rough iterative grain — feeds the noisy end and the texture lanes
Decision check
  • Listen back: is there handling rumble under the good hits? If yes, you'll high-pass it later — but note it now so you don't mistake it for content.
  • Do you have at least one strong gesture for each station on your spine, not just the two ends?
  • Did you capture enough silence around each object to cut it clean? If hits run together, re-record with bigger gaps.
Step 03

Import, slice, and classify

Get the files in cleanly
  • Copy every WAV into the project's Samples/Imported folder first — never work off the SD card or desktop.
  • Drag the source files onto the RAW IMPORT track. Confirm Warp is OFF on each clip (clip view → Warp button dark). A warped object is a smeared object.
  • Set the timeline ruler to m:ss — this piece is measured in seconds.
Slice into objects
  • Play through each long take. At every clean silence, ⌘ E to split, isolating one object per clip.
  • Trim each clip tight to the object: zoom in, drag the clip start to just before the attack, the end to where the tail meets the noise floor. Tight edges are what let objects be recombined freely.
  • Draw short fades (3–10 ms) on both clip ends with the clip-corner handles. This removes the click that a hard cut on a non-zero sample always produces.
  • Rename every clip with ⌘ R to its catalogue Object ID — OBJ-dull-thud, not ZOOM0042.
Classify by reduced listening
  • For each object, listen three times and fill its palette row: mass, grain, attack, motion, role, axis position. Forbid yourself the source word.
  • Drag each classified object onto its role lane: short directional sounds to GESTURES, sustained beds to TEXTURE A/B.
  • Park anything ambiguous on TRANSFORM — it may become an object once processed (Step 04).
Decision check
  • Can you now find any object by its qualities in under five seconds? If names are vague, re-name — the build speed depends on this.
  • Did classifying reveal a gap on the spine — a station with no object? Note it; Step 04 can manufacture one by transformation.
  • Are the clip edges genuinely click-free? Solo one and loop it; a tick at the seam means the fade is too short or on the wrong sample.
Step 04

Transform — turn objects into new objects

Transformation is how concrète moves a sound away from its source — and how you fill gaps on the spine. Apply on the TRANSFORM lane, then resample the result to audio (Audio From → Resampling, record the pass) so each transformed object becomes a real, classifiable file. Use one or two transforms per object, not a stack.

The core transformations
Each transform and what it changes
Reverse (clip view → Reverse)Swaps attack for swell — a struck object becomes a rising gesture. The cheapest way to make anticipation.
Speed / transposition (clip Warp off, change clip sample rate, or use a pitch device)Slowing pulls grain apart and deepens mass; speeding tightens it. Octave-down a metal strike and it becomes a cavernous texture.
Granular (Grain Delay, or a granular instrument)Freezes a moment into a cloud — turns any short object into a sustained texture. The classic gap-filler for the middle of the spine.
Spectral (Spectral Resonator / Spectral Time)Imposes pitch on noise (toward the pitched end) or freezes/smears spectra into drones.
Filtering (EQ Eight, Auto Filter)Narrows mass — a band-passed noise becomes a tonic-ish object; high-passing removes handling rumble.
Looping a tailLoop the decay of a ring to make an endless pitched bed from a single hit.
Manufacture the objects your spine is missing
  • Missing a pitched anchor? Spectral-Resonate the grey hiss onto a fixed note until noise becomes tone.
  • Missing a mid-axis texture? Granular-freeze a wood knock into a soft cloud.
  • Missing a transition? Reverse a scrape so it swells into the next section instead of away from it.
  • Resample each keeper, rename it (OBJ-strike-rev, TEX-knock-cloud), and add a palette row. A transformed object is a first-class object.
Decision check
  • After transforming, is the source less nameable? That's usually the goal. If you can still say "reversed metal", push one more transform — unless legibility is the point of that moment.
  • Did you keep the untouched original on RAW IMPORT? Always transform a copy.
  • Does each transformed object now occupy a station the raw captures didn't? If it just duplicates an existing one, cut it.
Step 05

Lay the texture bed

Find the loop, then extend it
  • Open your chosen opening texture. Hunt for the calmest 6–10 seconds with no distinct event — that's your loop core.
  • Split it out, then duplicate (⌘ D) end-to-end to fill the planned length. Overlap copies by 1–2 seconds and crossfade the seam (drag the clip fade handles over each other) so the join is inaudible.
  • Lay the bed across TEXTURE A for the whole piece's duration — it is the room everything else lives in.
Stack a second texture for depth
  • Put a contrasting bed (e.g. TEX-low-rumble under TEX-grey-hiss) on TEXTURE B.
  • EQ them apart: high-pass the bright bed around 300 Hz, low-pass the rumble around 400 Hz. They interlock instead of masking.
  • The two beds will crossfade across the piece as you travel the spine — one fading out as the other rises. Draw that with clip-gain or track-volume automation in Step 07.
Decision check
  • Loop the bed for two minutes. Can you hear the seam? If a pulse or a click recurs, lengthen the crossfade or pick a calmer loop core.
  • Does the bed sound like a place, or like a sample on repeat? If it loops obviously, it's too short or too eventful.
  • Flip to mono for ten seconds — does the bed survive? If it hollows out, your two textures are phase-cancelling; nudge one.
Step 06

Montage — place gestures on the timeline

This is composition itself. You are placing gestures across the texture bed so the piece travels your spine. Work in silence-respecting time: a sound-object needs room around it to be heard as an object rather than as part of a stream.

Mark the journey first
  • Right-click the scrub area → Add Locator at each station of the spine. For a noisy→pitched piece: NOISE FIELD, FIRST EVENTS, TURN, PITCH EMERGES, RESOLUTION.
  • Locators turn your one-sentence spine into physical targets you drag objects against. Aim for a 3–5 minute total.
A worked 4-minute arc (noisy → pitched)
0:00 – 0:45 / NOISE FIELDTexture bed alone. One or two distant gestures, low. Establish the room.
0:45 – 1:30 / FIRST EVENTSDry impulses enter — wood-knock, bright-tick — sparse, with silence between.
1:30 – 2:15 / TURNMoving gestures (scrape-up / zip-fall) raise activity. Bright bed begins fading toward rumble.
2:15 – 3:15 / PITCH EMERGESPitched objects (metal-strike, bowed-rise) recur; TEX-pure-tone rises underneath.
3:15 – 4:00 / RESOLUTIONActivity thins. A single held tone remains, then a final gesture, then silence.
Place gestures with the grammar of montage
  • Juxtapose for contrast: a sharp bright-tick over the flat bed reads as an event because the ground is still.
  • Chain for continuity: end one gesture's tail exactly where the next begins, so a struck object flows into a swell (metal-strike → reversed bowed-rise).
  • Echo a motif: reuse one object three times across the piece, each time slightly transformed. Recurrence is how a concrète piece earns coherence without melody.
  • Respect silence. Leave real gaps. A gesture surrounded by space has weight; a gesture in a stream is wallpaper.
  • Make cuts at zero-crossings or under a fade. Loop any suspicious seam with ⌘ L and audition it three times before accepting.
Vary rather than repeat
  • When an object returns, change one thing: its level, its pan, its transposition, or its reverb send. Identical repeats expose the loop; varied repeats build a motif.
  • Pan gestures deliberately across the field — concrète is spatial music. Spread events left-to-right so the listener's attention moves through the room.
Decision check
  • Play the whole montage. Does it travel the spine, or does it sit in one place? If it doesn't move, your objects aren't spaced along the axis — re-order.
  • Remove any single gesture. Is the piece weaker? If nothing is lost, leave it removed.
  • Is there at least one passage of near-silence that makes you lean in? If everything is full, the loud moments have nothing to be loud against.
  • Can a listener follow a thread — a recurring object — through the piece? If every sound is new, it's a sound-effects reel, not a composition.
Step 07

Shape the gesture — automation as performance

Two kinds of movement
  • Printed movement lives inside the recorded object — its own attack, grain, and decay. Fixed; only re-recordable.
  • Drawn movement is automation on top: volume swells, filter sweeps, pan trajectories, reverb blooms. This is where the acousmatic gesture is shaped.
  • In concrète, drawn movement is not decoration — it is how an object acquires trajectory in space, the thing Bayle's tradition is built on.
Draw the spatial and dynamic arc
  • A shows automation lanes; pencil (B) draws. Bend ramps into S-curves (right-click segment → Curve) so they read as intention, not as a ruler.
  • Texture crossfade: automate TEXTURE A volume down and TEXTURE B up across the TURN, so the bed itself travels the spine.
  • Gesture trajectory: automate Pan on a moving gesture so a scrape physically crosses the stereo field as it rises. Automate its reverb send upward as it ends so it dissolves into space.
  • The bloom: at PITCH EMERGES, ramp Return A (reverb) send up — space opens exactly as pitch arrives. The brightest spatial moment is the climax.
Performed gesture (optional but worth it)
  • ⌘ M for MIDI map mode. Map a filter cutoff or a reverb send to a hardware control, set Min/Max to the musical range, and perform a gesture's shape in real time while recording automation. A hand-drawn swell breathes in a way a straight ramp never will.
  • After: right-click each lane → Simplify Envelope to thin breakpoints while keeping the shape.
Decision check
  • Does each shaped gesture now have a direction — does it go somewhere? A flat object placed in a flat bed is inert.
  • Play 2:15 – 3:15 blind: does pitch arrive because the piece opened toward it, not because you stacked more objects?
  • If you can hear an automation move as a sudden "event", it's too fast. Slow it across more time — unless the jolt is the gesture you intend.
Step 08

Place everything in one room — the mix

Unify the space
  • Disparate captures — a desk object, a street ambience — betray themselves by their different rooms. Put a single convolution reverb on Return A (a real space impulse, decay 2–4 s) and send everything a touch to it. One shared room makes a collage sound composed.
  • Use Return B (Echo, high-passed around 250 Hz) to extend gestures into trajectories, not to thicken the bed.
Frequency separation
  • Bed: high-pass the bright texture (~300 Hz), low-pass the rumble (~400 Hz) so they occupy different bands.
  • Gestures: high-pass each contact-mic object to remove handling rumble below its real content — often 60–120 Hz.
  • Pitched anchor: let TEX-pure-tone own its narrow band; carve a small dip in the bed there so the tone reads clearly when it arrives.
Verification passes
Check before you call it done
Mono checkFlip a Utility to mono on the master. Anything vanishing means over-wide panning or phasey textures — pull them in.
Quiet checkListen once quietly. The real balance shows — if a gesture disappears or the bed swamps events, fix that first.
Phone-speaker checkBounce a rough and play it on a phone. Which objects survive a bad speaker are the ones carrying the piece.
Headroom checkPeaks ≤ -3 dBFS across the whole piece, including the reverb bloom at the climax.
Master bus
  • Spectrum + Utility on the master. No limiter — concrète lives on dynamics, and a limiter flattens the silence that gives gestures their weight.
  • Take the DT 990s off every 15 minutes. Their 7–10 kHz lift will have you cutting highs and hiss that were never wrong.
Decision check
  • Do all the objects sound like they share one space, or like a folder of clips? If they don't cohere, raise the shared reverb send.
  • Is the dynamic range intact — are the quiet passages genuinely quiet? If everything is the same loudness, you've over-compressed the life out of it.
Step 09

Finish, export, and step away

Consolidate and render
  • When sections are final, select their clips and ⌘ J consolidate — one clean clip per section keeps the timeline legible.
  • Select the full span (0:00 to the end). File → Export Audio / Video.

Archive the work
  • File → Collect All and Save — the project folder now holds every source file. Concrète projects break the instant one capture goes missing.
  • Keep the renamed source WAVs and the palette catalogue together. The catalogue is reusable material for the next piece.
Listen elsewhere, then revise once
  • Copy to phone. Listen in different rooms, on laptop speakers — brutally honest about mids.
  • Write up to 10 timestamped notes: 1:48 second tick lands too soon, not middle feels off.
  • Wait a day. Change one thing. Render v2. Compare.
Final decision check
  • Does the piece travel its one-sentence spine from first second to last?
  • Could a listener describe it in morphological terms — "noise resolving into a tone" — without naming a single source?
  • Is every object earning its place, or is something there because you recorded it and felt attached?
