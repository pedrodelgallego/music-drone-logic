Lab Note B2-002
Concrète · 01 — Overview

Musique concrète: making an acousmatic piece from recorded sound

A top-down brief for the Schaeffer/GRM method, end to end — capture, reduced listening, typo-morphology, montage, and shaped gesture. You will treat sound as material, not as a sign of its source. The finished piece must be fully original: your own recordings, your own montage, no borrowed material.

The goal

A 3–5 minute acousmatic piece

  • Built only from sounds you record — field and objects
  • Edited and shaped in Ableton Live 12 Suite
  • Fully original; the source must never become the subject

Fundamentals you'll practice

  • Reduced listening — hearing the sound-object, not its cause
  • Typo-morphology — classifying objects by mass, grain, dynamic
  • Montage, splice, and gesture as composition
Core objective

Understand how a recorded sound, stripped of its origin, becomes raw material with shape, weight, and motion you can compose with.

What "concrète" means

Pierre Schaeffer's term inverts the usual order. Classical composition is abstract: it begins with notation, an idea on paper, and only later becomes sound. Concrète composition begins with the concrete material — a recorded sound that already exists — and works toward form by listening, cutting, and assembling. The note is replaced by the sound-object: a fragment heard for itself.

The instinct to resist

  • Hearing a recording as "a door, a train, a voice"
  • Keeping sounds whole, in the order they happened
  • Reaching for melody, key, and meter to give it shape

The discipline to build

  • Hearing only mass, grain, attack, motion
  • Cutting sounds free of their timeline and recombining them
  • Letting morphology — not pitch — organise the piece

The skills transfer everywhere. Reduced listening sharpens sound design for film, games, ambient, and techno. Typo-morphology gives you a vocabulary for any sample. Montage is the root of all editing. You are not learning a 1948 genre — you are learning to hear sound as substance.

Principle

Listen to the sound, not to its cause. Compose the object, not the event.
