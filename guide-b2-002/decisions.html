Lab Note B2-002
Concrète · 03 — Decisions

Reduced listening & typo-morphology

The theory that makes the method work. Two ideas do all the lifting: reduced listening, which changes what you hear, and typo-morphology, which gives you words for it. Decide your stance on both before you record a single sound.

The three listening modes

Schaeffer separated the act of listening into modes. You are usually in the first two without noticing. The method asks you to deliberately enter the third — the one almost nobody uses.

ModeYou attend toExample
Causal (écouter)The source — what made the sound"That's a car door."
Semantic (comprendre)The meaning the sound carries as a sign"Someone has arrived."
Reduced (écoute réduite)The sound itself — its intrinsic qualities, source bracketed away"A short, dull thud with a hard attack and a fast decay."
Reduced listening, defined

To listen to a sound for its own sake, as a sound-object (objet sonore), independent of its cause and of any meaning it carries. The car door is no longer a door — it is a mass with an attack, a grain, and a dynamic profile. That object is your raw material.

The sound-object

What it is

  • A single, coherent acoustic event heard as a unit
  • Bounded — it has a start, a body, and an end
  • Defined by its own qualities, not by what produced it

What it is not

  • Not the physical thing that made it (the source)
  • Not the recording medium or file (the support)
  • Not a note, a sample-with-meaning, or a sound effect

Typo-morphology: words for the object

Once you hear the object, you need to describe it without naming its source. Schaeffer's typo-morphology is that vocabulary. Typology sorts objects into broad types; morphology describes the qualities within each. You will use these terms to classify every capture on the Palette page.

CriterionQuestion it answersRange you'll hear
MassHow is the sound's pitch-content occupied?Pure tone → tonic (clear pitch) → complex → nodal → white noise
Harmonic timbreWhat colour sits on the mass?Dark / full / bright / hollow / metallic
GrainWhat is the surface microstructure?Smooth → granular → rough → iterative (rapid repeated)
Attack (allure / dynamic)How does it begin and sustain?Sharp / struck / plucked / swelling / flat
Dynamic profileHow does loudness move over time?Impulse / decaying / sustained / crescendo / undulating
Mass profile (motion)Does the pitch-content move while it sounds?Fixed / rising / falling / oscillating / chaotic
Duration / factureHow is energy sustained — one gesture or many?Held / iterative / accumulated
The three workhorse axes

For a first piece, classify primarily on mass (pitched↔noisy), grain (smooth↔rough), and dynamic profile (impulse↔sustained). Those three alone let you tell whether two objects will contrast or blend — which is every montage decision you are about to make.

Decisions to make now

Decision 01

Where do you sit between the two poles?

Source-erased ↔ source-preserved
  • Schaeffer pole: cut, loop, and transform until no one can name the source. The object wins.
  • Ferrari pole: keep recordings legible; compose by framing and selection. The world wins.
  • Pick a target on this line for this piece. A first piece is easiest nearer the Schaeffer pole — transformation gives you obvious material to work with.
Decision check
  • If a listener instantly names every source, did you intend that? If not, transform harder.
  • If nothing is recognisable at all, is the piece losing the tension between thing and object? A trace of source can be the hook.
Decision 02

What is the organising contrast?

Choose one morphological axis to structure the piece
  • A concrète piece needs a through-line that isn't melody. Pick one axis as the spine: e.g. noisy → pitched, or impulse → sustained, or rough → smooth.
  • The piece becomes a journey along that axis. Parmegiani's pairing of natural and synthetic is exactly this, made audible.
  • Write it as one sentence: "This piece moves from dense granular noise toward a single held tone." That sentence is your arrangement.
Decision check
  • Can you state the spine in one sentence without using the word "then"? If you need "then... then... then", you have a list, not an axis.
  • Does every object you plan to capture sit somewhere measurable on that axis?
Decision 03

Gesture vs. texture — the two object roles

Every object will play one of two parts
  • Gesture: a sound with a clear shape and direction in time — an attack, a sweep, a hit. It is the event, the foreground.
  • Texture: a sustained, stable field with no strong internal direction — a drone, a hiss, a rumble. It is the ground.
  • A piece is gestures moving across textures. Decide roughly how many of each you need before capturing — the Palette page makes you catalogue them.
Decision check
  • If you only have gestures, the piece will feel like a list of events. Add a texture to be the room.
  • If you only have textures, nothing happens. Add a gesture to break the field.
Originality constraint

Every sound-object must come from your own recordings. No sample packs, no found audio, no archive material. The reason is not legal — it is pedagogical. You cannot practise reduced listening on a sound someone else already decided was musical.
