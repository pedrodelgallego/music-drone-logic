Lab Note B6-004
Tuning · 06 — The build

Step-by-step — a piece composed in a tuning you authored

This is the long page. We build a 6–8 minute drone piece entirely inside the 7-limit scale from the palette, applied through Live 12's tuning system from the setup. Every step is small, every step has a decision checkpoint, and the central craft — tuning two notes by listening to the rate at which they beat — is spelled out as a procedure you can repeat. Keep the setup and palette pages open in other tabs; this page assumes the tuning is already loaded and the drone already proved still.

The build, end to end

Load tuning → Lock the drone → Add the fifth → Voice the triad → The seventh → Arrange → Print & check
Step 01

Prepare the set and confirm the tuning

Open and verify
  • ⌘ N for a new Live Set. Tab to Arrangement view — the whole piece lives here. Tempo is almost irrelevant for a drone; set 60 so bar lines fall on convenient seconds.
  • Confirm the tuning chooser in the title bar shows your file's name (b6_drone_7limit.ascl), not "12-TET / Default." If it reads default, re-import per the setup page before doing anything else.
  • Confirm the root: a soft-synth playing the root degree should read 55.00 Hz on a tuner. This is the anchor; nothing downstream is right if this is wrong.
Create the tracks
  • ⌘ ⇧ T for MIDI tracks: DRONE ROOT, DRONE FIFTH, DRONE THIRD, DRONE SEVENTH. One pitch per track keeps each voice independently tunable, audible, and mutable.
  • Add one audio track PERC for an optional 12-TET percussive pulse, recorded or sampled later.
  • ⌘ ⌥ T for one return: A — REVERB. Drop in Hybrid Reverb, decay 8–12 s, Dry/Wet 100 %. Reverb adds space without bending pitch — the only large effect this piece wants.
  • ⌘ S → save as b6_tuning_001.als in a dedicated project folder. Drop a copy of the .ascl into the project folder so the tuning travels with the set.
Load the drone instrument
Operator on each DRONE track — a clean tone that exposes tuning
OscillatorA single sine, or sine + a quiet low partial. Sine tones make beating obvious; rich tones can mask it. Start clean.
Amp envelopeLong attack (1–3 s), full sustain, long release (3–6 s). The drone swells and never clicks.
VoicesSet to 1 per track — one held note per track. The chord is built across tracks, not within one.
FilterGentle low-pass, well open. Movement comes from slow filter rides, never from pitch.
  • Copy the same instrument to all four DRONE tracks. Identical timbres make the tuning, not the sound design, the variable.
Decision check
  • Does the tuning chooser show your file, and does the root read 55.00 Hz?
  • Is each drone voice a clean, sustaining tone with no vibrato, chorus, or detune anywhere in its chain?
  • Is the reverb the only big effect, and is it 100 % wet on a return so the send is the mix control?
Step 02

Lock the root drone

Sound the root and leave it
  • On DRONE ROOT, draw one long MIDI note on the root degree, spanning the full intended length of the piece (say 8 minutes). One note. It will not change pitch — it is the floor.
  • Play it solo. Confirm by tuner: 55.00 Hz. Let it sustain for a full minute and listen. A single tone has nothing to beat against, so it should be perfectly steady. If you hear pulsing, the cause is the synth (two slightly detuned oscillators) — fix it in the patch, not the tuning.
  • This take is your reference pitch for everything that follows. Do not touch it again.
Decision check
  • Is the root a single, dead-steady tone at exactly the root frequency?
  • Is any pulsing you hear coming from the patch's own oscillators rather than the tuning? Reduce to one oscillator if unsure.
Step 03

Add the fifth — and learn to hear beating

The interval the whole piece rests on
  • On DRONE FIFTH, draw a long note on the fifth degree (3/2 above the root = 82.50 Hz). Play it together with the root.
  • In your just tuning the two should lock: their partials coincide and the combined tone sits utterly still. This is the foundation chord, the 1/1 + 3/2 from the palette.
Beating rate — the craftsman's tuning method

Beating is the slow pulse in loudness you hear when two tones are almost the same frequency. The pulse rate equals the difference between the two frequencies. Tuners use this directly: bring two notes together and the beating slows; when it stops, they are identical. We use the same physics one level up — on the partials of an interval — to verify and to deliberately set the tuning.

Reading the beat rate as a frequency difference
Two tones 1 Hz apartOne beat (one swell-and-fade) per second — slow, easy to count
Two tones 2 Hz apartTwo beats per second
Beating stops entirelyThe two frequencies are identical — the interval is locked
Beating speeds up as you detuneYou moved the wrong way; reverse
  • For the fifth: the strong test is the coincidence near the 3rd harmonic of the root (≈ 165 Hz) and the 2nd harmonic of the fifth (≈ 165 Hz). In a pure 3:2 these land on exactly the same frequency and there is no beat. Detune the fifth a hair sharp and you will hear a slow pulse appear: that pulse is the mistuning, in Hz.
  • The exercise: temporarily nudge the fifth's pitch up by a few cents (Operator's coarse/fine tune, or a Pitch device). Listen to the beat appear and count its rate. Pull it back to the just value and the beat slows to nothing. You have just tuned by ear — the skill the entire drone lineage rests on.
  • Return the fifth to the exact scale degree and confirm: silent beating, dead-still chord.
A/B against 12-TET
  • Switch the set's tuning to 12-TET for ten seconds with root + fifth held. The 12-TET fifth is 2 cents narrow, so a very slow beat appears — roughly one pulse every couple of seconds at this register. Switch back: it stops. That tiny, audible difference is the thesis of this Lab Note in two notes.
Decision check
  • Can you make beating appear and disappear by detuning the fifth, and count the rate while it's there?
  • With the fifth on its exact degree, is the root + fifth dead still?
  • Can you hear the slow beat the 12-TET fifth introduces, and watch it vanish when you switch back to your tuning?
Step 04

Voice the just major triad

Add the third
  • On DRONE THIRD, draw a long note on the third degree (5/4 = 68.75 Hz). Bring it in slowly with the track's volume automation — the third changes the whole emotional cast, so let it arrive over 20–30 seconds.
  • You now have 1/1 + 5/4 + 3/2 — a just major triad in the ratio 4:5:6. Every interval inside it is pure: root–third (5:4), third–fifth (6:5), root–fifth (3:2). Held, it is utterly still — the chord 12-TET can only approximate.
  • A/B it: switch to 12-TET and the third's ~14-cent error makes the whole triad buzz restlessly. Switch back and it settles. Let this comparison teach your ear what "pure third" means.
Verify the third by beating
  • The cleanest coincidence for a 5:4 is the 5th harmonic of the root (≈ 275 Hz) against the 4th harmonic of the third (≈ 275 Hz). In a pure 5:4 they coincide and don't beat. Nudge the third sharp and a beat appears; pull it back and it stops. Same method, new interval.
  • Leave the third on its exact degree. Confirm the full triad is still.
Decision check
  • Does the full 4:5:6 triad sit still in your tuning and buzz in 12-TET?
  • Did you bring the third in slowly enough that its arrival was a colour change, not an event?
  • Can you verify the third by its own beating test, independently of the fifth?
Step 05

Introduce the harmonic seventh

The note the keyboard can't play
  • On DRONE SEVENTH, draw a long note on the seventh degree (7/4 = 96.25 Hz). This is the harmonic seventh — flat, dark, with no 12-TET equivalent. It is the moment the tuning stops sounding "nice" and starts sounding genuinely other.
  • Sound it with just the root: 1/1 + 7/4 (4:7) is open and dark and locked. Then with the foundation: 1/1 + 3/2 + 7/4 (4:6:7) — the septimal voicing from the palette. Against the root it locks; against the fifth it leans (the 7:6 between them is narrow). Use that slight lean as tension.
  • Keep it rare. The seventh is the piece's "strangeness" colour — bring it in once, hold it, let it define the central section, then take it away.
Why it can't be faked in 12-TET
  • Switch to 12-TET and try to find this note: the nearest grid pitch is the minor seventh at 1000 cents, fully 31 cents sharp of the just 7/4 (968.8 cents). There is no key that plays it. The only way to sound a harmonic seventh is to leave the 12 equal divisions behind — which is exactly what your tuning file did. Hold the contrast in your ears; it is the clearest demonstration the whole Lab Note offers.
Decision check
  • Does 1/1 + 7/4 lock, and does the 4:6:7 voicing have the dark, open colour you want?
  • Is the seventh used sparingly — a defining moment, not a constant?
  • Can you hear that 12-TET genuinely cannot reach this pitch? If you can, you have proved the point of the piece.
Step 06

Arrange the piece as a tuning structure

Structure is which intervals sound, when

A drone piece has no chord changes. Its structure is the schedule of which voices are present — the slow appearance and removal of intervals. This is exactly how the Young and Arkbro references work: the chord is fixed; the composition is the voicing over time. Use the track volume automation on each DRONE track to write that schedule.

An arrangement arc — section, voices present, the colour
0:00 – 1:30 / DRONERoot alone, then root + fifth. The foundation establishes. Nothing else.
1:30 – 3:00 / WARMTHThird swells in over 30 s → full 4:5:6 just major triad. The piece's warmest, most consonant region.
3:00 – 4:30 / SHIFTThird fades out; seventh swells in. The 4:6:7 septimal voicing takes over — dark, open, "other."
4:30 – 5:30 / PEAKBriefly all four: root + fifth + third + seventh. Dense, the inner 5:7 and 7:6 intervals adding restless motion against the still foundation. The one tense moment.
5:30 – 7:00 / STRIPSeventh and third gone. Back to root + fifth. The fullness just past makes the bare fifth read as vast.
7:00 – 8:00 / RESTFifth fades; root alone, then a long reverb tail. The piece returns to where it began.
Movement without touching pitch
Where the slow life of the piece comes from — none of it bends pitch
Filter ridesSlow low-pass cutoff automation on each voice, 16+ bars per move. Brightness changes; pitch does not.
Reverb sendBloom the send up into the PEAK and pull it back for the STRIP. Space opens and closes.
Voice volumeThe arrangement itself — the swelling and removal of intervals above.
Timbre / partial levelIf using Operator, slowly raise a higher partial's level on one voice. The tone enriches, the pitch stays exact.
  • No ramp shorter than 8 bars. Most should be 16–32. If you can hear a move as an "event," it is too fast.
  • Right-click any automation segment → Curve (or drag the segment middle) to bend ramps into S-curves so entries breathe rather than step.
The optional 12-TET pulse
  • If the piece needs a heartbeat, add one quiet percussive pulse on PERC — a soft kick or a woodblock, left in 12-TET because it is atonal enough not to clash.
  • If it has any pitch, tune its sample so the fundamental agrees with the root (55 Hz or an octave of it). A detuned pulse against a pure drone is the one thing that will undo all your tuning work.
  • Keep it sparse — one hit every bar or every two bars. The drone is the subject; the pulse is only a frame.
Decision check
  • Is the structure entirely a schedule of which intervals sound, with no pitch ever bending?
  • Does the PEAK feel dense because voices were added, and the STRIP feel vast because they were removed?
  • If there is a percussive pulse, is its pitch in agreement with the root, and is it sparse enough to stay a frame?
Step 07

Render, check the tuning survived, and step away

A spectral sanity check
  • Drop a Spectrum analyser on the master. Hold the foundation (root + fifth) and look at the peaks: you should see a peak at 55 Hz, one at 82.5 Hz, and — crucially — a shared peak where the root's 3rd harmonic and the fifth's 2nd harmonic coincide near 165 Hz. A single sharp shared peak means the interval is pure; two close peaks means it is beating.
  • This is the visual confirmation of what your ears already told you. If the shared peaks split, the tuning didn't apply to that voice — check the track's instrument is downstream of the global tuning.

Archive and listen away from the screen
  • File → Collect All and Save so the .ascl, the set, and any samples live together. A tuned piece whose tuning file is lost cannot be reopened correctly.
  • Listen on different systems. Crucially, listen loud and long — beating and stillness only reveal themselves over sustained time and at volume.
  • Write a short note next to the WAV: which intervals locked, which leaned, and whether the seventh's strangeness landed. Wait a day, change one thing, render v2.
Decision check
  • Do the spectrum peaks confirm pure coincidences where your ears heard stillness?
  • Did the tuning file get collected into the project so the piece is reproducible?
  • On a fresh listen, can you point to the exact moment the tuning announces itself — and is that moment the strongest in the piece?
The build in one line

You did not write a melody and find a tuning for it. You authored a tuning and let a held chord reveal what it makes possible — stillness where 12-TET shimmers, and a seventh the keyboard cannot reach.
