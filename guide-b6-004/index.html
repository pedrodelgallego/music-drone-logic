Lab Note B6-004
Tuning · 01 — Overview

Author the pitch grid, then let a drone reveal it

A single-topic deep reference on tuning as a system. The twelve equal steps per octave you have used your whole musical life are not nature — they are a four-hundred-year-old engineering compromise. This Lab Note treats pitch the way a builder treats a foundation: from the harmonic series up through just intonation, cents, and the cost of equal temperament, to authoring a scale in Scala, loading it into Live 12 as an .ascl tuning system, and tuning a held drone until it sits dead still. Inspired by the just-intonation drone lineage of La Monte Young, Catherine Christer Hennix, and Ellen Arkbro; the piece you make from it must be fully original.

The goal

A piece in a tuning you chose

  • A 6–8 minute drone-based original composed in a non-12-TET tuning
  • Built on exact small-integer ratios — a scale you authored, not inherited
  • The tuning is the composition; the held chord is the subject

Fundamentals you'll practice

  • The harmonic series, the ratios it gives, and reading intervals in cents
  • Designing a just scale in Scala; exporting .scl / .ascl
  • Loading a tuning system in Live 12; the MicroFreak's microtuning limits
  • Tuning by beating rate — letting your ears, not a needle, find pure
Core objective

Hear, once and unmistakably, the difference between a held interval in just intonation and the same interval in 12-TET. Everything else in this Lab Note serves that single audible fact.

Why this matters

The beginner instinct

  • Treat 12-TET as neutral — "in tune" — and never question the grid
  • Reach for more notes when the problem is the spacing of the notes you have
  • Blame the synth or the reverb when a sustained chord shimmers and won't settle
  • Think of microtuning as an exotic effect rather than a foundation decision

The opposite discipline

  • Choose the tuning system first; the notes follow from the ratios
  • Build on intervals the harmonic series actually contains — 3:2, 5:4, 7:4
  • Tune the drone before anything else, and prove it locked by ear
  • Treat the held chord, not the melody, as where the music happens

The skills transfer. Ratios, cents, and beating rates are the same in spectral composition, sound design, sound-art installation, microtonal guitar, gamelan, and any music that takes pitch seriously as material rather than as a fixed keyboard. You are not learning a trick — you are learning that the grid under every note was a decision, and that you are allowed to make it yourself.

Principle

Author the grid, tune the drone first, and let stillness be the achievement.
