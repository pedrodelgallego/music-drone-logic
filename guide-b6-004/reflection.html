Lab Note B6-004
Tuning · 07 — Reflection

Criteria & reflection

Most of the learning happens here, not while making the piece. The work of tuning is the work of listening, and listening is judged after the fact. Answer slowly. Write the answers down. The success of this Lab Note is not a finished track — it is an ear that can no longer hear 12-TET as neutral.

How you know it worked

Success signals
  • You can author a scale in Scala and explain every ratio in it.
  • You loaded a tuning file into Live 12 and confirmed it took by cents and by ear.
  • A held interval in your tuning sits dead still where its 12-TET version shimmers.
  • You can make beating appear and disappear on purpose, and count its rate.
  • The piece is built from a handful of pure intervals, not a full chromatic scale.
  • The structure is a schedule of which intervals sound, not a sequence of chord changes.
  • Nothing in the piece bends pitch — no vibrato, chorus, or detune fights the tuning.
  • The harmonic seventh is present and clearly outside what 12-TET can reach.
  • You can point to the moment the tuning announces itself as the strongest moment.
  • You can explain why each tuning decision was made.
Failure signals
  • The "just" chords still shimmer — the tuning never actually applied, or the root frequency is wrong.
  • A 12-TET pad or detuned percussion beats against the drone and undoes the tuning.
  • You treated microtuning as an effect rather than as the foundation of the piece.
  • The scale is so large you can't say what any given degree is for.
  • The piece needs melody and chord changes to stay interesting — the held chord can't carry it.
  • You tuned by a needle without ever hearing the beating stop.
  • The tuning file got lost, and the set no longer reopens in tune.

Ten reflection questions

  1. Which interval locked most convincingly — the fifth, the third, or the seventh?
  2. Could you hear the difference between your tuning and 12-TET without being told which was which?
  3. Which moment made the tuning "announce itself," and was it the strongest moment?
  4. Did the held chord carry the piece, or did you reach for melody to rescue it?
  5. Which voice could be removed without weakening the piece?
  6. Did the seventh sound "wrong" at first and then correct? What does that tell you about your ear?
  7. Was the beating-rate method something you could repeat reliably, or guesswork?
  8. Did the MicroFreak's microtuning limits change what you could build, and how did you work around them?
  9. Did the structure work as a schedule of intervals, or did it feel like nothing happened?
  10. What would you tune differently next time — the root, the ratios, or the limit?

The honest fault to look for

Most likely fault

The tuning didn't fully apply. The commonest failure is a chord that still shimmers because one voice's instrument sat outside the global tuning, or because the reference frequency was off, or because a detuned percussion part is beating against the pure drone. If anything shimmers when it shouldn't, suspect the signal chain before you suspect your ears — verify each voice reads the right cents, and that nothing pitched is in 12-TET alongside the drone.

Carry forward

The pitch grid was the most invisible decision in your music, made for you centuries before you were born. You authored one scale by hand, loaded it, and heard a drone lock. From here, 12-TET is no longer the default — it is one choice among many, and you know how to make the others. Carry the beating-rate method into every future tuning, and carry the habit of tuning the foundation before anything sits on it.

Final principle

The best result is not the most exotic tuning. It is the clearest demonstration that the grid under every note was a decision — and that a held chord, tuned by ear until it comes to rest, can be the whole composition.
