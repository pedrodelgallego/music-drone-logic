Lab Note B6-004
Tuning · 04 — Setup

From ratios to a playable grid

This page wires the rig for tuning work and walks the whole tooling path: design a scale in Scala, export it as a tuning file, load it into Live 12 as a tuning system that every soft-synth obeys, and understand exactly how far the MicroFreak can follow you off the 12-TET grid. The discipline of the whole Lab Note is here in one rule — tune the drone first, and prove it locked by ear before you build anything on top of it.

The rig and the signal path

Two flows run in parallel: audio, and the tuning information that decides what pitch each note actually sounds. The tuning flow is the new thing here. It lives entirely in the control path — a tuning file applied in Live, sent on as MIDI — and never touches the audio. Knowing where the tuning is applied tells you which sound sources you can retune and which you cannot.

Tuning path

Scala — ratios export .scl / .ascl Live 12 Tuning System retunes soft-synths in-box Ableton instruments MIDI out (MPE / pitch bend) MicroFreak

Audio path

MicroFreak Out → TS → M4 In 1 + Ableton soft-synth (drone) → in-box → Ableton → USB-C → M4 Phones → DT 990 Pro
Where tuning is applied

Live's Tuning System retunes soft-synths inside Ableton directly. For the MicroFreak, the tuning must be sent out as MIDI — and that path is limited. The most reliable, beat-tight drone in your tuning will come from a soft-synth in Ableton; use the MicroFreak for the parts where its limits don't matter, or retune it on the device itself.

Design the scale in Scala

Setup 01

Author the tuning file

Build the scale
  • Download and open Scala — the free, decades-old standard tool for designing tunings. It runs on macOS, Windows, and Linux.
  • Enter your scale as ratios, one degree per line, ending at the octave 2/1. For this Lab Note's worked piece, enter the sparse 7-limit drone set: 1/1 9/8 5/4 3/2 7/4 2/1.
  • Scala does the arithmetic for you: it converts every ratio to cents, checks the octave closes at exactly 1200 cents, and warns you if a degree is out of order.
  • The file is the tuning system — portable, human-readable, and version-controllable. You can open a .scl in a text editor and read the ratios.
Export the right format
File formats and what Live 12 wants
.sclThe classic Scala scale file: ratios/cents only, no reference pitch. Live 12 reads it but will ask you to set the root and reference frequency on import.
.asclScala's newer "advanced" format. Carries the scale plus a note-name mapping and a reference frequency, so Live can place the root on a specific MIDI note automatically. Prefer this for a clean, repeatable load.
Reference frequencySet it to your chosen root from the Decisions page — e.g. A1 = 55 Hz. This is the anchor the whole just scale hangs from.
  • Export, name the file for the piece (e.g. b6_drone_7limit.ascl), and save it where Live can reach it.
  • Keep the source ratios in your notes too — the file is the artifact, but the ratios are the decision.

Load the tuning in Live 12

Setup 02

Apply the tuning system

Import the file
  • Open a new Live Set. In the title bar, click the tuning chooser (the small tuning-fork control beside the key/scale area).
  • Choose Import... and select your .ascl file. It now appears in the tuning chooser by name.
  • Confirm the reference: the root should land where your .ascl said — e.g. MIDI note for A1 sounding 55 Hz. If you exported a plain .scl, set the root note and reference frequency here by hand.
  • The tuning is now global: every Ableton instrument in the set plays your ratios instead of 12-TET.
Prove it took
  • Drop a simple soft-synth (Operator or Wavetable) on a MIDI track. Play the root, then the degree your scale maps to the fifth. Watch a tuner plugin: the fifth should read +2 cents relative to a 12-TET fifth, because a just 3:2 is 702 cents.
  • Play the major-third degree. The tuner should read about −14 cents against 12-TET — that is the just 5:4 (386 cents) sitting where it belongs.
  • If every note reads as plain 12-TET, the tuning didn't apply: re-check the chooser shows your file's name, not "12-TET / Default."
Decision check
  • Does the tuning chooser display your file's name, and does the root sound at the exact frequency you set?
  • Do the third and fifth read the cent offsets you predicted from the ratios? If not, the file or the reference is wrong — fix it here, before you make sound you'll have to redo.

The MicroFreak's microtuning — and its limits

The MicroFreak can play in tunings other than 12-TET, but the path is narrower than Live's, and knowing the limits up front stops you from fighting the instrument. There are two routes, and they don't combine cleanly.

Routes to a retuned MicroFreak
On-device microtuningThe MicroFreak has built-in microtuning slots loadable over MIDI (via the MIDI Control Center / SysEx). It can hold scale tables, so its own keyboard and sequencer play your tuning directly — independent of Live. Best when the MicroFreak is the sound source and you want it self-contained.
Live retunes it over MIDILive's Tuning System can express microtuning to external gear only through per-note pitch information — pitch bend or MPE. The MicroFreak is paraphonic, not fully polyphonic per-voice, and its MPE support is limited, so per-note retuning of a held chord is unreliable: a single channel-wide pitch bend cannot set two held notes to two different offsets at once.
Practical consequenceFor a monophonic line (one note at a time), Live can pitch-bend the MicroFreak to any ratio cleanly. For a held two-note drone in just intonation, load the tuning onto the MicroFreak itself, or play that part with an Ableton soft-synth where per-voice tuning is exact.
  • The honest recommendation: make the locked just-intonation drone with an Ableton soft-synth (Operator or Wavetable) under Live's global tuning, where every voice is tuned exactly. Use the MicroFreak for a single retuned monophonic line, or load a microtuning table onto the device so its own keyboard plays your scale. Don't try to hold a pure two-note interval on the MicroFreak driven by Live's pitch bend — that is the one path that will quietly let you down.
  • Digitakt percussion: leave drums in 12-TET. If a kick is tuned, tune its sample's pitch so its fundamental agrees with your root frequency — that is a single sample-tune, not a scale.

Tune the drone first

Setup 03

Prove the lock by ear

The test
  • On the soft-synth under your tuning, hold the root and the fifth together. Use a slow attack and long release so the tone sustains cleanly.
  • Listen for beating. In your just tuning it should be absent or extremely slow — the interval sits dead still.
  • Now switch the set's tuning back to 12-TET for ten seconds. The same fifth now shimmers faintly. That audible difference is the entire reason this Lab Note exists. Switch back.
  • Repeat with the root and the major third. The just third (5:4) will sound startlingly calm next to the 12-TET third's restless buzz.
Decision check
  • Does a sustained chord in your tuning sit still where 12-TET shimmered? If not, your ratios aren't pure — recheck the Scala file and the reference frequency.
  • Did you tune and lock the drone before building anything on top of it? Everything later assumes a settled foundation.
  • Can you switch between your tuning and 12-TET on demand to A/B the difference? Keep both available — you'll use the comparison throughout the build.
Setup rule

The tuning is decided in software before a single sustained note is recorded. Lock the drone, A/B it against 12-TET, and only then move on. A take recorded against the wrong tuning cannot be re-tuned afterwards — it can only be re-recorded.
