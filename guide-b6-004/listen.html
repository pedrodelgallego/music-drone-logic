Lab Note B6-004
Tuning · 02 — Listen

Listen for stillness, and for the beats that aren't there

Before you build a scale, train your ears on music that already lives inside one. The just-intonation drone tradition is the clearest school there is, because it removes everything but pitch: no groove to distract you, no chord changes to move the goalposts. What is left is the interval itself, held long enough that you can hear it lock — or fail to. Listen first for the absence of beating, then for the slow shimmer when partials are close but not exact, then for how a whole piece can be made of one chord that never moves.

Reference lessons in this block

Open each rep lesson and listen on your best headphones, loud enough and long enough to let a sustained interval settle. The point is not the genre or the era. The point is to hear what a tuning system does to a held sound, so that when you build your own you know what success sounds like.

La Monte Young — The Second Dream of the High-Tension Line Stepdown Transformer

  • A single chord of four pitches in strict just intonation, held and re-voiced for the entire piece
  • The ratios are the score — the structure is which combinations sound when
  • Listen for the total absence of beating: the intervals sit perfectly still

Catherine Christer Hennix — The Electric Harpsichord

  • A just-tuned keyboard fed through tape and processing into a dense psychoacoustic cloud
  • Where Young pursues stillness, Hennix lets the partials swarm into a moving shimmer
  • Listen for combination tones — pitches you hear that are not being played

Ellen Arkbro — For Organ and Brass

  • Just-intonation pipe organ and brass holding slow, sparse chords
  • The slowness exposes every partial; tiny re-voicings change the whole colour
  • Contemporary proof the idea is alive — listen for how few notes carry it

Ben Johnston — String Quartet No. 4 (Amazing Grace)

  • A familiar tune set in extended just intonation, drifting through higher and higher harmonics
  • Strings can play any ratio — no fixed grid — so the tuning evolves as the piece does
  • Listen for how pure thirds and sevenths sound against the 12-TET version in your memory

How to run a tuning listen

Listen 01

Hear beating, then hear it stop

The pass
  • Pick one sustained interval from the Young or Arkbro reference. Loop 30–60 seconds of it and do nothing else.
  • Listen for beating — a slow pulsing in loudness, the "wow-wow-wow" of two partials that are close but not identical. In strict just intonation it should be absent or extremely slow.
  • Now hum the root, then the upper note, and try to feel where the interval "wants" to sit. A just fifth feels like it clicks into a slot; a tempered one feels like it is leaning.
  • Switch to a 12-TET reference you know well — any pop record with a sustained synth chord. The same interval now shimmers faintly. That shimmer is the cost of equal temperament made audible.
What to write down
  • One line per reference: did the held interval sit still, slowly shimmer, or actively swarm?
  • Name one moment where a voice moved and the colour of the whole chord changed — that is re-voicing inside a fixed tuning, the main compositional move in this music.
  • Note any pitch you heard that you are sure no one played — a combination or difference tone. Just intonation produces these cleanly; it is a sign the tuning is working.
Decision check
  • Can you now point at "beating" in a recording and say whether it is slow, fast, or absent?
  • Could you describe Young's piece to someone as a tuning rather than as a melody?
  • Did the Johnston quartet's pure thirds sound flat to you at first, then correct? That re-calibration is the ear learning the harmonic series.
  • Adjacent listening in this block: Harry Partch — Castor and Pollux (a full 43-tone just system on custom instruments), Arnold Dreyblatt — Nodal Excitation (overtone-based tuning on a re-strung bass), Duane Pitre — Feel Free (just-intonation ensemble drone), and Kyle Gann — Hyperchromatica (three retuned player pianos). Each answers "which ratios do we keep?" differently.
  • Run the full block index for the rest: Block 6 reference lessons.
Listening rule

You are not listening for melody or for production. You are listening for whether a held interval is at rest. Once you can hear rest, you can hear everything tuning does.
