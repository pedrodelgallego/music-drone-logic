04 — Arrangement

The arc of the piece

Not sections — weather fronts. One curve of density that rises past the middle and settles back to where it began. Times are guides for a 10-minute piece; stretch to taste.

0:00 – 2:00
Foundation alone. The ear acclimates; the partials and the slow modulators become audible one by one. Resist company.
2:00 – 4:00
The interval layer surfaces — a fade of at least sixty seconds, from silence to just-underneath. The beating emerges as the piece's pulse before the listener can say what arrived.
4:00 – 6:00
The air layer drifts in above; the reverb send blooms. The brightest, widest region of the piece — its "open filter" moment, made of level and space, not new notes.
6:00 – 8:00
Erosion. The Digitakt II texture bed rises; Saturator drive climbs a few dB. Density does the work a peak section does elsewhere. The air layer retreats as the dirt advances.
8:00 – 10:00
Subtraction. Texture sinks, interval sinks, the foundation is alone again — changed by everything the listener has heard since minute zero. The last sixty seconds are one layer and the room.

No drop, no climax, no return of a theme. The reward is depth of attention: the same tone at minute nine should feel like a different place than at minute one, though almost nothing happened in between.

Ten production rules

  1. No change completes in under sixty seconds.
  2. No LFO is synced, anywhere, to anything.
  3. Nothing starts or stops — everything surfaces and sinks.
  4. If you can hear a change happen, it was too fast.
  5. One interval for the whole piece; tune the beat rate, then leave it.
  6. Do not add a layer when a level ride would do.
  7. Do not let the reverb carry interest the tone has failed to earn.
  8. Minute 1 and minute 5 must be audibly different states.
  9. Listen to the held tone for five minutes before changing anything about it.
  10. Take the headphones off every fifteen minutes — the drone fatigues ears faster than the loop ever did.
Discipline

The listener should only notice that things are different — never that they changed.
