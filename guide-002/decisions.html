02 — Decisions

Key design decisions

Eight choices made up front, before any sound is recorded. Lab Note B1-001 made its eight for the loop; these are theirs for the drone.

One interval is the harmony

  • Two pitches — a fifth, a fourth, or an octave — for the entire piece
  • With no melody and no chords, this single distance is all the harmony there is
  • Choose it the way you would choose a key, and never revisit it mid-build

No grid — time is in minutes

  • No metronome, no bars, no synced LFOs anywhere
  • The timeline reads minutes and seconds; ramps are measured in breaths
  • Tempo exists only so Ableton's transport can record

Beating is the rhythm section

  • Two tones detuned slightly pulse against each other — no drummer required
  • The arithmetic is exact: a detune of 1 Hz beats once per second
  • Tune the beat rate like you would set a tempo: ~0.3–1.5 Hz for this piece

Three passes of one patch

  • The layers are the same voice recorded three times, not three patches
  • Root, interval, and air pass share one DNA — they fuse instead of stacking
  • Variety comes from register, detune, and level, never from new material

Level is the arrangement

  • No mutes, no entrances — layers rise and sink past each other
  • Which tone is in front is the compositional decision, minute by minute
  • Every fade is sixty seconds or longer; nothing starts, everything surfaces

Record long, edit little

  • Takes are ten minutes, printed whole — a drone is a performance of patience
  • The slow modulators need that long to show their full shape
  • Editing is trimming and crossfading ends, not arranging fragments

Headroom and ear discipline

  • Sustained tone has no gaps — it works the converter and your ears constantly
  • Aim peaks lower than Lab Note B1-001 (-15 to -12 dBFS) and trust the extra headroom
  • Phones at the mark, off every fifteen minutes — loudness creep is invisible inside a drone

Three tools, three roles

  • Ableton records, layers, rides levels, and exports — the tape machine
  • MicroFreak is the voice: every tonal layer comes from one patch
  • Digitakt II is the texture engine: it samples the voice and erodes it
Discipline

The listener should only notice that things are different — never that they changed.
