05 — The build

Step-by-step

Ten steps, each with a decision checkpoint. The wiring is the Lab Note B1-001 studio page, unchanged — keep it open in another tab. Only the settings that differ for beatless work are spelled out here.

Step 01

Create the project — gridless

Open a new set
  • ⌘ N, then Tab to Arrangement view. The whole build lives there.
  • Tempo can stay at any value — nothing will be synced to it. Metronome stays off for the entire project.
  • Right-click the timeline ruler → Time Ruler Format → Time. From here on you think in m:ss, never in bars.
  • ⌘ , → Audio: MOTU M4 in and out, 48000 Hz, Buffer 512 — nothing is played against a clock, so latency is irrelevant and the CPU headroom is free.
  • Record, Warp & Launch: WAV, 24-bit, and turn OFF auto-warp defaults exactly as in Lab Note B1-001 — ten-minute takes must never be quietly time-stretched.
Create the tracks
  • ⌘ T four times: FOUNDATION, INTERVAL, AIR, BED (audio). Inputs: mono Ext. In 1 for the first three (MicroFreak), stereo 3/4 for BED (Digitakt II).
  • ⌘ ⌥ T once: return A — REVERB, Hybrid Reverb, decay 8–12 s, Dry/Wet 100 %.
  • One return only. The echo in this piece sits as an insert on AIR, not on a shared return — its smear belongs to one layer.
  • ⌘ S → save as beatless_lab_002.als. End every session with File → Collect All and Save.
Decision check
  • Is the metronome off and the ruler in minutes? If either still shows bars, the grid will quietly dictate your ramp lengths later.
  • Can you record Ext. In 1 and Ext. In 3/4 cleanly? Run the 30-second silence test from the studio page and listen back for hiss level — a drone exposes the noise floor like nothing else.
Step 02

Design the voice

Choose the engine
  • Shift + press the Type encoder — always start from an initialised patch.
  • Engine: pick one and commit, exactly as Lab Note B1-001 commits to one kick.
Engine choices and what they buy you
HarmoThe default here. Additive, organ-adjacent, every partial addressable — Timbre literally redistributes the overtones the listener will spend ten minutes inside.
WavetableSmooth spectral morph under a slow LFO; darker and grittier than Harmo.
V.AnalogThe purest route — best when you want the beating to be the whole show.
SpeechEerie vowel drones; use only if the piece can absorb its character for its full length.
Starting positions
Oscillator + Filter + Envelope
Wave / Timbre / Shape40 / 60 / 50 — enough harmonic content for the modulators to act on
Filter — Cutoff10 o'clock, dark. The piece brightens by level and layers, not by this knob
Filter — ResonanceLow — resonance plus a 10-second reverb whistles
ENV — Attack2 s or longer; the tone must bloom, never speak
ENV — Sustain / ReleaseFull / long — the note has no edges
ParaphonicOff for the printed passes; each take is one voice
The two slow modulators — the aliveness mechanism
LFOSine, unsynced, one cycle per 15–20 s → Matrix to Timbre, amount ~25
Cycling EnvLoop mode, rise + fall totalling 30–60 s → Matrix to Cutoff or Wave, amount ~15
  • Two modulators at different unsynced rates never realign exactly — the tone cannot repeat itself for the length of any take. This is the same trick as Lab Note B1-001's drone, promoted from supporting role to the entire piece.
  • Hold one note and listen for three full minutes before judging the patch. The patch that bores you at thirty seconds and rewards you at three minutes is the right one.
  • Save as LAB2 VOICE. Every layer of the piece comes from this slot.
Decision check
  • Drop a Spectrum on the monitor track and watch a held note: can you see the two modulators moving different regions at different speeds?
  • Does the bare tone reward a minute of attention with no effects at all? If it needs the reverb to be interesting, the patch is not done.
Step 03

Print the foundation — ten minutes

Gain-stage for sustain
  • Set the M4 input-1 gain while the note holds, not on the attack. Sustained tone has almost no gap between its peaks and its body — it reads hotter and works the converter harder than any kick loop at the same peak value.
  • Target peaks -15 to -12 dBFS — deliberately lower than Lab Note B1-001's target. The headroom pays for the reverb bloom and the Saturator later.
  • Phones at the mark. Write the start time of the session down; you are taking a break at +15 minutes whether it feels necessary or not.
Record the take
  • Press Hold on the MicroFreak, play the root (A1 or to taste), and let it sustain hands-free.
  • Arm FOUNDATION, Monitor Auto, ⌘ ⇧ R — record ten minutes.
  • Minutes 0–2: hands off entirely; the internal modulators carry it. Minutes 2–8: at most three knob moves — cutoff or Timbre — each spread over a full minute. Minutes 8–10: drift back toward the opening state so the take ends near where it began.
  • If you can hear a knob move as an event, it was too fast — that take is raw material for the texture bed, not the foundation. Record again.
Decision check
  • A/B minute 1 against minute 5 of the take at matched level: audibly different states?
  • Could you point at the moment any change happened? You should not be able to.
  • Is the noise floor acceptable under the tone? If hiss intrudes, fix it at the source gain now — every later layer multiplies it.
Step 04

Print the interval — and tune the beating

The arithmetic
  • Two tones close in pitch pulse at their frequency difference: tones 1 Hz apart beat exactly once per second. The beat rate is not a vibe — it is a number you choose.
  • For this piece aim the slow interior pulse at roughly 0.3–1.5 Hz — between one pulse every three seconds and a calm walking pulse.
  • The beating you tune here lives between the foundation's harmonics and the interval layer's — a fifth beats against the root's overtones gently; an octave detuned a few cents beats strongly. The detune knob is your tempo knob.
Record the take
  • Same patch LAB2 VOICE, untouched. Disarm FOUNDATION, arm INTERVAL.
  • Loop the foundation take and monitor it while you record — the interval must be tuned against the real layer, not against memory.
  • Play the chosen interval and nudge the MicroFreak's fine tune while listening to the pair: stop where the slow pulse sits in the target range and feels like breathing, not wobble.
  • ⌘ ⇧ R — record ten minutes, hands off after the first minute. The two takes' modulators are already out of phase with each other; that drift is free and unrepeatable.
Place it
  • Pan FOUNDATION slightly left, INTERVAL slightly right — 15–25 % each, no more.
  • Utility on the master, Width to 0 % for ten seconds: the beating must survive mono. If the pulse vanishes when collapsed, it was a phase trick, not a tuning — narrow the panning and retune.
Decision check
  • Can you name the beat rate in cycles per second? Write it in the project notes.
  • Does the pair sound like one widened tone with a pulse inside — not two instruments?
  • Mono check passed?
Step 05

Print the air, sample the bed

The air pass
  • Same patch, two octaves up. Arm AIR, record five minutes — it will only be used from the piece's middle onward.
  • On AIR: EQ Eight high-pass at 800 Hz, then Echo — unsynced, 300–600 ms, feedback ≤ 30 %, Dry/Wet ~25 %, high-pass in the Echo around 500 Hz. The echo smears; at this feedback it cannot establish a rhythm.
  • Fader starts at -25 dB. The air is light on the ceiling, not a third instrument.
The texture bed — Digitakt II
  • Route per the studio page's Option D: MicroFreak out → Digitakt II inputs — or simpler, play the printed FOUNDATION take out of Ableton and sample the M4 loopback. Capture 30–60 seconds.
  • One track, one trig, sample length maxed, loop on — the fragment becomes a held tone again. Drop it an octave on the SRC page.
  • Track LFO: slowest speed, small depth, destination filter cutoff — the bed breathes over minutes.
  • Resample the playing bed once or twice inside the Digitakt: each generation through its filter and reverb gets darker and grainier. Two generations is usually the sweet spot between texture and mud.
  • Record one quiet pass into BED (Ext. In 3/4) — five minutes, riding only the Digitakt's filter, slowly. In Ableton: EQ high-pass 150 Hz, fader around -20 dB.
Decision check
  • Mute the bed: the piece should feel cleaner but smaller. If muting it changes nothing, it is too quiet or too similar; if muting it removes a rhythm, it has acquired a pulse — re-record without one.
  • All four layers are takes of, or descendants of, one patch. Solo each and confirm the family resemblance.
Step 06

Choreograph the levels

This is the arrangement
  • Lay the takes on the timeline per the arc: foundation from 0:00, interval surfacing at 2:00, air from 4:00, bed from 6:00.
  • A for automation. The only lanes that matter are four volume lanes and one reverb send. Draw with S-curves (right-click → Curve); a straight line over two minutes still reads as a ruler.
  • Every fade spans at least sixty seconds. The interval's entry fade at 2:00 should take 60–90 s from silence to its resting level.
  • Decide, per minute of the piece, which layer is in front — write the sequence on paper first (foundation → foundation/interval equal → air forward → bed forward → foundation). Then draw only what the paper says.
Ride it instead — optional but better
  • ⌘ M, map four Launchpad controls to the four track volumes. Set each mapping's Min/Max to the musical range (e.g. -inf to -8 dB) so the full gesture covers exactly the useful travel.
  • F9, play the whole piece, ride the levels slower than feels natural. Two takes; keep the calmer one. Then Simplify Envelope per lane.
Decision check
  • Play 1:30–3:00 blind: can you say when the interval arrived? You should only know that it is now present.
  • Is any automation lane doing something a listener could whistle? Delete it.
Step 07

Bloom and erosion

The middle blooms
  • Reverb send on FOUNDATION and INTERVAL: from -15 dB at 3:30 to -6 dB at 5:00, back down by 6:30. Space is this piece's brightness.
  • Optionally ease each layer's EQ high-pass down a touch through the bloom — more body exactly when there is more room.
The final third erodes
  • Saturator on BED (or on a group of BED + INTERVAL): Drive from +2 dB at 6:00 to +8 dB at 7:30, retreating by 9:00. Pull Output down to match — the density must rise while the meter does not. The mass-from-density lesson, applied as arrangement.
  • This is the only "intensity" the piece gets. It is made of harmonics, not level, and not events.
Decision check
  • Watch the master meter through 6:00–8:00: it should barely move while the piece audibly thickens. If the meter climbs with the drive, gain-compensation slipped.
  • Does the bloom read as the piece opening, or as "reverb got louder"? If the latter, halve the send automation's range.
Step 08

Subtract and end

Do this
  • From 8:00, sink the layers in reverse order of arrival: bed first, then air, then interval — each over at least sixty seconds.
  • The final 60–90 seconds are the foundation alone, drifting toward its opening state, then a master fade over the last 30–45 seconds into the reverb tail.
  • Where takes are trimmed, use long crossfades (4+ seconds) — a drone exposes every click a groove would hide.
  • Listen to the whole piece end to end once, away from the screen, before declaring the arrangement done.
Decision check
  • Does the ending feel like returning somewhere, not like running out?
  • Is there any moment after 8:00 where something stops? There should not be.
Step 09

The mix pass — sustain rules

Frequency separation
  • FOUNDATION owns everything below 200 Hz. High-pass INTERVAL at 150–200 Hz, AIR at 800 Hz, BED at 150 Hz.
  • Sweep for one buildup region per layer pair and carve 1–2 dB — sustained tones overlap constantly, so small EQ moves do what big ones do in a groove.
The sustain-specific checks
  • Peak vs body: this mix can read quiet on the peak meter and still be loud. Trust perceived loudness at the marked phones level, not the peak number. If your master peaks at -6 dBFS and it feels relentless, turn the layers down, not the master up.
  • Mono check: Width 0 % for ten seconds at three points in the piece — the beating, the bloom, and the erosion must all survive.
  • Quiet check: one full listen at well below the mark. A drone mix that only works loud is borrowing from level.
  • Fatigue protocol: phones off every fifteen minutes, and never make a judgment in the minute after putting them back on.
  • Master: Spectrum + Utility only, peaks ≤ -3 dBFS including the bloom. No limiter — same reasoning as Lab Note B1-001.
Decision check
  • Can you hear all four layers at the quiet listen, each doing its named job?
  • Is the 80–200 Hz region clear when foundation and bed overlap? If not, raise the bed's high-pass before touching anything else.
