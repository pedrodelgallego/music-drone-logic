Tape & Degradation · 01 — Overview

Tape and degradation as method

Every other transformation changes a sound once and stops. Tape and degradation make change accumulate — and make the accumulation the subject. The recording carries the evidence of every pass it survived. This Lab Note builds a whole piece on a single loop that disintegrates in front of the listener, so the path's history stops being invisible plumbing and becomes the music.

The goal

A degrading-loop original

  • One short, pretty loop, played through a degradation path until it dissolves
  • The composition is the sequence of generations — the loop wearing out over time
  • Fully original; the process is borrowed, never the source material

Fundamentals you'll practice

  • Wow & flutter, tape saturation, hiss, and generation loss as deliberate tools
  • Naming change as reversible, cumulative, or generational before committing
  • Resampling chains, generation logging, and arranging decay as form
Core objective

Understand how the deliberate erosion of a single idea — copy of a copy of a copy — can carry a whole piece, and why knowing which kind of change you are committing to is the entire discipline.

Why this exercise matters

The beginner instinct

  • Reach for "a tape plugin" as a one-shot texture, slapped on the master
  • Crank wow and hiss until the effect is obvious, then leave it static
  • Treat degradation as a finish, applied once, rather than a process over time

The opposite discipline

  • Set a single pass so subtle it is barely audible — drama comes from repetition
  • Decide up front how far down the one-way path you intend to walk
  • Let the listener hear a survivor, not a filter: generation 6 should sound like it lived through 1–5

Lab Note B4-004 sits behind Block 4 and its transformation work. It narrows that block to one engine: a medium that damages what passes through it and remembers the damage. The skill transfers anywhere a sound should carry its own history — ambient, hauntology, generative decay, sound design for memory and loss.

Principle

Do less to the loop, but let every pass eat the last. Make the path's history audible.
