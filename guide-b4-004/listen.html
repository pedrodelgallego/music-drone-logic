Lab Note B4-004
Tape & Degradation · 02 — Listen

Hear decay become form

Before you degrade anything, listen to records where the medium is the message. In each piece below, nothing dramatic "happens" — a loop simply survives, wears, and fades. Train your ear to follow the slow erosion as the event, and to accept hiss, wobble, and dropouts as the emotional content rather than as faults to repair.

Listen for the loop wearing out

What to listen for

In all three pieces the source material barely changes — it is the same short phrase looping. What changes is its condition: top end dulls, pitch wavers, noise rises, detail collapses toward the centre. The drama is cumulative and one-way. Notice that you cannot point to a moment where it "got worse"; you only notice that it has.

Listen 01

William Basinski — Disintegration Loop 1.1

Why it is here
  • The canonical case: a 20-year-old tape loop digitised while the oxide literally flaked off the plastic as it passed the head. The recording documents its own death in real time.
  • There is one loop and one process. No arrangement, no new elements — the generational loss of page 03 performed by a failing physical medium, captured once and left alone.
  • Listen for the moment the loop becomes more gap than tone, and ask what makes that moving rather than merely broken. The answer is that you remember generation 0.

Listen 02

Philip Jeck — Surf

Why it is here
  • Jeck works with worn vinyl, dansette turntables, and loops — the crackle, the locked groove, the surface noise are not removed, they are composed with.
  • This is cumulative change foregrounded: the dust and wear of a found record are the texture, layered into a slow tidal motion. The medium's faults are the instrument.
  • Listen for wow and flutter — the seasick pitch drift of a record that is not running true — and notice how it gives motion to material that is otherwise static.

Listen 03

The Caretaker — Everywhere at the End of Time (Stage 1)

Why it is here
  • Old 78rpm ballroom loops, processed to evoke memory and its loss. Stage 1 is the gentlest pass — recognisable, warm, only lightly worn — and the project degrades across later stages until the source is gone.
  • The whole work is the reversible-vs-generational distinction made into a structure: each stage is one rung further down a one-way ladder. Stage 1 is your "weathered, still beautiful" sweet spot.
  • Listen for restraint — how little is done per stage — and decide where on that ladder your own track will live. You do not have to reach dust.

Before you move on
  • I heard at least one piece where a single loop, not new material, carries the whole duration.
  • I can hear the difference between wobble (pitch drift) and hiss (rising noise floor) as separate symptoms.
  • I noticed that the decay is one-way — there is no point where it recovers.
  • I picked a sweet spot on the ladder — worn, or fully dissolved — that I want my own track to reach.
