Lab Note B4-004
Tape & Degradation · 03 — Decisions

Name the change before you make it

Degradation is a one-way door. Before the first pass you have to decide what kind of change you are committing to, and how far down the path you intend to walk. This page is the theory: what tape actually does to a signal, the three kinds of change, what each generation removes, and the handful of decisions that determine whether your track sounds like a survivor or like a filter someone forgot to switch off.

What tape does to a signal

Wow & flutter

  • Slow speed drift (wow) and fast speed jitter (flutter) — the reels never run perfectly steady
  • Heard as pitch instability: a seasick wobble or a fine warble
  • Detunes sustained tones against the grid; gives motion to anything static
  • A little is character; a lot is the sound of a failing machine

Tape saturation

  • The medium is nonlinear: it softly compresses and adds harmonics
  • Rounds transients, thickens the body, tames harsh peaks
  • Hotter recording level = more saturation; record level is sound design
  • Band-limits the extremes — the top and the deep bottom roll off

Hiss & noise floor

  • Tape has a self-noise — a constant bed of hiss under everything
  • Every copy adds its own hiss on top of the inherited hiss
  • Signal erodes while noise grows; eventually they cross over
  • Hiss is the clock of the piece — it tells the ear how worn this copy is

Dropouts & oxide loss

  • Physical media shed material — flakes of oxide, scratches, dust
  • Heard as momentary holes, stutters, and gaps in the loop
  • Irregular and unrepeatable — the source of a tape's "character"
  • The endpoint of all of this together is silence or pure noise
Path question

Tape gathers every other transformation into one box: it is nonlinear (saturation), it has speed-dependent pitch error (a kind of time distortion), and crucially its damage is cumulative across passes. The question is no longer level-in / level-out — it is "what does the tenth copy of a copy sound like, and is any of that drift reversible?"

Three kinds of change — name yours before you start

Reversible vs cumulative vs generational
ReversibleUndo the device or back off the knob and the source returns. A gentle low-pass, a small soft-saturation, a wobble you can switch off. Nothing is lost.
CumulativeDamage adds within a single pass and cannot be undone, but stays bounded. A clipped peak, a printed grain freeze, a record's surface noise. One-way, single dose.
GenerationalEach new pass degrades the previous degraded copy. Loss compounds — copy of a copy of a copy. The endpoint is silence or noise. This is the engine of this Lab Note.
Why the distinction is the lesson

A beginner reaches for "a tape plugin" as a texture. The discipline here is to know which kind of change you are committing to. Generational degradation is a one-way door: you cannot half-undo three passes. Decide how far down the path you intend to walk before you take the first step — and archive generation 0 so the contrast survives even when the source does not.

Generation loss — the copy of a copy

gen 0 copy → gen 1 copy → gen 3 copy → gen 6 …→ dust

What each pass removes

  • High frequencies first — each copy is a little duller than the last
  • Stereo width and detail collapse toward the centre
  • Noise floor rises — hiss accumulates while signal erodes
  • Wow/flutter compounds: pitch drift layered on drift

The Basinski idea

  • A tape loop replayed until the oxide flakes off the plastic
  • The recording documents its own decay in real time
  • The process of vanishing becomes the composition
  • The path's history is not a side effect — it is the entire piece
Why generation 0 must be beautiful

Decay is only moving if there was something worth losing. If the source loop is dull, the degraded version is just noise. Spend real care on generation 0 — a phrase you would happily loop untouched — because the whole emotional arc is the distance between that and the dust. The listener mourns what they heard at the start.

The decisions you actually make

Decision set

Five choices that define the track

1 — How far down the path
  • Pick the endpoint now: worn-but-recognisable (Caretaker Stage 1), heavily eroded, or all the way to dust (Basinski). This sets how many generations you print and how aggressive each pass is.
  • You do not have to reach dust. A track can live entirely in the "weathered, still beautiful" zone and never resolve into noise.
2 — Smooth decay or lurching decay
  • Consistent record level pass to pass = the decay reads as a smooth slide. Vary the level pass to pass = the decay lurches and stumbles.
  • Both are valid. Decide which serves the feeling — gentle fading memory, or a machine actively breaking down.
3 — Which symptom leads
  • You can weight the path toward any one symptom: pitch wobble (wow/flutter forward), noise (hiss forward), dullness (filter forward), or holes (dropouts forward).
  • Leading with one symptom gives the decay a personality. Leading with all of them equally gives generic "lo-fi" — usually the wrong choice.
4 — Real medium or emulation
  • A cassette 4-track or dictaphone loop is the genuine article — flutter and oxide loss no plugin fully models. It is also slow, noisy, and unrepeatable.
  • An in-the-box resampling chain (Saturator + Redux + Vinyl Distortion) is fast, repeatable, and loggable. Page 04 wires up both.
5 — Is there a way back
  • There should not be. The irreversibility is the emotional content. If you find yourself able to "undo" the decay, you built a reversible chain by accident — confirm the loss compounds.
  • The one exception: always keep generation 0 archived, outside the chain. The source survives even though no copy does. That preserved original is what makes the loss legible.
Decision check
  • Have I written down my endpoint — worn, eroded, or dust — before printing a single generation?
  • Is my intended change generational (each pass eats the last) and not accidentally reversible (the same gentle FX, never compounding)?
  • Have I chosen which symptom leads, so the decay has a personality?
  • Is generation 0 a phrase I would happily loop untouched — beautiful enough to be worth mourning?
  • Is gen 0 archived outside the degradation chain, so the contrast survives?
  • Carry forward: you now have an endpoint, a decay shape, a lead symptom, a medium, and a guarantee of irreversibility. Page 04 turns those decisions into a physical or virtual rig; page 06 prints the generations against a log.
