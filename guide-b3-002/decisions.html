Lab Note B3-002
Probability · 03 — Decisions

The theory of conditional events, and the choices it forces

Probability is a compositional decision before it is a knob. This page is the conceptual core: what kinds of uncertainty exist, how they differ, and where the small budget for surprise should be spent so a loop stays coherent.

Two kinds of uncertainty

Principle

Every conditional event is either independent (decided fresh each pass, with memory of nothing) or periodic (decided by a schedule, with perfect memory of the count). Knowing which one you want is the first decision.

Independent — true probability

  • Each pass is a fresh coin flip; the past does not constrain the future
  • You can never say "it will play next bar" — only "about half the time"
  • This is the % condition; it produces genuine, non-repeating surprise

Periodic — deterministic phrasing

  • A perfectly regular clock layered over the loop: every Nth pass
  • You can predict it exactly, yet it still breaks the bar-to-bar sameness
  • This is the A:B condition; it writes a multi-bar phrase from one pattern
  • Why the distinction is load-bearing: independent events give texture — a loop that feels improvised. Periodic events give form — a loop that feels arranged. A great conditional pattern uses both: periodic conditions sketch a phrase you could notate, while a couple of independent conditions keep that phrase from ever printing the same way twice.

Bounded vs. unbounded randomness

The trap to avoid

Unbounded randomness — randomising pitch, sample, timing, and presence all at once — destroys identity. The pattern stops being a thing and becomes a cloud. Bounded randomness changes one dimension of one hit within a fixed range.

Decision 01

Which dimension is allowed to vary

Pick one axis of freedom per hit
  • Presence — whether the hit plays at all (the % condition). The safest place to start; it never alters the sound, only its appearance.
  • Velocity / volume — the hit always plays but at a varying level. A ghost note that occasionally surfaces.
  • Micro-timing — the hit always plays, on time, but nudged a few ticks. Bounded to a few percent of a step.
  • Sample / pitch — the most dangerous axis; vary it only on a hit that is already rare, and only between two or three pre-chosen options.
The compounding rule
  • One hit may carry several rules at once — a 25 % presence, a low velocity-lock, and a small timing nudge — and still read as a single human gesture.
  • But across the whole pattern, keep the count of uncertain hits tiny: two or three. The skeleton must dominate.

Where to spend the uncertainty budget

Decision 02

Protect the strong beats, vary the weak ones

The placement logic
Where conditions belong
Downbeats (kick)Never conditional. The pulse is the anchor everything else surprises against.
Backbeat / main hatMostly fixed. A high-probability condition (65–80 %) at most, so its rare absence becomes the event.
Offbeat ghostsThe home of independent probability. 25–50 % perc, rim, or hat hits.
Phrase markersPeriodic A:B conditions — a hit every 4th or 8th cycle that signals a turn without a new sound.
Reading probability musically
What each percentage feels like
10–25 %Rare sparkle. A hit you forget exists until it lands and re-colours the bar.
35–50 %The workhorse. Present enough to feel intended, absent enough to surprise.
65–80 %Mostly there. Its absence becomes the event — a hole where a hit should be.

Coherence without repetition

Decision 03

Use dependency to keep surprise from sounding loose

Chains make randomness sound intentional
  • A lone random hit can sound like a glitch. The same hit, followed by a dependent (PRE) tail, sounds like a phrase that either arrives whole or not at all.
  • Dependency converts a pile of independent dice rolls into a small rule system — cause and effect the ear can follow.
  • The decision: for each uncertain hit, ask whether it should be free-standing or the trigger for something else. A track of only free-standing probabilities feels twitchy; a track with one or two chains feels composed.
Decision check
  • Have I named, for every uncertain hit, whether it is independent or periodic?
  • Is each uncertain hit varying exactly one dimension, within a bounded range?
  • Is the skeleton large enough that I could hum the pattern from one bar?
  • Is at least one surprise dependent on another, so the randomness reads as logic?
  • If I removed every condition, would a solid, playable loop remain? (It must.)
Principle

Decide the rules before you hear them. A condition chosen on purpose is composition; a condition added until it "sounds random enough" is noise with extra steps.
