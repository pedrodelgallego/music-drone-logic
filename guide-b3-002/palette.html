Probability · 05 — Palette

One conditional pattern, built one condition at a time

A worked example you can program in five minutes and then study for an hour. We start from a dead-static skeleton and add exactly one condition per step, naming what each one buys. By the end the loop never repeats — and you can point to the rule responsible for every surprise.

  • Four kicks, four offbeat hats. Program this with no conditions at all and loop it. It is deliberately boring — that is the point. This is the body the surprises will surprise against. If this skeleton can't hold its own for 30 seconds, fix it before adding a single condition.

Layer 1 — one independent ghost (%)

Condition 01

A 35 % perc hit on step 7

Program it
  • On the perc track place a trig on step 7. Hold it, set TRG condition to 35 %.
  • Loop and count: roughly one bar in three, a perc taps just after the second kick. You can't predict which bar.
  • What it buys: the first crack in the loop's sameness. With one trig, "bar 1" and "bar 2" are no longer guaranteed identical.

Layer 2 — a dependent tail (PRE)

Condition 02

A PRE hit on step 12

Program it
  • On the same perc track, place a trig on step 12. Set its condition to PRE.
  • Step 12 now fires only when step 7 (the previous conditional trig on this track) fired. The two become a single gesture: either a two-note figure or silence.
  • What it buys: the random hit on 7 stops sounding like a glitch. It now reads as the head of a phrase — cause and effect the ear can follow.
  • Try the inverse for contrast: set step 12 to the inverted PRE and it fires only when step 7 didn't — a fill-in that covers the gap. Same two trigs, opposite logic.

Layer 3 — a periodic accent (A:B)

Condition 03

A 1:4 accent on step 15

Program it
  • Place a perc trig on step 15 and set its condition to 1:4. It fires on the first pattern cycle of every four — once a bar group, like clockwork.
  • Unlike steps 7 and 12, this one is fully predictable. That is its job: it imposes a four-bar phrase on a one-bar pattern.
  • What it buys: form. The loop now has a felt downbeat-of-the-phrase. The random hits give texture; this gives architecture.

Layer 4 — the high-probability hat (absence as event)

Condition 04

Hat step 15 at 80 %

Program it
  • Take the final hat (step 15) and set it to 80 %. It plays most bars — but one bar in five it vanishes.
  • Because the hat is otherwise reliable, the ear treats its rare absence as the surprise. A hole where a hit should be.
  • What it buys: a second flavour of uncertainty. Steps 7 and 12 surprise by appearing; this one surprises by disappearing.

  • Reading the pattern: kick and three hats are guaranteed — the skeleton. Hat 15 plays 80 % (its absence is the event). Perc 7 plays 35 % (genuine surprise); perc 12 only plays as a tail when 7 did (PRE chain); perc 15 is a deterministic accent every fourth cycle (1:4 phrasing). Four uncertainties — two that appear, one that disappears, one periodic — over one fixed body. The loop never repeats and never wanders.

Optional layer 5 — a cross-track binding (NEI)

Condition 05

A reverb-tail sample bound to the perc by NEI

Program it
  • On the track directly below perc, load a short reverb-tail or click sample. Place a trig on step 7 and set its condition to NEI.
  • It now fires only when the perc's step-7 condition fired — the tail only ever follows a perc hit that actually happened.
  • What it buys: the appearing hit gains a shadow. Two tracks move together by chance, deepening one event instead of adding a fifth independent one.
Decision check
  • Can I name the rule behind every surprise in this loop?
  • Did I keep the uncertain hits to a handful while the skeleton stayed dominant?
  • Do I have both flavours present — appearing (%) and disappearing (high %) — and both kinds (independent and periodic)?
  • After three minutes of looping, does it still surprise me, or have I memorised the schedule?
