Lab Note B3-002
Probability · 02 — Listen

Hearing conditional logic in finished music

Before you program a single trig, train the ear. These three recordings treat uncertainty as composition. Don't copy them — listen for the mechanism: what stays fixed, what shifts, and how the shift is governed. Each links to a full replication lesson in Block 3.

Three studies in controlled uncertainty

Jlin — "Erotic Heat"

  • Footwork where hits seem to appear and vanish, yet the pulse never breaks
  • Listen for a fixed low spine with everything above it re-weighted constantly
  • Surprise that always lands against a recognisable bar

Replication lesson →

RP Boo — "Bangin' on King Dr."

  • Stark, hand-built footwork: a few samples re-triggered into shifting cells
  • Hear how absence is used — the missing hit is the event
  • A masterclass in doing more with conditional placement than density

Replication lesson →

Rian Treanor — ATAXIA

  • Algorithmic rhythm where probability and ratio drive the form
  • Listen for periodic events (A:B-like cycles) under genuine randomness
  • Coherence held by rule, not by a memorised arrangement

A structured way to listen

Pass 01

Find the skeleton

What never changes
  • Play the first minute twice. On the second listen, ignore everything that moves and name the one or two elements that are always there.
  • That fixed body is what makes the uncertainty legible. Without it the surprises would be noise.
  • In all three references the spine is low and rhythmic — usually a kick or a bass figure that anchors every bar.
Pass 02

Find the uncertain hits

What appears and disappears
  • Pick one bar and try to predict the next one. Where you guess wrong is where a condition lives.
  • Ask of each shifting hit: does it feel random (different every time, no schedule) or periodic (a slow, regular change every few bars)? That distinction is the whole of page 03.
  • Count how few uncertain elements there really are. It is almost always two or three — the rest is fixed.
Pass 03

Find the dependencies

Events that cause events
  • Listen for pairs: when one hit lands, does a second always follow it? That is a chain — a PRE-style dependency by ear.
  • Listen for fills: a bar where the rules briefly change, then snap back. Note that the good ones re-weight the groove; they do not roll over it.
  • Write down, in plain words, the rule set you'd need to reproduce 30 seconds of the track. That sentence is your brief for the build.
  • The originality constraint: these recordings are ear-training, not templates. Take the mechanism — fixed body, measured uncertainty, dependency — and build something that is entirely yours. The track you make in this Lab Note must not reproduce any reference's melody, sample, or arrangement.
