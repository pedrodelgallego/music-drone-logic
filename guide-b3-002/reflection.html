Lab Note B3-002
Probability · 07 — Reflection

Fault-finding, carry-forward, and the principle that survives

Most of the learning happens here, not while programming. Answer slowly, write it down, and be honest about which surprises were composed and which were luck you can't reproduce.

How you know it worked

Success signals
  • The pattern never repeats exactly, yet every bar sounds like the same piece.
  • A clear skeleton plays through the whole track and anchors the surprises.
  • I can name the rule behind every uncertain hit.
  • Independent (%) and periodic (A:B) events do different jobs — texture and form.
  • At least one surprise depends on another (PRE or NEI) and reads as a phrase.
  • Fills re-weight the groove rather than rolling over it.
  • Stripping every condition still leaves a playable loop.
  • The track holds attention for several minutes without new elements.
Failure signals
  • The pattern dissolved into a haze — too many independent randomisations at once.
  • A condition became a memorised hook because its schedule was too obvious.
  • Surprises sounded like glitches because nothing depended on anything.
  • The skeleton wasn't strong enough to carry the bar when a hit dropped out.
  • The fill turned into an EDM-style crescendo and broke the trance.
  • I randomised the sound (pitch/sample) of a frequent hit, destroying the loop's identity.
  • I can't tell which surprises were scheduled and which were chance.

Fault → cause → fix

Diagnosing a probabilistic pattern
Feels twitchy / looseToo many independent % — convert one to A:B for a predictable anchor.
Feels mechanical / memorisedAn A:B is too frequent — lower it (1:8) or swap to %.
Surprises sound orphanedNo dependency — add a PRE tail or NEI binding so a hit becomes a phrase.
Bar collapses when a hit dropsSkeleton too thin — that hit was load-bearing; make it fixed and move the uncertainty elsewhere.
Loses identity over timeYou varied sound, not presence — bound randomness back to a single axis within range.
Can't reproduce a great barIndependent % can't be re-rolled — keep the audio take; curate luck in editing.

Carry-forward

  • To groove & micro-timing: the velocity and timing nudges on conditional hits are where probability meets feel. A surfacing ghost note that's also slightly late is the most human-sounding event you can program.
  • To polymeter & phase: a conditional perc on a 14-step track length stacks chance on top of phase — two independent sources of non-repetition.
  • To arrangement: A:B at long ratios (1:8, 1:16) is a way to write structural turns without leaving one pattern — the section marker that needs no second pattern.
  • To the workshop: bring the rule-system habit to every replication. Ask of any track: what is fixed, what is uncertain, and is the uncertainty random or scheduled?

Reflection questions

  1. Which condition created the most life: %, A:B, PRE, NEI, or FILL?
  2. Was my skeleton strong enough to carry every dropped hit?
  3. Did I keep the uncertain hits to a handful, or did the pattern drift toward a haze?
  4. Could I name the rule behind every surprise, or did some "just happen"?
  5. Did periodic and independent events do genuinely different jobs?
  6. Did my fills re-weight the groove, or did one slip into a crescendo?
  7. If I stripped every condition, would a good loop remain?
  8. Which axis did I let vary — and was it bounded?
  9. Did the track feel like a loop that evolves, or a loop pasted for eight minutes?
  10. What single rule would I change in the next session?
Final principle

Compose the rules, not the variations. A loop that never quite repeats but always could is built from a strong fixed body, a tiny budget of bounded uncertainty, and the discipline to know which surprise is random and which is scheduled.
