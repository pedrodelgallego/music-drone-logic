Self-playing Patches · 05 — Palette

Modulation-habitat recipes

Four habitats with exact rates and depths. Each is a complete self-player you can dial in and trust. The numbers are starting points chosen so no two periods share a small factor — copy them, run the ten-minute test, then nudge to taste. Every rate here is free-running, never tempo-synced.

How to read these recipes

  • Period is one full cycle of the modulator, given in seconds — easier to reason about than Hz. To convert: Hz = 1 / period. So 14 s = 0.071 Hz.
  • Depth is how far the modulator pushes its target, as a rough percentage of the available range. Depth is the leash length; set it once and leave it.
  • The periods within a recipe are deliberately unrelated. If you change one, keep it from landing on a simple ratio of the others.

Recipe 1 — Minimum viable organism

The smallest patch that passes the test: two modulators, one shaping the sound, one shaping the first. Build this before anything fancier.

Two unsynced modulators on a held chord
AudioHeld chord (root + fifth), filter dark, long attack/release
LFO 1 → Filter cutoffSine, period 14 s, depth 40 %
LFO 2 → LFO 1 rateSine, period 23 s, depth 25 %
Combined realignment~322 s — longer than your listening window
LFO 2 · 23 s → rate of → LFO 1 · 14 s → cutoff of → Filter

Recipe 2 — Three-body habitat

Add a third independent motion on the timbre. Three unrelated periods push the full realignment past an hour — the richest you can get from sine LFOs alone.

Three modulators, three targets, three periods
LFO 1 → Filter cutoffSine, period 14 s, depth 40 %
LFO 2 → Wave / timbreTriangle, period 23 s, depth 30 %
LFO 3 → LFO 1 rateSine, period 37 s, depth 20 %
Filter range clampmin ~600 Hz, max ~3.5 kHz — system never turns harsh or muddy
Decision check
  • With hands off, does the timbre and the brightness drift on clearly different clocks?
  • Does the brightest moment still sit below harsh, and the darkest above inaudible?
  • Can you hear any one LFO as an obvious wobble? If yes, halve its depth.

Recipe 3 — Phasing loops (the Eno tape model)

Instead of LFOs, use loops of different lengths — the literal Music for Airports mechanism. Record a few sustained notes, loop each at a different prime-ish length, and let them drift.

Loop lengths chosen to phase, not align
Loop A (note 1)length 17.3 s
Loop B (note 2)length 21.1 s
Loop C (note 3)length 29.7 s
Combined cycleseveral minutes before any two re-coincide as they started
Treatmentlong reverb on a 100 % wet send; each loop slightly different level
  • The notes should be consonant — pick from one chord so any overlap is harmonious. The system is the phasing; the pitch world is fixed by your choice of notes.
  • Avoid round lengths (16 s, 20 s, 24 s) — they realign too soon. The decimals are doing real work.

Recipe 4 — Coin-gated rare events

Layer this on top of any recipe above. A slow clock through a probability gate fires an occasional swell or retrigger — the seasoning that keeps a calm habitat from feeling featureless.

Probabilistic events — sparse by design
Slow clockone pulse every ~11 s (free, unsynced)
Probability (coin)25 % — fires roughly once every 44 s, never on a schedule
Targeta slow ADSR → VCA swell, or a reverb send bump, or a brief filter open
Recoverylong release so the event fades back into the wash, no abrupt edges
Restraint

One rare event source is enough. Two coins firing make the listener start predicting; the magic is in events that arrive seldom and never on a beat you could count.
