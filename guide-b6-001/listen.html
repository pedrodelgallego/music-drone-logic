Lab Note B6-001
Rule-based Sequencing · 02 — Listen

Three pieces where the rule is the composition

Before you write a single condition, listen to music that was authored as a rule-set rather than performed note by note. In each case the composer's real work was upstream of the sound: defining what the system was allowed to do, then letting it run. Listen for the boundary, not the surprise.

How to listen this time

You already know how to hear melody and groove. The new skill is hearing the rule — the invariant the composer fixed, and the small space they left open for the system to move. Ask of each piece: what never changes, and what is allowed to?

Find the invariant

  • What pitch grid, pulse, or spine never moves?
  • That fixed thing is the fence the composer built first

Find the freedom

  • Where does the music wander — and how far can it go?
  • The width of that wandering is the rule's tolerance

Trace the time-scale

  • Does change arrive per bar, per minute, or per movement?
  • Long evolution from short rules is the whole trick

The rep lessons

Rep 01

Laurie Spiegel — The Expanding Universe

Why it belongs here
  • Spiegel built her own software (GROOVE, later Music Mouse) so that processes — slow algorithmic transformations — could carry the piece while she steered. The note-to-note detail is delegated; the shape is authored.
  • Listen for how little "happens" per second and how much has happened per minute. That gap is exactly what a good rule-set buys you.
  • This is the patient end of generative music: rules tuned so the evolution is felt, never witnessed as an event.
Open the rep lesson
  • Full listening guide: rep-laurie-spiegel-the-expanding-universe.html
  • Carry-back to your build: a process that changes one parameter slowly is itself a rule. Write it as "over N bars, X drifts from A to B."
Rep 02

Roland Kayn — Tektra

Why it belongs here
  • Kayn called his work "cybernetic music": he configured networks of feedback and let them self-organise, intervening only to set conditions and constraints. No melodies, no metre — only systems settling into and out of states.
  • This is the extreme case of "the system decides." It teaches you what unbounded behaviour sounds like, and why even Kayn's apparent chaos sits inside carefully built electronic boundaries.
  • Listen for textures that breathe and reorganise on their own. Ask: if I muted my hands entirely, would my system still earn the next minute?
Open the rep lesson
  • Full listening guide: rep-roland-kayn-tektra.html
  • Carry-back to your build: feedback is a rule too — "the output of step N feeds the condition of step N+1." The Digitakt's PRE condition is a miniature of this idea.
Rep 03

Lejaren Hiller & Leonard Isaacson — Illiac Suite, Experiment 1

Why it belongs here
  • The earliest score composed by a computer (1956): the ILLIAC ran rules of species counterpoint, generating notes at random and rejecting any that broke the rule. Generate, test, keep what passes — the exact loop you will run by ear in this Lab Note.
  • Experiment 1 makes the method audible: it is the sound of bounded randomness, where the boundary is a written rule-book and chance only proposes candidates.
  • Listen for how lawful it sounds despite being machine-generated. That lawfulness is the rule-book doing its job — the same job your Digitakt conditions and Live's Scale effect do.
Open the rep lesson
  • Full listening guide: rep-lejaren-hiller-and-leonard-isaacson-illiac-suite-experiment-1.html
  • Carry-back to your build: "generate-and-reject" is your curation pass. Let the rule propose; you keep only what passes the ear.
Listening principle

In each piece, the composer's real authorship happened before any sound: they chose the fence. Your job in this Lab Note is to do the same — and to be honest later about which decisions were yours and which were the system's.
