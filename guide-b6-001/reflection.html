Rule-based Sequencing · 07 — Reflection

Fault, carry-forward, principle

Most of the learning happens here, not while making the track. The defining question of rule-based work: can you still separate what the system decided from what you decided in advance? Answer slowly, and write the answers down.

How you know it worked

Success signals
  • You can recite the whole rule-set from memory in under five sentences.
  • The spine stayed unconditional, and the listener can lock to it.
  • Variation reads as intended, not as accidental — surprise has a fence behind it.
  • There is exactly one chance condition, and the piece would survive without it.
  • The long-form motion comes from drift, not from new elements entering.
  • You recorded more than you kept and curated by a criterion you can name.
  • You can point at any kept moment and say what the rule decided versus what you decided.
  • The rules.txt alone would let someone recreate the piece's behaviour.
Failure signals — and the fault behind each
  • It sounds arbitrary — fault: a rule with no fence, or too many coins. Bound it or cut it.
  • You can't predict anything — fault: probability used where a counted rule belonged.
  • The spine wobbles — fault: a condition crept onto the kick or the offbeat hat.
  • It sounds the same at ten seconds and ten minutes — fault: no drift rule; the system loops instead of evolving.
  • You kept the first take — fault: no curation pass; you recorded too little to hear the system's habits.
  • You can't say what the rule did versus what you did — fault: a decision got baked into audio before it was authored in the control path.

Eleven reflection questions

  1. Which rule produced the most musical surprise — and was it a counted rule or the coin?
  2. Which rule could you remove without weakening the piece?
  3. Did the PRE chain actually read as cause and effect, or did it sound like coincidence?
  4. Did the bounded-choice bass ever find a phrase you would not have written by hand?
  5. Was the single coin earning its place, or was it decoration you should have muted?
  6. Did the drift carry the long form, or did you reach for new elements anyway?
  7. How long did you record, and did that reveal habits a short take would have hidden?
  8. What was your reject criterion during curation — and did you actually honour it?
  9. For your favourite moment: what did the system decide, and what did you decide by keeping it?
  10. Did keeping the audio path and control path separate help, or did you fuse them too early?
  11. If you handed someone only the rules.txt, what would they get right — and what would be lost?

Carry-forward

  • Carry the fence, not the values. The specific notes {A1, C2, E2} are disposable; the habit of writing a pitch / rhythm / dynamic fence around every chance is the transferable skill.
  • Carry the order of reaching. Deterministic, then counted/logical, then chance — last and once. Next time, notice how often you want to start at chance and don't.
  • Carry the long record. Run any future generative system longer than feels necessary, then curate. The first take is rarely the system at its best.
  • Carry it into code. The same six sentences are a Pbind, a Pd patch, a Tidal one-liner, a VCV rack. The discipline outlives any one box.
Final principle

Randomness without boundaries is drift. Boundaries without randomness is a loop. Rule-based music is the narrow band between them — and the composer's real work is drawing the band, then being honest about which side of it each sound came from.
