Rule-based Sequencing · 03 — Decisions

Turning an intention into explicit rules

A rule is a musical intention made literal enough for a machine to execute without you. Before any randomness, the discipline is writing down what should happen and under what condition — so the system has somewhere to surprise you from. This page is the theory you decide on before the rig is even powered on.

Intention → rule → boundary

Every generative patch is really two paths kept apart: the audio path (what makes sound) and the control path (what decides). A rule lives entirely in the control path. Write it before you touch a sound.

Musical intention → Explicit condition → Bounded outcome → Audio path

A vague wish

  • "The percussion should feel less mechanical."
  • "I want it to evolve."
  • Nothing here can be executed — it's a feeling, not a rule.

The same wish as a rule

  • "The rimshot on step 11 plays only on 1 of every 4 pattern cycles."
  • "When the previous trig played, this one is silent."
  • Now a machine can run it, and you can hear whether you were right.
Principle

A rule you cannot state in one sentence is not yet a rule — it is a hope.

The audio path and the control path

Keeping these two separate is the single most important decision of the whole exercise. It is what lets you change how something is decided without changing what it sounds like, and vice versa.

What lives in each path
Control pathConditions, probabilities, sequencers, LFOs as modulators, MIDI generators, Max logic. It decides when and which — and emits no sound itself.
Audio pathOscillators, samples, filters, the signal that reaches the speakers. It is told what to do; it never decides.
The interfaceA note, a gate, a trig, a CC value. The control path speaks here; the audio path listens.
  • Why it matters in practice: if your rules are MIDI flowing into an instrument, you can swap the instrument, re-voice the part, or print a different sound — the composition (the rule) survives. If you bake the decision into a recorded audio take, the rule and the sound fuse and can never be separated again. Generate in the control path; commit to audio only when curating.

Bounded versus unbounded randomness

Unbounded

  • Any note, any time, any velocity
  • Feels arbitrary because it is — no fence to read against
  • Sounds the same after ten seconds as after ten minutes

Bounded

  • Three allowed notes, on allowed steps, within a velocity range
  • Surprise reads as choice because there's a wall behind it
  • This is the entire craft: design the box, let chance move inside it

A boundary can fence three independent dimensions. Decide each one on purpose — most weak generative patches forgot one.

The three fences of any bounded system
Pitch fenceA scale, a chord, or a fixed set of allowed notes. The Scale MIDI effect and the Digitakt's per-track scale are pitch fences.
Rhythm fenceWhich steps are even eligible to fire, and the swing/micro-timing window around them. The grid is a fence; conditions narrow it further.
Dynamic fenceA velocity / volume range. Bounded loudness is what keeps a probabilistic part sitting inside the mix instead of poking through it.

Rule, chance, and the order to reach for them

Not all conditions are equal. A logical rule is something you can predict and defend; a probabilistic one is a coin you delegated a decision to. Spend the budget in this order.

From most authored to least
1 — Fixed / deterministicThe spine: kick, root, pulse. Never conditional. The listener must be able to lock to it.
2 — Counted / logical1:4, PRE, FIRST, every-N. Predictable, defensible variation. Most of your conditional budget goes here.
3 — ProbabilisticThe percentage condition. One coin, rarely, never as backbone. It produces motion you did not author — use it last.
  • The trap: probability is the easiest condition to reach for and the weakest. 1:4 is a rule — you can predict and defend it. 35 % is a coin — it produces motion you didn't author. Spend your conditional budget on logic first, chance last.

The choices you are actually making

Decide these before the build page
What is the spine?The one or two parts that are always fixed. Write them down; they are off-limits to every rule.
What is allowed to vary, and how?For each conditional part: which dimension (pitch / rhythm / dynamic) and which condition type.
Where does each rule get written?Digitakt trig conditions, Live MIDI clip, a MIDI effect, Max for Live, or a code path. One home per rule.
How long must it run to reveal itself?A 1:8 condition needs at least eight cycles before you've heard the rule, not an accident.
What is your reject criterion?How will you decide, by ear, which generated passes you keep? Name it now, before you're attached to a take.
Decision check
  • Can I recite my whole rule-set out loud in under five sentences? If not, it is too complex to evaluate honestly.
  • Is the spine genuinely unconditional, or did a rule sneak onto the kick?
  • For every chance condition, can I name what it does and why a logical rule wouldn't do it better?
  • Is every rule in the control path, with nothing decision-making baked into audio yet?
Carry forward

Randomness without boundaries is drift. Boundaries without randomness is a loop. Generative music is the narrow band between them — and you draw the band.
