Lab Note B6-001
Rule-based Sequencing · 06 — The build

Build a piece whose pattern your rules generate

Now you write the rule-set down, then make it audible. Every step is small, every step has a decision checkpoint, and every condition, value, and route is spelled out. Keep the setup page open for routing and the rule vocabulary open for the named shapes — this page assembles a track out of exactly those. The result must be fully original: you author the conditions, no borrowed pattern.

Step 01

Write the rule-set on paper first

State the whole system before any gear
  • This is the actual composition. Write it out in full sentences before powering anything on. If you cannot recite it, you cannot later separate what the system did from what you intended.
  • Here is the rule-set this build uses. Copy it; then change one value so it is yours.
The rule-set — five named shapes, nothing more
R1 — Spine (Fixed)Kick on steps 1, 5, 9, 13, no condition. Closed hat on offbeats 3, 7, 11, 15, no condition. Never left to a rule.
R2 — Rim (Counted)Rim on step 11, condition 1:4. Punctuates once every four loops.
R3 — Ghost (Conditional chain)Ghost snare on step 7, condition PRE, scanned after the rim. Answers only when the rim fired.
R4 — Bass (Bounded choice)Bass note per trig chosen only from {A1, C2, E2}, on allowed steps 1, 4, 7, 11, 14, velocity 70–100. Root A1 always on step 1 (fixed within the rule).
R5 — Flutter (Chance)Closed-hat retrig on step 15, condition 25 %. The single coin in the whole piece.
R6 — Drift (Process)Bass filter cutoff opens 600 Hz → 3 kHz over bars 1–96, holds, then closes to 800 Hz over bars 160–200.
Mark the fence for each rule
  • Beside each rule, note which of the three fences it respects: R4 has a pitch fence (three notes) and a dynamic fence (vel 70–100); R5 has a rhythm fence (only step 15 is eligible).
  • R1 is the spine — off-limits to every other rule. Underline it twice.
  • Tempo 124, 4/4, A natural minor. Pattern length 16 steps, scale 2× so the loop spans two bars before repeating.
Decision check
  • Can I recite all six rules from memory? If not, cut one until I can.
  • Is the spine genuinely unconditional? Re-read R1 — no 1:4 crept onto the kick.
  • Does each conditional rule name its fence? A rule with no fence is unbounded drift.
  • Is there exactly one chance condition? Two coins and you stop being able to predict your own piece.
Step 02

Wire the clock and prepare the project

One clock, verified
  • ⌘ N, Tab to Arrangement. Tempo 124, 4/4. Right-click the ruler → Time Ruler Format → Time so positions read as m:ss.
  • Sync per the setup page: Live is clock master; Digitakt II and MicroFreak receive clock + transport; neither sends clock. Press play and confirm both boxes read 124 and start/stop instantly.
  • Why this gates everything: a 1:4 rule counts pattern passes. If the Digitakt counts against a drifting clock, R2 fires on the wrong bar and the rule you wrote is not the rule you hear.
Tracks
  • ⌘ T twice for audio tracks: DT DRUMS (Audio From → Ext. In 3/4) and MF BASS (Ext. In 1).
  • ⌘ T once for a MIDI track RULES → MF that will host the bass rule and route MIDI out to the MicroFreak (MIDI To → MicroFreak, channel 1).
  • ⌘ ⌥ T for return A — ECHO (Echo, sync 3/16, feedback 40 %, HP 250 Hz, 100 % wet).
  • ⌘ S → save as labnote_b6-001.als in a dedicated project folder.
Step 03

R1 — program the fixed spine on the Digitakt II

Set the canvas
  • New Digitakt II project B6-001, pattern A01. Per-track scale length 16, scale multiplier 2× (two bars per loop). Per-track key: A minor.
  • Assign tracks: T1 KICK, T2 HAT, T3 RIM, T4 GHOST. Leave 5–16 empty — they are headroom, not a to-do list.
  • Choose one sample per track and commit: short dark kick (tune its fundamental to A), tight closed hat (HP ~500 Hz), a rim/click, a soft ghost snare.
Place the spine — no conditions
  • Grid-record T1 KICK on steps 1, 5, 9, 13. Confirm COND is off on all four — verify by holding each trig; the condition field must read blank.
  • Grid-record T2 HAT on 3, 7, 11, 15, COND off. P-lock alternating velocity (hold trig, turn level) so offbeats breathe instead of ticking.
  • Add 51–54 % swing. Micro-time the hats a hair late for roll.
  • Loop it and listen for two minutes. This is the frame; it must feel locked and unremarkable. If it already bores you in a good way, it is doing its job.
Decision check
  • Can I nod to this without thinking? The spine must be lockable.
  • Are all spine trigs unconditional? Hold each one and confirm the condition field is blank.
  • Does it leave rhythmic room on steps 7 and 11 for the conditional layer to land?
Step 04

R2 + R3 — the counted rim and its conditional answer

R2 — Rim, counted
  • On T3 RIM, place one trig on step 11. Hold it and turn COND to 1:4.
  • Loop for at least four cycles (eight bars). The rim must appear on exactly one loop in four. Count it out loud — if it lands somewhere else, your clock sync (Step 02) is wrong.
  • Why counted, not chance: you can predict the rim. That predictability is the rule announcing itself — the listener learns to anticipate it.
R3 — Ghost, conditional chain
  • On T4 GHOST, place one trig on step 7. Hold it and set COND to PRE.
  • PRE looks at the previous conditional trig in scan order. Because the rim (step 11) of the previous loop is the most recent conditional event, the ghost answers it. Verify by listening: the ghost should appear only on the loop right after the rim fired, never on its own.
  • If the chain feels wrong, check scan order — PRE chains across the conditional trigs as the sequencer scans them, so the rim must be the conditional event immediately preceding the ghost.
Optional NEI branch (the else)
  • Want an if/else? Add a quiet alternative hit on step 7 with condition NEI (negated PRE) on a second track: when the rim did not play, this fills the gap instead. Two branches, one decision.
  • Keep it optional — if the groove is already alive without it, leave it out. Restraint is the rule too.
Decision check
  • Loop for three minutes. Can I predict the rim? I should — that's a rule, not noise.
  • Does the ghost only ever appear after the rim? If it drifts free, the PRE chain is broken — recheck scan order.
  • Is the counted/conditional layer still clearly subordinate to the spine?
Step 05

R4 — the bounded-choice bass, generated in Live

Build the control path in Ableton
  • On the MIDI track RULES → MF, create a 2-bar clip. Place notes only on the allowed steps 1, 4, 7, 11, 14. Put A1 on step 1 and leave the other four as any allowed note for now — the rule will choose.
  • Add MIDI effects in this order (the order is the rule):
The bass rule as a MIDI effect chain — control path
1 — RandomChance 100 % on the four free steps, Choices 2, Scale (semitone step) set so it deviates within the triad's span. This is the bounded choice.
2 — ScaleA natural minor. The pitch fence: any deviation Random produced is pulled back into key. Now stray notes are impossible.
3 — VelocityRange 70–100, Random amount small. The dynamic fence — the bass can vary in weight but never poke out of the mix.
4 — Per-note chance (clip)Set step 1 (the root) to 100 %; set steps 4, 7, 14 to ~80 % so the bass occasionally leaves a gap. Step 1 root is fixed within the rule.
  • Free-code equivalent: this whole chain is one Pbind in SuperCollider — \degree, Pwrand([0,2,4],[0.5,0.3,0.2],inf) with \amp ranged — or in TidalCycles a fenced n pattern over an A-minor scale. The Digitakt route: lock the three allowed notes across the eligible trigs and add a % condition for the gaps.
Audio path — the MicroFreak voice
  • Shift + press Type to init a patch. Wavetable, Paraphonic off, monophonic. Cutoff 11 o'clock, resonance 9 o'clock, fast attack, short decay — the note must speak instantly against the kick.
  • The MicroFreak is told what to play by the rule; it decides nothing. Save the patch as B6 BASS.
  • Loop the rule for three minutes. Listen for whether the choice reads as a choice — it should feel composed because the fence is behind it, not arbitrary.
Decision check
  • Does every generated note land in A minor? If even one is out, the Scale effect is misplaced in the chain — it must come last among the pitch rules.
  • Does the bass ever leave a gap (the 80 % steps)? Gaps are where the kick and the listener breathe.
  • Is the velocity staying inside 70–100? If a note jumps out, tighten the dynamic fence before touching the fader.
Step 06

R5 — the single coin

One chance condition, placed last
  • On T2 HAT, add a trig on step 15 as a retrig (hold trig → RTRG → 1/16, two or three repeats). Set COND to 25 %.
  • This is the only place in the whole piece where the system, not you, authors an event. Honour that by using it exactly once.
  • Loop and listen. The flutter should surprise you when it lands precisely because everything else is predictable.
Decision check
  • Mute the 25 % flutter for two minutes. Does the groove still feel alive? If yes, the coin was decoration — leave it muted and you have a stronger, more defensible piece.
  • Is this genuinely the only probabilistic condition? Count them. The answer must be one.
Step 07

R6 — the drift that carries the long form

A slow rule on one parameter
  • Drop an Auto Filter (low-pass, 24 dB) on MF BASS. A for automation, pencil (B) for drawing.
  • Draw the drift exactly as R6 states it:
Bass Auto Filter drift (times → bars at 124 BPM)
Cutoff @ 0:00 (bar 1)~ 600 Hz — closed, the rule starts dark
Cutoff @ 3:05 (bar 96)~ 3 kHz — fully open, the brightest point
Cutoff @ 3:05 – 5:10 (bars 96–160)hold ~ 3 kHz — let the openness sit
Cutoff @ 6:25 (bar 200)~ 800 Hz — closes back, the circle nearly completes
  • Right-click each segment → Curve into gentle S-curves. No ramp shorter than 8 bars; most span 32–64. If you can hear the filter move as an event, the ramp is too short.
  • Add a second, slower drift on a different parameter — e.g. Echo send rising over bars 120–150 then falling — at a rate that never aligns with the filter. Two unaligned drifts never repeat exactly; that is the long-form mechanism doing the work no new element could.
Decision check
  • Play any 60-second window: can I hear the filter change, or only notice it is different? It must be the latter.
  • Do the two drifts ever line up into an obvious repeat? If so, offset one rule's length so they stay out of phase.
Step 08

Run the system long and capture it

Record more than you will keep
  • A 1:4 rule needs at least four loops to be heard; the 25 % coin needs dozens of cycles before its real behaviour shows. Short recordings hide the system.
  • Arm DT DRUMS and MF BASS (monitor Auto). ⌘ ⇧ R. Record a single continuous pass of at least 8 minutes — twice the final length — so the rules have room to reveal their habits.
  • Keep your hands off the conditions during the take. The rules are the performer; you are recording what they do, not overriding them. The only live moves are R6's drifts (drawn, not played) and, optionally, performing the Digitakt FILL a couple of times.
Gain-stage and verify
  • Drums peak just below -6 dBFS; bass -12 to -6. Headroom matters: the coin can stack a retrig onto a loud loop unexpectedly.
  • Sidechain the bass to the kick: Compressor on MF BASS, sidechain from DT DRUMS Pre-FX, ratio 4:1, attack 1 ms, release 120 ms, ~3 dB reduction per kick.
Decision check
  • Did I record long enough that the 1:8-scale behaviours actually occurred several times? If a rule only fired once, I have an anecdote, not a sample of the system.
  • Is the take clean — no clipping when the coin and the rim coincide?
Step 09

Curate — the second composition pass

Generate-and-reject, by ear
  • This is the Illiac Suite method (page 02): the rule proposed many bars; you keep only what passes. Curation is composition, not cleanup.
  • Listen through the 8-minute pass and mark, with locators, the windows where the rules produced the most compelling behaviour — the loop where the rim's PRE ghost answered most musically, the stretch where the bounded bass found a phrase you would not have written.
  • Name your reject criterion out loud before you cut: e.g. "keep windows where the variation feels intended; cut windows where the coin fired so often it read as noise."
Assemble the arrangement
  • Add locators at section boundaries: INTRO (0:00), RULES IN (1:00), FULL (2:00), PEAK (3:05), STRIP (5:10), OUTRO (6:25).
  • Cut on kick downbeats only (⌘ E, grid snap on). Overlap takes by one bar with 4-beat crossfades — butt-joins click.
  • INTRO: spine only, no conditional layer — let the listener learn the frame. RULES IN: the rim and ghost enter; the rule announces itself. FULL: bounded bass joins; R6 drift climbing. PEAK: nothing new enters — the filter drift reaching 3 kHz is the peak. STRIP: drop the bass for 32 bars; the rules feel bigger by their absence. OUTRO: spine and drift close the circle, cutoff returning to 800 Hz.
  • ⌘ J consolidate each finalised section into one clean clip.
Decision check
  • For every kept window, can I say what the rule decided and what I decided by keeping it? That sentence is the whole exercise.
  • Does the PEAK arrive because the drift opened, not because something was added?
  • Did I reject any window for an honest reason I can name, or just because I got bored? Name it.
Step 10

Mix, export, and archive the rule-set

Quick mix and master check
  • EQ HP 25–30 Hz on drums; bell-cut the bass at 200–300 Hz if woolly. Below 200 Hz: kick owns 40–80, bass owns 80–180, nothing else.
  • Faders: drums -10, bass -12, echo return -10. Master peaks ≤ -3 dBFS including any coincident hits. No limiter — this is a study, not a release.
Export and preserve the system
  • Select 0:00 → final length. File → Export Audio / Video. WAV, 48 kHz, 24-bit, Normalize OFF. Filename labnote_b6-001_v1.wav.
  • File → Collect All and Save. Save the Digitakt project and the B6 BASS patch.
  • Archive the rule-set itself as labnote_b6-001_rules.txt beside the WAV — the six sentences from Step 01. The piece exists in four recoverable layers now: the written rules, the Digitakt conditions, the Live MIDI chain, and the audio. The rules are the most important of the four.
Decision check
  • Could someone else recreate this piece's behaviour from the rules.txt alone? If yes, you authored a system, not just a take.
  • Listen once quietly on laptop speakers: does the bounded bass still sit, or does the coin jump out? Fix the fence, not the fader.
Principle

You wrote six sentences, ran them, and recorded more than you kept. The track is the part of the system's behaviour you were willing to defend. That is rule-based composition.
