Set Structure & Rehearsal · 05 — Arc & panic map

The two maps you tape to the rig

Memory fails under stage adrenaline. Two written maps do not. The first is the energy arc — section, time, and the single thing that changes. The second is the panic table — symptom, action, and the floor you recover to. Print both, tape them where you glance, and rehearse against them.

The 32-minute arc map

Minutes are targets, not a click track. The point is the proportion: a long settle, the longest develop, a peak by opening, a strip that flatters it.

Settle → Establish → Develop → Peak → Strip → Resolve
Section → time → the one thing that changes
Settle — 0:00–4:00Drone + atmosphere only. No kick. Let the room arrive.
Establish — 4:00–10:00Kick in at 4:00, bass under it by 6:00. Hat unmuted ~8:00, perc ~9:00 — one at a time.
Develop — 10:00–18:00Bass filter climbs 600 Hz → 2 kHz across the whole span. Resample layer enters ~14:00.
Peak — 18:00–23:00Filter to 3.5 kHz, reverb send up, feedback near its ceiling. Nothing new enters.
Strip — 23:00–28:00Perc out at 23:00, hat thinned by 25:00, drum gain down 2 dB. The space returns.
Resolve — 28:00–32:00Bass out ~28:00, kick out ~30:00. Drone + atmosphere carry the close. Fade the master over the final minute.
The ride controls across the arc — slower than feels natural
Bass filter cutoffClosed through Settle/Establish; climbs across Develop; brightest at Peak; home by Resolve
Reverb send (drone)Low until Develop; blooms at Peak; recedes through Strip
Echo feedback40 % default; brief swell to ~65 % into the Peak, then back — never parked high
Drum saturation+3 dB default; +8 dB through Peak for density without a new element
Hands at restLong holds inside Settle and Develop — the map should show where you do nothing
The mistake the map prevents

Peaking early. A set that hits maximum energy at minute ten has thrown away its arc and left twenty minutes with nowhere to climb. The map keeps your hands honest when the room's energy tempts you to spend everything at once.

The panic table

Symptom on the left, the action you have rehearsed in the middle, the floor you head for on the right. When something goes wrong you read across one row — you do not invent.

Symptom → action → recover floor
Digitakt II crashes / freezesMute its channel, lean on MicroFreak + Ableton, reboot it
Recover: drone + kick from Ableton/MF, fold Digitakt back on a bar line—
Laptop / Ableton freezesHardware holds its last state; carry the set on the boxes alone
Recover: Digitakt groove + MicroFreak drone until Ableton is back, re-sync on next play—
Feedback / resample runs awayHit the kill pad; limiter is the backstop
Recover: silence the path, drop to drone + kick, rebuild slowly—
A cable dies / synth drops outMute that channel cleanly on the bar so it reads as an arrangement choice
Recover: hold the thinned groove, reseat the cable, fold the voice back in—
Wrong scene / wrong unmuteCommit to it for a bar, correct on the next downbeat
Recover: make it sound intentional; never flinch mid-bar—
Clock drift (boxes off-tempo)Stop transport, restart; hardware re-syncs cleanly
Recover: do it during a natural drop, not mid-groove—
The recover floor

Every row lands in the same place: drone plus kick at a steady level, everything else muted. One destination, reachable from anywhere. You are never improvising a rescue — you are heading for a room you have stood in a hundred times in rehearsal.

  • Tape both maps to the rig. The arc on the left of the controller, the panic table on the right. Under adrenaline you will not remember; you will glance.
  • The panic table is rehearsed, not read. By show time you should not need to look — but it is there for the night your hands forget and your eyes have to.
  • One floor, many doors. Whatever fails, the destination is identical. That single shared endpoint is what makes recovery fast enough to be invisible.
