Lab Note B7-004
Set Structure & Rehearsal · 07 — Reflection

Fault, carry-forward, principle

Most of the learning happens here, after the run, not during it. Listen back to your recorded set with the arc map beside you. Answer slowly, and write the answers down — adrenaline lies about how it went.

How you know it worked

Success signals
  • The whole set reads as one statement, not a sequence of tracks.
  • The peak arrived because things opened, not because layers were added.
  • There were stretches where your hands were still and the set stayed alive.
  • Transitions overlapped — you cannot point to where a section ended.
  • The settle held long enough to feel like a risk.
  • The strip made the peak feel bigger in hindsight.
  • Every failure you drilled recovered to the same floor without a flinch.
  • Mute, kill, and recover were each findable blind.
  • You can explain why each section changes the one thing it changes.
Failure signals
  • The set peaked early and had nowhere left to climb.
  • Energy came from stacking layers, not from opening what was there.
  • Your hands never rested — you rode everything and flattened the evolution.
  • Transitions were cuts; the set sounded like a playlist.
  • The settle was rushed because the silence felt uncomfortable.
  • A failure mode you never rehearsed appeared, and you froze.
  • The recover floor was a vague intention, not a rehearsed destination.
  • You looked down to find a panic control mid-crisis.

Eleven reflection questions

  1. Did the set hold together as one arc, or did it fragment into sections?
  2. Where did the peak actually land — and was it earlier than the map said?
  3. Which transition was the most invisible, and why did it work?
  4. Which section was doing too much? What would you remove?
  5. Where were your hands still — and where should they have been?
  6. Did the two-minute hands-off test really pass, or did you intervene?
  7. Which failure was hardest to recover from, and is it reflex yet?
  8. Could you find mute, kill, and recover without looking?
  9. Was the recover floor a place you reached calmly, or improvised toward?
  10. Did the strip make the peak feel bigger, or just smaller?
  11. What would you change in the arc before the next run?
The fault worth naming

Name the single biggest fault in this run — the early peak, the rushed settle, the failure you froze on. One fault, named precisely, is worth more than a vague sense that "it went fine." The named fault is your next rehearsal's agenda.

Carry forward

Carry two things into the next set: the one section that worked best, to protect, and the one fault you named, to fix. Everything else can wait. A set improves one decisive change at a time — the same discipline you applied to the arc, applied to your own practice.

Final principle

The best set is not the busiest one. It is the one developed statement that breathes on its own — and the rig that recovers from anything without the room ever knowing. Shape the time; rehearse the failure; then trust both.
