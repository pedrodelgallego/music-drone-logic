Drone Pathway Map

Drone is the workshop's flagship skill. Block 2 introduces it and the Held Tone Test certifies it. Every block after that builds on the ability to sustain a sound with intention — making it move slowly, making it sit in a room, and eventually making it say something personal. This map helps you find the right lab based on the thing you are currently struggling with.

Why drone?

A drone removes rhythm as a crutch. When there is no beat to anchor the listener, every other decision — pitch, texture, movement, space — carries more weight. Learning to hold a tone well teaches you to hear the things you have been hiding behind the kick drum. That is why drone is not one topic among many: it is the test that runs through everything else in this workshop.

Find your lab by weakness

Read the left column. Find the sentence that matches what you are currently stuck on. The lab in the right column is your next step.

What you are struggling with Lab What it proves
I cannot hold a single tone without wanting to add something or move on Lab 004b First held tone — you sustain one sound for an entire session without breaking the rule
My sounds move too fast; I cannot make a change feel slow and deliberate Lab 005 Slow modulation — you set an LFO (a slow automatic knob-mover) so that the change takes minutes, not seconds
I have never made a piece that is long enough to feel like a drone work Lab 005b Long-form drone — you produce and print a piece that runs for at least ten minutes
I am not sure my instrument is actually in tune; I cannot rely on my ear yet Lab 026 Drone as tuning proof — you use a held tone to verify and correct intonation before any recording session
I do not know how to use silence or when to let a sound fully disappear Lab 033b Drone as threshold and silence — you score the exact moment a sound crosses from presence to absence
I can make drone technically but it does not feel like mine; it sounds like everyone else's ambient music Lab 034 / Capstone C7 Drone as personal voice — you finish a piece that only you could have made, using your rig and your decisions
How to use this map

Pick the one row that matches your current weakness. Do that lab. Do not skip ahead to "drone as personal voice" before you can hold a tone. The sequence exists because each skill is a building block for the next one. The Held Tone Test in Block 2 is the checkpoint — if you have not passed it, that is your starting point regardless of which lab number you are on.

The Held Tone Test

The test at the end of Block 2. You pass by doing all three things.

One sound onlyNo layers, no second synth, no percussion. One oscillator or one sample, running continuously.
Minimum ten minutesThe recording must be long enough that the listener stops waiting for something to happen and starts listening to what is already there.
One intentional changeSomething must change — filter, pitch, room size — but slowly enough that you cannot hear the moment it started moving. The listener notices the result, not the gesture.
  • You can repeat any lab. If Lab 005 feels shaky, redo it before moving to Lab 005b. The map is a guide, not a lock.
  • Drone skills transfer. Every technique you build here — slow modulation, long form, space, silence — applies directly to the club tracks, the performance work, and the capstone pieces later in the workshop.
