The Field Kit

A recorder, contact mics, and an induction coil give you three completely different ways to capture the same place. This bench lab has you assemble the kit, learn how to handle it, and finish by recording the same object three ways: through air, through its surface, and through its electrical field.

The point

In the studio, a bad level means you do it again. In the field, there is no again — the train passes once, the ice cracks once. Setting the right level is the most important skill for field recording. Everything else can be fixed later; a bad level cannot.

How this lab works

The three capture paths

  • Air: the recorder’s microphones pick up what the room or street carries through the air
  • Solid: a contact mic (or piezo) pressed against a surface picks up vibrations traveling through the material itself
  • Electromagnetic: an induction coil picks up electrical fields that circuits leak — sounds your ears cannot hear at all without it
  • Liquid: a hydrophone dropped below the surface picks up what water carries — a world your ears can never enter directly

Gain in the field

  • You have one preamp (the recorder’s built-in) and one meter — and no second take
  • Set the level for the loudest thing that might happen, not the quietest thing happening right now
  • Rehearse any level change before the moment arrives — the same way a photographer pre-focuses

Two checklists, one habit

  • The kit checklist: count everything out, count everything back
  • The ethics checklist: cause no harm — to people, places, or the gear
  • Check both before you leave and when you return — every time, without exception
Pass standard
Minimum passThe kit is assembled and verified. The three-path comparison exists. The field log, fault entry, and system diff are filed.
Solid passEvery recording in the comparison is usable: no clipping, no wind blast, no handling thumps, and the levels are defensible.
Advanced transferYou can walk into an unfamiliar location, pick the right capture path before recording, and explain why.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
Air vs solid vs electromagneticThree ways to pick up sound from the same object. A microphone hears what the air carries. A contact mic (or piezo) hears what surfaces carry — press it to a fence and the fence's vibrations become the recording. An induction coil hears what electrical circuits leak — a hum or chatter your ears cannot detect at all. Same object, three completely different sounds.
The liquid path — the hydrophoneA hydrophone is a sealed microphone designed to work underwater. Drop it below the surface and it picks up boat hum, ice groans, shrimp snapping, rain striking from above — sounds that simply do not exist in air. Like the contact mic, it bypasses air completely. Water carries sound so efficiently that a distant engine can hit your recorder much louder than you expect, so watch your level. Rinse and dry the cable before you store it.
The recorder’s gainThe recorder’s input level (gain) is the one knob between the world and your file. Set it for the loudest thing that might happen, not the quietest sound you hear right now. A level that works for birdsong will clip and distort when the truck arrives.
Self-noiseEvery microphone and preamp produces a faint background hiss — this is called self-noise. In the studio, room sound covers it up. In a silent forest with the gain turned up high, that hiss can become the loudest thing in your recording.
Wind and handlingTwo of the most common ways to ruin a field recording. Wind hitting a bare microphone does not sound like wind — it makes a loud, low blast that drowns everything. Moving your fingers on the recorder makes thumps. Both are easy to prevent: put foam over the capsule to block wind, and hold still (or set the recorder down) once you press record.
Monitoring in the fieldUse closed headphones so you are listening to what the recorder is capturing, not just the location with your own ears. The two will sound different more than you expect. Keep one ear free near roads and rails — wearing both headphones fully sealed means you cannot hear approaching traffic, which is a safety risk.

Listening — the hypothesis source

Ten recordists, each making a different choice about which capture path to use. For each one, read what to listen for before you press play, then check yourself with the question after.

01 Jana Winderen — Energy Field below the surface

Winderen placed hydrophones under ice and in open water. These are sounds you can never hear with your own ears — they exist only because she chose the right tool for that medium.

Listen for
  • Sounds with no airborne equivalent: creaks, pressure groans, crackles that only exist underwater.
  • The sense of depth and distance — how far away events feel in water, which carries sound very differently from air.
  • How little is “musical” by design, yet how structured it sounds.
After — verify

Could any of this have been recorded with a normal microphone above the ice? No. That is the first lesson: the choice of tool happened before the recording, and it decided everything. Name one sound you could not identify. Keep it unidentified — not being able to name it is exactly the evidence that you reached a different medium. If everything sounded familiar, relisten for the high crackle and ask yourself what in air ever sounds like that.

02 Christina Kubisch — Five Electrical Walks the coil hears the invisible

Kubisch walks city streets wearing headphones that use electromagnetic induction — the same technique as this lab’s third path. Security gates, tram lines, and ATMs all produce electrical fields that her coil picks up as sound.

Listen for
  • Rhythms no one composed: switching power supplies pulsing, data lines chattering.
  • How different two “silent” machines sound — every circuit leaks its own pattern.
  • The complete absence of room sound: no footsteps, no voices, no echo — electrical fields do not travel through air.
After — verify

What is missing that every microphone recording has? Room. There is no reverb, no sense of distance, no background — because electromagnetic fields do not bounce off walls. Did any source have a rhythm? It should have: machines repeat, and the coil makes that repetition audible as a kind of music nobody wrote. If you missed the lack of room sound, relisten and try to place any of the sounds in a physical space — you will not be able to.

03 Francisco López — La Selva the field unedited

López recorded a rainforest and presented it as a finished piece, with no processing. His point: a field recording that is carefully framed and listened to already is music — it does not need to be processed to count.

Listen for
  • Layers at every distance: insects close, birds in the middle, weather far away — a natural mix that nobody mixed.
  • How the density changes over time, like an arrangement that has no arranger.
  • When your attention shifts from “a recording of a forest” to hearing something with shape and form.
After — verify

Where was the craft, if nothing was processed? In the choices before the file existed: where to stand, what time of day, what level to hold through the loud and quiet. Those are the same decisions this lab teaches. Did the piece develop even though nobody edited development into it? Yes — choosing when and where is itself composition. If it stayed “just a forest” for you, relisten to ten minutes and log every entrance and exit as if they were tracks in a mix.

04 Toshiya Tsunoda — Extract from Field Recording Archive the solid world

Tsunoda uses contact mics on small vibrating objects — pipes, railings, a bottle on a pier. Where he places the transducer matters: move it a hand’s width and the recording changes completely.

Listen for
  • Sounds with weight but no sense of room: vibration traveling through a solid material, arriving dry and close.
  • How large tiny events become — a wire’s hum or a floor’s tremor filling the whole frame.
  • What ordinary object is implied: what is this surface, and what is making it vibrate?
After — verify

Why do these recordings feel like they happen inside your head rather than in a space? Because the sound never traveled through air. No air means no room, no distance, no echo — the vibrating surface speaks directly into the transducer. Could you guess what was exciting each surface? Partly at best, and that gap is the whole point: the surface filters the world into its own voice. If it all still sounded like “a weird mic,” relisten to one track and ask: where would I have had to press the contact mic to get this sound?

05 Jacob Kirkegaard — Eldfjall the earth as source

Kirkegaard put accelerometers — sensors that detect physical movement — into geothermally active ground in Iceland, where steam and pressure move rock. The earth itself becomes the sound source.

Listen for
  • The bottom of the spectrum: rumbles and low pulses below where most music lives.
  • Textures that sound like synthesis — drones and grainy hiss that no synthesizer made.
  • How slowly things change: geological events unfold over minutes, not seconds.
After — verify

If you heard this without knowing what it was, would you call it electronic music? Almost certainly. That confusion is the point: putting the right sensor in the right place makes the earth sound identical to a synthesizer. What was the loudest event, and did it arrive suddenly or build slowly? Slow build is geology’s pattern; a synth would jump. If you cannot remember, relisten to one track and follow only the lowest layer from start to finish.

06 BJ Nilsen — The Invisible City the urban field

Nilsen treats city sounds — ventilation, traffic, machinery, weather — with the same patience a naturalist brings to birdsong. The city becomes the subject, not the background.

Listen for
  • The city’s drones: ventilation and distant traffic holding steady tones beneath everything.
  • Layered rhythms nobody planned — footsteps against machinery against dripping water.
  • Where recordings blend together: can you find the joins between locations?
After — verify

What did the city sound like once you stopped hearing it as noise? Like an ensemble — sustained tones, pulses, and accents across every frequency range. Could you find the joins between locations? Probably not, and that takes skill: matching levels and tone across separate recordings makes them feel like one continuous space. Your own street is a source library. If it still sounded like noise, relisten and find the lowest continuous tone, then guess what machine is making it.

07 Chris Watson — Stepping into the Dark the atmosphere of places

Watson records single locations and holds them long enough for them to become pieces. He sets up the kit, commits to staying, and lets time do the composing.

Listen for
  • The patience: one place, unbroken, with no cutting away when nothing “happens.”
  • How clean the recordings are — no wind blast, no handling noise, no hiss underneath. This is the technical target.
  • How much more of the place you notice after a few minutes compared to the first few seconds.
After — verify

What did you hear at minute four that you could not hear at minute one? More detail — not because the place changed, but because your attention settled. Staying long enough to really hear a place is itself a decision, just like setting the gain. Did you catch any technical flaw — a wind gust, a handling thump, audible hiss? You should not have. That cleanliness is the bar this lab trains you toward. If you stopped listening before the end, go back and sit through one full track.

08 Stephen Vitiello — World Trade Center Recordings: Winds After Hurricane Floyd the building as instrument

During a 1999 residency on the 91st floor of World Trade Center Tower One, Vitiello taped contact mics directly to the windows. When Hurricane Floyd passed, the building’s structure became audible. The right transducer, in the right place, in the right weather.

Listen for
  • The building physically moving: creaks and groans of a skyscraper swaying — vibrations the contact mic found in the “solid” glass.
  • The storm heard through glass: wind as pressure on a pane, not wind blasting directly into a microphone.
  • The city far below, ghosting through the structure — traffic and bells arriving distorted and filtered by hundreds of floors of steel.
After — verify

What was the instrument — the storm or the window? The window. The same wind hitting a microphone on the roof would be nothing but blast and rumble. Through the glass, it became the voice of the building. The storm played what the contact mic had already turned into a vibrating body. If it sounded like “just wind,” relisten for the structure’s own creak and ask yourself what object in air could creak like that.

09 Alvin Lucier — Sferics the sky on a wire

Lucier set out antennas in open fields at night — no microphone anywhere. He was capturing natural VLF (very low frequency) radio: the electromagnetic crackle of distant lightning, and the "whistlers" the atmosphere makes from it. This is the same electromagnetic path as the induction coil, but on a planetary scale.

Listen for
  • Sferics — dry clicks and pops, each one a lightning stroke hundreds or thousands of kilometers away, arriving at radio speed.
  • Whistlers: those clicks stretched into a falling glide by their long journey through the upper atmosphere — a tone shaped entirely by physics, not by a composer.
  • The complete absence of room sound — like the induction coil recordings, no echo, no air, no sense of distance.
After — verify

Who pitched the whistlers? Physics. A lightning click contains all frequencies at once. The long path through the atmosphere delays the low frequencies, which smears the click into a downward sweep that nobody composed. The connection to this lab: the coil hears a phone charger leak from a few centimeters; Lucier’s antenna hears the atmosphere leak from the other side of the planet. Same kind of ear, different scale. If you only heard static, relisten for one clean descending whistle and follow it all the way down.

10 Geir Jenssen — Cho Oyu 8201m the expedition as form

In 2001, Geir Jenssen (Biosphere) climbed Cho Oyu — the sixth-highest mountain on earth — carrying a recorder. The album follows the climb in sequence: base camp ceremonies, radio chatter, wind, breath, crampons on ice. As the altitude rises, the recordings get thinner, just like the air.

Listen for
  • The thinning: how much less there is to hear — and to record — the higher the climb goes. Emptiness is its own sound.
  • Logistics made audible: radios, camp life, gear being handled — the practical work of the expedition, not just the landscape.
  • The recordist’s body in the file: heavy breathing at altitude, effort printed into the takes, recordings that end because the hands holding the kit were too cold to continue.
After — verify

What determined the structure of this album? The route. The track order is the climb itself, and the form was decided by weather and the body’s need to acclimatize, not by aesthetics. Notice what altitude did to capture discipline: adjusting gain with gloves on, takes cut short by cold and thin air. Every recording is also a document of what it cost to make. If it sounded like a travel diary, relisten to the highest-altitude tracks and count what remains: wind, breath, footsteps — an expedition reduced to three sounds.

Before you pack anything, write a one-sentence hypothesis in your logbook. Draw on what you just heard: a transducer under ice, a coil finding electricity where your ears find silence, an unedited frame, a surface as filter, the ground as oscillator, a city as ensemble, duration as a recording decision, a building as instrument, the sky on a wire, an expedition as form. Aim for something like: the kit chooses what the world can say — pick the path before the place. Write it in your own words. The three-path comparison below is what will test it.

  • Gear principle: the entries in the table above describe capabilities, not specific brands. Any recorder you can control the gain on, any contact mic that picks up surface vibrations, and any coil that picks up electrical fields will work. The minimum fallback: a phone recorder plus a DIY piezo element covers two of the three paths while you build the full kit.
Field safety & ethics — read before every session, not once
  • Consent and privacy when recording people — if a voice is identifiable, you ask; if you cannot ask, you do not keep it
  • Batteries and storage — charged, spares packed, card space checked before leaving, files backed up on return
  • Weather and moisture — electronics and rain do not negotiate; bag the kit, watch condensation moving between cold and warm
  • Traffic and public-space awareness — closed phones remove your ears from the street; one ear free near roads, rails, and bikes
  • Cable strain and trip hazards — no cable crosses a path at ankle height; tape it or reroute it
  • Caution with electromagnetic pickups around unknown electrical installations — the coil listens from a distance; nothing gets touched, opened, or probed
Required comparison — this lab’s deliverable

Record the same object three ways: air (the recorder’s mics), contact (the piezo on its surface), and electromagnetic (the coil near its circuits). One object, three files, one logbook page comparing what each path could and could not hear. A refrigerator, a fence in the wind, a ticket machine — anything that hums, vibrates, and leaks electricity will work.

Reference & diagnosis

Pass gate — the lab is complete when all exist
  • The kit list verified — everything counted out and back, every capability present or a fallback named
  • The three-path comparison recordings — air, contact, and electromagnetic captures of the same object, with defensible levels
  • Field log — location, time, weather, gain setting for each capture, and anything the place did that you did not expect
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, and the rule to prevent it (Block 2 fault focus: field level and source handling)
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the field worked like X; standing in it, I found it worked like Y”

The kit and its checklists are living documents. Update them when new gear arrives, check them before every field session, and carry them into R2, C2, and every capture that follows.

Bench principle

The field does not offer retakes. Choose your capture path before you arrive, set the gain for what might happen, and leave with everything: the recording, the kit, and the place undisturbed.
