Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: capture at syllable scale

Copy one clear section of Akufen — “Deck the House”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: hours of radio captured, chopped at syllable scale, mapped to pads, and quantized into a microhouse groove over a steady 4/4 kick. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Source recordAkufen — “Deck the House” (2002, My Way)

Before you start
  • Gear: any live stream — FM radio into the M4, or a podcast/stream resampled inside Live; the Digitakt II for pads if you prefer hardware chopping; DT 990 Pro in the phones jack
  • New set saved as REP-DECK, tempo 126 BPM — microhouse pocket
  • Radio levels jump wildly between voices, ads, and songs — set capture gain on the loudest material you hear in a minute of scanning, and keep phones at the lab-001 mark while auditioning slices; attacks at this density fatigue faster than drones
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop — the harvest is fast and fun, and the discipline is stopping at thirty-plus cuts instead of two hundred
  • What you will make: a short replica of Akufen — “Deck the House”. Focus on this effect: melodic-percussive confetti - sub-second fragments of FM radio landing like drum hits, each carrying its own room and hiss.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: hours of radio captured, chopped at syllable scale, mapped to pads, and quantized into a microhouse groove over a steady 4/4 kick.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Akufen — “Deck the House”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How long is a typical slice?
    Answer

    Sub-second - single words, half-syllables, horn stabs, a breath of a string section. Each is placed like a drum hit: it has an attack, a pitch, and a job, and it is gone before it can explain itself.

  2. Where does the groove live — in the sources or in the cuts?
    Answer

    In the cuts. None of the source material was funky; the funk is the editing - where each fragment starts, how hard its attack lands on the grid, which sixteenth it occupies. The rhythm section is a pair of scissors.

  3. How many distinct sources collide in one bar?
    Answer

    Count and write a number - most people land between four and eight. Each arrives with its own capture history audible in miniature: its own room, hiss, level, and era. The bar is a crowd, not a band.

  4. Do the slices share a fidelity?
    Answer

    No - and that mismatch is the tell of micro-capture. A phone voice sits next to a studio horn next to FM compression, and nothing is smoothed to match.

  5. What is the 4/4 kick doing under the confetti?
    Answer

    Holding the floor still so the chaos can read as funk. With the kick steady on quarters, every fragment’s odd placement becomes syncopation against a known pulse; without it, the same confetti would be noise collage.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your groove lands on the same confetti — sub-second fragments snapping on a sixteenth grid over a steady kick, each cut audibly from a different world — at matched loudness.
  • Every fragment under 400 ms — long enough to have a pitch, too short to have a meaning.
  • The fragment’s source meaning is irrelevant by design: choose cuts for attack and pitch only. If a slice still says a recognizable word, shorten it.
  • One steady 4/4 kick underneath, dead on the grid — the only citizen of the track with a fixed address.

The piece’s machinery in one row: five minutes of radio captured once, thirty-plus sub-second cuts harvested from it, mapped across pads, and sequenced as rhythm-pitch confetti on a 16-step grid over a 4/4 kick at ≈ −12 dBFS.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the net — capture five minutes of airwaves

Phase focus: the reference and the net — capture five minutes of airwaves. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename REF, drag “Deck the House” in, loop a representative minute, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility so it peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Live Pick a stream with variety — talk radio with ad breaks beats a music station;
  3. Live speech gives the best syllables.
  4. Live + Recorder Route it in: radio/phone into an M4 input, or resample the computer’s own output to a Live track.
  5. Live + Recorder Set capture gain on the loudest minute you can find while scanning, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — radio compression will hold most material near that ceiling.
  6. Live Record five unbroken minutes to a track named NET.
  7. Live No pausing when something good or bad happens — the rule is everything gets caught.
  8. Live Listen back once at speed, dropping a marker (⇧ ⌘ L on a locator, or just notes on paper) wherever an attack jumps out — a plosive, a stab, a laugh, a ding.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS and five unbroken minutes of raw airwaves sit on NET, peppered with markers where the attacks live.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The capture is one long song with no speechMusic station — pretty fish, wrong netSwitch to talk, news, or a podcast with ads; syllables and stabs are the prey
Levels lurch 20 dB between segmentsGain set on a quiet voice, then an ad arrivedReset on the loudest material; quiet slices can be lifted later, clipped ones are dead
Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

The harvest — thirty cuts, none longer than a syllable

Phase focus: the harvest — thirty cuts, none longer than a syllable. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Work through your markers and cut 30 or more fragments from NET, each 80–400 ms: half-words, plosives, stabs, dings, one drum hit from a passing song.
  2. Cut tight to the attack — dead air in front of a slice is a timing error you will pay for at sequencing.
  3. Audition each cut twice as you make it: once asking “what is it saying?” — if you can answer, shorten it — and once asking “what note is it?” Log nothing;
  4. keep moving.
  5. Sort the survivors by job, not source: low thumps, mid barks, high ticks — the way you would sort a drum kit.
  6. Digitakt II + Live Map them: select all slices → right-click → Slice to New MIDI Track into a Drum Rack, or transfer your sixteen favorites to Digitakt II pads over USB.
  7. Digitakt II + Live Either bench works; pads under fingers audition faster.
  8. Digitakt II + Live Play the pads by hand for two minutes, no recording.
  9. Digitakt II + Live You are learning the kit you just built from the airwaves.
Expected result

Thirty-plus sub-second fragments, none of which says anything anymore, sorted and mapped across pads — a drum kit whose every voice used to be a meaning.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Slices still read as words and quotesCuts too long — meaning needs only half a second to reassembleHalve them; keep the attack and the first vowel, lose the rest
Slices feel limp and late when played on padsSilence or breath left before each attackRe-trim every start to the transient; at this scale 30 ms of slop is a flam
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

The groove — sixteen steps, kick on the floor, confetti between

Phase focus: the groove — sixteen steps, kick on the floor, confetti between. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Digitakt II Kick first: a plain house kick — from the Digitakt II’s pool or one low thump from your harvest — on steps 1, 5, 9, 13 at 126 BPM.
  2. Digitakt II No variation, no fills.
  3. Digitakt II This citizen never moves.
  4. Digitakt II + Live Sequence a 16-step pattern with 6–8 fragments per bar: program on the Digitakt II grid or draw in a Live MIDI clip, fully quantized — this groove’s snap comes from exactness, the opposite of the Burial lesson.
  5. Digitakt II + Live Place by ear-logic: a low bark answering the kick, ticks on offbeat sixteenths, one bright slice as the bar’s punchline.
  6. Digitakt II + Live Audition pitch neighbors — two slices a third apart start writing a melody by accident;
  7. Digitakt II + Live keep accidents that groove.
  8. Digitakt II + Live Leave gaps.
  9. Digitakt II + Live The record’s funk breathes through its empty sixteenths;
  10. Digitakt II + Live resist filling every step.
  11. Digitakt II + Live Loop it and adjust each fragment’s level until no slice dominates: the groove should read as one machine made of thirty worlds, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
Expected result

A one-bar groove that moves like house and sparkles like static — kick steady, 6–8 fragments snapping on the grid, gaps audible, no slice explaining where it came from.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The groove sounds like a collage, not a trackKick missing or moving — no fixed floor for the chaos to push againstQuarters, dead on, no exceptions; the radical material needs the conservative anchor
Fragments flam and smear against the gridUntrimmed starts surviving from phase 2Re-trim the offenders to their transients; quantization can only align what starts where it claims to
Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

The density experiment — overfill it, then prune to the record

Phase focus: the density experiment — overfill it, then prune to the record. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Digitakt II Duplicate the pattern.
  2. Digitakt II On the copy, add fragments one at a time — after each addition, loop four bars and ask: still funky, or just busy?
  3. Digitakt II Log the count where it tips; most grooves clutter between 10 and 12 fragments per bar.
  4. Digitakt II Prune back down one slice at a time until the groove breathes again.
  5. Digitakt II Note which removals mattered — usually the slices that shared a register with a neighbor, not the loudest ones.
  6. Live Relisten to one bar of REF and count its distinct fragments, twice on two different bars.
  7. Logbook Write the record’s number next to yours.
  8. Digitakt II + Live Settle your final pattern at the breathing count, extend to a 2-minute arrangement — drop the confetti for two bars, bring it back, mute the kick for one bar near the end — and play it end to end at ≈ −12 dBFS.
Expected result

Two logged numbers — your clutter threshold and the record’s working density — and a two-minute groove sitting deliberately at the count where funk lives.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Adding slices never seems to clutterNew fragments landing in empty registers — you built a bigger kit, not a denser barAdd into occupied registers and occupied sixteenths; clutter is collision, not count alone
The pruned groove feels empty, not funkyYou removed the conversation — slices that answered each other — instead of the duplicatesRestore the call-and-response pairs and cut the register-doublers instead
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your groove at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Could a listener name any source fragment?
    Answer

    They should not have time. At 126 BPM a sixteenth lasts about 120 milliseconds, and recognition needs more runway than that — by the time the ear begins to parse “was that a word?”, three more fragments have landed. If any slice in your groove still gets recognized on a casual listen, it is too long or too alone: shorten it, or crowd it with a neighbor. Relisten to the record trying to transcribe any single word; the failure is the design working.

  • How many distinct fragments fit in one bar before it clutters?
    Answer

    Your experiment gave you a number — typically the groove breathes at 6–8 and chokes past 10 — and your count of the record should land in the same neighborhood, bar after bar. That consistency is the finding: density on this record is a composed quantity, held like a tempo. The deeper point is that the limit is perceptual, not technical — the sampler would happily play sixteen slices; the ear files for divorce at eleven. Relisten and count one more bar; then check that your final pattern sits at the record’s number, not at your maximum.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 007 — every slice in your groove kept its own capture history audible: its room, its hiss, its compression. A bar of micro-captures is thirty tiny generational fingerprints playing at once, which is the lab’s claim — the path is audible — multiplied by the number of fish in the net.
