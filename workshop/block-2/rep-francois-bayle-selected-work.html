Replicate: composing by sound-image

Steal one structural idea from François Bayle's L'Expérience Acoustique. Organise your sounds by what each one is and does — its shape, its mass, its motion, and where it seems to stand in space — instead of by pitch or rhythm. Build a short take and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

You are not copying Bayle's tape sounds, his instruments, or the GRM studio. You are copying the mechanism: Bayle composes with i-sons (sound-images) — sonic objects that each have their own shape and behaviour. He builds the form by relating those images: a throwing gesture answered by a settling object, a near sound set against a far one. Shape and depth carry the piece. Notes do not.

The system you are replicating
Source recordFrançois Bayle — L'Expérience Acoustique (1969–72)
The effectYou hear events, not instruments — sounds that lunge, hang, settle, and recede, each with its own behaviour and position in space
The mechanismMaterial is organised by sound-image shape (attack, mass, motion, grain) and by how near or far each sounds; relating those images is the form — not melody, not metre

Before you start
  • Three or four short raw recordings of your own — a struck object, a scrape, a blown or bowed sound, anything with a strong shape — on Digitakt II tracks
  • MicroFreak ready on Live's input to sculpt or generate a sound-image from scratch
  • Headphones or a stereo monitor setup — depth is half the mechanism, and a reverb send plus pan is your near/far tool, so a mono check will not do
  • What you will make: a 60–90 second study built from three or four sound-images, arranged by gesture and spatial depth. Focus on this effect: the listener perceives sonic objects that behave, not sounds that merely play.
  • What you will turn off: pitch thinking, key, beat, and the urge to "play a line." No notes, no groove — only shapes in space.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: morphology and depth carry the whole form.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about two minutes of L'Expérience Acoustique (or Jeîta). Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Pick one sound. Is it a gesture (a motivated motion — a throw, a strike, a sweep) or an object (a stable held mass)?
    Answer

    Bayle's pieces are full of both, often in pairs. A gesture has energy and direction — it goes somewhere, like a thing thrown or scraped — and ends. An object just is: a held resonance or drone that stays in place. If you cannot decide, you are probably hearing a gesture that settled into an object, which is exactly the kind of event he composes.

  2. Does that sound seem near or far — and how do you know?
    Answer

    You know from depth cues, not volume. Near sounds are dry and detailed, with sharp transients; far sounds are reverberant, smeared, and soft-edged, with their high-frequency detail lost in the imagined space. Bayle moves sounds along this near/far axis as deliberately as a composer moves pitch — distance is a real compositional tool here.

  3. How do two sounds relate to make an event — does one answer the other?
    Answer

    Constantly. The basic unit is not a chord, it is cause-and-consequence: a launching gesture is answered by a settling object; a sharp near attack triggers a slow far bloom. The form is built from these relationships — one sound-image sets up an expectation, and the next either meets it or refuses it.

  4. How much of this could you transcribe as notes on a stave?
    Answer

    Almost none. There is pitch in there, but it is a property of the sound — its colour — not the organising grid. Try to notate it and you lose what matters: the shape of the attack, the direction of travel, the distance. Shape and depth are all that carry the piece, and they do.

Now build with the same logic. Do not copy his sounds — create your own images and arrange them by shape and space.

  • Sound target: three or four distinct sound-images, each with a clear identity — one strong gesture, one stable object, and one or two between — that a listener could describe as behaving creatures.
  • Gesture pole: an energetic, motivated sound — a throw, strike, or sweep — that travels and ends.
  • Object pole: a stable, held mass — a sustained resonance or drone — that occupies space without moving.
  • The form: how the images answer each other and where each sits on the near/far axis — nothing else.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Capture and name your sound-images

Phase focus: build a small cast of sound-images and decide what each one is before deciding what it does. Compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track and gain-match it with a Utility so A/Bs are honest.
  2. Pick one strong gesture: a struck or scraped sample with a sharp attack and motion. Trim it so the energetic shape is the whole sound.
  3. Make one stable object: a held, slowly evolving tone or resonance — no rhythm, no melody, just a mass that sits.
  4. Name each image by morphology, not pitch: write a one-line description per sound — "a throw that lands," "a held breath," "a grain that crumbles." If you cannot name its shape, it is not an image yet.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from François Bayle — L'Expérience Acoustique (1969–72) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — Capture and name your sound-images — as the smallest Live set that proves Material is organised by sound-image shape (attack, mass, motion, grain) and by how near or far each sounds; relating those images is the form — not melody, not metre.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects You hear events, not instruments — sounds that lunge, hang, settle, and recede, each with its own behaviour and position in space.

Phase 2 — ~30 minutes

Place the images in depth

Phase focus: give each sound-image a position on the near/far axis so distance becomes part of the composition, not just a finishing touch.

near (dry, sharp, detailed) → mid (some send, softened edge) → far (wet, smeared, soft)
Do this
  1. Set a depth bus: one reverb on a return so you can dial each image from dry/near to wet/far by send amount, not just volume.
  2. Place your gesture near: keep it dry, transient intact, panned with intent. It should feel like it is in the room with you — foreground, close.
  3. Place your object far: push its reverb send up, roll off a little high end, and let it sit behind the gesture as background — a presence at a distance.
  4. Audition near against far: they should feel like they share a space but stand at different distances. Write down each image's depth position.

Phase 3 — ~25 minutes

Make the images answer each other

Phase focus: relate the sound-images into events and record the journey. This is the whole piece.

Do this
  1. Write the relationships first: a short list of paired events in order, e.g. near throw → far bloom answers it → grain settles between. This list is your score, written in behaviour, not bars.
  2. Trigger gesture into object: launch a near gesture and let the far object answer, so one image's energy seems to cause the next. Cause-and-consequence, not just sounds in sequence.
  3. Record the journey in one pass, moving through the list. No beat — let each gesture's natural length and each object's decay set the timing, the way speech is timed.
  4. Listen back with eyes closed: can a stranger feel objects behaving — one thing acting on another across space — without being told what the sounds are?

Verify against the record

A/B your take against a comparable stretch of L'Expérience Acoustique — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Can a listener point to a moment where one sound-image clearly acts on another across a distance?

Answer

If not, you have sounds in a row, not a form. The mechanism lives in the relationship — a near gesture that seems to set off a far object proves you composed by image and depth, not just by placing clips end to end. If yours has no such moment, go back to phase 3 and build at least one true cause-and-consequence pair.

Does the form survive with no melody and no beat — do shape and depth alone carry it?

Answer

It should. That is the mechanism's whole claim: shape and space can be a form. If your take only works because a pulse or a tune crept in, you replaced the mechanism instead of testing it. Mute the rhythm or pitch you leaned on and check whether the behaviour of the images still holds the piece together — in Bayle it does.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: "Matched: …" · "Missed: … (best guess why)" · "Change next: …"
  • Name the one sound-image that did the most work — the gesture or object that gave the piece its life.
  • Replica audio stays in the logbook as study evidence; it is not a release.
