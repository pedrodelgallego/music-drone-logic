Block 2 · Lab 007 · Track lab

The Sampler Eats Itself

Your rig can record its own output three different ways: inside the Digitakt, through the computer over USB, and out through real cables and back in again. This lab runs the same sound through all three — once, and then three times over — and teaches you to hear what each pass costs and what each pass gives.

Track lab · S2 · D2 E3 M4 F2 · texture · full cycle · new concepts: 5

listen → hypothesis → draw the path → calibrate → build → fault → diagnose → print → delta

The point

A sound can become source material for its own next generation — and the path you resample through is audible in generation three even when generation one sounds identical. Choose the path like you choose an instrument.

How this lab works

The three paths

  • Internal: the Digitakt resamples its own output, all digital, no cables
  • USB: the Digitakt’s audio bounced through Live and rendered back
  • Analog loop: out of the box, through the M4’s converters, and back in

The generation test

  • The same source through each path, once
  • Then each first generation fed back through its own path, twice more
  • Compare generation one against generation three, per path, at matched loudness

A piece from third generations

  • The track is built only from your own third-generation material
  • Each path’s fingerprint becomes an arrangement decision
  • 6–8 minutes, fully original — the source is yours, three times over
Pass standard
Minimum passAudible original piece, three-path diagram, fault entry, print map, and system diff exist.
Solid passYou can name, by ear, which path produced each third generation in a blind shuffle of your own renders.
Advanced transferYou can predict what a fourth or fifth generation would do on each path — and say which path you would pick for a given musical job.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
ResamplingRecording the output of your own system back into it as new material. The sampler stops being a playback box and becomes a loop: what comes out goes back in, ready to be played, chopped, and recorded again.
GenerationEach pass through a path. The original capture is generation zero; record it once and you hold generation one; record that and you hold generation two. Like photocopying a photocopy — and every path ages the copy differently.
The three architecturesInternal: the Digitakt records itself, digital end to end, near-lossless. USB: digital too, but the sound crosses into the computer and back, picking up conversion and driver quirks. Analog loop: out through the M4’s output, down a real cable, back through the M4’s input — converters and gain knobs both touch it, twice per pass.
Generational characterThe accumulated fingerprint of a path after several passes. One pass through any path can sound like nothing happened; three passes and the path’s habits — its noise, its softening, its image shift — pile up into a sound of their own.
The diagram as deliverableThe three-path resampling map you draw and keep, with level notes on every leg. It is this lab’s bench artifact: the page you will consult every time the workshop resamples again — and it does, in Blocks 4 and 7.

Listening — the hypothesis source

Seven records in which a sampler ate its own material — or someone else’s — and the generations became the music. Each gets a brief: what to listen for before you press play, and how to check yourself after.

01 Burial — “Archangel” the orphaned voice

Vocals resampled and pitched until they belong to no one — the generations strip away the singer and leave only the song.

Listen for
  • What survives the processing: melody and ache, not fidelity — the voice is blurred but the feeling is sharp.
  • Where the voice sounds like a recording of a recording — distance that no reverb alone produces.
  • How the crackle and hiss sit around the voice like weather, not like a fault.
After — verify

Could you say whether the singer is a man or a woman? You probably hesitated — the pitching and regeneration have orphaned the voice from its body, and that ambiguity is the design. What did survive every generation? Hum the line back: the melody made it through intact while the identity did not. If you missed that, relisten following only the vocal’s tune, ignoring its texture.

02 Oneohtrix Point Never — “Replica” loops as elegy

Fragments of old television ads, resampled into something the source never contained — the copies carry a grief the originals were too busy selling to notice.

Listen for
  • The seams: where a fragment loops, and how the loop point becomes a rhythm.
  • The mood — nothing in an advertisement sounds like this; the feeling lives in the treatment, not the material.
  • How short the actual source moments are versus how long the piece dwells on them.
After — verify

Could the source material have sounded like this in its original context? No — and that is the test passed: resampling here is not preservation, it is authorship. Name one fragment you could imagine in its original ad. If you cannot find a single one, that measures how far the generations carried the material from home; relisten to the piano figure and try to hear it as a jingle.

03 The Caretaker — An Empty Bliss Beyond This World resampling as memory

Ballroom 78s looped and worn until the wear itself is the subject — the album is about what repetition and copying do to a remembered thing.

Listen for
  • The surface noise: it is louder than some of the music, and it is kept on purpose.
  • What the degradation hides and what it cannot — the band recedes, the melody stays reachable.
  • Loops that end mid-phrase, like a memory that cuts off at the same spot every time.
After — verify

Is the crackle a flaw or an instrument here? Instrument — remove it and the piece loses its meaning, not just its texture. That is the strongest version of this lab’s claim: the path’s damage is the content. Where did you stop hearing the original dance band and start hearing the wear? If you never made that flip, relisten to one track and attend only to the noise, treating the music as background.

04 Akufen — “Deck the House” micro-capture

Seconds of FM radio chopped into a groove — capture and resample at the smallest scale, where a sample is a syllable, not a phrase.

Listen for
  • How short the slices are — single words, horn stabs, half-syllables, each placed like a drum hit.
  • The groove living in the cuts: the rhythm is the editing, not the sources.
  • How many different recording qualities collide per bar — every slice carries its own room, hiss, and level.
After — verify

Pick one bar: how many distinct sources did you count? Most people land between four and eight — and each arrived with its own capture history audible in miniature. Could you hear that the slices come from different worlds? That patchwork of mismatched fidelities is the tell of micro-capture; if every slice sounded equally clean to you, relisten on headphones and compare the hiss behind any two adjacent slices.

05 Actress — “Hubble” the haze of generations

Texture that sounds three copies deep on purpose — the blur is not an accident of cheap gear but a chosen depth of field.

Listen for
  • The smeared edges: nothing attacks cleanly; every element sounds slightly out of focus.
  • What stays solid underneath — the pulse keeps time while the surfaces drift.
  • Whether the haze is constant or breathes — listen for moments where the focus briefly sharpens.
After — verify

Does this sound like a bad recording or like a decision? Decision — the proof is consistency: an accident would be uneven, but this haze coats everything at the same depth, start to finish. That uniform coat is what several generations through one path produce. Could you hear the pulse staying precise inside the blur? If not, relisten and tap along: the timing is clean even where the timbre is fog.

06 DJ Shadow — “Building Steam with a Grain of Salt” the sampler as studio

A whole world assembled from records — the sampler is not an effect in this music, it is the entire studio, and every sound passed through its converters at least once.

Listen for
  • The shared grain: drums, piano, and voices from different decades wearing one common texture.
  • Layers that were clearly resampled together — combined sounds that move as one object, not as a stack.
  • The low end: how vinyl bass survives sampling, summing, and re-recording.
After — verify

Why do sources from different rooms and decades sound like one band? Because they all share a final parent: everything was resampled through the same machine, and that last common pass glues them. Could you find a layer that escapes the house grain? You should not — and noticing the glue is the lesson: a shared resample path is a mix bus made of generations. If unsure, relisten comparing the drum texture to the piano texture: same dust.

07 Four Tet — “Unspoken” acoustic material re-chopped

Folk sources — harp, drums, voice — cut until they sequence like a machine, while keeping the wood and breath of the originals.

Listen for
  • Acoustic timbre running with machine timing — the player’s touch survives, the player’s tempo does not.
  • Repeated fragments that are exactly identical — the giveaway that you are hearing one capture, recycled.
  • Where new phrases are built from re-chopped old ones — material answering itself.
After — verify

How did you know the drums are sampled rather than played? The repeats: a drummer never plays two hits identically, a sampler never plays them differently — exact repetition is the fingerprint of capture. What kept the track warm despite the grid? The acoustic generations underneath: room, wood, fingers. If you could not hear the exact repeats, relisten to eight bars of the drums and compare hit number one to hit number five.

Measurement

The lab’s test is fixed: compare first- and third-generation renders, per path, at matched loudness. Generation one is allowed to fool you; generation three is not allowed to fool anyone.

Bench — the three paths, drawn

Draw all three before any cable moves. The corrected drawing — with a level note on every leg — is the three-path resampling diagram, this lab’s bench artifact. It outlives the piece.

Path A — internal · digital, near-lossless

Digitakt tracks → Digitakt resample bus → new sample slot → next generation’s track

Path B — USB · digital, with conversion and driver quirks

Digitakt → USB → Live track → render → transfer → Digitakt sample slot

Path C — analog loop · converters and gain touch it, twice per pass

Digitakt main out L/R → 2 × TRS → M4 in 3/4 → USB-C → Live Live → USB-C → M4 out 3/4 → 2 × TRS → Digitakt inputs L/R → sample
  • Honest rig note: the Digitakt II’s stereo input pair is what closes the analog loop — the return lands there as a normal sampling source. If your sampler lacks inputs, the loop closes at the M4 instead: record the return in Live and transfer it back over USB; the converters and cable are the path, and they are what this lab studies.
  • Level note on every leg: the diagram is not finished until each arrow carries the level you measured at that point — that is what turns a sketch into the bench artifact.

Diagnosis & report

The full report bundle — ten items, filed in the logbook
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, master peaking −3 dBFS, built from third generations only
  • Routing diagram — the three-path resampling diagram with level notes, drawn before patching, corrected after
  • Recording plan — the six comparison renders: generations one and three, per path
  • Patch notes — the settings that mattered, including every input level in the analog loop
  • Session notes — per path: noise, latency, image, degradation, in your own words
  • Reflection answers, including the listening comparison — which record contributed what to the hypothesis
  • One removal decision — what was cut from the finished piece and what cutting it did
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule (faultType: routing)
  • Print map — which generations exist as audio, where each lives, and which parents are now unrecoverable
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the system worked like X; after building it, I discovered it worked like Y.”
  • Minimum pass gate: audible output · routing diagram · fault entry · print map · system diff. Without these five, the lab is incomplete regardless of audio quality — a musically strong piece cannot bypass the engineering spine.
Track-lab principle

Every pass through a path leaves the path’s signature. By generation three, the signature is the sound — so choose your generation’s parents on purpose.
