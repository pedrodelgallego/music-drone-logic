The Sampler Eats Itself

Your rig can record its own output three different ways: inside the Digitakt, through the computer over USB, and out through real cables and back in again. This lab sends the same sound through all three paths — once, and then three times — so you can hear what each pass takes away and what it adds.

The point

You can feed a sound back through your own rig to create new material — this is called resampling. The path you use leaves its own mark on the sound, and by the third pass that mark is clearly audible. So choose your path on purpose, the way you choose an instrument.

How this lab works

The three paths

  • Internal: the Digitakt resamples its own output, all digital, no cables
  • USB: the Digitakt’s audio bounced through Live and rendered back
  • Analog loop: out of the box, through the M4’s converters, and back in

The generation test

  • The same source through each path, once
  • Then each first generation fed back through its own path, twice more
  • Compare generation one against generation three, per path, at matched loudness

A piece from third generations

  • The track is built only from your own third-generation material
  • Each path’s fingerprint becomes an arrangement decision
  • 6–8 minutes, fully original — the source is yours, three times over
Pass standard
Minimum passAudible original piece, three-path diagram, fault entry, print map, and system diff exist.
Solid passYou can name, by ear, which path produced each third generation in a blind shuffle of your own renders.
Advanced transferYou can predict what a fourth or fifth generation would do on each path — and say which path you would pick for a given musical job.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
ResamplingRecording your own system’s output back into it as new material. The sampler stops being a playback device and becomes a loop: what comes out goes back in, ready to be played, chopped, and recorded again.
GenerationEach pass through a path produces a new generation. The original capture is generation zero. Record it once and you have generation one. Record that and you have generation two. Think of it like photocopying a photocopy — and every path ages the copy differently.
The three architecturesInternal: the Digitakt records itself, entirely digital, near-lossless. USB: also digital, but the sound crosses into the computer and back, picking up conversion and driver quirks. Analog loop: the signal leaves the Digitakt through real cables, passes through the M4’s converters and gain controls, and returns — twice per pass.
Generational characterThe mark a path leaves after several passes. One pass through any path may sound like nothing changed. Three passes and the path’s habits — its noise floor, its softening, its stereo image shift — stack up into a sound of their own.
The diagram as deliverableThe three-path resampling map you draw and keep, with a level note on every leg. It is this lab’s main artifact: the reference you will use every time you resample in this workshop — in Blocks 4 and 7.

Listening — the hypothesis source

Ten records where the copying and re-recording became the music. Each entry tells you what to listen for before you press play, and how to check yourself after.

01 Burial — “Archangel” the orphaned voice

Burial resampled and pitch-shifted vocals until you can barely tell they came from a human voice. The processing strips away the person and leaves only the melody.

Listen for
  • What survives the processing: melody and ache, not fidelity — the voice is blurred but the feeling is sharp.
  • Where the voice sounds like a recording of a recording — distance that no reverb alone produces.
  • How the crackle and hiss sit around the voice like weather, not like a fault.
After — verify

Could you tell whether the singer is a man or a woman? You probably hesitated. The pitching and resampling stripped the voice of its identity — that ambiguity is intentional. What did survive? Hum the melody back: the tune made it through even when the timbre did not. If you missed that, listen again following only the melodic line.

02 Oneohtrix Point Never — “Replica” loops as elegy

Oneohtrix Point Never took tiny pieces of old TV ads and resampled them into something the source never contained. The copies feel emotional in a way the originals never did.

Listen for
  • The seams: where a fragment loops, and how the loop point becomes a rhythm.
  • The mood — nothing in an advertisement sounds like this; the feeling lives in the treatment, not the material.
  • How short the actual source moments are versus how long the piece dwells on them.
After — verify

Could the source material have sounded like this in its original context? No — resampling here is not preservation, it is composition. Try to name one fragment you could imagine back in a TV ad. If you cannot find one, relisten to the piano figure and try to hear it as a jingle — it won't fit, and that distance is the point.

03 The Caretaker — An Empty Bliss Beyond This World resampling as memory

The Caretaker looped old ballroom recordings until the crackle and decay became the point. The album is about what happens to a memory when you replay it over and over.

Listen for
  • The surface noise: it is louder than some of the music, and it is kept on purpose.
  • What the degradation hides and what it cannot — the band recedes, the melody stays reachable.
  • Loops that end mid-phrase, like a memory that cuts off at the same spot every time.
After — verify

Is the crackle a flaw or a deliberate choice? It is deliberate — remove it and the piece loses its meaning, not just its texture. The path’s damage is the content. At what point did you stop hearing the original dance band and start hearing the wear? If you never made that shift, relisten to one track and focus only on the noise, treating the music as background.

04 Akufen — “Deck the House” micro-capture

Akufen recorded seconds of FM radio and chopped them into individual syllables and sound bursts, then arranged those tiny pieces into a groove. Each slice is a sample; the editing is the rhythm.

Listen for
  • How short the slices are — single words, horn stabs, half-syllables, each placed like a drum hit.
  • The groove living in the cuts: the rhythm is the editing, not the sources.
  • How many different recording qualities collide per bar — every slice carries its own room, hiss, and level.
After — verify

Pick one bar and count the distinct sources. Most people count between four and eight. Could you hear that the slices came from different recordings? Each arrives with its own background noise, room sound, and quality level. If every slice sounded equally clean to you, relisten on headphones and compare the hiss behind two adjacent slices.

05 Actress — “Hubble” the haze of generations

Actress made a texture that sounds deliberately blurry — like a sound copied several times over. The haziness is not a mistake; it is the intended result of running material through multiple generations.

Listen for
  • The smeared edges: nothing attacks cleanly; every element sounds slightly out of focus.
  • What stays solid underneath — the pulse keeps time while the surfaces drift.
  • Whether the haze is constant or breathes — listen for moments where the focus briefly sharpens.
After — verify

Does this sound like a bad recording or a deliberate choice? It is a choice — the proof is consistency. An accident would be uneven, but this haze coats everything equally from start to finish. That is what several generations through one path do. Could you hear the pulse staying precise inside the blur? If not, relisten and tap along: the timing is tight even where the sound is foggy.

06 DJ Shadow — “Building Steam with a Grain of Salt” the sampler as studio

DJ Shadow built this entire track from vinyl records fed through a sampler. The sampler is not an effect here — it is the whole studio. Every sound you hear was recorded and re-recorded through the same machine.

Listen for
  • The shared grain: drums, piano, and voices from different decades wearing one common texture.
  • Layers that were clearly resampled together — combined sounds that move as one object, not as a stack.
  • The low end: how vinyl bass survives sampling, summing, and re-recording.
After — verify

Why do sounds from different rooms and decades end up sounding like one band? Because they all passed through the same machine at the end — that shared final pass unifies them. Try to find a layer that escapes the shared texture. You should not be able to. If unsure, relisten and compare the drum texture to the piano texture: same grain, same dust.

07 Four Tet — “Unspoken” acoustic material re-chopped

Four Tet took acoustic recordings — harp, drums, voice — and chopped them until they play with machine-like precision. The human warmth survives, but the human timing does not.

Listen for
  • Acoustic timbre running with machine timing — the player’s touch survives, the player’s tempo does not.
  • Repeated fragments that are exactly identical — the giveaway that you are hearing one capture, recycled.
  • Where new phrases are built from re-chopped old ones — material answering itself.
After — verify

How did you know the drums are sampled rather than played live? The exact repeats give it away. A live drummer never hits twice identically; a sampler never plays them differently. What kept the track warm despite that mechanical grid? The original acoustic recordings underneath — the room, the wood, the breath. If you could not hear the exact repeats, relisten to eight bars and compare hit one to hit five.

08 Fennesz — Endless Summer the song under the static

Fennesz fed guitar and pop chord progressions through heavy digital processing until they dissolved into shimmering noise. The processing destroys almost everything about the source — yet somehow the warmth survives.

Listen for
  • The melody surviving inside the static — chord changes you can follow even when no clean note ever sounds.
  • Moments where the guitar surfaces almost intact, then dissolves again — the source and its ruin trading places.
  • The temperature: for all the digital damage, nothing here is cold; the harmony keeps the wreckage warm.
After — verify

Could you hum a tune from this record? Probably yes — which is remarkable, because almost no moment contains an unprocessed note. The melody outlasts the destruction of every surface that carried it. What told you there was a guitar underneath? If you never caught one breaking through, relisten to the title track and wait for the strums that surface briefly before dissolving again.

09 Jan Jelinek — Loop-Finding-Jazz-Records loops from the in-between

Jan Jelinek cut tiny pieces of vinyl jazz records — not the notes, but the space between them: room tone, cymbal air, the surface noise of the record itself. He looped these into a soft machine groove. The sample is texture, not melody.

Listen for
  • The absence of any quotable jazz: no riff, no solo, no recognizable phrase — yet the whole thing audibly comes from jazz records.
  • Loops built from what falls between the notes: room tone, brushed-cymbal air, vinyl surface, the tail of a chord.
  • How the grooves stay soft-edged and warm at machine regularity — funk made from material that was never rhythmic.
After — verify

Could you name a single song, player, or instrument being sampled? You should not be able to. The loops come from the silent spaces where no musical event happened. Yet you probably knew it was a jazz record underneath — the surface noise and warmth of vinyl give it away even when every note is gone. If you missed that, relisten to one track and ask what the loop was doing on the original record — the answer is usually “waiting between phrases.”

10 Vladislav Delay — Multila dub smeared past the bar line

Vladislav Delay ran clicks and chords through long, irregular delays until the loop’s edges disappeared. There is no clear bar line here — time is kept by feel, not by the grid.

Listen for
  • Where the loop’s edges should be — and are not: phrases that overrun the bar line and land somewhere later, intact but displaced.
  • The dub machinery at unusual lengths: delays too long and too uneven to read as echo, smearing each event into the next one’s territory.
  • What actually keeps the time — not a bar line but a recurring weight, a pulse you feel arriving without being able to count toward it.
After — verify

Try to count a bar of four anywhere on this record. You will probably lose the count — yet the music never feels lost. The delays have pushed each phrase past where the bar line should be. Did you notice the moment you stopped counting and just floated with it? That shift is the intended effect. If you never floated, relisten with your foot deliberately still and let the recurring weight tell you when “one” arrives — notice it comes late every time, and you stop minding.

Measurement

The lab’s test is fixed: compare first- and third-generation renders, one path at a time, with both clips matched to the same loudness. Generation one may sound nearly identical to the original. Generation three should not fool anyone.

Bench — the three paths, drawn

Draw all three paths before you move any cables. After building, add a level note to every leg of the drawing. That corrected drawing is the three-path resampling diagram — this lab’s main deliverable. You will refer back to it in later blocks.

Path A — internal · digital, near-lossless

Digitakt tracks → Digitakt resample bus → new sample slot → next generation’s track

Path B — USB · digital, with conversion and driver quirks

Digitakt → USB → Live track → render → transfer → Digitakt sample slot

Path C — analog loop · converters and gain touch it, twice per pass

Digitakt main out L/R → 2 × TRS → M4 in 3/4 → USB-C → Live Live → USB-C → M4 out 3/4 → 2 × TRS → Digitakt inputs L/R → sample
  • Rig note: the Digitakt II’s stereo input pair closes the analog loop — the return signal arrives there as a normal sampling source. If your sampler has no inputs, close the loop at the M4 instead: record the return in Live and transfer it back over USB. The converters and the cable are the path, and that is what this lab studies.
  • Level note on every leg: the diagram is not finished until each arrow shows the level you measured at that point. That is what turns a sketch into a usable bench reference.

Diagnosis & report

The full report bundle — ten items, filed in the logbook
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, master peaking −3 dBFS, built from third generations only
  • Routing diagram — the three-path resampling diagram with level notes, drawn before patching, corrected after
  • Recording plan — the six comparison renders: generations one and three, per path
  • Patch notes — the settings that mattered, including every input level in the analog loop
  • Session notes — per path: noise, latency, image, degradation, in your own words
  • Reflection answers, including the listening comparison — which record contributed what to the hypothesis
  • One removal decision — what was cut from the finished piece and what cutting it did
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule (faultType: routing)
  • Print map — which generations exist as audio, where each lives, and which parents are now unrecoverable
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the system worked like X; after building it, I discovered it worked like Y.”
  • Minimum pass gate: audible output · routing diagram · fault entry · print map · system diff. Without these five items, the lab is incomplete no matter how good the audio sounds. A strong piece does not substitute for the engineering record.
Track-lab principle

Every pass through a path leaves the path’s mark on the sound. By generation three, that mark is the sound. So choose your path on purpose.
