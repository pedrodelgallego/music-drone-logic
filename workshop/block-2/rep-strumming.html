Replicate: sustain from repetition

Copy one clear section of Charlemagne Palestine — Strumming Music. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record’s core mechanism: repetition past the speed of counting, plus resonance (the natural ringing of the instrument’s body). Every strike rings into the next, so the instrument accumulates tones that no single note contains. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring. The Digitakt II is not needed this lesson. You don't have a concert grand piano: the equivalent here is a long-release patch repeated fast by the arpeggiator. The release tails stacking up work like the sustain pedal, and the lesson works the same way.
  • New set saved as REP-STRUMMING — tempo is not relevant; the arpeggiator runs freely, unsynced to the grid.
  • Fast repeated attacks wear out your ears faster than a smooth drone. Keep phones at the lab-001 mark and take a real break every 15 minutes.
  • What you will make: a short replica of Charlemagne Palestine — Strumming Music. The goal is this effect: two alternating notes, played fast with the sustain pedal held down, fuse into a continuous drone with a cloud of overtones growing above them.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and anything that hides whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism — repetition past the speed of counting, plus resonance. Every strike rings into the next, so the instrument accumulates a layer that no individual note contains.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences come from your source choices or gear limits, not from missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Charlemagne Palestine — Strumming Music. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many different notes is the player playing?
    Answer

    Two, alternating. It sounds like far more because what you hear is not just the notes but everything they set ringing: strings vibrating in sympathy, the sustain pedal holding every tail in the air.

  2. Can you hear individual attacks — and for how long?
    Answer

    At first, yes — each strike lands separately. Then somewhere the counting fails and the attacks become a continuous surface, the way a fast drum roll stops being individual hits. The piece never announces the crossing; you only notice you are already on the other side.

  3. What is the high shimmer above the playing made of?
    Answer

    Accumulated resonance — overtones (higher harmonics) from hundreds of strikes all ringing together inside the piano’s body. No single keystroke contains this sound. Nobody plays the shimmer; it builds up on its own.

  4. What does the sustain pedal contribute?
    Answer

    Everything that connects one strike to the next. With the dampers lifted, every note rings until it fades naturally. Each new attack lands on top of all the still-ringing previous ones.

  5. What changes over twenty minutes, when the hands never stop doing the same thing?
    Answer

    Density and the cloud. The rate speeds up and slows down, a note is occasionally added or dropped, and the resonance above thickens over time. But the material is the same two notes from the first minute to the last.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your recording lands on the same fusion — repeats too fast to count, tails ringing into each other, a faint accumulated shimmer above notes that never change.
  • Two notes and nothing else — the same pair for the whole recording. No melody, no third note.
  • The only thing you may adjust during the take is the repeat rate. Density is the entire performance; nothing else in the patch moves.
  • The tails must overlap. If there is audible silence between repeats, you have a rhythm exercise, not this one.

The piece in one row: two notes alternated by the arpeggiator, a release long enough that every repeat rings into the next, a rate knob moved across the fusion threshold (the point where individual strikes blur into one sound), and minutes of it recorded whole.

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Phase 2 · ≈ 20 min

The patch — a strike that refuses to stop ringing

Phase focus: the patch — a strike that refuses to stop ringing. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog. Open the filter enough that each strike has a bright edge — this lesson needs overtones (harmonics), because the cloud is made of them.
  2. Envelope: attack 0, decay/release long — start around 5 s.
  3. Set LFO to zero, no filter movement. Nothing in the patch may move except the envelope decaying after each strike.
  4. Turn the arpeggiator on, pattern Up, sync off so Rate runs freely.
  5. Hold two notes a fifth apart — C2 and G2 work well — and press Hold so your hands are free for the rate knob.
  6. At a slow rate — three or four notes per second — listen: each strike must still be audibly ringing when the next one lands.
  7. If there is a gap of silence between strikes, lengthen the release until there is not.
  8. Set the M4 input-1 gain at your fastest intended rate, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS. Overlapping tails stack level, so the fastest setting is the loudest one.
  9. Optional: add one small reverb on the master, set once and left alone — it stands in for the resonance of the piano's body, not for a room sound.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Find the threshold, then print minutes of it

Phase focus: find the threshold, then print minutes of it. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Record-arm STRUM and start a take.
  2. Start well below fusion — three or four attacks per second, clearly countable.
  3. Raise Rate in small steps, ten or fifteen seconds between each step, and say the count out loud as you go.
  4. The moment counting fails, hold the knob still. Note the spot and write your estimated rate in attacks per second next to your phase-1 guess.
  5. Keep recording: stay above the fusion threshold for two or three minutes, push slightly denser, then come back down through the threshold once at the end.
  6. Total take: 4–6 minutes, all in one recording.
  7. One rule: the Rate knob is the only thing your hands touch.
  8. No filter movement, no level changes, no second patch. Density is the performance.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Listen for what accumulated

Phase focus: listen for what accumulated. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the full take end to end, no touching, phones at the mark.
  2. Mark where you hear fusion arrive and where it leaves on the way back down.
  3. Compare the two crossings: fusion will arrive at one speed going up and let go at a different speed coming down.
  4. Write both numbers. The gap between them is normal — your ear holds onto fusion slightly longer than it takes to reach it.
  5. Listen once more, this time with your attention above the notes, looking for the accumulated layer — the faint cloud your ringing tails build.
  6. A/B against REF at matched loudness: is your cloud thinner than the record’s?
  7. It will be. Write how much thinner, in plain words.
  8. Take the scheduled break — repeated attacks wear out your ears faster than smooth drones, and a tired ear hears fusion everywhere.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
No cloud above the notes — just the notesPatch too pure — a near-sine tone has almost no overtones and leaves nothing to accumulateOpen the filter, add harmonic content, re-record. The cloud is made of overtones, not of the fundamental.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your print at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • At what repeat rate did fusion happen — and is that a property of the sound or of you?
    Answer

    You should have a number — most ears reach fusion somewhere past eight to ten attacks per second, earlier with longer tails. If you caught the up-crossing and down-crossing disagreeing, you have two numbers, and both are correct. The sound never fused; your perception did. That is why this lesson moves only the rate: the whole piece happens at a boundary inside the listener, not inside the instrument.

  • What accumulated while the notes stayed identical?
    Answer

    The tails. Every repeat added a decaying copy of itself, and the sum of hundreds of decays builds a layer with its own slow behavior — your cloud, the record’s cloud. If your version had no cloud, the patch was too pure. The cloud is made of overtones, and a sine tone has none to give.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
