Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: sustain from repetition

Copy one clear section of Charlemagne Palestine — Strumming Music. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: repetition past the speed of counting, plus resonance - every strike rings into the next, so the instrument’s body accumulates what the hands never play. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring; the Digitakt II rests this lesson. You do not own a Bösendorfer: the honest analogue is a long-release patch repeated fast by the arpeggiator — the release tails stacking up are your sustain pedal, and the lesson works the same
  • New set saved as REP-STRUMMING — tempo irrelevant; the arp runs free of the grid
  • Fast repeated attacks at sustained level fatigue the ear faster than a smooth drone — phones at the lab-001 mark, a real break every 15 minutes
  • What you will make: a short replica of Charlemagne Palestine — Strumming Music. Focus on this effect: two alternating piano notes, played fast with the sustain pedal down, fuse into a continuous drone with a harmonic cloud growing above it.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: repetition past the speed of counting, plus resonance - every strike rings into the next, so the instrument’s body accumulates what the hands never play.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Charlemagne Palestine — Strumming Music. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many different notes is the player playing?
    Answer

    Two, for long stretches - alternating between the hands. It sounds like far more, because what you hear is not the notes but everything they wake up: strings ringing in sympathy, the pedal holding every tail.

  2. Can you hear individual attacks — and for how long?
    Answer

    At first, yes - each strike has an address. Then somewhere the counting fails and the attacks become a surface, the way a fast drum roll stops being hits. The piece never announces the crossing; you only notice you are on the other side.

  3. What is the high shimmer above the playing made of?
    Answer

    Accumulated resonance - overtones from hundreds of strikes ringing together in the piano’s body, sound that no single keystroke contains. Nobody plays the shimmer; the instrument secretes it.

  4. What does the sustain pedal contribute?
    Answer

    Everything that connects one strike to the next. With dampers lifted, every note rings until it dies naturally - so each attack lands on the still-sounding wreckage of all the previous ones.

  5. What changes over twenty minutes, when the hands never stop doing the same thing?
    Answer

    Density and the cloud. The rate swells and relaxes, notes are occasionally added or dropped, and the resonance above thickens like weather - but the material is the same two notes from the first minute to the last.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your print lands on the same fusion — repeats too fast to count, tails ringing into each other, a faint accumulated shimmer above material that never changes.
  • Two notes and nothing else — the same pair for the whole print, no melody, no third pitch to rescue you.
  • The only thing you may ride is rate — density is the entire performance; nothing else in the patch moves.
  • The tails must overlap — if there is audible silence between repeats, you have a rhythm lesson, not this one.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag Strumming Music into it.
  2. Live Loop two sections: a minute from the opening where attacks are still countable, and a minute from deep in the piece where they are not.
  3. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the louder section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Live REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. Live ⌘ T once; rename: STRUM, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
  6. Logbook Write in the logbook your guess, in attacks per second, for where the record’s fusion happens.
  7. Logbook Phase 3 will check it.
Expected result

Two looped reference sections at ≈ −12 dBFS, one named, routed, armed track, and a written fusion guess waiting to be wrong.

Phase 2 · ≈ 20 min

The patch — a strike that refuses to stop ringing

Phase focus: the patch — a strike that refuses to stop ringing. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, filter open enough that each strike has a bright edge to ring from — this lesson wants harmonics;
  2. MicroFreak they are what the cloud will be made of.
  3. MicroFreak Envelope: attack 0, decay/release long — start near 5 s.
  4. MicroFreak Zero LFO, zero filter movement: nothing in the patch may move except the envelope doing its job on every repeat.
  5. Digitakt II Turn the arpeggiator on, pattern Up, sync off so Rate runs free.
  6. Digitakt II Hold two notes a fifth apart — say C2 and G2 — and press Hold so your hands can leave the keys for the knob.
  7. Digitakt II At a slow rate — three or four notes a second — listen: each strike must still be ringing clearly when the next lands.
  8. Digitakt II If there is a gap, lengthen the release until there is not.
  9. Digitakt II Set the M4 input-1 gain at your fastest intended rate, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — overlapping tails stack level, so the fast setting is the loud one.
  10. Digitakt II Optional: one small reverb on the master, set once and never moved — it stands in for the soundboard, not for space.
Expected result

Two alternating notes with no silence between them at any rate, a Rate knob as the only moving part, and gain that survives the densest setting.

If it fails

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Find the threshold, then print minutes of it

Phase focus: find the threshold, then print minutes of it. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Record-arm STRUM and start a take.
  2. Begin well below fusion — three or four attacks per second, plainly countable.
  3. Raise Rate in small steps, ten or fifteen seconds per step, and say the count out loud as you go.
  4. Logbook The moment counting fails, hold the knob still — note the spot, and write your felt rate in attacks per second next to your phase-1 guess.
  5. Live Keep recording: live above the threshold for two or three minutes, push slightly denser, then come back down through the threshold once at the end.
  6. Live Total print 4–6 minutes, one take.
  7. Live The rule, enforced without mercy: the Rate knob is the only thing your hands touch.
  8. Live No filter, no level, no second patch — density is the performance.
Expected result

One 4–6 minute take that crosses the fusion threshold twice — up and back down — with a written number for where your counting failed. Expect it somewhere past eight to ten attacks per second; long tails pull it earlier.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Even at maximum rate it still reads as fast notes, never one soundTails too short or too quiet — rate alone cannot fuse dry strikesLengthen release further and re-check the overlap test from phase 2
The take clips at high densityGain was set at a slow rate — stacked tails added levelReset input gain at the fastest rate, ≈ −12 dBFS, and re-print
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Listen for what accumulated

Phase focus: listen for what accumulated. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the full take end to end, no touching, phones at the mark.
  2. Mark with the insert marker where you hear fusion arrive and where it leaves on the way down.
  3. Compare the two crossings: fusion will arrive at one rate going up and let go at a different rate coming down.
  4. Logbook Write both numbers — the gap between them is your ear’s hysteresis, and it is normal.
  5. Logbook Listen once more with attention held above the notes, hunting for the accumulated layer — the soft halo your tails build.
  6. Live Then A/B against REF at matched loudness: is your halo dimmer?
  7. Logbook It will be; write by how much, in plain words.
  8. Logbook Take the scheduled break — repeated attacks fatigue faster than smooth drones, and a tired ear hears fusion everywhere.
Expected result

Two marked fusion crossings with two written rates, one honest sentence comparing your halo to the record’s cloud, and the discovery that the notes never changed while the music did.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your print at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • At what repeat rate did fusion happen — and is that a property of the sound or of you?
    Answer

    You should have a number — most ears land somewhere past eight to ten attacks per second, earlier with long tails — and two numbers if you caught the up-crossing and down-crossing disagreeing. The signal never fused; your perception did. That is why this lesson rides only rate: the entire piece happens at the boundary inside the listener. Relisten to the record’s opening and notice it spends minutes deliberately below the threshold before taking you across.

  • What accumulated while the notes stayed identical?
    Answer

    The tails. Every repeat added a decaying copy of itself to the air, and the sum of hundreds of decays is a layer with its own slow behavior — your halo, the record’s cloud. The hands deposit; the resonance compounds. If your version had no halo, the patch was too pure — the cloud is made of overtones, and a sine has none to give. Relisten to minute fifteen of the record and ask what fraction of what you hear was ever actually struck.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
