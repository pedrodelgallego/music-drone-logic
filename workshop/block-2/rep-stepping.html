Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: duration as a level decision

Copy one clear section of Chris Watson — Stepping into the Dark. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: long unattended takes: the kit deployed, gain committed once with headroom for the unpredictable, and the recordist gone. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordChris Watson — Stepping into the Dark (1996)
The effectsingle locations, many of them recorded at night, held unbroken until they become presences rather than backdrops — with no flaw and no recordist audible anywhere
The mechanismlong unattended takes: the kit deployed, gain committed once with headroom for the unpredictable, and the recordist gone

Before you start
  • Honest analogue, stated plainly: Watson left his kit overnight in nature reserves and caves. You will not. A balcony, a room at night, or a garden you can see from a window is the reachable version — the discipline of absence transfers exactly, the wilderness does not need to
  • Gear: the handheld recorder with foam fitted, charged batteries, empty card — the Lab 008 air path. Phones stay home until retrieval; there is no monitoring in this lesson, on purpose. Back at the bench: Live 12, the M4, DT 990 Pro at the mark
  • New set saved as REP-DARK; the Lab 008 safety checklist travels — weather and moisture above all (a recorder left out negotiates with rain exactly once), and never deploy where the kit is out of your control in public space
  • What you will make: a short replica of Chris Watson — Stepping into the Dark. Focus on this effect: single locations, many of them recorded at night, held unbroken until they become presences rather than backdrops.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: long unattended takes: the kit deployed, gain committed once with headroom for the unpredictable, and the recordist gone.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Chris Watson — Stepping into the Dark. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How long does the place hold before anything is cut away?
    Answer

    Minutes - and through stretches where nothing “happens” at all. Watson does not cut to the next event; the not-happening is kept, because it is what lets the events mean something when they come.

  2. Can you hear the recordist anywhere?
    Answer

    No - no breath, no fabric, no shifting weight, no footstep. The absence is not luck; it is the technique. The kit was placed and the human left, so the place behaves as if unobserved.

  3. What can you hear at minute four that you could not at minute one?
    Answer

    Layers and distances - small sounds behind the obvious ones, the depth of the space.

  4. How do the loud events sit against the quiet floor?
    Answer

    Cleanly - a sudden call or movement arrives startling but undistorted, riding far above a noise floor you barely notice.

  5. If nothing was arranged, what composed the piece?
    Answer

    Three choices made before recording started: where to put the kit, when to start, and how long to stay. Everything inside the take is the place’s own behavior; the composition is the frame around it.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a 30–60 minute unattended take with no clipping, no handling, no human trace — and inside it, three minutes where the place is most itself, trimmed out untouched. Closeness to the record is measured in cleanliness and patience, not in exotic wildlife.
  • One spot, one take, one gain decision. The gain is set before the take starts and never touched again.
  • You are not present during the recording. Walking back to check it counts as presence and restarts the clock.
  • No processing on the result — the three minutes are found, not made.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a spot chosen for what might visit it, a gain committed once against a written worst case, a long take with nobody attending it, and an audit that finds the three minutes worth keeping.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference, the spot, and the worst-case list

Phase focus: the reference, the spot, and the worst-case list. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a track from Stepping into the Dark into it.
  2. Live Audio Effects → Utility, trim to peak ≈ −12 dBFS, loop it.
  3. Live It stays one solo-press away.
  4. Live Choose the spot: a balcony, your room at night with the window open, a garden corner.
  5. Live Criteria — undisturbed for an hour, sheltered from rain, and yours: nobody else can reach the kit.
  6. Logbook Write the worst-case list in the logbook: the three loudest events that could plausibly happen there.
  7. Logbook A slammed door, a passing siren, a gull landing on the rail.
  8. Logbook Be honest; the list is a prediction the file will grade.
  9. Logbook Below the list, predict the take: five sounds you expect the file to contain.
  10. Logbook Seal the prediction — phase 4 compares it to reality.
  11. Logbook Check the weather and the card: rain forecast means the spot moves indoors, and the card needs space for an hour of audio plus margin.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, a chosen and defensible spot, a written worst-case list, and a sealed five-sound prediction.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
No spot feels worth an hourJudging spots by how interesting they sound now, while you stand in themThat is the lesson inverted — pick for what might visit, not what is performing; boring at minute zero is normal
The worst-case list is empty or vagueImagining the average hour, not the loud oneAsk “what is the loudest thing that happened here this week?” and write that down
Phase 2 · ≈ 10 min

The one gain decision and the deployment

Phase focus: the one gain decision and the deployment. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Recorder At the spot, recreate the loudest item on your worst-case list as nearly as you dare — close the door hard, clap at the rail’s distance — and set gain so that event peaks ≈ −12 dBFS on the recorder’s meter.
  2. Recorder The real surprise gets the remaining headroom.
  3. Recorder Sanity-check the other direction: in the quiet, the meter should still flicker above its floor.
  4. Recorder If the place reads as nothing at all, move the recorder closer to where the life is.
  5. Recorder Foam on if outdoors.
  6. Recorder Set the recorder down on something soft and stable — no hands, no tripod wobble — and route the strap or cable so nothing can drag it.
  7. Logbook Press record, confirm the timer is counting, note the start time in the logbook — and leave.
  8. Logbook Go inside, go to another room, do something else entirely.
  9. Logbook Do not come back early.
  10. Logbook A check-in visit prints your footsteps into the take and teaches the place you are still watching.
Expected result

A recorder running alone at a committed gain, you elsewhere, and a logbook line stating the knob position and the worst case it was set against.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The urge to monitor the take is unbearableStudio habit — every other lab lets you hear the file as it happensThat discomfort is the lesson; write it down and stay away. The file is being made by your absence
Rain starts mid-takeForecast ignored or unluckyRetrieve the kit immediately — electronics and rain do not negotiate; restart indoors at a window instead
Phase 3 · 30–60 min

The wait — you are the fault being removed

Phase focus: the wait — you are the fault being removed. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Leave the take running for 30 minutes minimum, 60 if the spot allows.
  2. Live Meanwhile, listen to one more track of REF at the mark — this time asking only: how long would I have lasted at this location before touching something?
  3. Logbook Write one logbook line about what you imagine your file contains right now.
  4. Logbook You will be wrong in a specific, instructive way.
  5. Logbook At the planned time, retrieve the kit.
  6. Logbook Check it physically — dry, intact, still recording — stop the take, and note the end time.
Expected result

One unbroken 30–60 minute file, a dry recorder, and a written guess about its contents waiting to be graded.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The recorder stopped partway throughBattery or card ran out — the Lab 008 checklist skippedKeep what recorded, log the fault and its prevention rule, and redeploy with spares verified
The take is broken into several filesAuto-split or an accidental stop at deploymentFine if continuous — note the split points; if you touched it mid-take, the clock honestly restarts
Phase 4 · ≈ 25 min

The audit — grade the decision, find the three minutes

Phase focus: the audit — grade the decision, find the three minutes. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Import the take into REP-DARK on a track named FIELD.
  2. Live Look before listening: zoom the waveform, find the tallest peak, and check it — flat top means clipped, headroom means your decision held.
  3. Live + Logbook Run the clipping audit in the logbook: tallest peak’s dBFS, the event that made it, and whether it was on your worst-case list.
  4. Live + Logbook Listen through at the mark — yes, all of it, at least at 1× attention for the first fifteen minutes.
  5. Live + Logbook Mark every event your sealed prediction missed.
  6. Live + Logbook Find the three minutes where the place is most itself — not the loudest stretch;
  7. Live + Logbook the one a stranger could hear and know the place.
  8. Live Trim it to a clip named THE 3 MIN, untouched, no fades beyond the edit.
  9. Live A/B the three minutes against REF at matched loudness, judging only cleanliness and presence: no thump, no blast, no hiss bed, no you.
Expected result

A graded gain decision, a list of events your prediction missed, and three untouched minutes that hold up next to the record on its own terms — clean, patient, unattended.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The loudest event has a flat-topped waveformGain set for the place as it was, not as it might be — the worst case underestimatedLog the miss honestly and redeploy; the list gets one louder entry next time
The quiet stretches hiss when raised to the markGain too cautious — the floor printed below the kit’s self-noiseCloser placement or higher gain next deployment; the worst case and the floor bracket the answer from both sides
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and THE 3 MIN at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • What arrived that you would never have caught while present?
    Answer

    Something — there is always something: the bird that lands once you stop moving, the neighbor’s routine, the building’s own night noises, the second layer of traffic you stopped consciously hearing years ago. Compare the file against your sealed prediction; the gap between them is a measurement of how much your presence costs a place. Relisten to the record knowing every track contains hours of the same patience, and notice that its best moments are exactly the kind your prediction missed.

  • Did your one gain decision survive the loudest surprise?
    Answer

    The waveform answers, not your memory: zoom to the tallest peak and read it. If it clipped, the worst-case list was too polite — and note that you only get to know this now, an hour after the decision, which is the entire difference between field and studio gain. If it held with headroom and the quiet floor stays above the hiss, you made the same bet Watson makes every night, and won it. Relisten to the loudest moment on the record once more; that cleanliness is hundreds of such bets, won.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 008 — “set for the loudest thing that might happen” was a rule; this record shows what the rule buys when the stakes are real. Duration joins gain as a capture decision: how long you stay is set before you press record, like everything else that matters.
