Replicate: duration as a level decision

Copy one clear section of Chris Watson — Stepping into the Dark. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: long unattended takes — recorder deployed, gain set once with headroom for the unexpected, and the recordist gone. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordChris Watson — Stepping into the Dark (1996)
The effectsingle locations, many recorded at night, held long enough that they stop feeling like a backdrop and start feeling like a presence — with no flaw and no recordist audible anywhere
The mechanismlong unattended takes — recorder deployed, gain set once with headroom for the unexpected, and the recordist gone

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Honest version: Watson left his recorder overnight in nature reserves and caves. You won't. A balcony, a room at night with a window open, or a garden you can see from inside is perfectly fine — the discipline of leaving the recorder alone transfers exactly. The wilderness does not.
  • Gear: handheld recorder with foam windshield, charged batteries, empty card — the Lab 008 air path. Headphones stay away until you retrieve the recorder; monitoring during the take is not allowed in this lesson, on purpose. Back at the desk: Live 12, the M4, DT 990 Pro at the mark.
  • New set saved as REP-DARK. Follow the Lab 008 safety checklist — weather and moisture above all. A recorder left out in rain is damaged immediately. Never deploy in a public space where the kit could be moved or taken.
  • What you will make: a short replica of Chris Watson — Stepping into the Dark. The goal is this effect: a single location, recorded long enough that it stops feeling like a backdrop and starts feeling like a presence.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and anything that hides whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism — a long unattended take, with the recorder deployed, gain set once with headroom for the unexpected, and the recordist gone.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences come from your source choices or gear limits, not from missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Chris Watson — Stepping into the Dark. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How long does the place hold before anything is cut away?
    Answer

    Minutes — including stretches where nothing “happens” at all. Watson does not cut to the next event. The quiet is kept because it is what gives the events meaning when they arrive.

  2. Can you hear the recordist anywhere?
    Answer

    No — no breath, no clothing, no footstep, no shifting weight. This is not luck; it is the technique. The recorder was placed and the person left, so the location behaves as if no one is watching.

  3. What can you hear at minute four that you could not at minute one?
    Answer

    Layers and distances — small sounds behind the obvious ones, the depth of the space.

  4. How do the loud events sit against the quiet floor?
    Answer

    Cleanly. A sudden call or movement arrives surprising but not distorted, riding far above a quiet background you barely notice.

  5. If nothing was arranged, what composed the piece?
    Answer

    Three decisions made before pressing record: where to place the recorder, when to start, and how long to leave it. Everything inside the take is the location’s own behavior. The composition is the frame around it.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a 30–60 minute unattended take with no clipping, no handling noise, no sign of the person who left the recorder. Inside it: three minutes where the location is most itself, trimmed out and left untouched. How close you are to the record is measured in cleanliness and patience, not in exotic wildlife.
  • One spot, one take, one gain decision. Set the gain before rolling and never touch it again.
  • You are not present during the recording. Going back to check on it counts as presence and the clock restarts.
  • No processing on the result. The three minutes are found, not made.

The piece in one row: a spot chosen for what might visit it, a gain set once against a written worst case, a long take with nobody attending it, and an audit that finds the three minutes worth keeping.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference, the spot, and the worst-case list

Phase focus: the reference, the spot, and the worst-case list. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a track from Stepping into the Dark into it.
  2. Audio Effects → Utility, trim to peak ≈ −12 dBFS, loop it.
  3. It stays one solo-press away.
  4. Choose the spot: a balcony, your room at night with the window open, a garden corner.
  5. Criteria — undisturbed for an hour, sheltered from rain, and yours: nobody else can reach the kit.
  6. Write the worst-case list in the logbook: the three loudest events that could plausibly happen there.
  7. A slammed door, a passing siren, a gull landing on the rail.
  8. Be honest; the list is a prediction the file will grade.
  9. Below the list, predict the take: five sounds you expect the file to contain.
  10. Seal the prediction — phase 4 compares it to reality.
  11. Check the weather and the card: rain forecast means the spot moves indoors, and the card needs space for an hour of audio plus margin.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Chris Watson — Stepping into the Dark (1996) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference, the spot, and the worst-case list — as the smallest Live set that proves long unattended takes — recorder deployed, gain set once with headroom for the unexpected, and the recordist gone.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects single locations, many recorded at night, held long enough that they stop feeling like a backdrop and start feeling like a presence — with no flaw and no recordist audible anywhere.
  1. Use Analog Rytm MKII for this phase: translate The reference, the spot, and the worst-case list into drum voices or samples, trig conditions, parameter locks, scenes, performance macros, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates long unattended takes — recorder deployed, gain set once with headroom for the unexpected, and the recordist gone, not just the surface of Chris Watson — Stepping into the Dark (1996).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, a chosen and defensible spot, a written worst-case list, and a sealed five-sound prediction.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
No spot feels worth an hourJudging spots by how interesting they sound right now, while you stand in themThat is the lesson backwards. Pick for what might visit, not what is performing. Boring at minute zero is normal.
The worst-case list is empty or vagueImagining the average hour, not the loud oneAsk: “what is the loudest thing that happened here this week?” Write that down.
Phase 2 · ≈ 10 min

The one gain decision and the deployment

Phase focus: the one gain decision and the deployment. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. At the spot, recreate the loudest item on your worst-case list as closely as you can — slam the door hard, clap at the distance from the rail — and set the gain so that event peaks ≈ −12 dBFS on the recorder’s meter.
  2. The real surprise gets whatever headroom is left.
  3. Check the other direction too: in the quiet, the meter should still flicker above its lowest mark.
  4. If the location reads as silence, move the recorder closer to where activity happens.
  5. Foam on if outdoors.
  6. Set the recorder down on something soft and stable — no hands, no tripod wobble — and route the strap or cable so nothing can drag it.
  7. Press record, confirm the timer is counting, note the start time in the logbook, and leave.
  8. Go inside, go to another room, do something else entirely.
  9. Do not come back early.
  10. Going back to check prints your footsteps into the take and the location stops behaving as if no one is there.

Phase 3 · 30–60 min

The wait — your absence is the technique

Phase focus: the wait — your absence is the technique. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Leave the take running for 30 minutes minimum, 60 if the spot allows.
  2. Meanwhile, listen to one more track of REF at the mark — this time asking only: how long would I have lasted at this location before touching something?
  3. Write one logbook line about what you think your file contains right now.
  4. You will be wrong — and the specific way you are wrong is instructive.
  5. At the planned time, retrieve the kit.
  6. Check it physically — dry, intact, still recording — stop the take, and note the end time.

Phase 4 · ≈ 25 min

The audit — grade the decision, find the three minutes

Phase focus: the audit — grade the decision, find the three minutes. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the take into REP-DARK on a track named FIELD.
  2. Look before listening: zoom the waveform, find the tallest peak, and check it — flat top means clipped, headroom means your decision held.
  3. Run the clipping audit in the logbook: tallest peak’s dBFS, the event that made it, and whether it was on your worst-case list.
  4. Listen through at the mark — yes, all of it. At least 1× attention for the first fifteen minutes.
  5. Mark every event your sealed prediction missed.
  6. Find the three minutes where the location is most itself — not the loudest stretch, but the one a stranger could hear and recognize the place.
  7. Trim it to a clip named THE 3 MIN, untouched, no fades beyond the edit.
  8. A/B the three minutes against REF at matched loudness, judging only cleanliness and presence: no thump, no blast, no hiss bed, no you.

Expected result

A graded gain decision, a list of events your prediction missed, and three untouched minutes that hold up next to the record on its own terms — clean, patient, unattended.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The loudest event has a flat-topped waveformGain was set for the place as it was, not as it might be — worst case underestimatedLog the miss honestly and redeploy. Add a louder entry to the list next time.
The quiet stretches hiss when raised to the markGain was too cautious — the quiet floor was printed below the recorder’s own noise levelGet closer or raise the gain on the next deployment. The worst case and the quiet floor set the boundaries from both sides.
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and THE 3 MIN at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • What arrived that you would never have caught while present?
    Answer

    Something — there is always something: the bird that lands once you stop moving, the neighbor’s routine, the building’s night sounds, the layer of traffic you stopped noticing years ago. Compare the file against your sealed prediction. The gap between them shows how much your presence changes a place. The record’s best moments are exactly the kind your prediction missed.

  • Did your one gain decision survive the loudest surprise?
    Answer

    The waveform answers, not your memory. Zoom to the tallest peak and read it. If it clipped, the worst-case list was not loud enough — and you only find this out an hour after the decision, which is the whole difference between field and studio gain. If the peak held with headroom and the quiet floor stays above the noise, you made the same bet Watson makes every recording, and won it.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 008 — “set for the loudest thing that might happen” was a rule. This record shows what that rule earns when the stakes are real. Duration is also a capture decision: how long you stay is chosen before you press record, like everything else that matters.
