Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: repetition makes the object

Copy one clear section of Pierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: short recorded fragments repeated exactly, alternated, and cut hard against each other - composed on disc cutters, before tape, with almost nothing but the edit. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone for the captures — or a mic into M4 input 1 with gain set by the lab-001 ledger if your sounds live near the desk; DT 990 Pro for the edit. The MicroFreak rests this lesson
  • New set saved as REP-CHEMINS — no tempo worth setting; the clock of this piece is the cut, not the metronome
  • Field line: ask before recording people, watch traffic while wearing headphones, monitor at a moderate fixed level — the lab-006 field rules apply to even a ten-minute outing
  • What you will make: a short replica of Pierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer”. Focus on this effect: recognizable train sounds detach from their source and become rhythm cells - pattern replaces scenery, in under three minutes.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: short recorded fragments repeated exactly, alternated, and cut hard against each other - composed on disc cutters, before tape, with almost nothing but the edit.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Pierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many distinct source sounds does the étude use?
    Answer

    Fewer than it feels like - a handful of whistles, chuffs, wheel rhythms, and coach interiors, multiplied by repetition into the impression of plenty.

  2. What happens to a fragment right after you first hear it?
    Answer

    It comes back - usually at once, and usually exactly.

  3. When does a train stop being a train?
    Answer

    At the first exact repeat, for most listeners - the real world does not repeat exactly, so an exact return is the ear’s proof that someone is composing. After two or three returns the label falls off entirely and only the shape remains.

  4. Where is the rhythm coming from?
    Answer

    Two places: from inside the captures - wheels on rail joints are already a drum pattern - and from the cuts themselves, which land like downbeats. Half the percussion on this record is the edit.

  5. How much processing do you hear?
    Answer

    Almost none - cuts, repeats, and the occasional speed change, which is all a 1948 disc cutter allowed. The piece is editing presented as composition, with nothing downstream to hide behind.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: ~90 seconds whose cells are recognizably your recordings on first play and pure pattern by the third repeat — a solo-flip against REF lands on the same hard-cut grammar and the same confidence of return.
  • 4–6 source sounds, each cut to a short cell — no effects, no reverb, no EQ, no pitch. The edit is the whole instrument.
  • Only three moves exist: repeat a cell exactly, alternate two cells, cut hard to a new one.
  • The trains are unreachable; the grammar is not. Your subject is the mechanical day you already live in — doors, kettles, drums of washing machines, traffic.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a pocketful of short recordings of machines, trimmed to one-gesture cells on named tracks, and an Arrangement whose only events are exact repeats, alternations, and hard cuts, mixed at ≈ −12 dBFS.

doors · kettle · traffic · machine drum → recorder or phone · fixed gain → 4–6 trimmed cells · named in Live → repeat / alternate / cut · ≈ −12 dBFS

One cell across three panels. Nothing about the cell ever changes — the count does the detaching. The first play is a door; by the third the label is loosening; by the seventh only the shape remains, and the shape is a rhythm.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the capture list

Phase focus: the reference and the capture list. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Étude aux chemins de fer” into it.
  2. Live Loop the first minute.
  3. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Live REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. Logbook In the logbook, transcribe the looped minute as a cut list in plain words: “whistle ×2 — wheel rhythm ×4 — cut — coach — whistle again.” This sentence chain is the grammar you will imitate.
  6. Logbook Write your capture list: 4–6 mechanical or transport sounds reachable within ten minutes — a latching door, a kettle ramp, a washing-machine drum, passing traffic, a stairwell gate.
  7. Logbook One line each: sound · place · distance.
Expected result

The étude loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, its first minute exists on paper as a cut list, and a capture list of 4–6 named sounds is ready to walk out the door with.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The cut list is impossible to write — everything blurs togetherListening for trains, not for editsRelisten following only the joins; tap a finger at every cut and write down what each tap lands on
REF later feels punchier than anything you recordUtility never trimmed — level conflated with grammarTrim until the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, same as your cells
Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

The captures — short, close, and logged

Phase focus: the captures — short, close, and logged. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Recorder Set the recorder or phone gain once — by the lab-001 ledger if you go through the M4 — on the loudest sound on your list.
  2. Recorder It does not move again.
  3. Recorder Capture each sound on the list 3–4 times, close enough that the source dominates the room — one step closer than feels polite.
  4. Recorder Hold a second of stillness before and after each event: no fingers shifting, no cable brushing a sleeve.
  5. Recorder The mic does not know your touch is not the subject.
  6. Recorder Log every capture the lab-006 way: place · distance · level, one line each.
  7. Recorder Back at the desk, import everything and keep only the cleanest take per sound.
  8. Recorder Delete nothing yet — “cleanest” sometimes changes its mind in phase 3.
Expected result

4–6 sounds captured at one fixed gain, each with stillness around the event and a log line behind it — raw material with clean attacks, waiting to become cells.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Handling noise on every takeThe recorder moved in your grip during the eventStart recording, then move nothing — or set the recorder down and step back
The source drowns in room washToo far away — the room won the perspective decisionHalve the distance and retake; you compose the recording with your feet
Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The cells — cut, trim, name, count

Phase focus: the cells — cut, trim, name, count. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. One track per source, named after it: DOOR, KETTLE, TRAFFIC, DRUM — all faders at 0 dB.
  2. Live Cut each take down to its best single event (⌘ E); trim the front so the attack sits exactly on the clip start.
  3. Live Keep cells between half a second and two seconds.
  4. Live Allow a few milliseconds of fade at each end to kill clicks — that is editing hygiene, not an effect.
  5. Live No other processing of any kind.
  6. Live If a cell is dull, the capture was dull — retake it or choose another;
  7. Live nothing downstream rescues it.
  8. Logbook Loop each cell eight times and write the play count at which its label fell off: “door until the 3rd, rhythm after.” One line per cell — phase 4 and the verification both use this table.
Expected result

4–6 named one-gesture cells with their attacks on the clip start, each with a detachment count in the logbook — the étude’s vocabulary, rebuilt from your own day.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Clicks at the cut pointsEdits landing mid-waveformNudge the cut to a zero crossing or add a 2 ms fade
A cell never detaches, no matter the countToo long — it is still a scene, not a gestureShorten it until one attack and one tail are all that remain
Phase 4 · ≈ 30 min

The arrangement — repeat, alternate, cut

Phase focus: the arrangement — repeat, alternate, cut. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live + Logbook Paper first: write your ~90 seconds as a cut list in the same plain words you used for REF — “door ×4 — kettle ×2 — cut — traffic — door ×4.”
  2. Live Build it in Arrangement: exact repeats by duplication (⌘ D), alternations (ABAB), hard cuts to new cells.
  3. Live Place repeats by ear — the cuts are the clock, and there is no metronome to obey.
  4. Live Mostly one cell at a time; at most one sustained capture (traffic, the machine drum) may run underneath as a bed.
  5. Live Layering is not this lesson’s grammar.
  6. Live The rule, enforced without mercy: a cell that appears once either returns later or gets cut from the piece — single appearances are scenery, and scenery is the thing being abolished.
  7. Live Render ~90 seconds and listen end to end at the mark, no touching.
Expected result

Ninety seconds in which every sound is one of your captures and every event is a repeat, an alternation, or a cut — and somewhere in the first third, a door audibly stops being a door.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
It reads as a sound diary, not musicNo exact repeats — everything plays once and moves onDuplicate, don’t re-perform; the exact return is the move that declares material
The pattern feels stiff and griddedCells quantized to Live’s metronomeTurn the grid off and place repeats by ear — the étude’s clock is the cut, not the bar
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your ninety seconds at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Which repetition count detached each cell from its source?
    Answer

    A number exists for every cell, and the phase-3 table holds your first guesses — now check them against the finished piece, where context speeds the detachment up. Cells with strong pitch or a tight rhythm let go fastest, usually by the second or third exact repeat; long, wash-like cells may never let go, which is why they work as beds, not events. Relisten to the étude and notice it almost never trusts a sound to a single play — Schaeffer knew the count mattered before anyone had named the effect.

  • Does your edit grammar match the étude’s?
    Answer

    Put your two cut lists side by side — the one you transcribed from REF and the one you wrote for yourself. The étude’s cuts arrive in confident, regular volleys: short cells, immediate returns, hard joins. If your list hedges — long gaps, single appearances, fades doing the work of cuts — the grammar drifted toward collage. Relisten to the record’s first minute one more time for its cut rhythm alone, then re-cut your weakest passage to match its confidence.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2, and the detachment-count table seeds the one-minute miniature in Lab 006.
  • Carries into the lab: the étude’s miniature is “selection and editing only” — this lesson is the proof that selection and editing alone are enough to compose, and the 1948 record is the receipt.
