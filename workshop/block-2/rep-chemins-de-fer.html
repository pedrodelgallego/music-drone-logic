Replicate: repetition makes the object

Copy one clear section of Pierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill is about one technique: take a short recorded fragment, repeat it exactly, alternate two of them, and cut hard between them. Schaeffer did this with disc cutters in 1948 — before tape existed — using almost nothing except the edit itself. Do only the steps that make that technique audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone for the captures — or a mic into M4 input 1 with gain set by the lab-001 ledger if your sounds live near the desk; DT 990 Pro for the edit. The MicroFreak rests this lesson
  • New set saved as REP-CHEMINS — no tempo worth setting; the clock of this piece is the cut, not the metronome
  • Field line: ask before recording people, watch traffic while wearing headphones, monitor at a moderate fixed level — the lab-006 field rules apply to even a ten-minute outing
  • What you will make: a short replica of Pierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer”. Focus on this effect: recognizable train sounds detach from their source and become rhythm cells - pattern replaces scenery, in under three minutes.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: short recorded fragments repeated exactly, alternated, and cut hard against each other - composed on disc cutters, before tape, with almost nothing but the edit.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Pierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many distinct source sounds does the étude use?
    Answer

    Fewer than it feels like. A handful of whistles, chuffs, wheel rhythms, and coach interiors — repeated so often they create the impression of variety.

  2. What happens to a fragment right after you first hear it?
    Answer

    It comes back — usually immediately, and usually exactly.

  3. When does a train stop being a train?
    Answer

    At the first exact repeat, for most listeners. Real life doesn’t repeat exactly, so an exact return signals that someone is composing. After two or three returns the “train” label drops away and only the rhythm remains.

  4. Where is the rhythm coming from?
    Answer

    Two places: from inside the recordings (wheels on rail joints are already a drum pattern) and from the cuts themselves, which land like downbeats. Half the percussion on this record is the edit.

  5. How much processing do you hear?
    Answer

    Almost none. Cuts, repeats, and the occasional speed change — all a 1948 disc cutter could do. There is nothing to hide behind here: the piece is editing, presented as composition.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: ~90 seconds where your recordings feel recognizable on the first play, then feel like pure rhythm by the third repeat. A solo-flip against REF should land on the same hard-cut style and the same confident returns.
  • 4–6 source sounds, each cut to a short cell — no effects, no reverb, no EQ, no pitch changes. The edit is the whole instrument.
  • Only three moves: repeat a cell exactly, alternate two cells, or cut hard to a new one.
  • You can't get train sounds right now, but you can get the same grammar. Record mechanical sounds from your day — doors, kettles, washing machine drums, traffic.

The whole system in one row: a few short machine recordings, trimmed to one-gesture cells on named tracks, arranged using only exact repeats, alternations, and hard cuts, mixed at ≈ −12 dBFS.

doors · kettle · traffic · machine drum → recorder or phone · fixed gain → 4–6 trimmed cells · named in Live → repeat / alternate / cut · ≈ −12 dBFS

One cell across three panels. Nothing about the cell changes — repetition does all the work. The first play is a door. By the third, you start hearing rhythm. By the seventh, the "door" label is gone and only the shape remains.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the capture list

Phase focus: the reference and the capture list. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Étude aux chemins de fer” into it.
  2. Loop the first minute.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. In the logbook, transcribe the looped minute as a cut list in plain words: “whistle ×2 — wheel rhythm ×4 — cut — coach — whistle again.” This sentence chain is the grammar you will imitate.
  6. Write your capture list: 4–6 mechanical or transport sounds reachable within ten minutes — a latching door, a kettle ramp, a washing-machine drum, passing traffic, a stairwell gate.
  7. One line each: sound · place · distance.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Pierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer” (1948) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the capture list — as the smallest Live set that proves short recorded fragments repeated exactly, alternated, and cut hard against each other — composed on disc cutters, before tape, with almost nothing but the edit.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects recognizable train sounds detach from their source and become rhythm cells — pattern replaces scenery, in under three minutes.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the capture list into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates short recorded fragments repeated exactly, alternated, and cut hard against each other — composed on disc cutters, before tape, with almost nothing but the edit, not just the surface of Pierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer” (1948).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The étude loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, its first minute exists on paper as a cut list, and a capture list of 4–6 named sounds is ready to walk out the door with.

The captures — short, close, and logged

Do this

  3. Capture each sound on the list 3–4 times, close enough that the source dominates the room — one step closer than feels polite.

  6. Log every capture the lab-006 way: place · distance · level, one line each.

Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The cells — cut, trim, name, count

Phase focus: the cells — cut, trim, name, count. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. One track per source, named after it: DOOR, KETTLE, TRAFFIC, DRUM — all faders at 0 dB.
  2. Cut each take down to its best single event (⌘ E); trim the front so the attack sits exactly on the clip start.
  3. Keep cells between half a second and two seconds.
  4. Allow a few milliseconds of fade at each end to kill clicks — that is editing hygiene, not an effect.
  5. No other processing of any kind.
  6. If a cell is dull, the capture was dull — retake it or choose another;
  7. nothing downstream rescues it.
  8. Loop each cell eight times and write the play count at which its label fell off: “door until the 3rd, rhythm after.” One line per cell — phase 4 and the verification both use this table.

Phase 4 · ≈ 30 min

The arrangement — repeat, alternate, cut

Phase focus: the arrangement — repeat, alternate, cut. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Paper first: write your ~90 seconds as a cut list in the same plain words you used for REF — “door ×4 — kettle ×2 — cut — traffic — door ×4.”
  2. Build it in Arrangement: exact repeats by duplication (⌘ D), alternations (ABAB), hard cuts to new cells.
  3. Place repeats by ear — the cuts are the clock, and there is no metronome to obey.
  4. Mostly one cell at a time; at most one sustained capture (traffic, the machine drum) may run underneath as a bed.
  5. Layering is not this lesson’s grammar.
  6. Rule: if a cell appears only once, either bring it back later or cut it. A sound that plays once and disappears is background scenery — this technique turns sounds into repeating patterns, not scenes.
  7. Render ~90 seconds and listen end to end at the mark, no touching.

Expected result

Ninety seconds in which every sound is one of your captures and every event is a repeat, an alternation, or a cut — and somewhere in the first third, a door audibly stops being a door.

If it fails

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your ninety seconds at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Which repetition count detached each cell from its source?
    Answer

    Your phase-3 table has your first guesses — compare them against the finished piece. Context speeds things up: cells with a strong pitch or tight rhythm stop sounding like objects after two or three exact repeats. Long, wash-like cells may never detach, which is why they work as beds, not events. Relisten to the étude and notice Schaeffer almost never plays a sound only once.

  • Does your edit grammar match the étude’s?
    Answer

    Put your two cut lists side by side — the one from REF and the one you wrote for your own piece. The étude’s cuts are confident: short cells, immediate returns, hard joins. If your list has long gaps, single appearances, or fades where there should be cuts, the style drifted toward collage. Relisten to the record’s first minute for its cut rhythm alone, then re-cut your weakest passage to match.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2, and the detachment-count table seeds the one-minute miniature in Lab 006.
