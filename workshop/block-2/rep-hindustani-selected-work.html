Replicate: the drone as ground

Steal one structural idea from a Hindustani raga alap — a slow Nikhil Banerjee sitar alap is a clear model. Hold a single unchanging drone underneath everything, and introduce a small, rule-bound set of pitches above it very slowly so each note earns its place. The drone never moves; every note's meaning comes from its relationship to that fixed ground. Build a short take and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

You are not copying an actual raga, its melody, or the sitar's tone. You are copying the mechanism: a fixed drone on the tonic (Sa) and the fifth (Pa) acts as a tonal ground — an unchanging reference pitch that plays the whole time — while a small, self-imposed set of allowed notes is introduced above it in free, unmetered time. The drone-as-ground is the form. Everything else is decoration.

Before you start
  • A way to hold a continuous, unending drone — a sustained MicroFreak patch or a looped Digitakt II sample that never decays to silence
  • One pitch chosen as your Sa (tonic). Write it down — it will not change for the whole study
  • Metronome and beat OFF in Live. This study is unmetered; a click would replace the mechanism with rhythm
  • What you will make: a 60–90 second study where an unchanging Sa–Pa drone runs underneath, and you introduce a small fixed set of notes one at a time, slowly, above it. Focus on this effect: each note is heard against the ground, so consonance and tension come from the drone, not from any chord change.
  • What you will turn off: beats, chord progressions, key changes, and any second drone pitch. One ground only.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a fixed drone as ground, with a rule-bound pitch set unfolded slowly above it.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about three minutes of a slow alap. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Does the drone ever change pitch, harmony, or position across the whole opening?
    Answer

    No. The tanpura holds the same pitches — the tonic Sa and usually the fifth Pa — without ever moving. There is no chord change, no key change, no progression. It is a ground, not a backing track: a single fixed reference that stays put so everything above it can be measured against it.

  2. How does a single melodic note get its emotional weight if nothing underneath it changes?
    Answer

    From its distance (interval) to the drone. Against an unmoving Sa, a flat second sounds tense and a fifth sounds like rest — every time, because the reference never shifts. In Western pop the same note would feel different over each chord; here the meaning is fixed because the ground is fixed. The drone is what turns a bare interval into an event.

  3. How fast are new notes introduced, and how does the player move between them?
    Answer

    Extremely slowly, one at a time. The alap often spends a long stretch on just Sa and its near neighbours before reaching higher notes, lingering on each, bending and sliding into it (meend), and repeatedly returning to the ground before going further. The order in which new pitches are introduced is itself the composition.

  4. Is the player free to use any note, or is there a rule set — and how can you tell?
    Answer

    There is a strict rule set: the raga fixes which notes are allowed, which are flat or sharp, characteristic phrases, and often different notes ascending versus descending. You can tell because certain notes never appear and certain melodic moves recur like signatures. That rule set is what makes it a mode rather than free noodling — staying inside the rules is what gives the slow unfolding its shape.

Now build the ground and your own rule set. Do not copy an actual raga's notes — invent your own rule-bound mode and unfold it the same way.

  • Sound target: an unbroken drone on Sa with Pa a fifth above, present the entire study, under a slow line that introduces a small fixed pitch set one note at a time.
  • The ground: tonic Sa plus the fifth Pa, sustained without gaps. It never changes — no second chord, no movement.
  • Your rule set: five or six allowed notes you choose in advance — which are flat/sharp, a couple of characteristic moves, and an ascent/descent shape. Once chosen, you stay inside it.
  • The form: the slow unfolding of that rule set above the fixed ground, always resolving back toward Sa — nothing else.

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Lay the ground: an unchanging Sa–Pa drone

Phase focus: build a drone that never stops and never moves, so every later note has a fixed reference. Compare against REF before moving on.

Sa (tonic) + Pa (fifth above) → continuous ground — never changes
Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track and gain-match it with a Utility so A/Bs are honest.
  2. Set the drone: hold a sustained patch on your chosen Sa, with a long or infinite release so it never falls silent. This is your ground.
  3. Add the fifth: layer Pa, a fifth above Sa, on a second voice or a Digitakt II sample tuned to it. Sa and Pa together, sounding constantly.
  4. Play it for a full minute untouched. It must feel like one stable, unmoving pitch-centre — not a chord that wants to go somewhere, not a pad that swells. A ground, not a progression.

Phase 2 — ~30 minutes

Fix your rule set: a small mode, not free notes

Phase focus: choose a small set of allowed notes and a couple of characteristic moves, in writing, before you improvise. The rules are what make it a mode.

Do this
  1. Write your allowed notes as intervals above Sa — five or six only, e.g. Sa, flat second, fourth, fifth (Pa), flat sixth. Decide which are flat or sharp now and never use a note outside this set.
  2. Write two characteristic moves — short signature phrases that recur, e.g. slide up to the fourth then fall back to Sa. These are the fingerprints of your mode.
  3. Decide an ascent and descent shape — optionally a note you skip going up but use coming down, the way a raga differs between aroha and avaroha.
  4. Play each allowed note against the drone, slowly, and listen to how it feels against Sa. Note which feel like rest (Sa, Pa) and which feel tense — that map is your whole palette, because the fixed ground is what gives each interval its character.

Phase 3 — ~25 minutes

Unfold the mode slowly above the ground

Phase focus: perform a slow, unmetered exposition that introduces your notes one at a time over the fixed drone, and record it. This is the whole piece.

Do this
  1. Write the unfolding order first: the sequence in which notes enter, e.g. Sa alone → reach the flat second, return to Sa → reach the fourth → settle on Pa → touch the flat sixth → descend home. This is your score.
  2. Turn off the metronome and any beat. Time is free — let each note last as long as it wants, with long silences allowed. No grid, no tempo.
  3. Record in one pass, drone running underneath the whole time. Introduce notes slowly, linger on each, bend or slide into them, and return to Sa before reaching further. Never let the drone stop.
  4. Resolve home. However far you climb, the line keeps gravitating back to the ground. End on Sa over the drone. Then listen back: can a stranger feel the slow opening-up without being told a rule?

Verify against the record

A/B your take against a comparable stretch of the alap — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Does each note in yours get its weight from its relationship to the unmoving drone — not from anything changing underneath?

Answer

It should. With a fixed ground, the interval between a note and Sa is the harmony. Mute the melody line: the drone alone should sit perfectly still, and a tense note should only feel tense because of the fixed reference underneath it. If your piece only works because a chord moved or a pad swelled, you replaced the mechanism with a progression — strip the movement and check whether the relationship still carries it.

Did the pitch space unfold slowly and stay inside your rule set, or did it spill into free noodling?

Answer

Check your written rule set against the take, note by note. Every pitch should be one of your allowed notes, and the higher ones should arrive late, after returns to Sa. The most common miss is impatience — reaching the top notes too early, leaving nothing left to unfold. The second miss is a stray note from outside the set, which instantly sounds wrong because the fixed ground makes every interval audible. If both checks pass, you made a mode, not noodling.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: "Matched: …" · "Missed: … (best guess why)" · "Change next: …"
  • Name the one interval against the drone that did the most work — the note whose relation to Sa sold the whole study.
  • Replica audio stays in the logbook as study evidence; it is not a release, and your invented mode is never a copy of a real raga.
