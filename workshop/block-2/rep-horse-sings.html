Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: sustain as practice

Copy one clear section of Pauline Oliveros — Horse Sings from Cloud. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a performer sustains accordion and voice tones under one instruction - do not change a tone until the change asks for itself - so every move is forced by the holding, never chosen by taste. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — or, for the braver route, a mic into M4 input 1 and your own voice
  • The accordion is unobtainable — you are replicating the system: a tone sustained by continuing effort under one instruction. A finger holding the MicroFreak keybed qualifies; your breath qualifies better; the Hold button does not qualify at all — it removes the one thing this lesson is about
  • New set saved as REP-HORSE — no tempo; the only clock is your endurance
  • Phones at the lab-001 mark; if you sing, monitor quietly or on one ear to keep your pitch honest, and watch for level creep during long holds
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes — half of it is one unbroken take; do not schedule this lesson tired
  • What you will make: a short replica of Pauline Oliveros — Horse Sings from Cloud. Focus on this effect: held tones whose changes are predictable before they happen - the wavering asks, then the tone moves; the effort of holding is plainly audible.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a performer sustains accordion and voice tones under one instruction - do not change a tone until the change asks for itself.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Pauline Oliveros — Horse Sings from Cloud. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How long does each tone hold before it moves?
    Answer

    Long - often a minute or more, far past where an ordinary performance would have moved on. The holds are not waiting for anything; they are the content.

  2. What happens just before a tone changes?
    Answer

    It wavers - the pitch shivers, the breath shortens, an edge starts swelling. The change asks for itself audibly before it happens; nothing on this record moves without announcing it.

  3. Could a machine have held these tones?
    Answer

    It could have held the pitches, and the piece would be gone. What you hear is the effort - bellows that must be pumped, a breath that runs out - a hold that could fail at any moment and does not.

  4. Who decides when a tone changes?
    Answer

    The holding decides. The instruction forbids changing by taste, so the only legal trigger is untenability - the breath gone, the arm done, the ear unable to bear the interval longer.

  5. What is the music here — the tones, or the holding?
    Answer

    The holding. The pitches are few and plain; what fills the time is the audible labor of sustaining them and the wavering negotiations before each move.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a take in which every change can be heard coming — wavering first, then the move — and in which a listener can tell your held tones from machine-held ones blind. Closeness to the record’s discipline is the measure.
  • One tone at a time, held by effort — a finger on the keybed or your own breath. No Hold button, no sustain pedal, no loop.
  • Change only when holding becomes untenable — never because you got bored, never because it would sound good.
  • After the take, every change gets a log line: when it asked and why.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one instruction on paper, one tone held by effort until holding becomes untenable, a single unbroken take, and a log that records when each change asked and why — plus one machine-held take printed afterwards, as the control.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the timing

Phase focus: the reference and the timing. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Horse Sings from Cloud” into it, and loop four or five characteristic minutes.
  2. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Logbook Time two full holds with a stopwatch — settled to changed — and write both numbers in the logbook.
  4. Logbook They are your minimum.
  5. Logbook For each of those changes, write one line on what asked for it: wavering, breath, a swelling edge.
  6. Logbook You are building the vocabulary your own log will use.
  7. Live ⌘ T; rename HELD, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, two hold durations and two “what asked” lines are on paper, and one armed track waits for the take.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The holds seem short when you time themYou timed from first wavering, not from settledRestart the watch when the tone settles; the wavering belongs to the hold, not the change
No meter movement when testing the inputExt. In channel does not match the physical jackSource on 1 — the lab-001 wiring table
Phase 2 · ≈ 10 min

The instruction and the setup

Phase focus: the instruction and the setup. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Logbook Write the instruction on a card and prop it on the desk: “Hold the tone.
  2. Logbook Do not change it until the change asks for itself.” This card is the score;
  3. Logbook nothing else is.
  4. MicroFreak MicroFreak route: Type → V.Analog, attack ≈ 2–3 s, sustain full, release long, filter set once, zero LFO.
  5. MicroFreak The note sounds only while your finger holds the keybed — Hold stays off.
  6. MicroFreak Voice route: mic into M4 input 1, one comfortable vowel, one comfortable pitch.
  7. Live Quiet monitoring, or one ear off.
  8. Live Set input-1 gain on a held tone, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — hold for a full minute while you check;
  9. Live long holds drift.
  10. Live Choose only your starting pitch — a low, cheap-to-hold note.
  11. Live Every later pitch must be chosen by the moment, not now.
Expected result

An instruction card in view, a static patch or a ready voice, gain proven on a one-minute hold, and exactly one decision made in advance.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The tone sustains after your finger liftsHold engaged or release too longDisengage Hold; the lesson is void if the machine does the holding
The patch breathes on its own during the test holdAn LFO or cycling envelope survivedZero all patch movement — wavering must be earned, not synthesized
Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

The held take — one pass, no mercy

Phase focus: the held take — one pass, no mercy. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Record-arm HELD, start Arrangement recording, settle, and begin the first tone.
  2. Live Hold.
  3. Live When the urge to change arrives, check it against the card: is holding actually untenable — finger burning, breath gone, the interval with the room unbearable — or are you merely bored?
  4. Live Boredom holds.
  5. Live When a change genuinely asks, let it happen the way the record does: the wavering will already be on tape before you move.
  6. Live Settle into the new tone and hold again.
  7. Live Continue for 10–12 minutes — aim for only four to six changes in that span;
  8. Live your phase-1 numbers are the minimum hold.
  9. Logbook Stop, rest one minute, then relisten with the logbook open.
  10. Logbook For every change, write two things: when it asked (timestamp of the first wavering) and why (the physical reason).
  11. Logbook No change may go unexplained.
Expected result

One unbroken 10–12 minute take with a handful of changes, each preceded by audible wavering, and a log in which every change has a timestamp and a physical cause.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Changes every twenty or thirty secondsBoredom dressed up as untenabilityRe-take with the card in view; the phase-1 hold times are the law, and boredom is not on the list of causes
A change has no audible wavering before itThe move was chosen, not forced — taste got inMark it in the log as illegal and re-take; a forced change always announces itself first
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The machine control — what effort sounds like

Phase focus: the machine control — what effort sounds like. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename MACHINE, Ext. In 1.
  2. MicroFreak Engage Hold on the MicroFreak and print the same pitch sequence as your take — same order, roughly the same durations, hands off between changes.
  3. Live A/B HELD against MACHINE at matched loudness, one minute each.
  4. Logbook Write down every difference you hear — be concrete: shimmer, drift, breath, the swelling before changes.
  5. Live A/B HELD against REF: is your wavering as legible as the record’s?
  6. Live Can you hear your changes coming the way you hear theirs?
  7. Live Log the verdict: which of the two takes is music, and what exactly the other one is missing.
Expected result

Two takes of the same pitches that do not sound alike, a concrete list of the differences, and a logbook line naming what the machine take lacks.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The two takes sound identicalYour held take was too safe — short holds, no strain ever reached the tapeRe-take phase 3 with longer holds; the differences live at the edge of failure, and you never went there
The held take just sounds sloppy, not alivePitch drifting constantly, not wavering before changesPick a cheaper pitch to hold and steady the body; the target is stable holds with legible asking, not continuous wobble
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your held take at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Listening back, can you hear each of your changes coming?
    Answer

    You should — wavering, a shortening breath, an edge that swells — the same cues the reference pass taught you to find in the record. A cold listener should be able to point at the tape and say “it’s about to move” a few seconds early, and be right. Any change with no warning on tape was chosen by taste, and the instruction forbade it. Relisten to the record’s changes once more; not one of them is unannounced.

  • What does the machine-held take lack?
    Answer

    Nothing a meter can see and everything an ear can. The pitch is steadier, the level flatter — and there is no risk in it, so there is no attention in it: a hold that cannot fail makes no claim on the listener. The held take carries effort, and effort is the content of this piece. Relisten to the machine take once, then the record — the distance between them is the entire lesson.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 005 — the System Spec’s human role reads “a hand moves only when the tone stops breathing.” You have now practiced exactly that rule, and you know what its violations sound like: changes without asking.
