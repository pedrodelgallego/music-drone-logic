Replicate: displacement as composition

Steal one idea from Bill Fontana's Landscape Sculpture with Fog Horns: take a sound whose place of origin is instantly recognisable and move it somewhere it does not belong. The composition is not the sound and not the processing — it is the gap between where the sound comes from and where it now plays. Build a short take and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

You are not copying Fontana's harbour, fog horns, or equipment. You are copying the mechanism: a sound that clearly belongs to one place, moved into a space that contradicts it, so the listener's room starts to feel like somewhere else. Moving the sound is the compositional act. You barely process it — you relocate it.

The system you are replicating
Source recordBill Fontana — Landscape Sculpture with Fog Horns (1982); see also Distant Trains (1984)
The effectA sound that obviously belongs to a vast outdoor place is heard inside a small room, and the room starts to feel like the harbour, rail yard, or far-off place the sound implies
The mechanismA source with a clear sense of place is moved into a space that contradicts it; the gap between where the sound seems to be from and where it now plays is the form — not melody, not timbre, not processing

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • One field recording of your own — a sound whose place is unmistakable (a passing train, surf, a church interior, a busy kitchen), captured on your phone or recorder and loaded onto a Digitakt II track
  • A small set of impulse responses for convolution reverb in Live — at least one "big outdoor / large hall" IR and one "small dry room" IR, so you can re-place the sound into either
  • Headphones or a stereo monitor setup — displacement is heard, not measured; the new space has to play in real L/R
  • What you will make: a 60–90 second study where one clearly-located sound is relocated into a space it does not belong to, and the listener's sense of "where am I" shifts. Focus on this effect: the room you are sitting in becomes the place the sound implies.
  • What you will turn off: heavy processing, melody, beat, and any second idea. Keep the source legible — the more you mangle it, the less the displacement reads.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a legible source moved into a contradictory space, where the mismatch carries the whole form.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to a few minutes of Landscape Sculpture with Fog Horns (or any documented Fontana relocation). Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What place does the sound belong to — and how fast can you name it?
    Answer

    Almost instantly: a harbour, a coastline, a working waterfront. That quick recognition is the whole prerequisite. Fontana chooses sounds whose origin is clear in a second or two — fog horns, gulls, open water. If the source were ambiguous, there would be nothing to displace, because the listener would have no implied place to be moved out of.

  2. How much has the recording been processed or "designed"?
    Answer

    Very little. The sounds are close to raw — the work is relocation, not processing. That restraint is deliberate: each layer of processing erodes the clarity that makes the displacement work. The composition is the choice of source and the choice of new space, so the sound itself is left mostly untouched so it still clearly says where it is from.

  3. Where is the composition, if it is not in the notes or the timbre?
    Answer

    In the mismatch. The sound implies a vast outdoor place; you hear it somewhere it could never naturally occur — a gallery, a plaza, a room. The composed event is the tension between those two facts. Fontana's instrument is the pairing of a source-place and a playback-place that contradict each other. What he is shaping is the listener's sense of "where am I."

  4. What does the displacement do to your sense of the room you are actually in?
    Answer

    It fades. When a sound that implies open water fills a small room, the room stops feeling convincing as itself — the ear trusts the sound's implied scale over the walls. Your room becomes the harbour. That sense of being moved somewhere while you stay still is the effect you are trying to rebuild: you get it with one well-chosen sound and one well-chosen contradictory space.

Now build the displacement you just described. Do not build from memory of the sound — build from the structure: legible source, contradictory space, minimal processing.

  • Sound target: one legible source sound, re-placed into a space that contradicts its origin, so a listener relocates without being told to.
  • The source: your own field recording, kept dry and intact — its spatial signature must survive so the place stays nameable.
  • The new space: a convolution IR (or a contradictory paired layer) that puts the sound somewhere it does not belong — vast made intimate, or intimate made vast.
  • The form: the tension between implied place and playback place — nothing else. No tune, no beat, no decoration.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Capture a source with a legible place

Phase focus: get one sound whose origin is unmistakable, kept clean enough that its spatial signature survives. Without legibility there is nothing to displace. Compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track and gain-match it with a Utility so A/Bs are honest.
  2. Choose your source by its place, not its sound: write the single sentence "anyone hearing this knows they are at ………". If you cannot finish that sentence, the source is too ambiguous — pick another.
  3. Load your field recording dry on a track. Keep its own room tone and reverb tail intact — that captured space is the spatial signature you will later contradict.
  4. Play it to a stranger (or yourself, eyes closed) and check they name the place in a second or two. That recognition is position 0; everything after this displaces from it.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Bill Fontana — Landscape Sculpture with Fog Horns (1982); see also Distant Trains (1984) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — Capture a source with a legible place — as the smallest Live set that proves A source with a clear sense of place is moved into a space that contradicts it; the gap between where the sound seems to be from and where it now plays is the form — not melody, not timbre, not processing.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects A sound that obviously belongs to a vast outdoor place is heard inside a small room, and the room starts to feel like the harbour, rail yard, or far-off place the sound implies.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate Capture a source with a legible place into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.

  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.

One clean, dry source whose place of origin a listener names instantly — a legible "where" you can now move.

Phase 2 — ~35 minutes

Transplant it into a contradictory space

Phase focus: re-place the sound somewhere it does not belong, using convolution to overwrite its implied scale — or by pairing it with material from a contradictory space. Keep processing minimal so the source stays legible.

dry source (place A) → convolution IR / contradictory space (place B) → listener relocated to B
Do this
  1. Decide the contradiction first: write "from ……… into ………" — e.g. from open harbour into a small bathroom, or from a tiny kitchen into a cathedral. The bigger the gap in implied scale, the stronger the displacement.
  2. Re-place with convolution: put Live's Convolution Reverb (or Hybrid Reverb's convolution mode) on the source and load the IR of the new space. The IR re-paints the sound's surroundings — it is how you move a sound without warping the sound itself.
  3. Or pair, don't process: let the MicroFreak hold a quiet sustained tone that belongs to the contradictory space (a dry, close, intimate hum under a vast field recording), so the two spaces argue inside the same stereo image.
  4. Set the wet/dry so the new space is convincing but the source is still nameable. The displacement only reads while the listener can still tell where the sound is from.

Phase 3 — ~25 minutes

Curate the displacement and record it

Phase focus: the composition is the curation of the displacement, not the timbre. Shape how and when the relocation lands, then capture one pass. This is the whole piece.

Do this
  1. Write the relocation as a short score: when the dry source is heard in its own place, when the new space takes over, and whether you ever let the two compete. The order of those reveals is the form.
  2. Stage the turn: consider opening with a few seconds of the source nearly dry (so the listener fixes its real place), then bring the contradictory space up so the relocation is felt as an event, not a setting.
  3. Use the stereo field as part of the new space: let the contradictory ambience spread or close the image so the room dimensions audibly change shape around the listener.
  4. Record the relocation in one pass — no metre, no beat — and let the durations sit long enough that the listener settles into the new place.
  5. Listen back with eyes closed: does a stranger feel themselves relocate, without being told what the sound is or where it now plays?

If it fails

Verify against the record

A/B your take against a comparable stretch of Landscape Sculpture with Fog Horns — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Can a listener still name where your source comes from while it plays in the wrong place?

Answer

They must — that recognisability is the whole mechanism. The displacement only exists in the gap between a nameable origin and a contradictory new space. If your processing erased the source's identity, you have an ambient texture, not a relocation. Fontana keeps the source almost untouched for exactly this reason. If yours has gone abstract, go back to phase 2 and reduce the treatment until the place comes back.

Does the listening room stop being itself — does the new place take over with no melody, beat, or trick holding it up?

Answer

It should. The whole claim is that a sound's implied place can overwrite the room you are sitting in. If your take only works because you added a drone swell, a rhythm, or a dramatic edit, you replaced displacement with conventional production. Remove the addition and check whether the relocation still holds on the contradiction alone. In Fontana, the bare mismatch is enough.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: "Matched: …" · "Missed: … (best guess why)" · "Change next: …"
  • Name the one choice that did the most work — the source whose place was most legible, or the contradiction that opened the widest gap.
  • Replica audio stays in the logbook as study evidence; capture your own sources and never copy Fontana's actual transmissions — it is not a release.
