Replicate: the contact mic as microscope

Copy one clear section of Crys Cole — “Sand/Stones”. Build a short study from contact-mic micro-sounds amplified into the main material, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill is about one technique: take contact-mic micro-sounds — rubbing, scraping, tiny frictions too quiet for an air mic — and amplify them until they become the main material. The contact mic hears vibration through a surface, not air, so faint events become loud, detailed and strange. Do only the steps that make those amplified micro-sounds the subject.

Before you start
  • Gear: a contact mic (piezo) into M4 input 1 — high gain, this is the lab-008 contact-mic path; DT 990 Pro for judging; the lab-006 capture log with a column for surface and action
  • New set saved as REP-SAND-STONES — no tempo; this is timed in seconds and moves at the speed of a hand
  • Contact mics are quiet at source and need heavy gain — set gain on the busiest scrape, watch for thumps when you touch the mic, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS; phones at the lab-001 mark
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — one round of contact-mic captures, one edit pass, one short study, then file it and stop
  • What you will make: a short replica of Crys Cole — “Sand/Stones”. Focus on this effect: tiny frictions amplified into vivid, full-sized material.
  • What you will turn off: the air mic, big gestures, and any source that is already loud — the lesson lives in the very quiet.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: contact-mic micro-sounds (rubbing, scraping) amplified into the main material.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are surface and action, not a loud source standing in for the amplified micro-world.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Crys Cole — “Sand/Stones”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What scale do these sounds feel like?
    Answer

    Microscopic, magnified. You hear textures that would be inaudible across a room — the friction of grains, the click of grit — blown up to fill the foreground. The contact mic acts like a microscope for sound.

  2. How were these recorded — air mic or contact mic?
    Answer

    Contact mic, clearly. The sounds are dry, close, and have no room around them — they come through the surface, not the air. There is a tactile, in-the-hand quality an air mic could never get this loud.

  3. What kind of action makes them?
    Answer

    Small, slow, deliberate — rubbing, dragging, turning, scraping. The gestures are tiny but the gain makes them enormous. The performance is in the hand, not in volume.

  4. Why amplify something so quiet?
    Answer

    Because amplification reveals a world. At normal level a finger on sand is nearly silent; at high gain it is a dense, detailed event. The piece is an argument that the quietest material is the richest if you get close enough.

  5. Where is the composition, if it is just rubbing and scraping?
    Answer

    In the choice of surfaces, the pacing of gestures, and the editing of the captures. Choosing what to rub, how slowly, and how to sequence the takes — those decisions are the form.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Contact mic: press the piezo flat to the surface, raise input gain high, set gain on the busiest scrape, and keep your hand off the mic body so handling thumps do not swamp the take.
  • Sound target: one 60–90 second study built from amplified micro-sounds, where tiny frictions are the full-sized material.
  • 6–10 contact-mic captures across at least three surfaces: grit/sand, stone, paper or wood — rubbing, scraping, turning.
  • The interest comes from amplified detail and gesture, not from loud sources or processing.
  • Editing, level and gentle EQ to tame handling thumps only — adding reverb or synthesis breaks the close-up world.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the contact-only rule

Phase focus: the reference and the contact-only rule. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag the reference section in, and loop it at ≈ −12 dBFS with Audio Effects → Utility.
  2. Write the rule in full: every sound comes through a contact mic from a tiny gesture, amplified hard — no air mic, no loud sources.
  3. List surfaces and actions: sand or grit (drag a finger), stones (turn in the hand), paper (slow scrape), wood (rub a nail), fabric (slide).
  4. Pick at least three surfaces so the study has timbral variety from material, not from effects.
  5. Decide the membership test: if the gesture is loud enough to hear across the room without the mic, it is too big for this study.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Crys Cole — “Sand/Stones” and use audio clips, gain, and clip envelopes — no synthesis.
  2. Build the phase goal — the reference and the contact-only rule — as the smallest Live set that proves contact-mic micro-sounds (rubbing, scraping) amplified into the main material.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects whether the micro-detail stays the subject.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: capture the contact mic into recorder buffers, then translate the reference and the contact-only rule into Flex machines, slices, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the control path so it demonstrates contact-mic micro-sounds (rubbing, scraping) amplified into the main material, not just the surface of Crys Cole — “Sand/Stones”.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main move in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the micro-detail stays the subject.
Expected result

The reference loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, the contact-only rule is on paper, and a list of at least three surfaces with their actions is written before any capture.

Phase 2 · ≈ 35 min

Capture the micro-world

Phase focus: capture the micro-world. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Press the contact mic flat to the first surface, raise the gain high, and set it on the busiest scrape so peaks land ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Record 20–40 seconds per surface of slow, deliberate gestures — drag, rub, turn — keeping your hand off the mic body.
  3. Vary speed and pressure within each take; the detail changes enormously with tiny changes in the hand.
  4. If a handling thump or a too-loud knock spikes, pause and restart — don't ride the gain mid-take.
  5. Log each take with surface and action — the lab-006 log gains a surface/action column here.
  6. Trim each capture to its most detailed continuous stretch.

Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

The study — pace the close-up

Phase focus: the study. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Lay your captures in Arrangement and build one 60–90 second passage by selection and pacing — let the micro-sounds breathe.
  2. Sequence surfaces for contrast: grit's busy hiss against stone's sparse clicks against paper's dry drag.
  3. Keep some space — silence between gestures makes the amplified detail land harder when it returns.
  4. Resist reverb: the close, dry, no-room quality is the whole sound; if you add space you lose the microscope.
  5. A gentle high-pass to remove low handling rumble is allowed; nothing that smears the detail.
  6. Print the study to one audio track named PRINT.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your study at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does it feel close and magnified, like a microscope?
    Answer

    That is the pass condition. A working study puts the listener right inside the gesture — dry, detailed, in-the-hand, with no room around it. If it sounds like a normal recording of someone fiddling, the gain or the contact is wrong. Push the gain and the contact harder. The record passes this constantly; its whole effect is the magnification of the tiny.

  • Are the quiet gestures genuinely the main material?
    Answer

    Check that nothing loud is carrying the study for you. If a knock or a thump is the most interesting event, you have leaned on a big sound instead of the micro-world. Re-edit so the amplified frictions are the subject and the loud transients are trimmed away — the reference earns its drama from rubbing and scraping, not from impacts.
