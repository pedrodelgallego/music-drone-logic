Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: tuning as the motion

Copy one clear section of Kali Malone — No Sun to Burn. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: two sustained voices in strict stepwise counterpoint, one chord per tens of seconds; close intervals beat fast, wide intervals sit almost still - the interval choices compose the rhythm. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: Ableton Live and the DT 990 Pro carry this lesson; the MicroFreak is the optional second route, on the lab-001 wiring as ever
  • The pipe organ is unobtainable — you are replicating the system: two exactly pitched voices that sustain without decay, moving by rule. Live’s Wavetable holds a chord forever at exact pitch, which is the only organ property this piece needs; everything else is the counterpoint’s doing
  • New set saved as REP-NOSUN — tempo is irrelevant; the score is measured in seconds per chord
  • Held chords at steady level fatigue faster than they feel — phones at the lab-001 mark, a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes — a third of it at the desk with a pencil, before any sound exists
  • What you will make: a short replica of Kali Malone — No Sun to Burn. Focus on this effect: held organ chords that pulse though no one is playing a pulse, changing at glacial speed with the inevitability of a rule being followed.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: two sustained voices in strict stepwise counterpoint, one chord per tens of seconds.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Kali Malone — No Sun to Burn. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is pulsing inside the held chord?
    Answer

    Nothing anyone is playing. Two pipes tuned close enough to interfere produce the throb themselves - the pulse is a physical property of the interval.

  2. How fast does the music move?
    Answer

    One chord per tens of seconds - far below playing speed, near the speed of breathing slowly. Nothing happens between changes except the beating. Relisten and time three chords with a stopwatch; the numbers calibrate your own score’s clock.

  3. Do the chord changes feel chosen, or obeyed?
    Answer

    Obeyed. Each voice moves by step or holds; nothing leaps, nothing surprises, and every arrival sounds like the consequence of the one before - counterpoint as law, not gesture.

  4. Which intervals beat hard, and which sit almost still?
    Answer

    The close ones beat - seconds and narrow sevenths churn audibly - while octaves and pure fifths sit nearly motionless. The piece’s drama is the alternation: tension is a fast beat, resolution is the beating slowing toward stillness.

  5. Where is the expression — the dynamics, the rubato?
    Answer

    Nowhere. No swells, no accelerando, no touch - the organ holds every chord at identical level until the rule says move. All the feeling is delegated to the intervals: the composer expresses by choosing distances, not by performing them.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a progression whose changes feel like law arriving, with audible beating inside the close dyads and near-stillness inside the wide ones — solo-flipped against REF, the same severity at the same patience.
  • The score is written on paper before any sound exists — whole notes, stepwise motion, strict rules; the pencil composes, the rig only obeys.
  • One chord per 20–30 seconds, identical level throughout — no swells, no touch, no expression except interval choice.
  • The beating is the texture: listen into each dyad, and time what you hear.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a two-voice score written by rule on paper, realized as two edgeless sustained tracks at one chord per 20–30 seconds, flat level throughout, with the beating inside each dyad — the tuning, not the playing — supplying every bit of motion.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the pulse clock

Phase focus: the reference and the pulse clock. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “No Sun to Burn” into it, and loop four or five minutes.
  2. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Logbook Time three chord durations — change to change — and write them down.
  4. Logbook They set your seconds-per-chord.
  5. Logbook Pick two chords, one churning and one still, and count the beats in ten seconds for each.
  6. Logbook Two pulse rates, on paper.
  7. Logbook Write one line predicting which of your own planned intervals will beat hardest.
  8. Logbook Phase 4 grades this prediction.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, three chord durations and two beat counts are logged, and a falsifiable prediction is on paper.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
You cannot find the pulse to countListening to the chord instead of into itPick the loudest dyad and attend to its loudness ripple, not its pitch — count the ripple
The chords seem to have no clear boundarySome voice moves while another holds — changes overlapTime the moments any voice moves; the counterpoint staggers on purpose, and that counts as a change
Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The paper score — rules first, taste never

Phase focus: the paper score — rules first, taste never. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Take paper.
  2. Two staves, or just two rows of note names — voice 1 and voice 2.
  3. The rules, fixed before writing: whole notes only · each voice moves by one step or holds · the voices never leap · never both leap · open and close on a stable interval — unison, fifth, or octave · eight to ten chords total.
  4. Logbook Write the progression obeying the rules even when the result surprises you — especially when it surprises you.
  5. Logbook Where a rule forces a second or a seventh, a fast-beating chord has been composed;
  6. Logbook that is the texture arriving by law.
  7. Logbook Under each dyad, write the interval’s name and your prediction: beats fast / beats slow / still.
  8. Logbook Assign each chord a duration from the phase-1 timings — 20 to 30 seconds each.
  9. Logbook Total the piece: four to five minutes.
Expected result

A complete score on paper — eight to ten dyads, every move legal, every interval named with a beating prediction and a duration — and no instrument touched yet.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The progression sounds “nice” in your head and nowhere beatsTaste smuggled in — steps chosen to avoid all dissonanceRe-derive two middle chords strictly from the rules; at least two dyads must be close intervals, or there is no texture to replicate
The voices drift far apart and stop relatingBoth voices stepping in the same direction every chordAdd the classical guard: after parallel motion, move one voice against the other or hold it
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Realize the score — flat, exact, inhumanly slow

Phase focus: realize the score — flat, exact, inhumanly slow. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak + Live Two MIDI tracks, VOICE 1 and VOICE 2, each with Instruments → Wavetable: a plain, slightly hollow table, filter mostly open, attack ≈ 1 s, sustain full, release ≈ 2 s, zero LFO — an organ’s patience without an organ’s name.
  2. Live Draw the score as MIDI: one whole-note clip per voice, each note lasting its written duration — at 120 BPM, a 30-second chord is 15 bars;
  3. Live draw the notes that long and let the grid go unused.
  4. MicroFreak + Live Alternative route: print each voice from the MicroFreak instead — same patch both takes, Hold engaged, one note per chord, re-tuned carefully against a tuner between notes.
  5. MicroFreak + Live Slower, braver, the same system.
  6. MicroFreak + Live Both voices at identical, fixed level, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — no automation anywhere;
  7. MicroFreak + Live expression was spent in phase 2.
  8. MicroFreak + Live Play the piece end to end once, score in hand, checking only correctness: right notes, right durations, no swells.
Expected result

Four to five minutes of two flat, exact voices executing the paper score — and, audibly, each dyad already pulsing at its own rate with nobody playing a pulse.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
No beating anywhere, even on the secondsVoices too pure and too far apart in register, or hard-pannedBring both voices into the same octave region and toward center — beating needs the tones to share air
A chord wobbles in a cyclic, synthetic wayAn LFO or unison-detune survived in the patchZero all patch movement and unison; the only legal interference is between the two voices
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Listen into the chord — and grade the prediction

Phase focus: listen into the chord — and grade the prediction. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the piece end to end, no touching, attention inside each chord — the ripple, not the pitches.
  2. Logbook For every dyad, count beats over ten seconds and write the rate on the score next to your phase-2 prediction.
  3. Logbook Grade each prediction right or wrong.
  4. Logbook Listen for the law: does each change feel like an event arriving by rule, the way the record’s do — predictable a moment before it lands?
  5. Live A/B two of your chords against the two REF chords you timed in phase 1, at matched loudness: is your still as still, your churn as fast?
  6. Live Log the full beat-rate table — interval name, predicted, measured.
  7. Live This table is the lesson in numbers.
Expected result

A score annotated with measured beat rates beside predictions, an A/B that holds at matched loudness, and the felt confirmation that the changes arrive by law, not taste.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The changes feel arbitrary, not inevitableThe score broke its own rules somewhere — a leap, a skipped stepAudit the score against the phase-2 rules; fix the illegal move and re-draw that chord
Beat rates do not change between chordsAll intervals landed at similar distances — the score lacks contrastRe-derive two chords so the progression alternates wide and close; the drama is the alternation
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does each chord change feel like an event arriving by law, not taste?
    Answer

    It should feel obeyed — the next chord lands as a consequence, predictable a breath before it arrives, exactly as the record’s changes were when you tried predicting them in the reference pass. If a change feels like a choice, find where taste touched the score and let the rule overrule it. Inevitability cannot be performed; it can only be written.

  • Did the beating speed change between intervals — and did you time it?
    Answer

    Yes, and measurably: the close dyads should count several beats per second, the fifths and octaves nearly none, and the rate should jump the instant a chord changes — a property no tremolo or LFO imitates honestly. Your beat-rate table is the proof. Relisten to the record’s stillest chord once more; stillness, you now know, is also a tuning decision.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
