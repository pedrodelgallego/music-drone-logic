Replicate: tuning as the motion

Copy one clear section of Kali Malone — No Sun to Burn. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches how the record works: two sustained tones move in strict stepwise counterpoint (each voice moves by one step at a time, following a rule). One chord lasts tens of seconds. Close intervals produce fast beating (a pulsing throb); wide intervals sit almost still. The interval choices are what creates the rhythm. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: Ableton Live and the DT 990 Pro carry this lesson; the MicroFreak is the optional second route, on the lab-001 wiring as ever
  • The pipe organ is unobtainable — you are replicating the system: two exactly pitched voices that sustain without decay, moving by rule. Live’s Wavetable holds a chord forever at exact pitch, which is the only organ property this piece needs; everything else is the counterpoint’s doing
  • New set saved as REP-NOSUN — tempo is irrelevant; the score is measured in seconds per chord
  • Held chords at steady level fatigue faster than they feel — phones at the lab-001 mark, a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes — a third of it at the desk with a pencil, before any sound exists
  • What you will make: a short replica of Kali Malone — No Sun to Burn. Focus on this effect: held organ chords that pulse though no one is playing a pulse, changing at glacial speed with the inevitability of a rule being followed.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: two sustained voices in strict stepwise counterpoint, one chord per tens of seconds.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Kali Malone — No Sun to Burn. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is pulsing inside the held chord?
    Answer

    No one is playing the pulse. Two tones tuned close enough to each other create the throb by interfering with each other. The pulse is a physical property of that interval (the distance between the two notes).

  2. How fast does the music move?
    Answer

    One chord every tens of seconds — much slower than normal playing speed, close to the pace of slow breathing. Nothing happens between changes except the beating. Relisten and time three chords with a stopwatch; those numbers set the pace for your own score.

  3. Do the chord changes feel chosen, or obeyed?
    Answer

    Obeyed. Each voice moves by one step or stays put. Nothing jumps, nothing surprises, and every new chord sounds like the logical result of the one before. The counterpoint follows rules, not instincts.

  4. Which intervals beat hard, and which sit almost still?
    Answer

    Close intervals beat fast — seconds and narrow sevenths churn audibly. Octaves and pure fifths sit nearly motionless. The drama is the alternation: a fast beat is tension, and the beating slowing toward stillness is resolution.

  5. Where is the expression — the dynamics, the rubato?
    Answer

    Nowhere. No swells, no speeding up, no expressive touch. The organ holds every chord at the same level until the rule says to move. All the feeling comes from the intervals themselves. The composer expresses by choosing distances between notes, not by performing them.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a progression where each change feels inevitable, with audible beating inside close intervals and near-stillness inside wide ones. Solo-flipped against REF, both should share the same austerity and the same patience.
  • Write the score on paper before any sound exists — whole notes, stepwise motion, strict rules. The pencil composes; the rig only plays back.
  • One chord per 20–30 seconds, the same level throughout. No swells, no expressive touch. Interval choice is the only expression.
  • The beating is the texture. Listen into each interval and time what you hear.

How the piece works: a two-voice score written by rule on paper, played back as two sustained tracks at one chord per 20–30 seconds, flat level throughout. The beating that happens inside each interval — caused by the tuning, not by anything you perform — supplies all the motion.

paper score · two voices · rule-bound → Wavetable organ patch · two tracks (or printed MicroFreak takes) → one chord per 20–30 s · flat level → listen into the beating · stopwatch it

Eight chords of two-voice counterpoint. Each voice moves by one step or holds. Where the two lines get close, the chord beats fast. Where they are far apart, it sits almost still. The tick marks under the staff represent the pulse the tuning itself creates.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the pulse clock

Phase focus: the reference and the pulse clock. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “No Sun to Burn” into it, and loop four or five minutes.
  2. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Time three chord durations — change to change — and write them down.
  4. They set your seconds-per-chord.
  5. Pick two chords, one churning and one still, and count the beats in ten seconds for each.
  6. Two pulse rates, on paper.
  7. Write one line predicting which of your own planned intervals will beat hardest.
  8. Phase 4 grades this prediction.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Kali Malone — No Sun to Burn (2019, The Sacrificial Code) and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.

  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects held organ chords that pulse even though no one is playing a pulse, changing extremely slowly as if following a rule that must be obeyed.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The paper score — rules first, taste never

Phase focus: the paper score — rules first, taste never. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this

  2. Two staves, or just two rows of note names — voice 1 and voice 2.
  3. The rules, fixed before writing: whole notes only · each voice moves by one step or holds · the voices never leap · never both leap · open and close on a stable interval — unison, fifth, or octave · eight to ten chords total.
  4. Write the progression obeying the rules even when the result surprises you — especially when it surprises you.
  5. Where a rule forces a second or a seventh, a fast-beating chord has been composed;
  6. that is the texture arriving by law.
  7. Under each dyad, write the interval’s name and your prediction: beats fast / beats slow / still.
  8. Assign each chord a duration from the phase-1 timings — 20 to 30 seconds each.

Realize the score — flat, exact, inhumanly slow

  2. Draw the score as MIDI: one whole-note clip per voice, each note lasting its written duration — at 120 BPM, a 30-second chord is 15 bars;
  3. draw the notes that long and let the grid go unused.

Listen into the chord — and grade the prediction

Phase focus: listen into the chord — and grade the prediction. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does each chord change feel like an event arriving by law, not taste?
    Answer

    It should feel obeyed — the next chord arrives as a consequence, predictable a moment before it lands, the same way the record’s changes felt when you tried predicting them in the reference pass. If a change feels like a personal choice, find where taste crept into the score and let the rule override it. That sense of inevitability cannot be performed; it can only be written in advance.

  • Did the beating speed change between intervals — and did you time it?
    Answer

    Yes, and measurably. The close intervals should count several beats per second; fifths and octaves should have almost none. The rate should jump the instant a chord changes — no tremolo or LFO (an automatic knob-mover) can imitate this honestly. Your beat-rate table is the proof. Relisten to the record’s stillest chord once more. Stillness, you now know, is also a tuning decision.
