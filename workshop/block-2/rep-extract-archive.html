Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the surface as filter

Copy one clear section of Toshiya Tsunoda — Extract from Field Recording Archive. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: contact mics and stationary probes fixed to vibrating solids - railings, pipes, floors - held in a fixed, unmoving frame; placement decides everything. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: the Lab 008 solid path — contact mic / piezo (AKG C411 class or the DIY piezo) with mounting putty and gaffer tape, into the handheld recorder or M4 input 1; closed phones; Live 12 hosts the series at the end
  • One location, five solids, all reachable from it: a window pane, a radiator or water pipe, a table, the floor, a door. The location needs steady ambient life — a street outside, plumbing at work, a household moving around
  • Field checklist items that apply indoors: cables taped or routed so nothing crosses a path at ankle height, and nothing mounted where the mic can fall — putty holds, tape backs it up
  • New set saved as REP-TSUNODA — five clips, numbered, in order; that is the whole session
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — five matched takes plus the listening that compares them
  • What you will make: a short replica of Toshiya Tsunoda — Extract from Field Recording Archive. Focus on this effect: the same world arriving five different ways - vibration with body and no room, tiny events filling the frame, each surface speaking in its own voice.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: contact mics and stationary probes fixed to vibrating solids - railings, pipes, floors - held in a fixed, unmoving frame; placement decides everything.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Toshiya Tsunoda — Extract from Field Recording Archive. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Why is there no room in these recordings?
    Answer

    Because the sound never traveled through air. Solid-borne vibration goes straight from the surface into the transducer - no air, no reflections, no distance cues - so everything arrives dry, close, and inside your head.

  2. Can you tell what is exciting each surface?
    Answer

    Partly at best - wind, traffic, water, footsteps, guessed more than known. That gap between the world and what the surface reports is the instrument: the solid filters reality into its own voice.

  3. What actually changes from track to track?
    Answer

    The filter, not the world. The harbor keeps being a harbor; what changes is which solid is reporting it and where the probe sits. Move the transducer a hand’s width and the piece changes - placement is composition here.

  4. Why does the presentation feel like measurement — numbered takes, dry factual titles?
    Answer

    Because the frame is fixed and the claim is modest: this surface, this probe, this duration - observed, not dramatized. The titles refuse drama so the vibration can carry all of it; the restraint is what lets tiny events feel enormous.

  5. How big are these events, really?
    Answer

    Tiny - a wire’s hum, a floor’s tremor, millimeters of motion. The contact path magnifies: with no room and no distance, the surface’s smallest behavior fills the entire frame.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: five matched-length stationary takes — one world, five filters — presented in numbered order with dry factual titles, levels defensible, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS. The series, not any single take, is the piece.
  • One location and one stretch of hours, so the same ambient life excites all five solids.
  • The frame is fixed: mount, leave, record — no touching, no repositioning mid-take.
  • Titles name surface, side, and time. Nothing else — the restraint is part of the replication.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one piezo, five solids in one room, putty-mounted stationary takes of matched length while the same life happens outside them, and a numbered series whose titles report facts only.

One world, three of your five filters. The street stays the street — what changes is what each solid keeps. The glass keeps the engines, the pipe answers everything at its own note, the floor keeps only what walks on it.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference, the location, and five predictions

Phase focus: the reference, the location, and five predictions. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live In Live: ⌘ T, rename REF, drag in one Tsunoda track, loop two minutes, trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Live Choose the location: a room with a street outside, working plumbing, or a household in motion — life that will keep happening for the next two hours without your help.
  3. Live Fix the five solids: window pane, radiator or water pipe, table, floor, door.
  4. Live Touch each with flat fingertips for ten seconds first — you can feel which ones live.
  5. Logbook Write one prediction per solid in the logbook: “the pane will keep the bus engines and kill the voices,” “the pipe will ring at one pitch no matter what.” Five sentences, committed before any take.
  6. Logbook Decide the take length — 3 minutes each — and the order.
  7. Logbook Both are now fixed.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, five solids are chosen and felt, and five written predictions wait to be wrong in interesting ways.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A chosen solid feels dead under the fingersDecoupled or massive — some walls and tables swallow vibrationSwap it for a living relative: the pane’s frame, a thinner door, a different stretch of pipe
The ambient life is too intermittent to count onWrong hour — the constant must stay constant across five takesMove the session to the location’s busy hours; the world is the control variable
Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

Mount and gain — per solid, logged

Phase focus: mount and gain — per solid, logged. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Recorder Mount the piezo on the first solid with putty pressed full and flat — no edge floating — and tape the cable to the surface a hand’s width away, so cable motion never reaches the disc.
  2. Recorder Phones on: you should hear the solid’s version of the room immediately, dry and close, with the air version gone.
  3. Recorder Set gain for the loudest thing this surface might carry during three minutes — the bus for the pane, the heating firing for the pipe — peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Recorder Log the gain number next to that solid’s prediction.
  5. Recorder Every solid gets its own setting and its own line.
  6. Recorder Rehearse the exit: mount, press record, step away, stand still.
  7. Recorder Your footsteps are in-band for the floor take especially — plan where you will stand.
Expected result

A mounting and gain recipe proven on the first solid — full putty contact, strain-relieved cable, a logged gain — repeatable four more times without invention.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The take sounds like a quiet, muffled air micPoor coupling — putty bridging unevenly or an edge floatingRemount: clean surface, fresh putty, press the whole disc flat until it sits without rocking
Crackle when anything moves in the roomCable motion reaching the disc — strain relief missingTape the cable to the surface near the mount, then again at the floor; the disc must feel no tug
Phase 3 · ≈ 35 min

The series — five matched stationary takes

Phase focus: the series — five matched stationary takes. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Run the five takes in the fixed order, 3 minutes each, same hour, one after another: mount, set the logged gain, record, stand still, stop, move to the next solid.
  2. During each take, note in pencil what the surface is reporting — one line, plain words, while it is fresh.
  3. Title each file on the spot, in Tsunoda’s idiom — surface, side, time: No.
  4. 1 — window pane, street side, 17:40.
  5. No adjectives anywhere.
  6. Do not relisten between takes beyond a head-check for damage — comparison waits for phase 4, so the later mounts are not biased toward “fixing” the earlier sounds.
  7. After the fifth take: cables coiled, putty collected, the location exactly as found — counted out, counted back.
Expected result

Five files of matched length with factual titles, five pencil lines, and one ambient world held constant across all of them — the experiment complete, the comparison not yet begun.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The world changed mid-series — street died, heating cycled offThe constant stopped being constantFinish the series anyway and log the change; if the comparison collapses, rerun the dead takes at a matching hour tomorrow
One solid records almost nothing at its logged gainA genuinely quiet surface — they existOne remount and one gain revision, logged as a revision; if it stays silent, silence is that solid’s honest report — keep it
Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

Sequence, compare, and write the transfer functions

Phase focus: sequence, compare, and write the transfer functions. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Import all five into REP-TSUNODA, in numbered order on the Arrangement timeline, levels matched toward ≈ −12 dBFS with clip gain only.
  2. Live Listen to the series end to end at the mark — fifteen minutes, no skipping.
  3. Live The form of the series is the comparison your ears run.
  4. Logbook Write each solid’s transfer function in plain words, two clauses: what it keeps, what it kills.
  5. Logbook Five sentences against five predictions — score them.
  6. Live A/B your strongest take against REF at matched loudness — judge dryness, distance, and whether tiny events fill the frame.
  7. Logbook Mark in the logbook which take could only be a solid — the one no air mic could fake.
Expected result

A fifteen-minute series that plays as one experiment, five scored predictions, five transfer functions in plain words, and one take flagged as impossible in air.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Two takes sound nearly identicalPlacements too alike — same material, same coupling, same lifeRemount one a hand’s width away or on the solid’s frame instead of its face; placement is the variable, use it
The series bores you by take threeListening for events instead of filtersRestart the listen asking one question per take: what did this surface kill that the last one kept?
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your series at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Same world, five filters — can you state each solid’s transfer function in plain words?
    Answer

    Five sentences, each with both clauses: the pane keeps the engines and kills the consonants; the pipe answers everything at its own note and kills the world’s actual pitches; the floor keeps footsteps and kills the rest. If two sentences read the same, the experiment had a dead variable — two placements too alike — and one take deserves a remount. Relisten to Tsunoda with your five sentences open: his tracks are transfer functions too, written by piers and railings instead of your room.

  • Which take could only be a solid?
    Answer

    The one with body and zero room — events with mass arriving from no direction, a surface ringing at its own note, detail with no distance behind it. An air mic in that room, at any gain, could not have produced it. If no take qualifies, the piezo was hearing air leakage through a loose mount the whole session, and the mounting recipe — not the concept — is what failed. Relisten to your candidate beside its nearest air-recorded cousin from Lab 008’s comparison and let the impossibility be audible.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 008 — the solid path’s placement lesson, certified: move the transducer a hand’s width and the piece changes. When the lab’s three-path comparison asks for the contact capture, you now choose the surface on purpose.
