Replicate: the surface as filter

Copy one clear section of Toshiya Tsunoda — Extract from Field Recording Archive. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: contact mics (sensors pressed against solid surfaces to pick up vibration) fixed to railings, pipes, and floors — held completely still in a fixed position; where you mount the sensor decides everything. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: the Lab 008 solid path — contact mic / piezo (AKG C411 class or the DIY piezo) with mounting putty and gaffer tape, into the handheld recorder or M4 input 1; closed phones; Live 12 hosts the series at the end
  • One location, five solids, all reachable from it: a window pane, a radiator or water pipe, a table, the floor, a door. The location needs a consistent source of ambient life — a street outside, active plumbing, a household in motion
  • Field checklist items that apply indoors: cables taped or routed so nothing crosses a path at ankle height, and nothing mounted where the mic can fall — putty holds, tape backs it up
  • New set saved as REP-TSUNODA — five clips, numbered, in order; that is the whole session
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — five matched takes plus the listening that compares them
  • What you will make: a short replica of Toshiya Tsunoda — Extract from Field Recording Archive. Focus on this effect: the same world arriving five different ways - vibration with body and no room, tiny events filling the frame, each surface speaking in its own voice.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: contact mics and stationary probes fixed to vibrating solids - railings, pipes, floors - held in a fixed, unmoving frame; placement decides everything.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Toshiya Tsunoda — Extract from Field Recording Archive. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Why is there no room sound in these recordings?
    Answer

    Because the sound never traveled through air. Vibration goes straight from the solid surface into the sensor — no air, no reflections, no distance cues — so everything arrives dry, close, and inside your head.

  2. Can you tell what is making each surface vibrate?
    Answer

    Partly at best — wind, traffic, water, footsteps, guessed rather than known. That gap between the outside world and what the surface reports is the instrument: the solid filters reality into its own voice.

  3. What actually changes from track to track?
    Answer

    The filter, not the world. The harbor stays a harbor; what changes is which solid is reporting it and where the sensor sits. Move the contact mic a hand’s width and the piece changes. Placement is the composition here.

  4. Why does the presentation feel like measurement — numbered takes, plain factual titles?
    Answer

    Because the frame is fixed and the claim is modest: this surface, this probe, this duration — observed, not dramatized. The plain titles leave all the drama to the vibration itself. That restraint is what lets tiny events feel significant.

  5. How big are these events, really?
    Answer

    Tiny — a wire’s hum, a floor’s tremor, millimeters of movement. The contact mic magnifies them: with no room and no distance, the surface’s smallest behaviour fills the entire recording.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: five matched-length stationary takes — one outside world, five solid filters — presented in numbered order with plain factual titles, levels consistent, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS. The series as a whole is the piece, not any individual take.
  • One location, one continuous stretch of hours, so the same ambient life is driving all five solids.
  • The sensor stays fixed: mount it, step away, record — no touching, no repositioning mid-take.
  • Titles name surface, side, and time. Nothing else. The restraint is part of the replication.

The piece in one row: one piezo, five solids in one room, putty-mounted and left still for matched-length takes while the same outside life drives all of them, then a numbered series with titles that state only facts.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference, the location, and five predictions

Phase focus: the reference, the location, and five predictions. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. In Live: ⌘ T, rename REF, drag in one Tsunoda track, loop two minutes, trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Choose the location: a room with a street outside, working plumbing, or a household in motion — life that will keep happening for the next two hours without your help.
  3. Fix the five solids: window pane, radiator or water pipe, table, floor, door.
  4. Live Touch each with flat fingertips for ten seconds. You can feel which ones are carrying vibration.
  5. Logbook Write one prediction per solid: “the pane will keep the bus engines and lose the voices,” “the pipe will ring at one pitch no matter what.” Five sentences, written down before any recording.
  6. Decide the take length — 3 minutes each — and the order.
  7. Both are now fixed.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Toshiya Tsunoda — Extract from Field Recording Archive (1990s series) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference, the location, and five predictions — as the smallest Live set that proves contact mics fixed to vibrating solids — railings, pipes, floors — held completely still; where you mount the sensor determines what the recording sounds like.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects the same outside world arriving five different ways — vibration with body and no room sound, tiny events filling the recording, each surface speaking in its own voice.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference, the location, and five predictions into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2.   3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, five solids are chosen and felt, and five written predictions wait to be wrong in interesting ways.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

Mount and gain — per solid, logged

Phase focus: mount and gain — per solid, logged. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Mount the piezo on the first solid with putty pressed full and flat — no edge floating — and tape the cable to the surface a hand’s width away, so cable motion never reaches the disc.
  2. Phones on: you should hear the solid’s version of the world immediately — dry and close, with no room sound at all.
  3. Set gain for the loudest thing this surface might carry during three minutes — the bus for the pane, the heating firing for the pipe — peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Log the gain number next to that solid’s prediction.
  5. Every solid gets its own setting and its own line.
  6. Rehearse the exit: mount, press record, step away, stand still.
  7. Your footsteps are in-band for the floor take especially — plan where you will stand.

Phase 3 · ≈ 35 min

The series — five matched stationary takes

Phase focus: the series — five matched stationary takes. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Run the five takes in the fixed order, 3 minutes each, same hour, one after another: mount, set the logged gain, record, stand still, stop, move to the next solid.
  2. During each take, note in pencil what the surface is reporting — one line, plain words, while it is fresh.
  3. Name each file immediately, in Tsunoda’s style — surface, side, time: No. 1 — window pane, street side, 17:40. No adjectives anywhere.
  4. Do not relisten between takes beyond a quick check for obvious damage. The comparison waits until phase 4, so the later mounts do not get biased toward “fixing” what the earlier sounds did.
  5. After the fifth take: cables coiled, putty collected, the location exactly as found — counted out, counted back.

Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

Sequence, compare, and write the transfer functions

Phase focus: sequence, compare, and write the transfer functions. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import all five into REP-TSUNODA, in numbered order on the Arrangement timeline, levels matched toward ≈ −12 dBFS with clip gain only.
  2. Listen to the series end to end at the mark — fifteen minutes, no skipping. The form of the series is the comparison your ears run.
  3. Write each solid’s transfer function (what it does to the signal) in plain words, two clauses: what it keeps, what it removes.
  4. Compare your five sentences against your five predictions and score them.
  5. A/B your strongest take against REF at matched loudness — judge dryness, distance, and whether tiny events fill the frame.
  6. Mark in the logbook which take could only be a solid — the one no air mic could fake.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your series at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Same world, five filters — can you state each solid’s transfer function in plain words?
    Answer

    Five sentences, each with both clauses: the pane keeps the engines and loses the consonants; the pipe adds its own note to everything and overrides the world’s actual pitches; the floor keeps footsteps and loses the rest. If two sentences read the same, two placements were too alike and one take deserves a remount. Relisten to Tsunoda with your five sentences in hand: his tracks are transfer functions too, written by piers and railings instead of your room.

  • Which take could only be a solid?
    Answer

    The one with weight and zero room — events with mass arriving from no particular direction, a surface ringing at its own pitch, detail with no echoing distance behind it. An air mic in the same room, at any gain, could not have made it. If no take qualifies, the piezo was picking up air leakage through a loose mount the whole session — the mounting, not the idea, is what failed. Relisten to your candidate beside its nearest air-recorded version from Lab 008’s comparison and let the difference be audible.
