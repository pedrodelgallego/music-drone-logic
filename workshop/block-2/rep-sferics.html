Replicate: the sky on a wire

Copy one clear section of Alvin Lucier — Sferics. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches how the record works: antennas recording natural VLF (very low frequency) radio at night — lightning-stroke sferics, tweeks, and whistlers. These are electromagnetic events that have no acoustic (sound-wave) existence at all; the antenna converts them into something you can hear. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Honest analogue, stated plainly: Lucier used VLF antennas in open country; the ionosphere is not reachable from your desk with a pickup coil. Two legitimate stand-ins — the Lab 008 induction coil hunting the quietest EM environments you can find for faint, irregular impulses, or an AM/shortwave radio tuned between stations at night, whose crackle genuinely is distant lightning. Either path copies the system; say in the logbook which you took
  • Gear: the Lab 008 electromagnetic path — induction coil (or telephone pickup) into the recorder — and/or any radio with an AM band; closed phones. Back at the bench: Live 12, the M4, DT 990 Pro at the mark. The Digitakt II and MicroFreak rest
  • New set saved as REP-SFERICS; safety per Lab 008 — the coil listens from a distance, nothing electrical gets touched, opened, or probed, and a night session in public space keeps one ear free and stays where you would stand anyway
  • What you will make: a short replica of Alvin Lucier — Sferics. Focus on this effect: sparse impulse weather - clicks, pops, and brief descending chirps scattered without rhythm across a near-empty field, untouched by any hand.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: antennas recording natural VLF radio at night - lightning-stroke sferics, tweeks, whistlers - electromagnetic events with no acoustic existence at all.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Alvin Lucier — Sferics. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What physically made these sounds?
    Answer

    Lightning — strokes hundreds or thousands of kilometres away, each one radiating a radio impulse that an antenna catches and a recorder writes down.

  2. How many kinds of event can you distinguish?
    Answer

    Three families. Dry clicks and pops — sferics, the raw stroke. Clicks with a short falling ring on the tail — tweeks, the same impulse dispersed by bouncing between the earth and the ionosphere (a layer of the upper atmosphere).

  3. Is there any rhythm?
    Answer

    None — and that is the signature. The impulses cluster, thin out, pile up, and vanish according to global thunderstorm activity, not any clock. A machine settles into a repeating period. The sky never does.

  4. Why night?
    Answer

    Two reasons. At night the ionosphere’s lowest, most absorbing layer fades, so VLF signals travel farther. The antenna’s reach extends across continents, meaning more of the world’s lightning arrives in the recording.

  5. Where is the space — the room, the distance?
    Answer

    There is none. The sounds were never in a room. They traveled as radio waves and were converted directly to audio.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: three minutes of sparse, irregular impulses on a near-empty background — clicks and crackle with no groove. Solo-flipped against REF, both should match in sparseness, dryness, and especially in refusing to lock to any rhythm.
  • Untreated: no reverb, no EQ, no rhythmic editing. Selection and level are the only moves allowed.
  • Steady hum is the enemy here. Continuous mains hum and switching whines are machine sounds — they belong to a different lesson. You want the gaps between machines, where irregular events live.
  • If your impulses fall on a regular grid, you recorded a machine. Find quieter air and try again.

How the piece works: a quiet patch of the electromagnetic world, a wire that picks it up, enough gain to lift faint crackle above the noise floor, and a presentation that adds nothing. The weather composed it.

the quietest EM you can find · night → coil or AM radio between stations → recorder · long take · headroom for spikes → 3 minutes selected · untreated

The test your capture must pass, drawn as two timelines. The top lane is a machine: impulses that repeat on a period, however irregular it looks. The bottom lane is weather: clusters, gaps, and no clock anywhere. In phase 4, you lay your three minutes against a grid and ask which lane it belongs to.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the choice of wire

Phase focus: the reference and the choice of wire. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a section of Sferics into it.
  2. Audio Effects → Utility, trim to peak ≈ −12 dBFS — the impulses are spiky;
  3. trim against the loudest click, not the silence.
  4. Loop four minutes.
  5. Choose the path and write it down: COIL — hunt the quietest EM environments for faint irregular impulses — or RADIO — an AM or shortwave set tuned between stations at night.
  6. Both is better.
  7. Copy the reference pass tally into the session plan: clicks, ringed clicks, glides.
  8. You are about to hunt for the first family and, on the radio path, maybe the second.
  9. Schedule the capture for after dark — write the planned hour next to the path.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Alvin Lucier — Sferics (1981) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.

  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.

Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

Hunt the quiet — find air the machines aren’t using

Phase focus: hunt the quiet — find air the machines aren’t using. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Coil into the recorder, closed phones on, gain low to start.
  2. Walk the home and map the loud spots — chargers, the router, the fridge’s compressor — and then walk away from all of them until the field drops to near silence.
  3. In the quietest spot, raise gain until the residue is audible: tiny ticks, distant switching, irregular crackle.
  4. Leave headroom — impulses spike far above the average level.
  5. Aim the loudest tick at ≈ −12 dBFS.
  6. Radio path, after dark: AM band, tune to a dead stretch between stations, volume modest, recorder or line connection capturing the output.
  7. The crackle that remains — and strengthens with distance from your electronics — is the sky.
  8. Try the band’s low end; switch off nearby chargers and LED dimmers, the great polluters.
  9. Whichever path: log the location, the time, and what you switched off.
  10. The off-switch list is part of the recording’s provenance.
  11. Safety, restated: the coil and the radio listen from a distance.
  12. Nothing electrical gets touched, opened, or probed — especially outdoors, especially anything you did not install.

Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

The night capture — let the weather happen

Phase focus: the night capture — let the weather happen. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Roll a single take of at least 20–30 minutes at the committed gain.
  2. Set the coil or radio down — no holding; handling reaches the file on this path too, as thumps and as your own body detuning the field.
  3. Stay nearby but still.
  4. Log timestamps of anything notable: a cluster, a louder pop, a stretch of nothing.
  5. Do not chase quiet mid-take — if a hum wanders in (a neighbor’s appliance, a thermostat), let the take run and log it;
  6. phase 4 edits around it.
  7. Optional second sample for the verify step: ten minutes of the same spot in daytime tomorrow, same gain.
  8. The day/night comparison becomes evidence rather than lore.

Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

Assemble the impulse weather, then run the periodicity test

Phase focus: assemble the impulse weather, then run the periodicity test. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the take into REP-SFERICS on a track named SKY.
  2. Audition with the event log open.
  3. Choose three minutes that breathe like the record: at least one cluster, at least one long gap, no steady hum anywhere.
  4. Cut around the logged intruders.
  5. No fades beyond the edit, no treatment of any kind.
  6. The periodicity test: set Live to any tempo, turn the metronome on softly, and play your three minutes against it.
  7. Listen for thirty seconds.
  8. Then change the tempo and listen again.
  9. Your impulses must refuse both grids equally.
  10. Stronger version: zoom the waveform, measure three consecutive gaps between impulses with the time ruler, and write the three numbers down.
  11. A machine’s gaps repeat to the millisecond; yours must not.
  12. A/B against REF at matched loudness, judging sparseness, dryness, and irregularity — nothing else is on trial.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your three minutes at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Is irregularity the signature — does anything in yours groove?
    Answer

    Nothing may groove. Play yours against two different metronome tempos and against the period of any machine you logged. If the impulses land on any of them, a circuit composed your piece — not the sky. The gap measurements are the hard evidence: three unequal numbers. This is the rare lesson where the pass mark is a failure. Your material must fail to lock to any grid you own. Relisten to the record while tapping a steady pulse and feel it refuse to sync. Yours should refuse the same way.

  • What changes at night?
    Answer

    Two things extend at once. The ionosphere’s absorbing layer fades, so atmospheric signals travel farther. On the radio path, more of the planet’s lightning reaches your band gap. And the local electromagnetic environment quiets — fewer machines switching means a lower noise floor for faint events to clear. If you took the daytime control sample, A/B it now: busier hum, fewer and weaker sky events. Lucier recorded at night because that is when the antenna’s reach grows from a neighbourhood to a hemisphere. Relisten to Sferics with that scale in mind.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.

  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 008 — the coil’s path splits in two: machines produce regular periods (Kubisch’s world), weather produces statistics (Lucier’s). Timing is how you tell which one you recorded. Irregularity is nature’s signature. Learn to hear it and you can audit any “field recording” for the machine hiding inside it.
