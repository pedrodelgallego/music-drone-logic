Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the sky on a wire

Copy one clear section of Alvin Lucier — Sferics. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: antennas recording natural VLF radio at night - lightning-stroke sferics, tweeks, whistlers - electromagnetic events with no acoustic existence at all. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Honest analogue, stated plainly: Lucier used VLF antennas in open country; the ionosphere is not reachable from your desk with a pickup coil. Two legitimate stand-ins — the Lab 008 induction coil hunting the quietest EM environments you can find for faint, irregular impulses, or an AM/shortwave radio tuned between stations at night, whose crackle genuinely is distant lightning. Either path copies the system; say in the logbook which you took
  • Gear: the Lab 008 electromagnetic path — induction coil (or telephone pickup) into the recorder — and/or any radio with an AM band; closed phones. Back at the bench: Live 12, the M4, DT 990 Pro at the mark. The Digitakt II and MicroFreak rest
  • New set saved as REP-SFERICS; safety per Lab 008 — the coil listens from a distance, nothing electrical gets touched, opened, or probed, and a night session in public space keeps one ear free and stays where you would stand anyway
  • What you will make: a short replica of Alvin Lucier — Sferics. Focus on this effect: sparse impulse weather - clicks, pops, and brief descending chirps scattered without rhythm across a near-empty field, untouched by any hand.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: antennas recording natural VLF radio at night - lightning-stroke sferics, tweeks, whistlers - electromagnetic events with no acoustic existence at all.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Alvin Lucier — Sferics. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What physically made these sounds?
    Answer

    Lightning - strokes hundreds or thousands of kilometers away, each radiating a radio impulse that an antenna catches and a recorder writes down.

  2. How many kinds of event can you distinguish?
    Answer

    Three families. Dry clicks and pops - sferics, the raw stroke. Clicks with a tiny falling ring on the tail - tweeks, impulses dispersed by bouncing between earth and ionosphere.

  3. Is there any rhythm?
    Answer

    None - and that is the signature. The impulses cluster, thin out, pile up, and vanish, on the statistics of global thunderstorm activity, not on any clock. A machine would settle into a period; the sky never does.

  4. Why night?
    Answer

    Two reasons, one celestial, one domestic. At night the ionosphere’s lowest, most absorbent layer fades, and VLF signals bounce farther - the antenna’s reach extends across continents, so more of the world’s lightning arrives.

  5. Where is the space — the room, the distance?
    Answer

    There is none.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: three minutes of sparse impulse weather — irregular clicks and crackle on a near-empty field — that a solo-flip against REF matches in sparseness, dryness, and above all in refusal to groove.
  • Untreated: no reverb, no EQ, no rhythmic editing. Selection and level are the only moves.
  • Hums are the enemy this time. Steady mains hum and switching whines are Kubisch’s material — machines performing. You want the gaps between machines, where the irregular lives.
  • If your impulses fall on a grid, you recorded a machine. Find quieter air and go again.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a quiet patch of the electromagnetic world, a wire that hears it, gain enough to lift faint crackle above the kit’s floor, and a presentation that adds nothing — the weather is the composer of record.

the quietest EM you can find · night → coil or AM radio between stations → recorder · long take · headroom for spikes → 3 minutes selected · untreated

The test your capture must pass, drawn as two timelines. The top lane is a machine: impulses on a period, however complicated. The bottom lane is weather: clusters, gaps, and no clock anywhere. Phase 4 lays your three minutes against a grid and asks which lane it belongs to.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the choice of wire

Phase focus: the reference and the choice of wire. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a section of Sferics into it.
  2. Live Audio Effects → Utility, trim to peak ≈ −12 dBFS — the impulses are spiky;
  3. Live trim against the loudest click, not the silence.
  4. Live Loop four minutes.
  5. Logbook Choose the path and write it down: COIL — hunt the quietest EM environments for faint irregular impulses — or RADIO — an AM or shortwave set tuned between stations at night.
  6. Logbook Both is better.
  7. Live Copy the reference pass tally into the session plan: clicks, ringed clicks, glides.
  8. Live You are about to hunt for the first family and, on the radio path, maybe the second.
  9. Logbook Schedule the capture for after dark — write the planned hour next to the path.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, a declared path in the logbook, and a capture hour set after dark.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
No AM radio in the house and the coil feels thin as a planThe kit table’s fallback forgottenA phone’s recorder near a cheap AM set, a car radio at the curb, a shortwave app is not valid (it streams, it does not receive) — the wire must be physical and local
REF seems almost silent at the trimTrimmed against the field instead of the impulsesRe-trim so the loudest click peaks ≈ −12 dBFS; the emptiness around it is the piece, leave it empty
Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

Hunt the quiet — find air the machines aren’t using

Phase focus: hunt the quiet — find air the machines aren’t using. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Recorder Coil into the recorder, closed phones on, gain low to start.
  2. Recorder Walk the home and map the loud spots — chargers, the router, the fridge’s compressor — and then walk away from all of them until the field drops to near silence.
  3. Recorder In the quietest spot, raise gain until the residue is audible: tiny ticks, distant switching, irregular crackle.
  4. Recorder Leave headroom — impulses spike far above the average level.
  5. Recorder Aim the loudest tick at ≈ −12 dBFS.
  6. Recorder Radio path, after dark: AM band, tune to a dead stretch between stations, volume modest, recorder or line connection capturing the output.
  7. Recorder The crackle that remains — and strengthens with distance from your electronics — is the sky.
  8. Recorder Try the band’s low end; switch off nearby chargers and LED dimmers, the great polluters.
  9. Recorder Whichever path: log the location, the time, and what you switched off.
  10. Recorder The off-switch list is part of the recording’s provenance.
  11. Recorder Safety, restated: the coil and the radio listen from a distance.
  12. Recorder Nothing electrical gets touched, opened, or probed — especially outdoors, especially anything you did not install.
Expected result

A found patch of quiet electromagnetic air, a committed gain with spike headroom, and faint irregular impulses audible above the kit’s floor.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Everything everywhere hums at a steady pitchYou live inside the grid — mains and switching supplies saturate the indoor fieldThe balcony, the garden, the park bench after dark; distance from wiring is the only filter that works on hum
The radio finds only stations or pure silenceStrong local broadcast, or a digital tuner muting between stationsTune the band’s edges, disable any “seek/mute” function, try after midnight when some stations sign off and the band opens
Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

The night capture — let the weather happen

Phase focus: the night capture — let the weather happen. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Roll a single take of at least 20–30 minutes at the committed gain.
  2. Set the coil or radio down — no holding; handling reaches the file on this path too, as thumps and as your own body detuning the field.
  3. Stay nearby but still.
  4. Log timestamps of anything notable: a cluster, a louder pop, a stretch of nothing.
  5. Do not chase quiet mid-take — if a hum wanders in (a neighbor’s appliance, a thermostat), let the take run and log it;
  6. phase 4 edits around it.
  7. Optional second sample for the verify step: ten minutes of the same spot in daytime tomorrow, same gain.
  8. The day/night comparison becomes evidence rather than lore.
Expected result

One long night take with logged events — sparse, irregular, mostly empty — and, ideally, a daytime control sample waiting for comparison.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Twenty minutes and almost no eventsEither genuinely quiet weather, or gain too low for the faint stuffCheck the floor: if the kit’s hiss is audible, gain is fine and the sky is calm — emptiness is a valid result; if dead silence, raise gain and extend the take
A steady pulse appears partway throughSome machine on a duty cycle woke up nearbyLog its period precisely — you will need it in phase 4 to prove your kept material is not it
Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

Assemble the impulse weather, then run the periodicity test

Phase focus: assemble the impulse weather, then run the periodicity test. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Import the take into REP-SFERICS on a track named SKY.
  2. Live Audition with the event log open.
  3. Live Choose three minutes that breathe like the record: at least one cluster, at least one long gap, no steady hum anywhere.
  4. Live Cut around the logged intruders.
  5. Live No fades beyond the edit, no treatment of any kind.
  6. Live The periodicity test: set Live to any tempo, turn the metronome on softly, and play your three minutes against it.
  7. Live Listen for thirty seconds.
  8. Live Then change the tempo and listen again.
  9. Live Your impulses must refuse both grids equally.
  10. Live + Logbook Stronger version: zoom the waveform, measure three consecutive gaps between impulses with the time ruler, and write the three numbers down.
  11. Live + Logbook A machine’s gaps repeat to the millisecond; yours must not.
  12. Live A/B against REF at matched loudness, judging sparseness, dryness, and irregularity — nothing else is on trial.
Expected result

Three untreated minutes of impulse weather that fail every grid they are played against, with three unequal gap measurements in the logbook as proof.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The impulses settle into a felt pulse against the metronomeA duty-cycled machine is in the kept material — the phase 3 intruder, or one never noticedCompare against the logged period; cut every impulse that lands on it and re-select from the take
The three minutes feel boring rather than sparseSelection kept the gaps but no clusters — weather without stormsRe-select around the densest logged cluster; the record earns its emptiness by occasionally raining
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your three minutes at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Is irregularity the signature — does anything in yours groove?
    Answer

    Nothing may. Play yours against two different metronome tempos and against the period of any machine you logged: if the impulses land on any of them, a circuit composed your piece and the sky did not. The gap measurements are the hard evidence — three unequal numbers. This is the rare lesson where the pass mark is a failure: your material must fail to synchronize with everything you own. Relisten to the record while tapping a steady pulse and feel it shrug you off; yours should shrug the same way.

  • What changes at night?
    Answer

    Two reaches extend at once. The ionosphere’s absorbing layer fades, so atmospheric signals travel farther — on the radio path, more of the planet’s lightning arrives in your dead stretch of band. And the local grid quiets — fewer machines switching means a lower floor for the faint events to clear. If you took the daytime control sample, A/B it now: busier hum, fewer and weaker sky events. Lucier recorded at night because night is when the wire’s catchment grows from a neighborhood to a hemisphere — relisten to Sferics with that scale in mind.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 008 — the coil’s path splits in two: machines perform periods (Kubisch), weather performs statistics (Lucier), and timing is how you tell which one you recorded. Irregularity is nature’s watermark; learn to hear it and you can audit any “field recording” for the machine hiding inside it.
