Replicate: capture sorted by physics

Copy one clear section of Bernard Parmegiani — De Natura Sonorum. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill is about one technique: sort sounds by how they behave physically — things that are struck and ring, things that pour, things that sustain through friction — not by what produced them. The movement titles in De Natura Sonorum name physics, not objects. Do only the steps that make that sorting technique audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone, or a mic into M4 input 1 with gain by the lab-001 ledger; DT 990 Pro for sorting and editing; the lab-006 capture log, with one extra column for behavior class
  • New set saved as REP-DE-NATURA — no tempo; the studies are timed in seconds, not bars
  • Strikes and pours spike fast — set gain on the hardest strike and the densest pour, not on room tone; phones at the lab-001 mark while auditioning, because rings and grains invite the knob up
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — one capture round indoors, one sort, three short studies, then file it and stop
  • What you will make: a short replica of Bernard Parmegiani — De Natura Sonorum. Focus on this effect: movements where unlike sources fuse into single studies because they behave alike - struck things ring together, grains pour together.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: sounds organized by physical behavior - incidence and resonance, accumulation, matter - rather than by what produced them; the movement titles name physics, not objects.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Bernard Parmegiani — De Natura Sonorum. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What do the sounds inside one movement share?
    Answer

    Not a source — a behavior. One movement is struck things and their ringing, another is small events accumulating, another is material rubbed and held. The rule for membership is physical: how the energy arrives and decays.

  2. Can you name the sources?
    Answer

    Sometimes — and it stops mattering within seconds. Electronic tones and recordings of real objects sit side by side in the same study, and the behavioral class absorbs both.

  3. What does “incidence/resonance” sound like?
    Answer

    A strike and what rings after — energy arriving in an instant, then sustaining in the object's body.

  4. What does accumulation sound like?
    Answer

    One small event is a tick. A thousand are a surface. Grains pile until you stop counting individual sounds and start hearing texture. Density becomes the parameter, and individual sounds dissolve into the pour.

  5. Where is the composition, if the sounds are a taxonomy?
    Answer

    In the sorting and the joins. Choosing the classes, deciding what belongs in each, ordering entries within a class, and handing off from one class to the next — those decisions are the form.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: three 30-second studies — one per behavior class — where unlike sources audibly cohere because their physics match.
  • 12–15 captures of your own; three classes: struck-and-ringing / granular pours / friction sustains.
  • Sort by behavior, never by source — sources mix freely inside a class and never define one.
  • Selection, editing, and level only — the coherence must come from the sorting, not from processing.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the class sheet

Phase focus: the reference and the class sheet. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag the record’s opening movement in, and loop it.
  2. Audio Effects → Utility, trim to ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Write the class sheet in the logbook, one line per class: STRUCK — hit once, rings after;
  4. POUR — many small events, heard as density; FRICTION — rubbed or dragged, energy held as long as the hand moves.
  5. List candidate captures under each class, aiming at 4–5 per class from what is in reach: strike a glass, a metal bowl, a radiator;
  6. pour rice, gravel, coins, cutlery; drag a chair, rub a wine glass rim, run a zip, drag a book across a table.
  7. One more line: the membership test — a capture joins a class by its envelope, never by its name.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Bernard Parmegiani — De Natura Sonorum (1975) and use Drum Rack, MIDI clips, Groove Pool, and clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the class sheet — as the smallest Live set that proves sounds organized by physical behavior — incidence and resonance, accumulation, matter — rather than by what produced them; the movement titles name physics, not objects.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects movements where unlike sources fuse into single studies because they behave alike — struck things ring together, grains pour together — and the form is the sorting itself.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the class sheet into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.

Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates sounds organized by physical behavior — incidence and resonance, accumulation, matter — rather than by what produced them; the movement titles name physics, not objects, not just the surface of Bernard Parmegiani — De Natura Sonorum (1975).

Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

Capture — hunt behaviors, not objects

Phase focus: capture — hunt behaviors, not objects. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Record 12–15 captures from the class sheet, close, with gain by the lab-001 ledger — set on the hardest strike for percussive takes, on the densest pour for the granular ones, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. For each struck capture: one clean hit, then hold still until the ring dies completely.
  3. The decay is the capture.
  4. For each pour: 8–10 seconds of steady density — pour from a constant height, refill and repeat rather than letting it trail off.
  5. For each friction: 8–10 seconds of even pressure and speed.
  6. If it squeaks into pitch, even better — but constant, not gestural.
  7. Log each take with place · distance · level plus its class letter — the extra column is this lesson’s addition to the lab-006 log.

Phase 3 · ≈ 15 min

The sort — names off, envelopes on

Phase focus: the sort — names off, envelopes on. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import everything into REP-DE-NATURA; trim each capture to its behavior — the hit-plus-full-ring, the steady core of the pour, the even stretch of the friction.
  2. Rename every clip class·number — STRUCK·1, POUR·3 — source names nowhere in the set.
  3. Lay the clips on three tracks, one per class.
  4. Audition each track top to bottom: does every clip belong with its neighbors as a shape?
  5. Re-file the misfits: a coin dropped once is a strike, not a pour, whatever the candidate list said.
  6. If you catch yourself sorting by what made the sound, re-sort by what it does.
  7. Note the borderline cases in the logbook — a capture that strikes then pours is tomorrow’s most interesting material, but today it gets filed by its dominant behavior.

Phase 4 · ≈ 35 min

Three studies — one class each, sources mixed freely

Phase focus: three studies — one class each, sources mixed freely. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Build three 30-second studies in Arrangement, one per class — selection, editing, and level only.
  2. STRUCK study: strikes from different sources answering each other;
  3. try handing one strike’s attack to another’s ring at least once — the Parmegiani seam.
  4. POUR study: layer and stagger pours into one changing density — thin to thick to thin;
  5. the parameter you compose is how many grains per second the listener receives.
  6. FRICTION study: overlap sustains from unlike sources into one held band;
  7. let pressure changes inside the takes be the events.
  8. Mix sources as freely as you can inside each study — the more unlike the sources, the louder the lesson when they cohere anyway.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your studies at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Do unlike sources cohere because their physics match?
    Answer

    In a working study, yes — a glass and a radiator stop being a glass and a radiator and become one ringing instrument with two colors. That’s exactly how the record fuses electronic tones and recorded objects inside one movement. If a study refuses to cohere, the cause is almost always one mis-sorted clip, not a lack of processing. This lesson has no processing precisely so the sorting gets all the credit or blame. Relisten to the record’s opening movement and count how many sources you can hear fusing; then count yours.

  • Could a listener name the class faster than the sources?
    Answer

    That’s the pass condition. Play one study to fresh ears — or to yourself tomorrow — and ask “what is happening?” before “what made it?” A working study gets “things being struck” or “something pouring” immediately, while identifying the individual sources takes guesswork. If the source name arrives first (“that’s rice”), the class was too thin or one source dominated. Widen the sources inside the class. The record passes this test in every movement — its titles name physics, not objects, because it was designed that way.
