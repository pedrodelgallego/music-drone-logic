Replicate: the quarter tone in free time

Steal one idea from a solo Arabic taqsim in maqam Rast or Bayati: tune a handful of pitches that sit between the normal piano keys, then improvise free phrases that explore the mode one four-note group (tetrachord) at a time and keep returning to its resting tones. Build a short take and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

You are not copying the oud player's notes or ornaments. You are copying two linked mechanisms: pitches that fall in the cracks of the standard keyboard — the neutral or "half-flat" note that is the defining colour of the maqam — and a free, unmetered melody that climbs the mode one tetrachord at a time and dwells on its resting tones. Snap that neutral pitch to the nearest key and the maqam disappears. The in-between pitch and the patient climb are the form. Everything else is decoration.

Before you start
  • One sustained, pitched sound you can tune freely — a MicroFreak patch, or a single sampled note on a Digitakt II track you can re-tune off the grid
  • A reference taqsim cued up in Rast or Bayati, gain-matched on a REF track in Live
  • A quiet room and your ears — this lesson is tuned by ear against the record, not by typing in numbers
  • What you will make: a 60–90 second unmetered solo on five or six tuned pitches, at least one of which sits roughly a quarter-tone — between two keys — off equal temperament. Focus on this effect: a phrase that has no beat but still settles and rises, around a pitch that is not on a keyboard.
  • What you will turn off: the metronome, the drum machine's clock, chords, and any pitch that snaps to a default key. One voice, free time, tuned by ear.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a microtonal degree carried in free, tetrachord-by-tetrachord development.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about two minutes of a taqsim in Rast or Bayati. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Find a pitch that sounds like it would be "wrong" on a piano. Is it sharp, flat, or sitting in between?
    Answer

    It sits in between — neither the natural nor the flat that your keyboard offers, but a neutral degree roughly halfway between the two. In Rast it is usually the third above the tonic; in Bayati it is the second. That "in-between" is not out of tune. It is the defining colour of the maqam. Your ear can only find the right spot by listening to the reference, not by entering a fixed number.

  2. Where does the line rest? Pick the one or two notes the player keeps returning to and pausing on.
    Answer

    Early on, the player circles the tonic (the home note) and one note higher that the lower phrases keep landing on. These resting tones are what keep a free, beatless melody oriented: every phrase leaves one and returns to it. The pauses on them are not gaps — they are the punctuation that makes the improvisation feel like sentences rather than wandering.

  3. Does the player use the whole range at once, or open low and climb later?
    Answer

    Open low and climb. A taqsim stays in the lower four notes — the lower tetrachord, the jins that names the maqam — long enough to really establish them before reaching up to the upper tetrachord. The climb to the upper register is the piece's one big structural event. Spending the whole take in one range skips the only shape the form has.

  4. Can you tap a steady beat to it?
    Answer

    No, and you should not be able to. A taqsim is unmetered — phrase lengths follow breath and feeling, not a grid. With no beat to carry it, the melody is held together entirely by pitch, by the pull of the resting tones, and by the gradual climb. That structure is exactly what you are going to rebuild.

Now build the mechanism you just described. Do not copy the player's actual melody — rebuild the system that lets any melody work.

  • Sound target: one sustained, expressive voice — bowed/blown in character, able to slide and hold — playing five or six tuned pitches in free time.
  • Lower tetrachord: four notes from the tonic up, one of them the neutral/half-flat degree tuned by ear. This is where the take lives first.
  • Resting tones: the tonic and one higher note the lower phrases keep landing on — your two anchors.
  • The form: dwell low, establish the microtonal colour, then ascend to the upper tetrachord and resolve. No metre, no chords, no second voice.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Tune the in-between note by ear

Phase focus: land at least one pitch off the equal-tempered grid, matched to the record's neutral degree by ear — not by a typed number. Compare against REF before moving on.

12-TET note → pitch-bend / fine-tune / sample tune → neutral degree (between two keys)
Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track and gain-match it with a Utility so A/Bs are honest. Identify the tonic and the neutral degree by ear.
  2. Sound the tonic and the equal-tempered version of the maqam's coloured degree (the major third for Rast, the second for Bayati). Notice it sounds too "clean" against the record.
  3. Detune that note off the grid using oscillator pitch / fine-tune (or pitch-bend held), nudging it down toward the record until it rings the same colour — roughly a quarter-tone, but trust your ear, not a fixed amount. The exact spot is contextual, so chase the record, not 50 cents.
  4. Alternatively, tune a single sampled note off the grid with the sample tune control until it lands between two keys at the same colour. Either device gives you the neutral degree.
  5. A/B your neutral note against the record's. They should sit at the same "wrong-on-a-piano, right-here" pitch.

Phase 2 — ~30 minutes

Build the lower tetrachord and its resting tones

Phase focus: assemble the lower four notes — including the neutral degree — and fix the one or two pitches your phrases will keep returning to.

Do this
  1. Lay out the lower tetrachord: tonic, second, third, fourth, with the maqam's neutral degree among them tuned as in phase 1. Write the four pitches down so you are improvising on a fixed set, not hunting.
  2. Mark the resting tones: the tonic, plus one higher note in the tetrachord the lower phrases lean on. Circle them on your written set — these are the two notes you are allowed to pause on.
  3. Improvise short phrases in the lower tetrachord only, each departing from and landing back on a resting tone, sounding the neutral degree on the way. No beat — let each phrase end when it wants to.
  4. A/B your low playing against the record's opening. The colour and the sense of "circling home" should match, even though the notes are your own.

Phase 3 — ~25 minutes

Ascend to the upper tetrachord and record the take

Phase focus: save the climb to the upper register as the take's main event, then perform and record the whole arc in free time. This is the piece.

Do this
  1. Extend the upper tetrachord: add the notes above the fourth, tuned to the maqam (the upper jins may carry its own coloured degree — tune it by ear too). Write them under the lower set.
  2. Write the arc, not a melody: a short plan such as dwell low → establish the neutral note → reach up → peak on the upper register → fall back and rest on the tonic. The plan is your score; the notes stay improvised.
  3. Kill the clock. Disarm the metronome and the drum machine — record with no grid so phrase lengths follow breath, not bars.
  4. Record the arc in one pass: establish the lower tetrachord and its colour, hold long enough that the climb feels earned, ascend, then resolve home. Let silences sit.
  5. Listen back: a stranger should hear it settle, then lift to the upper register, then come home — with no beat to tell them so.

A 60–90 second unmetered take that establishes the maqam low, carries its microtonal colour, climbs to the upper tetrachord as its peak, and resolves to the tonic.

Verify against the record

A/B your take against a comparable stretch of the taqsim — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Does your coloured note sit at the same in-between pitch as theirs — would snapping it to the nearest key change the maqam?

Answer

It should sit in the same crack between the keys, and snapping it to the nearest key should audibly ruin the colour — that is the proof you built the maqam rather than an ordinary scale. Play your neutral note, then the nearest white or black key, against the record: if the key version sounds blander or like a different scale, you have it. If your "neutral" note already sits on a key, you tuned by assumption rather than by ear — go back to phase 1.

Does the form survive with no beat — does the climb from lower to upper tetrachord carry it alone?

Answer

It should. The whole claim of a taqsim is that pitch, resting tones, and a saved ascent can hold a form together without any beat. If your take only works because you added a pulse or repeated a hook, you replaced the mechanism instead of testing it. Remove the addition and check whether the dwell-then-climb-then-rest arc still reads. In a real taqsim it does, held up by pitch and patience alone.
