Replicate: montage before tape

Copy one clear section of Walter Ruttmann — Weekend. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: sounds recorded on an optical film soundtrack and physically cut together, splice by splice — montage editing twenty years before tape made it easy. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone, charged, storage checked — the lab-006 field checklist applies in full. Bring the capture log (place · distance · level). The edit needs only Live and the DT 990 Pro.
  • New set saved as REP-WEEKEND — no tempo, no click. The pulse comes from where you place the cuts, not from Live’s grid.
  • Field safety: ask before recording people close enough to be identified, and listen to your surroundings, not just the phones — monitor at a moderate, fixed level.
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — one capture walk through your ordinary day, one cutting session, then file it and stop.
  • What you will make: a short replica of Walter Ruttmann — Weekend. The goal is this effect: tiny recognizable sound cells moving so fast that recognition becomes rhythm — sound cinema with no pictures.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and anything that hides whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism — sounds recorded and then physically cut together, splice by splice. Montage editing with hard cuts only.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences come from your source choices or gear limits, not from missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Walter Ruttmann — Weekend. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How long is a typical cell?
    Answer

    One to four seconds, often less. Many cells are gone before you finish naming them. The piece trusts that you recognize sounds faster than you need to settle into them.

  2. What kinds of cells alternate?
    Answer

    Three types, rotating: speech (voices, calls, word fragments), machines (saws, traffic, bells, work sounds), and music (scraps of song, whistling, instruments). The rotation is the form — each type refreshes the ear for the next.

  3. Where is the rhythm?
    Answer

    In the cuts. No instrument plays a beat anywhere. The cell lengths and cut placements create the pulse, the way film editing creates a visual rhythm. When you tap along, you are tapping the edits.

  4. Are there any transitions?
    Answer

    None. In 1930 the only tools were scissors and tape — no crossfades existed. Every join is a hard cut, and the abruptness is the style: the splice announces itself rather than apologizing.

  5. Why do recognizable sounds work at this speed?
    Answer

    Because naming is fast. A voice, a bell, or a saw registers in a fraction of a second, so a cell can be tiny and still carry meaning. Abstract sounds would need time to establish themselves. Recognizable sounds arrive already established.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a ninety-second montage of 20–30 hard-cut cells from your own day — speech, machine, and music types alternating — whose pulse a listener can tap without any instrument playing time.
  • Every cell 1–4 seconds. One audio track. Strictly in sequence, like the single film strip Ruttmann cut.
  • No transitions, no fades, no effects. Rhythm comes entirely from cut placement and cell length.
  • Every cell stays nameable. The piece runs on recognition, so nothing abstract and nothing processed.

The piece in one row: a day’s worth of tiny captures, one track, hard cuts only, and a pulse that exists nowhere except in where the cuts fall.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the shot list

Phase focus: the reference and the shot list. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag Weekend in, and loop two or three minutes.
  2. Audio Effects → Utility, trim to ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Write a shot list of 25–30 candidate captures from your actual day, sorted into the three registers — speech: radio voices, a phone call fragment, your own voice, café chatter;
  4. machine: kettle, keyboard, traffic, washing machine, a door buzzer;
  5. music: a hummed phrase, a ringtone, a few seconds of whatever is playing somewhere.
  6. Mark each candidate with its register letter — S, M, X.
  7. Aim for a rough third of each.
  8. One logbook line: the rotation you intend, e.g.
  9. S M X M S M X… — a plan you are allowed to break, but only on purpose.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Walter Ruttmann — Weekend (1930) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the shot list — as the smallest Live set that proves Berlin sounds recorded on an optical film soundtrack and physically cut together, splice by splice — montage editing twenty years before tape made it easy; speech, machine, and music cells alternating at cut speed.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects an eleven-minute city made of tiny recognizable sound cells moving so fast that the act of recognizing them becomes the rhythm — sound cinema with no pictures.

Phase 3 · ≈ 35 min

The cut — scissors, no glue

Phase focus: the cut — scissors, no glue. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. In Arrangement, build on one audio track named STRIP — strictly sequential, no overlaps, like a single piece of film.
  2. Trim each chosen capture to its cell: start on the onset, end before it outstays itself — 1–4 seconds, no exceptions.
  3. Switch off automatic clip fades (Options → Create Fades on Clip Edges unchecked, or zero the fades on each clip).
  4. If a join pops harshly, move the cut to a transient — solve it with placement, never with a fade.
  5. Lay roughly 90 seconds following your type rotation — speech, machine, music taking turns.
  6. Let a few cells repeat exactly — the montage equivalent of a drum hit returning.
  7. No effects of any kind, no level adjustments between cells. If one cell is much louder than its neighbors, that contrast is material — use it or swap the cell.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The pulse pass

Phase focus: the pulse pass. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the montage end to end and tap along on the desk.
  2. Note where your hand hesitates — those are the cuts that land in the wrong place.
  3. Fix each hesitation by adjusting the previous cell’s length. The cut lands where the cell before it ends.
  4. Check the type rotation: read the strip out loud as letters — S M X M S… If three of a kind sit in a row without intent, re-order.
  5. A/B thirty seconds against REF at matched loudness, judging two things only: are your cells as short, and are your joins as unapologetic?

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your montage at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Tap the montage’s pulse — where does the cut rhythm sit relative to a metronome?
    Answer

    Near a grid, never exactly on it. Turn on Live’s metronome at the tempo your tapping suggests and play the strip against it. The cuts should drift around the click — early here, late there — while the overall pulse stays tappable. That drift is the difference between montage rhythm and sequenced rhythm. Weekend’s pulse is felt, not quantized, because it was cut by hand. If your cuts land dead on the click, a grid crept into your trimming. If no relationship to the click exists at all, return to the pulse pass.

  • Which cells read as “words” and which as “percussion”?
    Answer

    Speech and song read as words — meaning arrives first and the cell is heard for something. Short machine bursts read as percussion — shape arrives first and the cell is heard as something. The change happens with length: cut any cell short enough and it loses its name and becomes a drum hit. Find one cell in your montage that sits exactly on the boundary — that is the most Ruttmann thing you made today.
