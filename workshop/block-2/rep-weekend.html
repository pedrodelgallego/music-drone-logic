Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: montage before tape

Copy one clear section of Walter Ruttmann — Weekend. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: Berlin sounds recorded on an optical film soundtrack and physically cut together, splice by splice - montage twenty years before tape made it easy. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordWalter Ruttmann — Weekend (1930)

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone, charged, storage checked — the lab-006 field checklist applies whole; the capture log (place · distance · level) comes along. The edit needs only Live and the DT 990 Pro
  • New set saved as REP-WEEKEND — no tempo set and no click; the pulse will come from your scissors, not Live’s
  • Field safety in one line: ask before recording people close enough to be named, and keep your ears on the traffic, not just the phones — monitoring at a moderate, fixed level
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — one capture walk through your ordinary day, one cutting session, then file it and stop
  • What you will make: a short replica of Walter Ruttmann — Weekend. Focus on this effect: an eleven-minute city made of tiny recognizable cells moving at a speed where recognition itself becomes rhythm - cinema for the ear, with no images.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: Berlin sounds recorded on an optical film soundtrack and physically cut together, splice by splice - montage twenty years before tape made it easy.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Walter Ruttmann — Weekend. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How long is a typical cell?
    Answer

    One to four seconds, and often less - many cells are gone before you finish naming them. Nothing is allowed to settle; the piece trusts that recognition is faster than comfort.

  2. What kinds of cells alternate?
    Answer

    Three registers, rotated: speech (voices, calls, fragments of words), machines (saws, traffic, bells, work), and music (scraps of song, whistling, instruments). The rotation is the form - each register refreshes the ear for the next.

  3. Where is the rhythm?
    Answer

    In the cuts. No instrument plays time anywhere - cell lengths and cut placements make the pulse, the way film editing makes a visual rhythm. When you tap along, you are tapping edits.

  4. Are there any transitions?
    Answer

    None - 1930 offered scissors and cement, not crossfades. Every join is a hard cut, and the bluntness is the style: the splice announces itself instead of apologizing.

  5. Why do recognizable sounds work at this speed?
    Answer

    Because naming is fast - a voice, a bell, or a saw reads in a fraction of a second, so a cell can be tiny and still carry meaning. Abstract material would need time to establish itself; recognizable material arrives pre-established.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a ninety-second montage of 20–30 hard-cut cells from your own day — speech, machine, and music registers alternating — whose pulse a listener can tap without any instrument playing time.
  • Every cell 1–4 seconds; one audio track; strictly sequential, like the single optical strip Ruttmann cut.
  • No transitions, no fades, no effects — rhythm comes entirely from cut placement and cell length.
  • Every cell stays nameable — the piece runs on recognition, so nothing abstract and nothing processed.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the shot list

Phase focus: the reference and the shot list. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag Weekend in, and loop two or three minutes.
  2. Live Audio Effects → Utility, trim to ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Recorder + Logbook Write a shot list of 25–30 candidate captures from your actual day, sorted into the three registers — speech: radio voices, a phone call fragment, your own voice, café chatter;
  4. Recorder + Logbook machine: kettle, keyboard, traffic, washing machine, a door buzzer;
  5. Recorder + Logbook music: a hummed phrase, a ringtone, a few seconds of whatever is playing somewhere.
  6. Recorder + Logbook Mark each candidate with its register letter — S, M, X.
  7. Recorder + Logbook Aim for a rough third of each.
  8. Logbook One logbook line: the rotation you intend, e.g.
  9. Logbook S M X M S M X… — a plan you are allowed to break, but only on purpose.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, and a register-labeled shot list of 25–30 captures is on paper before you leave the desk.

Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

The capture walk — tiny and many

Phase focus: the capture walk — tiny and many. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Recorder Walk the shot list with recorder or phone.
  2. Recorder For each candidate, record 3–8 seconds — enough to contain one clean 1–4-second cell with air on each side.
  3. Recorder Get close — the lab-006 perspective rule: one step closer and the source dominates.
  4. Recorder Cells must dominate; the room is dead weight at montage speed.
  5. Recorder Log each capture: place · distance · level, plus its register letter.
  6. Recorder Collect 25–30 usable captures.
  7. Recorder If a take has handling noise or a buried onset, re-take it on the spot.
  8. Live Back at the desk, dump everything into REP-WEEKEND on a holding track named BIN.
Expected result

A bin of 25–30 short, close, logged captures spanning all three registers — a day reduced to cells, ready for the scissors.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Cells sound distant and vague on playbackCaptured from listener distance, not montage distanceRe-take the weakest five from half the distance; vague cells cut badly
Half the bin is one registerThe walk followed convenience, not the listOne targeted second pass for the missing registers only — twenty minutes, no detours
Phase 3 · ≈ 35 min

The cut — scissors, no glue

Phase focus: the cut — scissors, no glue. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live In Arrangement, build on one audio track named STRIP — strictly sequential, no overlaps, like a single piece of film.
  2. Live Trim each chosen capture to its cell: start on the onset, end before it outstays itself — 1–4 seconds, no exceptions.
  3. Live Switch off automatic clip fades (Options → Create Fades on Clip Edges unchecked, or zero the fades on each clip).
  4. Live If a join pops harshly, move the cut to a transient — solve it with placement, never with a fade.
  5. Live Lay roughly 90 seconds following your register rotation — speech, machine, music taking turns;
  6. Live let a few cells repeat exactly, the montage equivalent of a drum hit returning.
  7. Live No effects of any kind, no level rides between cells — if one cell is far louder than its neighbors, that contrast is material;
  8. Live use it or re-choose the cell.
Expected result

Ninety seconds of hard-cut cells on one track — abrupt, fast, and recognizably your day — with a rhythm beginning to show through the joins.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The montage feels like a slideshow, not musicCells too uniform in length — even durations make no rhythmVary hard: pair a four-second cell with two one-second jabs; rhythm is contrast in lengths
Joins feel mushy despite hard cutsCells start late — dead air before the onset blurs every entranceRe-trim every cell to start exactly on its transient; the montage tightens immediately
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The pulse pass

Phase focus: the pulse pass. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the montage end to end and tap along on the desk.
  2. Note where your hand hesitates — those are the cuts that fall nowhere.
  3. Fix each hesitation by adjusting the previous cell’s length — the cut lands where the cell before it ends.
  4. Check the rotation: read the strip aloud as letters — S M X M S… If three of a kind sit in a row without intent, re-order.
  5. Live A/B thirty seconds against REF at matched loudness, judging two things only: are your cells as short, and are your joins as unapologetic?
Expected result

A ninety-second montage you can tap along to, with a register rotation you can read out loud — rhythm with no rhythm section.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
No pulse emerges anywhereCut placement is arbitrary — lengths chosen by content alonePick one short machine cell and repeat it at near-regular intervals; let it anchor the others
It pulses but feels mechanicalCells trimmed to identical lengths — a grid snuck inLengthen a few speech cells past comfort; the lurch against the pulse is Ruttmann’s feel
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your montage at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Tap the montage’s pulse — where does the cut rhythm sit relative to a metronome?
    Answer

    Near a grid, never on it. Turn on Live’s metronome at the tempo your tapping suggests and play the strip against it: the cuts should drift around the click — early here, late there — while the overall pulse stays tappable. That drift is the difference between montage rhythm and sequenced rhythm; Weekend’s pulse is felt, not quantized, because it was cut by hand and eye. If your cuts sit dead on the click, a grid crept into your trimming; if no relationship to the click exists at all, return to the pulse pass.

  • Which cells read as “words” and which as “percussion”?
    Answer

    Speech and song read as words — meaning arrives first and the cell is heard for something. Short machine bursts read as percussion — shape arrives first and the cell is heard as something. The flip happens with length: cut any cell short enough and it loses its name and becomes a drum. Relisten to the record with this sorting in mind, then find one cell in your montage that sits exactly on the boundary — that cell is the most Ruttmann thing you made today.
