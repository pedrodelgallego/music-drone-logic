Replicate: the song under the static

Copy one clear section of Fennesz — “Endless Summer”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: guitar chords run through granular processing (software that shreds audio into tiny grains and rearranges them) and saturation (deliberate overload that adds grit and warmth) on a parallel path. The composition is the slow change in balance between the clean song and the processed static. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring; the Digitakt II is not used this lesson; in Live you need only Grain Delay and Saturator (Granulator III is a useful extra, not a requirement)
  • New set saved as REP-FENNESZ, tempo around 100 BPM — slow enough for the chords to breathe
  • Saturation adds level as it adds harmonic content. Every increase in drive needs a matching output trim to keep levels safe. Check the wet chain’s peaks before riding it up, phones at the lab-001 mark.
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes, then file it and stop. The time goes into riding the balance, not into adding more devices.
  • What you will make: a short replica of Fennesz — “Endless Summer”. Focus on this effect: pop harmony submerged in granular shimmer and distortion, and surviving it - warmth heard through weather.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: guitar chords processed through laptop granular and saturation on a parallel path; the piece is the ride of the balance between song and static.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Fennesz — “Endless Summer”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Is there a song in here?
    Answer

    Yes — a simple, warm chord progression, closer to a pop ballad than to noise music. The test: you can hum it. Relisten and do exactly that. Hum along until the harmony is in your throat, ignoring everything that crackles.

  2. Is the distortion destroying the song or carrying it?
    Answer

    Carrying it. The harmony arrives through the grit, blurred but intact. Remove the static in your imagination and the track gets thinner, not clearer.

  3. What actually changes across the piece?
    Answer

    Mostly how deeply the song is buried under the static at any moment. The harmony loops without changing. The static thickens and thins. That slow change in balance is the entire form.

  4. Could the static stand alone?
    Answer

    No. By itself it is texture without a subject. The shimmer is interesting because something singable is being shimmered through it.

  5. At the most destroyed point, can you still find the harmony?
    Answer

    Yes — barely. That barely-audible threshold is what the whole record is calibrated to: maximum noise, song still singable. That is the exact limit you will hunt for in phase 4.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same quality — warm harmony heard through granular noise, singable even at its deepest point, at matched loudness.
  • One chord progression, recorded once — no new material after phase 2.
  • Two faders only: SONG and STATIC. Once the chain is calibrated, no device knobs move. The composition lives entirely in the balance between these two tracks.
  • At maximum static, you must still be able to sing the chords. If you cannot, the noise stopped being a veil and became a wall.

The piece in one row: a warm chord progression printed to one track, a fully processed copy of it on a second track, and two faders whose slow balance change — bringing the noise forward, then bringing the song back — is the entire composition.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag the title track of Endless Summer in, and loop two or three characteristic minutes.
  2. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. It stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  4. ⌘ T, rename SONG, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
  5. Write down your two timestamps from the reference pass — surface and deepest — in the logbook. These are the landmarks for the ride you will build.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Fennesz — “Endless Summer” (Endless Summer, 2001) and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.

  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects pop harmony buried in granular shimmer and distortion, yet still recognizable — warmth heard through noise.

Phase 2 · ≈ 20 min

The song — simple, warm, worth saving

Phase focus: the song — simple, warm, worth saving. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: a warm, sustaining patch — Type → Wavetable or V.Analog, filter partly closed so the top is soft, Paraphonic on, slowish attack, long release.
  2. No movement in the patch — the static track will provide all the movement later.
  3. Write a progression of three or four chords, eight bars — the kind a pop ballad would use. Try I–vi–IV–V in any key, or use the changes from a song you know and slow them down.
  4. Set the M4 input-1 gain on held chords, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  5. Record the eight bars into SONG, playing the chords by hand. Small timing variations are fine.
  6. Loop the clip.
  7. Sing the progression once away from the speakers. If you cannot sing it back, it will not survive phase 4 — simplify until you can.

Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The static — a parallel chain, calibrated once

Phase focus: the static — a parallel chain, calibrated once. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Duplicate the SONG clip onto a new track named STATIC — same audio, second path.
  2. On STATIC: Audio Effects → Grain Delay — Spray around 20–40 ms, Pitch at +12 (the upper-octave shimmer), Random Pitch a few clicks, Feedback around 30%, Dry/Wet at 100%.
  3. After it: Audio Effects → Saturator — Soft Sine, Drive around +12 to +18 dB, and pull the output down until STATIC peaks ≈ −12 dBFS like everything else.
  4. (Granulator III owners: resample the clip through it instead of Grain Delay; the chain stays the same shape.)
  5. Solo STATIC and tune by one test: you should hear shimmer and noise with the harmony’s shape still faintly inside it. Too clean — add more spray and drive. Pure noise with no pitch at all — dial back.
  6. Calibration ends here.
  7. Write the settings down. No device knob moves again this lesson.

Phase 4 · ≈ 30 min

Ride the veil — three minutes on two faders

Phase focus: ride the veil — three minutes on two faders. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Write out the ride in plain words: “surface — song in front, static underneath” → “first wash — both equal” → “fully under — static in front, song barely traceable” → “resurface.” Three or four states, with rough minute marks. That sentence chain is the score.
  2. Live Lay 3 minutes of both loops in Arrangement and move the two faders across the plan — record the automation, or MIDI-map the faders (⌘ M) and perform it live.
  3. Live Make each move take at least tens of seconds. The balance shifts gradually, like weather — it does not switch.
  4. Live At your deepest point, sing the progression out loud against the mix. If you cannot, raise SONG until you barely can. That threshold is the deepest point allowed — it is the record’s own rule.
  5. Live Play the three minutes end to end at the mark, then A/B against REF’s surface and deepest timestamps at matched loudness.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • At your most destroyed moment, can you still sing the harmony?
    Answer

    You must be able to. That is the pass condition, not a preference. If the chords are gone, the static stopped being a veil and became the whole subject — a different record than the one you are copying. Raise the song fader to the point where singing just barely works, then re-print. Relisten to the record’s deepest passage and confirm it follows the same rule.

  • Solo the dry track, then solo the wet one — which is “the piece”?
    Answer

    Neither. Solo’d dry, you have a pleasant chord loop with nothing at stake. Solo’d wet, you have texture with no subject underneath it. The piece only exists in the balance between them — which means the composition you built this hour lives on two faders and nowhere else. Relisten to your render: every moment you would call an event is a balance change. That is also how the Eleh lesson works — the same idea wearing a different sound.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.

  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 007 — damage as a creative decision. The lab measures what a signal path destroys per pass. This record shows that the damaged copy and the clean original can be used as two separate instruments, and the balance between them composed.
