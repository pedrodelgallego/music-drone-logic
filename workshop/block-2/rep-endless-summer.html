Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the song under the static

Copy one clear section of Fennesz — “Endless Summer”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: guitar chords processed through laptop granular and saturation on a parallel path; the piece is the ride of the balance between song and static. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring; the Digitakt II rests this lesson; in Live you need only Grain Delay and Saturator (Granulator III is a welcome extra, not a requirement)
  • New set saved as REP-FENNESZ, tempo around 100 BPM — slow enough for the chords to breathe
  • Saturation adds level as it adds harmonics — every drive move gets a matching output trim, phones at the lab-001 mark, and check the wet chain’s peaks before riding it up
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into the ride, not into collecting devices
  • What you will make: a short replica of Fennesz — “Endless Summer”. Focus on this effect: pop harmony submerged in granular shimmer and distortion, and surviving it - warmth heard through weather.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: guitar chords processed through laptop granular and saturation on a parallel path; the piece is the ride of the balance between song and static.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Fennesz — “Endless Summer”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Is there a song in here?
    Answer

    Yes - a simple, warm chord progression, closer to a pop ballad than to noise music. The proof is that you can hum it. Relisten and do exactly that: hum along until the harmony is in your throat, ignoring everything that crackles.

  2. Is the distortion destroying the song or carrying it?
    Answer

    Carrying it. A fire consumes its material; this is a veil - the harmony arrives through the grit, colored and blurred but structurally intact. Remove the static in your imagination and the track gets thinner, not clearer.

  3. What actually changes across the piece?
    Answer

    Mostly the depth of submersion - how far the song sits under the static at any moment. The harmony loops; the veil thickens and thins; that ride is the form.

  4. Could the static stand alone?
    Answer

    No - solo’d in your imagination it is texture without a subject, weather with no landscape under it. The shimmer is interesting because something singable is being shimmered.

  5. At the most destroyed point, can you still find the harmony?
    Answer

    Yes - barely, which is the calibration this whole record runs on: maximum veil, song still singable. That threshold is the exact setting you will be hunting for in phase 4.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same submersion — warm harmony heard through granular weather, singable at its deepest point, at matched loudness.
  • One progression, printed once — no new material after phase 2.
  • Two faders only: SONG and STATIC. After calibration, no device knob moves — the composition lives entirely in the balance.
  • The rule at maximum static: you must still be able to sing the chords. If you can’t, the veil became a fire.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a warm progression printed to one track, a fully wet granular-and-saturation copy of it on a second, and two faders whose balance — surface, submersion, return — is the entire score.

MicroFreak · warm chords · 8 bars printed → SONG track · dry + STATIC track · Grain Delay → Saturator · 100% wet → two faders · the submersion ride · 3 min

The submersion drawn as three snapshots. The same melody line runs through all three panels — that is the constraint. Only the balance between song and static moves, and even in the third panel the line must remain traceable.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag the title track of Endless Summer in, and loop two or three characteristic minutes.
  2. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Live It stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  4. Live ⌘ T, rename SONG, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
  5. Live + Logbook Transfer your two timestamps from the reference pass — surface and deepest — into the logbook as the ride’s landmarks.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one armed track waits for the chords, and two timestamps mark how far under the record dares to go.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
REF later seems to submerge deeper than yours ever canUtility never trimmed — its loudness reads as depthTrim until the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, same as your tracks
No meter movement when testing the inputExt. In channel doesn’t match the physical jackMicroFreak on 1 — the lab-001 wiring table
Phase 2 · ≈ 20 min

The song — simple, warm, worth saving

Phase focus: the song — simple, warm, worth saving. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak MicroFreak: a warm, sustaining patch — Type → Wavetable or V.Analog, filter partly closed so the top is soft, Paraphonic on, slowish attack, long release.
  2. MicroFreak No motion in the patch — the static will provide all the motion later.
  3. Logbook Write a progression of three or four chords, eight bars, the kind a pop ballad would not be ashamed of — try I–vi–IV–V in any key you like, or steal the changes from a song you love and slow them down.
  4. Logbook Set the M4 input-1 gain on held chords, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  5. Logbook Record the eight bars into SONG, playing the chords by hand — small timing leans are welcome.
  6. Live Loop the clip.
  7. Live Sing the progression once, away from the speakers.
  8. Live If you cannot sing it now, it will not survive phase 4 — simplify until you can.
Expected result

Eight bars of warm, singable harmony looping at ≈ −12 dBFS — plainly pretty, slightly boring, exactly right. The static will make it interesting; the song’s job is to be worth finding.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The progression is hard to hum backToo many chords, or root movement too cleverCut to three or four diatonic chords; this material must survive a storm
The chords sound brittle and thinFilter open, attack fast — edges the granulator will tear atClose the filter partway, slow the attack; warmth is the cargo
Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The static — a parallel chain, calibrated once

Phase focus: the static — a parallel chain, calibrated once. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Duplicate the SONG clip onto a new track named STATIC — same audio, second path.
  2. Live On STATIC: Audio Effects → Grain Delay — Spray around 20–40 ms, Pitch at +12 (the upper-octave shimmer), Random Pitch a few clicks, Feedback around 30%, Dry/Wet at 100%.
  3. Live After it: Audio Effects → Saturator — Soft Sine, Drive around +12 to +18 dB, and pull the output down until STATIC peaks ≈ −12 dBFS like everything else.
  4. Live (Granulator III owners: resample the clip through it instead of Grain Delay; the chain stays the same shape.)
  5. Live Solo STATIC and tune by one test only: you should hear weather with the harmony’s ghost inside it.
  6. Live Too clean — more spray and drive; pure noise with no pitch left — less.
  7. Live Calibration ends here.
  8. Logbook Write the settings down; no device knob moves again this lesson.
Expected result

Two tracks playing the same eight bars: one plainly the song, one all shimmer and burn with the harmony ghosting through — both at ≈ −12 dBFS, both on faders, the instrument built.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The wet chain is just noise — no pitch survivesSpray or feedback too high; the grains lost the chordPull Spray under 40 ms and Feedback under 40% until the ghost returns
The two tracks phase strangely when both are upGrain Delay’s dry signal leaking — Dry/Wet below 100%Set Dry/Wet to 100%; the dry song lives on its own track only
Phase 4 · ≈ 30 min

Ride the veil — three minutes on two faders

Phase focus: ride the veil — three minutes on two faders. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Plan the ride in plain words: “surface — song in front, static underneath” → “first wash — equals” → “fully under — static in front, song barely traceable” → “resurface.” Three or four states, with rough minute marks.
  2. That sentence chain is the score.
  3. Live Lay 3 minutes of both loops in Arrangement and ride the two faders across the plan — record the automation, or MIDI-map the faders (⌘ M) and perform it.
  4. Live Make each move take tens of seconds at least — the veil thickens like weather, it does not switch.
  5. Live At your deepest point, sing the progression out loud against the mix.
  6. Live If you cannot, raise SONG until you barely can — that threshold is the deepest allowed point, and it is the record’s own calibration.
  7. Live Play the three minutes end to end at the mark, then A/B against REF’s surface and deepest timestamps at matched loudness.
Expected result

A three-minute piece whose entire event list is one balance moving — surface, submersion, return — with the harmony singable even at the bottom of the dive.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The deep section reads as a different piece, not a deeper oneSong fader pulled to silence — the veil became a curtainKeep SONG above the singability threshold at all times
The ride feels like edits, not weatherFader moves too fast — switching instead of thickeningStretch every move past the half-minute mark and redo the passage
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • At your most destroyed moment, can you still sing the harmony?
    Answer

    You must be able to — that is the pass condition, not a preference. If the chords are gone, the static stopped being a veil and became the piece’s subject, which is a different record than the one you are copying. Raise the song to the threshold where singing just barely works and re-print. Relisten to the record’s deepest passage and confirm it holds itself to the same rule.

  • Solo the dry track, then solo the wet one — which is “the piece”?
    Answer

    Neither. Solo’d dry, you have a pleasant loop with nothing at stake; solo’d wet, weather with no landscape. The piece exists only in the balance between them — which means the composition you wrote this hour lives on two faders and nowhere else. Relisten to your render and notice that every moment you would call an event is a balance arriving somewhere; that was true of the Eleh lesson too, and it is the same discipline wearing distortion.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 007 — degradation as authorship. The lab measures what a path destroys per pass; this record shows that the destroyed copy and the clean parent can be mixed as two instruments, and the balance composed.
