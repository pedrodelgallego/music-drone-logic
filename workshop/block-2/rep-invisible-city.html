Replicate: the city as ensemble

Copy one clear section of BJ Nilsen — The Invisible City. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: field recordings (recordings made out in the world, not in a studio) of urban sounds cast into ensemble roles — one bed, mid voices, top detail — and balanced until the seams disappear. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: the handheld recorder with foam fitted, closed phones for field monitoring, spare batteries and card — the Lab 008 kit, air path only; the contact mic and coil rest this lesson. Back at the bench: Live 12, the M4, DT 990 Pro at the mark
  • New set saved as REP-CITY — no tempo worth setting; the city keeps its own
  • The Lab 008 field safety checklist travels: one ear free near roads, no identifiable voices kept without asking, cables and kit counted out and counted back
  • What you will make: a short replica of BJ Nilsen — The Invisible City. Focus on this effect: separate urban recordings fuse into one continuous place that exists nowhere - machines that never met performing as a band.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: field recordings of urban systems cast into ensemble roles - one bed, mid voices, top detail - and balanced at matched level until the seams disappear.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of BJ Nilsen — The Invisible City. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many separate locations can you count?
    Answer

    Fewer than there are. Nilsen layers captures from different places, but matching their levels and tone makes them read as one continuous space — you count rooms in a building that was never built.

  2. What holds the bottom of the spectrum, and does it ever stop?
    Answer

    A drone — ventilation, distant traffic, some machine holding a steady tone — and no, it never fully stops. That continuity is the glue: as long as the bed sustains, the layers above it can come and go without exposing a seam.

  3. Which sound is in front right now, and what put it there?
    Answer

    Level put it there. Nothing about a ventilation grille makes it a lead — it leads because it is louder and closer than the sounds around it, the way a singer leads a band.

  4. Does anything repeat like a composed rhythm?
    Answer

    Yes — machines repeat; that is what machines do. Compressors cycle, fans pulse, something drips in time. Nobody composed these rhythms, but layered against each other they read as arrangement.

  5. Where are the seams between recordings?
    Answer

    Mostly nowhere you can point to. Entrances arrive under cover of the bed, levels never jump, and tonal character is matched closely enough that your ear assumes continuity.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your composite lands on the same illusion — one continuous place, machines as a band, no audible seams — at matched loudness. Closeness is the measure of your balancing.
  • Six to eight captures collected; four make the piece — one bed, two mid voices, one top detail. The rest stay on muted tracks as the bench.
  • Balance only: faders, Utility trims, and edits. No reverb, no EQ rescue, no effects — the blend has to come from level and choice.
  • The bed never stops. Every entrance and exit above it happens under its cover.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a walk gathers the recordings, an audition picks the cast, four are assigned ensemble roles, and the faders mix them into one city that exists only in the file.

From many places to one, drawn as three snapshots. Eight captures from eight locations become four cast roles, and the four roles — balanced, never all moving at once — become a single place no map shows.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the capture list

Phase focus: the reference and the capture list. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a track from The Invisible City into it.
  2. Find four or five characteristic minutes — a steady bed with layers trading places above it — and loop them.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. Write the capture list in the logbook: six to eight urban systems within reach.
  6. Candidates — a ventilation grille, traffic from your window, the fridge, a server or router fan, an elevator or stairwell hum, a washing machine mid-cycle, a heating pipe.
  7. For each, guess its ensemble role: bed, mid, or detail.
  8. Pack against the Lab 008 kit table — recorder, foam fitted, phones, spares — and count the kit out.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from BJ Nilsen — The Invisible City (2010) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the capture list — as the smallest Live set that proves field recordings of urban sounds cast into ensemble roles — one bed, mid voices, top detail — and balanced until the seams disappear.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects separate urban recordings fuse into one continuous place that never existed — machines that never met, performing as a band.

Phase 2 · ≈ 35 min

The field walk — eight gain decisions

Phase focus: the field walk — eight gain decisions. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Walk the list.
  2. At each target, stand or set the recorder down, set gain for the loudest plausible event at that spot — the truck behind the traffic, the compressor kicking in behind the fridge hum — and record 60–90 seconds minimum.
  3. Hold still while rolling.
  4. Fingers shifting on the body print as thumps; if you cannot hold still, put the recorder down on a folded jacket.
  5. Monitor on the closed phones, one ear slipped free near roads — you are judging the file, not the street, but the street still has cars in it.
  6. Log each capture on the spot: location, gain setting, and what the place did that the plan did not predict.
  7. Collect at least six usable captures, then come home and count the kit back.

Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

Casting the band

Phase focus: casting the band. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import every capture, one track per file, named for its location.
  2. Audition all of them once at the mark before deciding anything.
  3. Cast four: the steadiest, lowest capture is the BED; two contrasting continuous sounds are the mid voices;
  4. one small repeating or textural sound is the top detail.
  5. Mute the rest — they stay in the set as the bench.
  6. Utility on each cast track; trim so each peaks ≈ −12 dBFS solo.
  7. Loop or extend the bed to fill four minutes of Arrangement — edit its loop seam during a busy moment so the join hides.
  8. Place the mids and the detail across the four minutes.
  9. Rule: nothing enters or exits except under the bed, and no two layers move at once.
  10. Balance with faders only.
  11. Decide which layer is in front for each minute and write the cast list in the logbook — minute one: traffic leads;
  12. minute three: the fridge takes the verse.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The composite test

Phase focus: the composite test. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the composite end to end, no touching.
  2. Listen as a visitor: where is this place, how big is it, what is making the lowest sound?
  3. A/B one minute of yours against REF at matched loudness, judging only two things: does yours hold together as one space, and does the front layer read as a lead rather than an accident?
  4. Run the seam audit: every entrance, exit, and loop join gets played at the mark, and any one you can point at goes back to phase 3.
  5. The lead test: pull the front layer down 6 dB and listen to another capture take the verse.
  6. Put it back.
  7. Log what changed — and notice that nothing changed but a number.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your composite at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does it read as one place that does not exist?
    Answer

    It should — a listener asked “where was this recorded?” should give one answer, not four. The illusion rests on two things: the bed never stopping, and no level ever jumping. If the listener says “and then we go somewhere else,” a seam is exposed; find it and slide it under the bed. Relisten to the record asking the same question; its locations are plural but its place is singular, and that gap is the whole composition.

  • Which layer is the lead, and what made it so?
    Answer

    Whichever you pushed — and nothing else made it so. The fridge has no more melody than the vent; it leads because you gave it 6 dB and a clear register, the same way a mix engineer makes a vocalist lead a band. In found sound, the lead role is assigned, not earned. Relisten to your piece and name each minute’s lead out loud; if you hesitate anywhere, the balance there is undecided, and undecided reads as noise.
