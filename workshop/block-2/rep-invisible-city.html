Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the city as ensemble

Copy one clear section of BJ Nilsen — The Invisible City. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: field recordings of urban systems cast into ensemble roles - one bed, mid voices, top detail - and balanced at matched level until the seams disappear. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Source recordBJ Nilsen — The Invisible City (2010)

Before you start
  • Gear: the handheld recorder with foam fitted, closed phones for field monitoring, spare batteries and card — the Lab 008 kit, air path only; the contact mic and coil rest this lesson. Back at the bench: Live 12, the M4, DT 990 Pro at the mark
  • New set saved as REP-CITY — no tempo worth setting; the city keeps its own
  • The Lab 008 field safety checklist travels: one ear free near roads, no identifiable voices kept without asking, cables and kit counted out and counted back
  • What you will make: a short replica of BJ Nilsen — The Invisible City. Focus on this effect: separate urban recordings fuse into one continuous place that exists nowhere - machines that never met performing as a band.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: field recordings of urban systems cast into ensemble roles - one bed, mid voices, top detail - and balanced at matched level until the seams disappear.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of BJ Nilsen — The Invisible City. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many separate locations can you count?
    Answer

    Fewer than there are. Nilsen layers captures from different places, but matched levels and matched tone make them read as one continuous space - you count rooms in a building that was never built.

  2. What holds the bottom of the spectrum, and does it ever stop?
    Answer

    A drone - ventilation, distant traffic, some machine holding a steady tone - and no, it never fully stops. That continuity is the glue: as long as the bed sustains, the layers above it can come and go without exposing a seam.

  3. Which sound is in front right now, and what put it there?
    Answer

    Level put it there. Nothing about a ventilation grille makes it a lead instrument - it leads because it is louder and closer than its neighbors, the way a singer leads a band.

  4. Does anything repeat like a composed rhythm?
    Answer

    Yes - machines repeat; that is what machines do. Compressors cycle, fans pulse, something drips in time. Nobody composed these rhythms, but layered against each other they read as arrangement.

  5. Where are the seams between recordings?
    Answer

    Mostly nowhere you can point at. Entrances arrive under cover of the bed, levels never jump, and tonal character is matched closely enough that your ear assumes continuity.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your composite lands on the same illusion — one continuous place, machines as a band, no audible seams — at matched loudness. Closeness is the measure of your balancing.
  • Six to eight captures collected; four make the piece — one bed, two mid voices, one top detail. The rest stay on muted tracks as the bench.
  • Balance only: faders, Utility trims, and edits. No reverb, no EQ rescue, no effects — the blend has to come from level and choice.
  • The bed never stops. Every entrance and exit above it happens under its cover.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a walk collects the players, the bench holds auditions, four captures get cast into ensemble roles, and the faders mix them into one city that exists only in the file.

field walk · 6–8 urban systems captured → import · one track per capture → casting · bed + 2 mids + 1 detail → mix · one composite city · ≈ −12 dBFS

From many places to one, drawn as three snapshots. Eight captures from eight locations become four cast roles, and the four roles — balanced, never all moving at once — become a single place no map shows.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the capture list

Phase focus: the reference and the capture list. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a track from The Invisible City into it.
  2. Live Find four or five characteristic minutes — a steady bed with layers trading places above it — and loop them.
  3. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Live REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. Logbook Write the capture list in the logbook: six to eight urban systems within reach.
  6. Logbook Candidates — a ventilation grille, traffic from your window, the fridge, a server or router fan, an elevator or stairwell hum, a washing machine mid-cycle, a heating pipe.
  7. Logbook For each, guess its ensemble role: bed, mid, or detail.
  8. Recorder Pack against the Lab 008 kit table — recorder, foam fitted, phones, spares — and count the kit out.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, a written list of six to eight targets with guessed roles, and a counted kit at the door.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The list has eight sounds but no plausible bedEverything chosen is an event, nothing is a droneAdd at least two continuous candidates — ventilation and distant traffic are the classic beds
REF later feels fuller in a way yours can never reachUtility never trimmed — density and level conflatedTrim until the looped section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, same as your composite
Phase 2 · ≈ 35 min

The field walk — eight gain decisions

Phase focus: the field walk — eight gain decisions. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Walk the list.
  2. Recorder At each target, stand or set the recorder down, set gain for the loudest plausible event at that spot — the truck behind the traffic, the compressor kicking in behind the fridge hum — and record 60–90 seconds minimum.
  3. Recorder Hold still while rolling.
  4. Recorder Fingers shifting on the body print as thumps; if you cannot hold still, put the recorder down on a folded jacket.
  5. Live Monitor on the closed phones, one ear slipped free near roads — you are judging the file, not the street, but the street still has cars in it.
  6. Live Log each capture on the spot: location, gain setting, and what the place did that the plan did not predict.
  7. Live Collect at least six usable captures, then come home and count the kit back.
Expected result

Six to eight clean captures — no clipping, no wind blast, no handling thumps — each with a logged gain decision, and the whole kit back on the bench.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Exterior captures carry low blasts that ruin the takeWind hitting bare capsules — foam skipped or insufficientFoam on, body as windbreak, or move the capture into a doorway; re-record, do not keep it
A capture hisses when auditioned at the benchGain set too low for a quiet source — the kit’s self-noise printed inRe-record closer to the source with gain up; never rescue it with the fader later
Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

Casting the band

Phase focus: casting the band. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import every capture, one track per file, named for its location.
  2. Audition all of them once at the mark before deciding anything.
  3. Cast four: the steadiest, lowest capture is the BED; two contrasting continuous sounds are the mid voices;
  4. one small repeating or textural sound is the top detail.
  5. Mute the rest — they stay in the set as the bench.
  6. Live Utility on each cast track; trim so each peaks ≈ −12 dBFS solo.
  7. Live Loop or extend the bed to fill four minutes of Arrangement — edit its loop seam during a busy moment so the join hides.
  8. Live Place the mids and the detail across the four minutes.
  9. Live Rule: nothing enters or exits except under the bed, and no two layers move at once.
  10. Live Balance with faders only.
  11. Logbook Decide which layer is in front for each minute and write the cast list in the logbook — minute one: traffic leads;
  12. Logbook minute three: the fridge takes the verse.
Expected result

A four-minute arrangement of four untouched captures over an unbroken bed, with a written cast list saying who leads when — and no moment where a join can be pointed at.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The composite reads as a collage of clips, not a placeThe bed stops, or entrances happen in the openExtend the bed to wall-to-wall, and slide every entrance under a busy moment
One layer always swallows the othersSpectral pile-up — two captures living in the same registerRecast: swap one mid for a benched capture from a different register; choice, not EQ
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The composite test

Phase focus: the composite test. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the composite end to end, no touching.
  2. Listen as a visitor: where is this place, how big is it, what is making the lowest sound?
  3. Live A/B one minute of yours against REF at matched loudness, judging only two things: does yours hold together as one space, and does the front layer read as a lead rather than an accident?
  4. Live Run the seam audit: every entrance, exit, and loop join gets played at the mark, and any one you can point at goes back to phase 3.
  5. Live The lead test: pull the front layer down 6 dB and listen to another capture take the verse.
  6. Live Put it back.
  7. Live Log what changed — and notice that nothing changed but a number.
Expected result

A four-minute city that survives the visitor’s questions, an A/B that comes down to continuity and casting, and a logbook line about a lead singer appointed entirely by a fader.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The piece holds for a minute, then attention slides offThe cast never changes — the same layer leads all four minutesHand the lead to a different capture at least twice across the piece, by level alone
Yours sounds smaller than REF at matched loudnessAll four layers sit in the same distance — nothing far, nothing closeRecast for depth: one capture recorded close, one through a window or down a corridor
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your composite at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does it read as one place that does not exist?
    Answer

    It should — a listener asked “where was this recorded?” should give one answer, not four. The illusion is built from two things you can check: the bed never stopping, and no level ever jumping. If the listener says “and then we go somewhere else,” a seam is exposed; find it and slide it under the bed. Relisten to the record asking the same question; its locations are plural and its place is singular, and that gap is the whole composition.

  • Which layer is the lead singer, and what made it so?
    Answer

    Whichever you pushed — and nothing else made it so. The fridge has no more melody than the vent; it leads because you gave it 6 dB and a clear register, exactly the way a mix engineer makes a singer lead a band. That is the lesson worth keeping: in found sound, fame is assigned, not earned. Relisten to your piece once more and name each minute’s lead out loud; if you hesitate anywhere, the balance there is undecided, and undecided reads as noise.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
