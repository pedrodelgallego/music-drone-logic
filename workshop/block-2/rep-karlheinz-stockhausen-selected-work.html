Replicate: the voice–tone continuum as form

Steal one structural idea from Stockhausen's Gesang der Jünglinge. Organise your material on a single axis running from recognisable voice to pure electronic tone. Movement along that axis — combined with where each sound sits in space — is the form. Build a short take and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

You are not copying the boy's voice, the German text, or the 1956 tape sound. You are copying the mechanism: a continuum from sung syllable to synthetic tone, where the piece's drama is the sound sliding between those two poles while moving between speakers. The continuum is the form. Everything else is decoration.

The system you are replicating
Source recordKarlheinz Stockhausen — Gesang der Jünglinge (1956)
The effectYou cannot always tell where the boy stops singing and the machine starts; the two feel like points on one spectrum, and the piece moves you along it
The mechanismMaterial is organised on a voice↔electronic-tone continuum; where each sound sits on that scale, plus its position in space, is the form — not melody, not metre

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • One recorded voice sample of your own — a single sung or spoken syllable, a few seconds, on a Digitakt II track
  • MicroFreak ready on Live's input for the electronic-tone end of the axis
  • Headphones or a stereo monitor setup — the spatial half of the mechanism needs real L/R, not a mono check
  • What you will make: a 60–90 second study where one syllable is transformed step by step into a pure tone, and back, while moving across the stereo field. Focus on this effect: the listener loses track of where voice ends and machine begins.
  • What you will turn off: melody, beat, lyrics, and any second idea. One axis only.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: the continuum and its spatial placement carry the whole form.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about two minutes of Gesang der Jünglinge. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Pick one moment. Is the sound closer to the voice end or the tone end of the axis — and can you say exactly?
    Answer

    Usually you cannot say exactly, and that is the point. Stockhausen places sounds at in-between positions — a vowel filtered until it rings like an oscillator, a sine burst shaped to breathe like a syllable — so your ear sits between the two poles. The uncertainty is a deliberate compositional event, not a flaw in your hearing.

  2. What changes when the material moves toward the electronic end — pitch, texture, or your sense of who is making the sound?
    Answer

    Mainly the last one. As the voice becomes more abstract you stop hearing a person and start hearing a process. The drama is that repeated loss and recovery — the piece keeps almost giving the boy back, then dissolving him again.

  3. Where does spatial movement happen, and does it line up with movement along the continuum?
    Answer

    Often yes — sounds scatter across speakers exactly when they are most abstract, and gather together when a recognisable syllable returns. Space is the second axis: spread out reads as "machine," pulled to centre reads as "voice." Use both together and the form needs no melody.

  4. How much actual pitched melody is there to hum?
    Answer

    Almost none. The structure is carried by timbre and position, not by tune. That is what lets the continuum be the form: with melody removed, the slide from voice to tone is the only event, so it carries everything.

Now build the axis you just described. Do not build from memory of the sounds — build from the structure.

  • Sound target: a five- or six-stage ramp from your raw syllable to a pure MicroFreak tone, where each stage is one clear step closer to the machine.
  • Voice pole: the dry recorded syllable, untreated, panned centre.
  • Tone pole: a single sustained MicroFreak note at the syllable's pitch, no formants, hard-panned.
  • The form: the journey between the two poles, plus the spatial spread that tracks it — nothing else.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Pin the two poles

Phase focus: establish the ends of the axis so every later stage has a destination. Compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track and gain-match it with a Utility so A/Bs are honest.
  2. Set the voice pole: load your one syllable on a track, dry, centred. This is position 0 on the axis.
  3. Set the tone pole: play one sustained note at roughly the syllable's pitch — a clean oscillator, no movement. This is position 5 on the axis.
  4. Audition the two poles back to back. They should sound like the same pitch from two different worlds.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Karlheinz Stockhausen — Gesang der Jünglinge (1956) and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — Pin the two poles — as the smallest Live set that proves Material is organised on a voice↔electronic-tone continuum; where each sound sits on that scale, plus its position in space, is the form — not melody, not metre.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects You cannot always tell where the boy stops singing and the machine starts; the two feel like points on one spectrum, and the piece moves you along it.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate Pin the two poles into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates Material is organised on a voice↔electronic-tone continuum; where each sound sits on that scale, plus its position in space, is the form — not melody, not metre, not just the surface of Karlheinz Stockhausen — Gesang der Jünglinge (1956).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.

Phase 2 — ~35 minutes

Build the intermediate stages

Phase focus: fill the gap between the poles with steps a listener can hear as a single direction of travel.

syllable (pos 0) → filtered / granular stages (pos 1–4) → pure tone (pos 5)
Do this
  1. Make pos 1–2 from the voice: pitch-lock the syllable, then thin it — narrow filtering, freeze/granular hold — until it rings more than it speaks.
  2. Make pos 3–4 from the tone: add a hint of formant, a slow vowel-like filter sweep, or a breath of noise so the pure note edges back toward speech.
  3. Line all six positions up in time order and audition the ramp. Each step should be one clear move toward the machine — no jumps, no backtracking yet.
  4. Mark the one position where you genuinely cannot tell voice from tone. That ambiguous middle is your strongest material.

Phase 3 — ~25 minutes

Let position and space be the form

Phase focus: perform a journey across the axis and the stereo field, and record it. This is the whole piece.

Do this
  1. Write the journey first: a short list of positions in order with a pan for each, e.g. 0 centre → 3 wide → 5 hard L → 4 → 1 centre. The list is your score.
  2. Tie pan to position: the more abstract the stage, the wider or harder the pan; the voice end pulls back to centre. Wide = machine, centred = voice.
  3. Record the journey in one pass, moving along the list. No beat — let durations breathe like speech.
  4. Listen back with eyes closed: can a stranger feel the move from human to machine and back without being told?

Verify against the record

A/B your take against a comparable stretch of Gesang der Jünglinge — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Is there a moment in yours where a listener could not say whether they hear voice or machine?

Answer

If not, the continuum has a gap in the middle and the form collapses into "voice, then synth." The ambiguous stage is the proof you built an axis rather than two separate sounds. If yours snaps cleanly between the poles, go back to phase 2 and close the gap.

Does the form survive with melody and beat absent — does the slide alone carry it?

Answer

It should. That is the mechanism's whole claim: position and space can be a form. If your take only works because a rhythm or a tune crept in, you replaced the mechanism instead of testing it. Strip the addition and check whether the journey still holds — in Stockhausen it does.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: "Matched: …" · "Missed: … (best guess why)" · "Change next: …"
  • Name the one stage on your axis that did the most work — the position that sold the whole journey.
  • Replica audio stays in the logbook as study evidence; it is not a release.
