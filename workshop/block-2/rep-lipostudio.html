Replicate: capture as provocation

Copy one clear section of Matmos — “Lipostudio (And So On…)”, from A Chance to Cut Is a Chance to Cure. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: close, clean recordings of a bodily source, chopped into drum hits and loops and placed on a strict grid, with the most identifiable sounds kept up front. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone for the capture — or a mic into M4 input 1, gain by the lab-001 ledger; DT 990 Pro for the edit. The lab-006 capture log (place · distance · level) rides along
  • New set saved as REP-LIPOSTUDIO, tempo 125 BPM
  • Safety is mostly social here: record your own body or your own appliances, nobody else’s without asking — and phones at the lab-001 mark; close captures of small sounds invite the volume knob up, and the knob stays put
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — one close-capture session, one chop-and-sequence session, then file it and stop
  • What you will make: a short replica of Matmos — “Lipostudio (And So On…)”, from A Chance to Cut Is a Chance to Cure. Focus on this effect: a clean house groove whose material is audibly a human body under surgery — and the unsettling pleasure of nodding along anyway.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: close, clean recordings of a bodily source, chopped into hits and loops and placed on a strict grid, with the most identifiable sounds kept up front.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or equipment limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Matmos — “Lipostudio (And So On…)”, from A Chance to Cut Is a Chance to Cure. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Which sounds still sound like a body?
    Answer

    The wet ones. Suction and squelch read as flesh even when trimmed to the grid, while the kick-and-hat skeleton stays anonymous.

  2. Where does the groove live — in the sounds or in their placement?
    Answer

    Placement. The captures were wet, irregular, and had no beat. The dance was imposed afterward — tight trims, strict positions, careful levels. The material supplies character and the grid supplies the nod. Neither can do the other’s job.

  3. How clean are the captures underneath the groove?
    Answer

    Very clean — no room noise, no handling thumps, no hiss bed. Capturing close and clean is what lets an uncomfortable sound feel composed rather than crude. Detail reads as craft, and craft is what keeps the provocation listenable.

  4. What does knowing the source change?
    Answer

    Not the beat — your body keeps nodding. The knowledge changes your reaction, not the music. The discomfort comes entirely from knowing the label. The track works either way: as house music if you don’t know, as a dare if you do.

  5. Why is it neither a joke nor a horror show?
    Answer

    Because it is played straight — well-made, unhurried house music with no wink and no scare chord.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a two-minute groove at ≈ 125 BPM whose hits and textures are audibly your chosen source — a listener should recognize what it was made of and nod along, in the same minute.
  • One source only, with a strong identity that “shouldn’t” be music — dental floss, a wet shave, chewing, a vacuum cleaner.
  • Capture close and clean; trim, chop, volume adjustment, and a seat-it-in EQ are allowed — anything that erases what the source was is not.
  • If processing hides what it was, you’ve left the lesson. That sentence is the whole rulebook.

The whole mechanism in one row: close, clean recordings of one provocative source, chopped into hits and one unmistakable loop, placed on a straight house grid, with the most identifiable sound kept up front.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the charged source

Phase focus: the reference and the charged source. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Lipostudio (And So On…)” in, and loop the grooviest minute.
  2. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. It stays one solo-press away all lesson.
  4. Choose your source: dental floss against teeth, a wet shave, deliberate chewing, a vacuum cleaner, an electric toothbrush.
  5. Strong identity, reachable today, recordable up close.
  6. Logbook Say the analogy out loud and write it down: the record’s source is surgery; mine is X; the system is the same. No pretending the stakes match — the mechanism is what you are copying.
  7. Logbook One more logbook line: why this source “shouldn’t” groove. If the line is boring, the source is wrong.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Matmos — “Lipostudio (And So On…)”, from A Chance to Cut Is a Chance to Cure (2001) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.

  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects a clean house groove whose material is audibly a human body under surgery — and the unsettling pleasure of nodding along anyway.

Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

Chop, grid, and keep the wet one up front

Phase focus: chop, grid, and keep the wet one up front. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. From RAW, slice and trim: one low thump for the kick role, one snap for the snare role, one short detail for hats, and one 1–2-bar wet loop — the most nameable stretch you captured, trimmed but otherwise untouched.
  2. Load the hits into a Drum Rack (or three Simplers); tighten each onset so it punches.
  3. Sequence two minutes at 125 BPM: four-on-the-floor from the thump, off-beat detail, snap on 2 and 4 — straight, unclever house.
  4. Open with the wet loop alone for two bars before the grid arrives.
  5. Allowed: trim, gain, velocity, a seat-it-in EQ, transposing a hit a few semitones.
  6. Forbidden: heavy reverb, extreme stretch, bit-crush — anything that erases identity.
  7. The rule again: if processing hides what it was, you’ve left the lesson.
  8. Balance so the wet loop sits clearly above the kit — recognition is a level decision too.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The recognition test

Phase focus: the recognition test. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the two minutes end to end at the lab-001 mark, no touching.
  2. Notice the first moment you stop hearing “floss” (or shave, or vacuum) and start hearing “track” — write the timestamp.
  3. A/B one minute against REF at matched loudness, judging two things only: is your groove as straight-faced, and is your wettest sound as exposed?
  4. Play it for someone, or for yourself tomorrow morning, with exactly two questions: what is this made of, and did you nod.
  5. Both answers go in the logbook verbatim.
  6. Mark where the flicker of “should I be enjoying this?” happened.
  7. If it never happened, note that too — it is the most useful miss you can record.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does a listener recognize the source and nod along?
    Answer

    Both must happen in the same listening, within the same minute. That combination is the whole composition. One without the other means you built either a documentary (named, not nodded) or an anonymous house track (nodded, not named). Each has its phase-3 fix. Listen to the record with the same question: it passes effortlessly, because the most identifiable loop re-announces itself every bar while the grid never hesitates.

  • Where exactly does the discomfort/delight line sit?
    Answer

    Usually at the moment of naming: the groove is accepted first, then the source lands a beat later, and the uncomfortable pleasure lives in that gap. Too early and the provocation crowds out the groove; too late and the piece is anonymous until the end. The record holds that gap open for its full length — recognition keeps arriving without ever fully settling. Find the timestamp in yours, decide which way it leans, and write one line on what would shift it.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: Lab 006 taught that a capture is raw material, not a subject you owe loyalty to. This record shows that keeping the source identifiable is itself a choice, and a deliberate one. Identity is a setting you choose at capture time and defend through the edit.
