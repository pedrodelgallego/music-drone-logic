Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: capture as provocation

Copy one clear section of Matmos — “Lipostudio (And So On…)”, from A Chance to Cut Is a Chance to Cure. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: close, clean captures of liposuction recordings, chopped into hits and loops and sequenced on a strict grid, with the wettest, most identifiable sounds kept up front. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone for the capture — or a mic into M4 input 1, gain by the lab-001 ledger; DT 990 Pro for the edit. The lab-006 capture log (place · distance · level) rides along
  • New set saved as REP-LIPOSTUDIO, tempo 125 BPM
  • Safety is mostly social here: record your own body or your own appliances, nobody else’s without asking — and phones at the lab-001 mark; close captures of small sounds invite the volume knob up, and the knob stays put
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — one close-capture session, one chop-and-sequence session, then file it and stop
  • What you will make: a short replica of Matmos — “Lipostudio (And So On…)”, from A Chance to Cut Is a Chance to Cure. Focus on this effect: an elegant house groove whose material is audibly a human body under surgery - and the queasy pleasure of nodding along anyway.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: close, clean captures of liposuction recordings, chopped into hits and loops and sequenced on a strict grid, with the wettest, most identifiable sounds kept up front.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Matmos — “Lipostudio (And So On…)”, from A Chance to Cut Is a Chance to Cure. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Which sounds still sound like a body?
    Answer

    The wet ones - suction and squelch read as flesh even when trimmed to the grid, while the kick-and-hat skeleton stays anonymous.

  2. Where does the groove live — in the sounds or in their placement?
    Answer

    Placement. The captures were wet, irregular, and gridless; the dance was imposed - tight trims, strict positions, disciplined levels. The material supplies character and the grid supplies the body’s nod, and neither can do the other’s job.

  3. How clean are the captures underneath the groove?
    Answer

    Very - no room mud, no handling thumps, no hiss bed. Close and clean is what lets a gross sound be elegant: detail reads as craft, and craft is the alibi that keeps the provocation listenable.

  4. What does knowing the source change?
    Answer

    Not the beat - your body keeps nodding; the knowledge changes you. The discomfort is added entirely by the label, and the track works on both sides of it: as house music for the ignorant, as a dare for the informed.

  5. Why is it neither a joke nor a horror show?
    Answer

    Because it is played straight - well-made, unhurried house music with no wink and no scare chord.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a two-minute groove at ≈ 125 BPM whose hits and textures are audibly your chosen source — a listener should recognize what it was and nod along, in the same minute.
  • One source only, with a strong identity that “shouldn’t” be music — dental floss, a wet shave, chewing, a vacuum cleaner.
  • Capture close and clean; trim, chop, gain, and a seat-it-in EQ are allowed — anything that erases identity is not.
  • If processing hides what it was, you’ve left the lesson. That sentence is the whole rulebook.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the charged source

Phase focus: the reference and the charged source. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Lipostudio (And So On…)” in, and loop the grooviest minute.
  2. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Live It stays one solo-press away all lesson.
  4. Live Choose your source: dental floss against teeth, a wet shave, deliberate chewing, a vacuum cleaner, an electric toothbrush.
  5. Live Strong identity, reachable today, recordable up close.
  6. Logbook Say the analogue out loud and write it down: the record’s source is surgery;
  7. Logbook mine is X; the system is the same. No pretending the stakes match — the mechanism is what you are copying.
  8. Logbook One more logbook line: why this source “shouldn’t” groove.
  9. Logbook If the line is boring, the source is wrong.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, a source is chosen and named, and the analogue is stated plainly on paper — provocation selected, not stumbled into.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The chosen source feels merely quirky, not chargedNo bodily or hygienic association — quirk is not provocationPick the candidate you would least like to admit to; that reluctance is the charge
You cannot choose between candidatesJudging by sound quality instead of identity strengthSay each name to an imagined listener — take the one that gets a face
Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

Capture close and clean

Phase focus: capture close and clean. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Recorder Mic or phone a hand-span from the source or closer.
  2. Recorder Set gain by the lab-001 ledger on a real performance, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — these sources spike harder than they sustain.
  3. Recorder Record 3–4 minutes of varied performances: steady runs, single events, short bursts.
  4. Recorder You are stocking a kit, not documenting a scene.
  5. Logbook Keep the lab-006 capture log: place · distance · level, one line per take.
  6. Logbook Listen back on the DT 990 before packing up — re-takes cost seconds now and are impossible later.
  7. Live Import everything into REP-LIPOSTUDIO on a track named RAW.
Expected result

Minutes of close, clean, varied captures at healthy level, logged, with the source’s identity unmistakable on playback — gross in the best way.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Room and hiss sit under every takeToo far from the source — the ratio, not the gain, is wrongHalve the distance and reset gain by the ledger; never rescue distance with the fader
Thumps and rustle ride along with the soundHandling noise — your grip is in the recordingSet the recorder down or brace your hand; the mic does not know your touch is not the subject
Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

Chop, grid, and keep the wet one up front

Phase focus: chop, grid, and keep the wet one up front. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. From RAW, slice and trim: one low thump for the kick role, one snap for the snare role, one short detail for hats, and one 1–2-bar wet loop — the most nameable stretch you captured, trimmed but otherwise untouched.
  2. Live Load the hits into a Drum Rack (or three Simplers); tighten each onset so it punches.
  3. Live Sequence two minutes at 125 BPM: four-on-the-floor from the thump, off-beat detail, snap on 2 and 4 — straight, unclever house.
  4. Live Open with the wet loop alone for two bars before the grid arrives.
  5. Live Allowed: trim, gain, velocity, a seat-it-in EQ, transposing a hit a few semitones.
  6. Live Forbidden: heavy reverb, extreme stretch, bit-crush — anything that erases identity.
  7. Live The rule again: if processing hides what it was, you’ve left the lesson.
  8. Live Balance so the wet loop sits clearly above the kit — recognition is a level decision too.
Expected result

A two-minute groove that moves like house music while the wet loop keeps announcing, every bar, exactly what everything is made of.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The groove is limpLoose onsets or timid placement — the grid is not actually strictRe-trim every hit to its transient and quantize hard; discipline first, swing later
The source is no longer nameableProcessing crept past the lineUndo back to trim-and-gain; rebuild the wet loop from the cleanest raw take
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The recognition test

Phase focus: the recognition test. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the two minutes end to end at the lab-001 mark, no touching.
  2. Logbook Notice the first moment you stop hearing “floss” (or shave, or vacuum) and start hearing “track” — write the timestamp.
  3. Live A/B one minute against REF at matched loudness, judging two things only: is your groove as straight-faced, and is your wettest sound as exposed?
  4. Live Play it for someone, or for yourself tomorrow morning, with exactly two questions: what is this made of, and did you nod.
  5. Logbook Both answers go in the logbook verbatim.
  6. Logbook Mark where the flicker of “should I be enjoying this?” happened.
  7. Logbook If it never happened, note that too — it is the most useful miss you can record.
Expected result

A groove that earns a nod, a source that gets named, and a timestamped note on where the discomfort and the delight crossed paths.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Listener names the source but does not nodYou built a documentary — the groove never took authorityBack to phase 3: stricter grid, tighter trims, louder thump; the dance must be unembarrassed
Listener nods but cannot name itYou built an anonymous house track — identity got processed awayRaise the wet loop, strip its processing, give it two bars alone at the top
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does a listener recognize the source and nod along?
    Answer

    Both must happen in the same body within the same minute — that simultaneity is the entire composition. One without the other means you built either a documentary (named, not nodded) or an anonymous house track (nodded, not named), and each has its phase-3 fix. Relisten to the record with the same test: it passes effortlessly, because the wet layer re-announces itself on every bar while the grid never blinks.

  • Where exactly does the discomfort/delight line sit?
    Answer

    Usually at the moment of naming: the groove is accepted first, the source arrives a beat later, and the queasy pleasure lives in that gap. Too early and the provocation fronts the track so hard the groove never recovers; too late and the piece is anonymous until the credits. The record holds the gap open for its whole length — recognition keeps re-arriving without ever resolving. Find the timestamp in yours, decide which way it errs, and write one line on what would move it.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: Lab 006 taught that a capture is raw material, not a subject it must stay loyal to — this record shows that loyalty is itself a choice, and what choosing it buys. Identity is a parameter you set at capture time and defend through the edit.
