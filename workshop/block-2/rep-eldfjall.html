Replicate: the planet as oscillator

Copy one clear section of Jacob Kirkegaard — Eldfjall. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: accelerometers (sensors that pick up vibration through solid objects, not through air) pressed against geothermal ground, capturing long takes until the machine sounds like a place. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordJacob Kirkegaard — Eldfjall (2005)
The effectthe earth’s vibration heard directly — drones, pulses, and granular hiss lower than most music, so similar to electronic synthesis that most listeners cannot tell them apart, shifting so slowly that changes only register over minutes
The mechanismaccelerometers and contact sensors pressed against geothermal ground — vibration captured through solid material in long takes, held long enough that the machine stops sounding like machinery and starts sounding like a place

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Gear: the Lab 008 solid path — contact mic / piezo (a small sensor you press against a surface to pick up vibration through it) with mounting putty and tape, into the handheld recorder or M4 input 1; closed phones; Live 12 for the layering; your weakest speaker (a phone) at the end for the monitoring check
  • You are replicating the capture system — sensors pressed against a vibrating solid, held for a long time — on whatever vibrating surfaces you have access to. Your building’s plumbing and machines are its version of geothermal ground. The method is identical.
  • Hot surfaces stay hot: press the piezo against the boiler casing or a pipe where your hand can rest comfortably. Never press it against anything that would burn you. Do not open or probe anything — surfaces only, per the field checklist.
  • Sustained low rumble can tire your ears without feeling loud. Keep phones at the lab-001 mark or lower, and take a real break every 15 minutes.
  • New set saved as REP-KIRKEGAARD — timebox: 90–120 minutes, most of it spent letting takes run
  • What you will make: a short replica of Jacob Kirkegaard — Eldfjall. Focus on this effect: geology heard directly - drones, pulses, and granular hiss below where most music lives, indistinguishable from synthesis.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: accelerometers and contact sensors on geothermal ground - solid-borne vibration captured in long takes, held until machine reads as place.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Jacob Kirkegaard — Eldfjall. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Heard blind, would you call this electronic music?
    Answer

    Almost certainly — drones, granular textures, slow filter-like swells. All the signatures of synthesis, but made by steam moving through rock.

  2. Do events arrive suddenly, or grow?
    Answer

    They grow. Pressure builds, a rumble swells, a texture thickens. The earth does not jump. Growth is the medium’s signature, and the easiest thing to fake badly.

  3. Where is the air?
    Answer

    Nowhere. No wind, no birds, no room sound — the sensors pick up only what travels through solid material, below anything that travels through air.

  4. How long does one texture hold?
    Answer

    Minutes — much longer than a typical arrangement would stay on one idea. That length does compositional work: held long enough, a vibration stops feeling like an event and starts feeling like the place you are standing in.

  5. What is the lowest thing you can hear — and is it heard or felt?
    Answer

    Felt first. The record’s lowest frequencies register in the chest before they register as sound. The granular hiss sits above them as the part you can actually hear.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a three-minute piece layered from two or three long contact-mic takes of the building’s machinery, where the result sounds like a place, not a named appliance. Low end intact, changes that happen over minutes, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS. The measure is how close you get to the record’s patience.
  • Solid-borne material only — no air microphones, nothing recorded from the room’s atmosphere.
  • Takes run long — five or more minutes each — and nothing in the final piece jumps or switches suddenly.
  • The low end stays in. The piece must also survive a check on the weakest speaker you own at the end.

The piece in one row: find the building’s active machines by touching them, press the piezo against two or three of them for long takes, then layer them into a three-minute piece that only moves slowly — and check it last on the weakest speaker you own.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the survey by hand

Phase focus: the reference and the survey by hand. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. In Live: ⌘ T, rename REF, drag in a track from Eldfjall, loop three minutes, trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Walk the home with flat hands: the boiler’s casing, radiator pipes near the boiler, the wall behind the washing machine mid-cycle, the floor beside the fridge, any ventilation duct.
  3. Ten seconds of palm per candidate.
  4. Log each candidate with a felt description — “steady tremor,” “slow pulse,” “cycles on and off” — and note which machines run on schedules you can summon (a wash cycle, the heating).
  5. Choose three sources with different characters: one continuous (fridge or boiler), one cyclic (washing machine, heating firing), one resonant (a long pipe run).
  6. Schedule the session so all three are alive — heating on, a cycle running.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Jacob Kirkegaard — Eldfjall (2005) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.

Phase 2 · ≈ 35 min

Long takes — let the building perform

Phase focus: long takes — let the building perform. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this

  2. Phones on, listen for a full minute before setting gain. Wait for the loudest event — the compressor kick, the heating firing, the pipe’s hardest knock.
  3. Set for that peak, ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Record 5+ minutes per source, hands off, you elsewhere in the room.
  5. Log on/off cycles and anything the machine did that surprised you.
  6. Repeat for the other two sources — fresh mount, fresh minute of watching, fresh gain, logged per source.
  7. Label the takes by organ: BOILER, PIPE, FRIDGE-FLOOR.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Layer the geology — three minutes at minute speed

Phase focus: layer the geology — three minutes at minute speed. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the takes into REP-KIRKEGAARD, one track each.
  2. Audition and keep the best two or three — one continuous floor, one cyclic, one resonant on top.
  3. Arrange three minutes: the floor runs throughout; the others enter and recede by fades spread over at least thirty seconds.
  4. If a listener can point to the exact moment a change happened, it was too fast.
  5. Clip gain and fades only — no EQ, and no high-pass “for safety”: the low end is the subject, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS on the sum.
  6. Keep the hiss and crackle. That high-frequency texture riding on top of the low end is the part small speakers can reproduce. Do not clean it off.
  7. Listen end to end at the mark, then take the scheduled break before judging anything.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The place test and the weakest speaker

Phase focus: the place test and the weakest speaker. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this

  2. Ask one question only: place, or machine?
  3. Log the one-word answer honestly.
  4. A/B one minute against REF at matched loudness — judge growth speed and how long textures hold, nothing else.
  5. Bounce the piece and play it on your weakest speaker — the phone, on the desk, arm’s length.
  6. Log what the low end does: vanished, ghosted, or distorted.
  7. Listen to what remains on the phone: the granular skin and the mid texture must still be a piece.
  8. Write one line on what the phone kept.
  9. Take the break — sustained low rumble has been loading your ears for an hour without announcing it.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does it read as place rather than machine?
    Answer

    It should — and duration is what makes the difference. A fridge heard for ten seconds is a fridge. Held for minutes, felt through the solid, it becomes somewhere you are standing in. Relisten to how long Kirkegaard holds one texture before anything changes — longer than you expect, every time. If yours still reads as machine, you moved on before the label wore off. The fix is patience, not processing.

  • What does the low end do on your weakest speaker?
    Answer

    Mostly vanish — a phone’s small speaker cannot reproduce the very low frequencies your felt floor lives on, so the piece you heard on headphones is not the piece the phone plays. This is the Block 1 monitoring lesson with real stakes: a piece built this low must carry audible texture above it — hiss, grain, mid-range detail — that survives when the low end disappears. Play the bounce on the phone and listen to what remains. If it is still music, the piece works on both systems.
