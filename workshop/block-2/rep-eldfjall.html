Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the planet as oscillator

Copy one clear section of Jacob Kirkegaard — Eldfjall. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: accelerometers and contact sensors on geothermal ground - solid-borne vibration captured in long takes, held until machine reads as place. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: the Lab 008 solid path — contact mic / piezo with mounting putty and tape, into the handheld recorder or M4 input 1; closed phones; Live 12 for the layering; your weakest speaker (a phone) waits at the end for the monitoring check
  • Say it plainly: you are replicating the capture system — sensors coupled to a vibrating solid mass, held for a long time — on the nearest tectonics you own. The building’s plumbing and machines are its geothermal field; the analogy is honest and the method is identical
  • Hot surfaces stay hot: putty the piezo to the boiler’s casing or the pipe where your hand can rest comfortably, never to anything that would burn it. Nothing gets opened, nothing gets probed — surfaces only, per the field checklist
  • Sustained low rumble loads the ear without announcing itself — phones at the lab-001 mark or lower, a real break every 15 minutes
  • New set saved as REP-KIRKEGAARD — timebox: 90–120 minutes, most of it spent letting takes run
  • What you will make: a short replica of Jacob Kirkegaard — Eldfjall. Focus on this effect: geology heard directly - drones, pulses, and granular hiss below where most music lives, indistinguishable from synthesis.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: accelerometers and contact sensors on geothermal ground - solid-borne vibration captured in long takes, held until machine reads as place.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Jacob Kirkegaard — Eldfjall. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Heard blind, would you call this electronic music?
    Answer

    Almost certainly - drones, granular textures, slow filter-like swells, every signature of synthesis, made by steam in rock.

  2. Do events arrive suddenly, or grow?
    Answer

    They grow. Pressure builds, a rumble swells, a texture thickens - geology does not jump, and a synth would. Growth is the medium’s signature and the easiest thing to fake badly.

  3. Where is the air?
    Answer

    Nowhere. No wind, no birds, no space - accelerometers hear the solid world only, below everything airborne.

  4. How long does one texture hold?
    Answer

    Minutes - long past where an arranger would have moved on. Duration is doing compositional work: held long enough, a vibration stops being an event and becomes a location you are standing in.

  5. What is the lowest thing you can hear — and is it heard or felt?
    Answer

    Felt first. The record’s floor sits at the bottom of the spectrum, registering in the chest before it reads as sound, with the granular hiss riding above it as the audible skin.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a three-minute piece layered from two or three long contact takes of the building’s machinery that reads as place, not appliance — low end intact, changes at geology’s tempo, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS. Closeness to the record’s patience is the measure.
  • Solid-borne material only — no air mics, nothing the room contributed.
  • Takes run long — five-plus minutes each — and the piece moves at minute speed; nothing jumps.
  • The low end stays in, and the piece must survive the weakest-speaker check at the end.

The piece’s machinery in one row: the building’s deep mechanical life found by hand, a piezo putty-coupled to two or three of its organs for long takes, and a three-minute layered piece whose only motion is slow growth, checked last on the weakest speaker you own.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the survey by hand

Phase focus: the reference and the survey by hand. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live In Live: ⌘ T, rename REF, drag in a track from Eldfjall, loop three minutes, trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Live Walk the home with flat hands: the boiler’s casing, radiator pipes near the boiler, the wall behind the washing machine mid-cycle, the floor beside the fridge, any ventilation duct.
  3. Live Ten seconds of palm per candidate.
  4. Live Log each candidate with a felt description — “steady tremor,” “slow pulse,” “cycles on and off” — and note which machines run on schedules you can summon (a wash cycle, the heating).
  5. Live Choose three sources with different characters: one continuous (fridge or boiler), one cyclic (washing machine, heating firing), one resonant (a long pipe run).
  6. Live Schedule the session so all three are alive — heating on, a cycle running.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS and the logbook maps the building’s underworld — three chosen organs with different tempers, all guaranteed to be running at session time.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Nothing in the building feels alive under the palmQuiet hour — compressors idle, heating offSummon the life: start a wash, run the heating, boil the kettle on its base; machines keep schedules, use them
Everything feels like the same faint mains tremorSurveying walls instead of organs — the hum travels everywhere, the character lives at the sourceMove the palm to the machine casings and the first meter of their pipes
Phase 2 · ≈ 35 min

Long takes — let the building perform

Phase focus: long takes — let the building perform. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Recorder Putty the piezo to the first source — full flat contact, cable taped for strain relief a hand’s width away — on a surface your bare hand can rest on comfortably.
  2. Recorder Phones on, watch a full minute before setting gain: catch the compressor kick, the heating firing, the pipe’s loudest knock.
  3. Recorder Set for that peak, ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Recorder Record 5+ minutes per source, hands off, you elsewhere in the room.
  5. Recorder Log on/off cycles and anything the machine did that surprised you.
  6. Recorder Repeat for the other two sources — fresh mount, fresh minute of watching, fresh gain, logged per source.
  7. Recorder Label the takes by organ: BOILER, PIPE, FRIDGE-FLOOR.
Expected result

Three long takes, each with its source’s full cycle inside it — kicks, swells, breathing — captured without a single handling thump, gains logged per organ.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The compressor kick clips mid-takeGain set on the idle hum — the first-impression mistakeRe-rehearse with the full minute’s watch; set for the kick and let the idle sit low — that distance is real
The take is one unchanging drone with no lifeTake too short, or the machine’s cycle never arrived inside itRun longer — ten minutes — or summon the cycle; the slow events are the material, wait for them
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Layer the geology — three minutes at minute speed

Phase focus: layer the geology — three minutes at minute speed. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the takes into REP-KIRKEGAARD, one track each.
  2. Audition and keep the best two or three — one continuous floor, one cyclic, one resonant on top.
  3. Arrange three minutes: the floor runs throughout; the others enter and recede by fades spread over at least thirty seconds.
  4. If a move can be pointed at, it was too fast.
  5. Live Clip gain and fades only — no EQ, and no high-pass “for safety”: the low end is the subject, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS on the sum.
  6. Live Keep the grain: the hiss and crackle riding on each take is the audible skin of the low end — do not clean it off.
  7. Live Listen end to end at the mark, then take the scheduled break before judging anything.
Expected result

A three-minute piece — a felt floor, slow swells, granular skin — whose events grow rather than arrive, built from nothing but the building.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Two low layers beat unpleasantly against each otherClose fundamentals interfering — two compressors at neighboring speedsDrop one layer, or treat the beating as material and slow its exposure; if it stays ugly, swap a take
The piece reads as “appliance noise,” immediately nameableThe cyclic layer’s on/off transitions are doing the talkingFade across the cycle boundaries so the kicks emerge from the floor instead of switching on
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The place test and the weakest speaker

Phase focus: the place test and the weakest speaker. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the three minutes end to end on the DT 990 Pro at the mark, no touching.
  2. Ask one question only: place, or machine?
  3. Log the one-word answer honestly.
  4. Live A/B one minute against REF at matched loudness — judge growth speed and how long textures hold, nothing else.
  5. Recorder Bounce the piece and play it on your weakest speaker — the phone, on the desk, arm’s length.
  6. Recorder Log what the low end does: vanished, ghosted, or distorted.
  7. Recorder Listen to what remains on the phone: the granular skin and the mid texture must still be a piece.
  8. Recorder + Logbook Write one line on what the phone kept.
  9. Recorder + Logbook Take the break — sustained low rumble has been loading your ears for an hour without announcing it.
Expected result

A logged verdict — place — an A/B that comes down to patience, and a phone-speaker line proving the piece has an audible skin above its felt floor.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The verdict keeps coming back “machine”Takes too short or moves too fast — the name never wore offStretch the fades and let textures hold longer; duration, not disguise, removes the label
On the phone, nothing is left at allThe piece was only fundamentals — no grain survived the low end’s lossBring the takes’ hiss and crackle forward in the balance; the skin is what travels to small speakers
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does it read as place rather than machine?
    Answer

    It should — and duration is what does it. A fridge heard for ten seconds is a fridge; held for minutes, coupled through the solid, it becomes somewhere you are standing. Relisten to how long Kirkegaard holds a single texture before anything changes — longer than feels reasonable, every time — and compare your own holds. If yours reads as machine, the label never wore off because you moved on before it could; the fix is patience, not processing.

  • What does the low end do on your weakest speaker?
    Answer

    Mostly vanish — the phone’s driver cannot move the air your felt floor needs, so the piece you mixed is not the piece the phone plays. This is the Block 1 monitoring lesson returned with stakes: a piece that lives this low must carry an audible skin — grain, hiss, mid texture — that survives the loss. Play the bounce on the phone once more and listen only to what remains; if it is still music, the piece is honest on both systems, which is what “low end intact” actually has to mean.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 008 — the solid path plus duration as a decision. The lab certifies clean capture; this record adds the second clause: hold the capture long enough and the source stops being a thing and becomes a place.
