Concrète & Field — four ways to capture a world

Three listening sessions focused on what happens before any processing: where the microphone stands, what enters the recording along with the sound, and what each recordist chose to include. Everything you capture in this block builds on what you hear here.

Listening programme · 3 sessions · one full cycle · feeds labs 005–008 and capstone C2 · scaffold

The point

A recorded sound is never just the sound. It also carries a place, a distance, a noise floor (background hiss or room sound), and whatever the recordist did with the gain (volume). These sessions train you to hear all four, because in Block 2 you become the person making those decisions.

Listening is the loop

These sessions are not separate from the labs — they are the first step. Every capture decision you make in this block should trace back to something you heard here. The questions below are the same five System Spec questions the labs use, plus a sixth.

New concepts — in plain words

Four terms run through this page. Here is what each one means before you encounter it.

ConceptIn plain words
Capture vs synthesis A synthesized sound is made from electricity alone — no microphone, no room. A captured (recorded) sound arrives with a world attached: a room, a distance, background noise, the traffic two streets over. Capture means accepting some of that world into your recording. The skill is deciding how much.
Reduced listening Hearing a sound as pure sound — its shape, texture, and weight — rather than as the thing that made it. A door slam stops being "a door" and becomes "a short dark thump with a bright edge." Pierre Schaeffer called this approach reduced listening. It is a skill you practice, not a fact you memorize.
Perspective Where the microphone stands is a creative decision. Close means intimate, dry, and detailed. Far means roomy and blended with the surroundings. Two recordings of the same event from two different distances are two different pieces of material — not two takes of the same thing.
The capture chain Mic → preamp → recorder. Every link in this chain shapes the final sound. The mic decides what vibrations are picked up at all. The preamp (a small amplifier) sets how loud and how noisy. The recorder decides what survives. This chain is already shaping your sound before you open the DAW.

How to listen — the six-question frame

Answer these six questions in your logbook for every work in this programme. A few lines per question is fine. The only wrong answer is a blank one.

  1. Inputs — what raw material enters the system, and from where was it captured?
  2. Transforms — what changes it, in what order?
  3. Feedback / variation — what keeps it alive over time?
  4. Constraints — what prevents clutter?
  5. Human role — what is performed, decided, curated?
  6. Likely audio path — what had to be routed, recorded, mixed, or performed for this to exist — and what capture chain stood between the world and the tape?

Use three passes per work. First pass: just listen — no notes, no pausing. Second pass: answer questions 1–5. Third pass: question 6 only. The sixth question is the hardest in this block. You are guessing at microphones, positions, and gain knobs you cannot see. Guessing is the exercise.

Contemplative pass — sit with one work

Once per programme, choose one work — preferably a long one — and listen to it for about twenty unbroken minutes with no notebook and no analysis. Do not track the sound; track yourself. What happened to your attention, your breathing, your sense of time? Where did boredom shift into something else, or not? Afterwards, write a few lines about what changed in you, not in the music. The analysis passes study the system. This pass studies what the system does to a listener — which is how your own work will be judged.

Context prompt — who made this, and how

For each work, answer in a line or two: who made this, and for whom? What resources did they have — which institution, studio, instruments, or funding? Whose land or tradition did the material come from? Recording technology is not neutral. A Paris studio, a field expedition, and a village ensemble do not arrive at the microphone on equal terms. Understanding how a recording was made is part of understanding what you are hearing.

Score prompt — what would this look like written down

For each work, sketch in words or a rough drawing what a score of it might look like. A timeline? A shape? A set of instructions, a map? Then ask: what does writing it down force you to decide, and what does it lose? Some of these works came from written notation; others deliberately reject it. Knowing which — and why — sharpens what you choose to record in your own logbook.

Session 1 — Records: four capture philosophies

These four records each answer the same question: what does it mean to bring the world into a piece of music? Listen to each using the six-question frame, then compare the approaches. The differences are the lesson. Any single record alone only teaches its own surface.

01 Pierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer” the founding capture

Train recordings made in 1948, edited into rhythm and form. This is the founding document of musique concrète (music built from recorded real-world sounds): the first time a recording was treated as raw material rather than a document of an event. Everything in this block traces back to the decisions in these three minutes.

Listen for
  • The moment a train stops sounding like a train and becomes a pattern — repetition strips the cause away
  • Which sounds keep their identity throughout, and which dissolve into pure rhythm
  • The edits: where one recording ends and another begins
After — verify

Can you name one sound you stopped hearing as its source? If every sound stayed "a train" the whole way through, try reduced listening: relisten to any repeated section and count the repetitions until the cause drops away.

02 Hildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk” the microphone as narrator

She describes what the microphone is doing while it does it — the gain moves, the distance, the city humming behind the barnacles. This is a rare piece where the recording process itself is the subject, narrated out loud as it happens.

Listen for
  • The zoom — from the wide soundscape down to the tiny detail of the barnacles
  • The city behind everything: how present it is at the start, and what happens to it later
  • The gain moves she names out loud, and the ones she doesn't name
After — verify

How did she make a tiny sound audible? She turned the city down. The gain (volume control) is her authorship. If you heard the barnacles get louder rather than the world get quieter, relisten to that passage and follow the background level instead of the foreground.

03 Chris Watson — El Tren Fantasma the field composed

Real field recordings edited into a train journey across Mexico that no single day actually contained. Watson's work has two parts: the skill and patience of capturing each location, and then the editing that joins separate places into one continuous narrative. The recordings are real; the journey is invented.

Listen for
  • The seams — where two places or two times are joined into one apparent moment
  • How close the microphone gets to machinery no person could safely stand beside
  • Which sounds feel like documents of a real moment, and which feel shaped for the listener's ear
After — verify

Did it feel documentary or composed? The honest answer is both. Name one seam you caught — one join between two different recordings. If you caught none, relisten to a transition between sections and ask whether the room sound before and after could be the same place.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Replicate it — step-by-step lesson →

04 Éliane Radigue — “Kyema” capture’s opposite pole

Almost nothing here is a "recorded world." This is an ARP 2500 synthesizer changing so slowly it breathes like weather. It sits in this programme as the opposite extreme: what does "source material" mean when nothing was ever in front of a microphone? Radigue's answer is that the tone's own character — its instability, texture, and slow drift — is the entry condition, approached with the same patience a field recordist brings to a location.

Listen for
  • How long it takes before you are certain anything has changed — then how far it has moved when you check back
  • The texture and instability inside the tone: the synthesizer's own noise and drift
  • What "source" means when no microphone was ever involved
After — verify

How is this still about sources? The choice of oscillator, its drift, and its noise are decisions of the same kind as where Westerkamp points her microphone. The tone's character is the source. If the piece felt like "nothing happening," relisten to any five minutes at low volume and track only the brightness.

Session 2 — Samples: captured vs processed

Session 2 · Deconstruction

Split one concrète work into two columns

Pick one concrète work — one of the Session 1 records, or any other work built from recordings. Loop a short passage and sort everything you hear into two columns: what was captured (the sound as the microphone took it — source, distance, environment) and what was processed (what was done afterwards — cuts, speed changes, layering, filtering, level). For each element, guess the capture chain and the processing chain in order. End with the sixth question for the whole passage: what recording path had to exist for this to exist?

Worksheet — one row per audible element
Element (e.g. train, gull, hiss bed)—
Captured: likely source, distance, environment—
Processed: likely chain, in order—
Confidencelow / mid / high
Note

You will be wrong — and being wrong in writing is the point. The hardest call in this block is deciding which column a quality belongs to. Is that sense of distance from the recording itself, or was reverb added later? A written guess can be corrected. A vague impression cannot.

This worksheet is a blueprint

In replication lab R2 you will re-make this passage using your own recordings pushed through the chain you analyzed. That is how you will find out whether each guess was right. Write each row clearly enough that someone could record and build from it. That someone is you.

After — verify

Every row needs something in both columns. A capture guess with no processing guess, or a processing guess with no source guess, is half a row. If a row resists the split entirely, mark it low confidence. R2 will test that row first. Full worksheet template forthcoming — the columns above are enough to run the session now.

Session 3 — Video: the hands in the field

Session 3 · Video

Field-recording documentary footage, watched twice

Find documentary footage of a field recordist at work — any era, any region. Consider someone outside the obvious Western canon. Watch it twice: once as a viewer, then again watching only the hands, the headphones, and the gain knob. The links below are searches, not curated picks. Choose footage where you can actually see the equipment being used.

Search: Chris Watson field recording documentarywatch for mic placement, waiting, and headphone monitoring
Search: Hildegard Westerkamp soundwalk interviewwatch for listening as a practice separate from recording
Search: field recording with contact microphoneswatch for handling noise and how surfaces are touched
  1. Monitoring — what is on the recordist’s headphones, and when do they adjust them?
  2. Hands and gain — where are the hands? When does gain move, and why then?
  3. Waiting — how much of the work is standing still, and what are they waiting for?
  4. Loss — what is unrecoverable: which sounds, once missed or clipped in the field, can never be taken again?
Watch for the unrecoverable

In the studio, most mistakes are fixable. In the field, most are not. Count the moments in the footage where a wrong gain setting or a slow hand on the record button would have lost the take for good. That count is why lab 008 exists.

After — verify

Can you describe what the recordist was listening to on headphones without rewatching? What was the longest wait you saw between arriving and pressing record? If you can't answer either, rewatch ten minutes — watching only the hands and the headphones.

Deliverable — the stolen principle

File all worksheets in the logbook. Then write down one stolen principle — a structural idea, not a sound or a style — before you use it in any build lab. It will be applied and judged at capstone C2. Naming it in advance makes it a commitment rather than a story you invent afterwards.

Pass standard
Minimum passThree sessions filed, six-question notes exist, the captured-vs-processed worksheet exists, and one stolen principle is named.
Solid passThe stolen principle is structural enough that another person could build from it without copying any sound.
Advanced transferYou can hear the same principle operating in a studio-synthesized work with no captures at all.
  • Valid: “the mic’s position is the melody” — perspective as the moving element
  • Valid: “turn the world down to make the detail loud” — gain as authorship, stolen from Westerkamp’s move, not her beach
  • Invalid: “train sounds like Schaeffer’s” — that is material, not structure
Programme L2 — pass gate
  • Four record analyses — six questions each, three passes per record
  • One deconstruction worksheet — captured vs processed, one row per element, confidence marked
  • One video worksheet — monitoring, hands and gain, waiting, the unrecoverable
  • One stolen principle — structural, named in advance, evaluated at C2
