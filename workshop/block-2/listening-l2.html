Block 2 · Programme L2 · Listening

Concrète & Field — four ways to capture a world

Three listening sessions about what happens before any processing: where the microphone stands, what enters with the sound, and what each recordist decides the world is. Everything you capture in this block starts from what you hear here.

Listening programme · 3 sessions · one full cycle · feeds labs 005–008 and capstone C2 · scaffold

The point

A recorded sound is never just the sound — it is the sound plus a place, a distance, a noise floor, and a hand on the gain. These sessions teach you to hear all four, because in Block 2 you become the person making those decisions.

Listening is the loop

These sessions are not homework beside the labs — they are the first step of the operating loop. Every capture decision you make in this block is stolen from here, and the questions below are the same five System Spec questions the labs use, plus a sixth.

New concepts — in plain words

Four terms carry this page. Here is what each one means, before any of them is used.

ConceptIn plain words
Capture vs synthesis A synthesized sound is born clean, alone, from electricity. A recorded sound arrives with a world attached: a room, a distance, a noise floor, the traffic two streets over. Capture means accepting some of that world into your system — the skill is deciding how much.
Reduced listening Hearing a sound as sound — its shape, its grain, its weight — instead of as its cause. A door slam stops being “a door” and becomes “a short dark thump with a bright edge”. This is Schaeffer’s founding move, and it is a skill you practice, not a fact you learn.
Perspective Where the microphone stands is a compositional decision. Close means intimate, dry, and detailed; far means roomy, blended, and contextual. Two recordings of the same event from two positions are two different pieces of material, not two takes of one.
The capture chain Mic type → preamp → recorder. Every stage shapes what “the world” ends up meaning: the mic decides what kind of vibration is heard at all, the preamp decides how loud and how noisy, the recorder decides what survives. The chain is part of the sound before you press a single button in the DAW.

How to listen — the six-question frame

Every work in this programme gets the same six questions, answered in writing in your logbook. A few lines per question is enough; vagueness is the only wrong answer.

  1. Inputs — what raw material enters the system, and from where was it captured?
  2. Transforms — what changes it, in what order?
  3. Feedback / variation — what keeps it alive over time?
  4. Constraints — what prevents clutter?
  5. Human role — what is performed, decided, curated?
  6. Likely audio path — what had to be routed, recorded, mixed, or performed for this to exist — and what capture chain stood between the world and the tape?

The method is three passes per work. First pass: just listen. No notes, no pausing. Second pass: answer questions 1–5. Third pass: question 6 only. The sixth is the engineering pass and the hardest in this block — you are guessing at microphones, positions, and hands on gain knobs you cannot see. Guessing is the exercise.

Session 1 — Records: four capture philosophies

Four answers to the same question — what does it mean to bring the world into a piece of music? Listen to each with the six-question frame, then compare the philosophies. The differences are the lesson; any one record alone would only teach its own surface.

01 Pierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer” the founding capture

Trains recorded in 1948 become rhythm and form. This is the founding document of musique concrète: the first time a recorded sound was treated as material to compose with rather than a document of an event. The whole block descends from the three minutes of decisions in this étude.

Listen for
  • The moment a train stops being a train and becomes a pattern — when repetition strips the cause away
  • Which sounds keep their identity throughout, and which dissolve into pure rhythm
  • The cuts: where one recording ends and another begins
After — verify

Can you name one sound you stopped hearing as its source? If every sound stayed “a train” the whole way through, the reduced-listening switch hasn’t flipped yet — relisten to any repeated section and count the repetitions until the cause falls away.

02 Hildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk” the microphone as narrator

She tells you what the microphone is doing while it does it — the gain moves, the distance, the city humming behind the barnacles. It is a recording about recording: the rare piece where the capture chain itself is the subject, narrated in plain words as it operates.

Listen for
  • The zoom — from the wide soundscape down to the tiny detail of the barnacles
  • The city behind everything: how present it is at the start, and what happens to it
  • The gain moves she names out loud, and the ones she doesn’t
After — verify

What did she do to make the tiny audible? She turned the city down — gain as authorship. If you heard the barnacles get louder rather than the world getting quieter, relisten to that passage and follow the background instead of the foreground.

03 Chris Watson — El Tren Fantasma the field composed

Field recordings arranged into a train journey across Mexico that no single day contains. Watson’s craft is double: the patience and technique of the capture, and then the editorial hand that joins places into a narrative. The field is real; the journey is composed.

Listen for
  • The cuts — the seams where two places, or two times, are joined into one apparent moment
  • How close the microphone gets to machinery a person could not stand next to
  • Which sounds feel documentary and which feel staged for the ear
After — verify

Did it feel documentary or composed? The honest answer is both — name one seam you caught, one join between recordings. If you caught none, relisten to a transition between sections and ask whether the acoustics before and after could be the same place.

04 Éliane Radigue — “Kyema” capture’s opposite pole

Almost nothing here is “recorded world” — an ARP 2500 synthesizer breathing at the speed of weather. It sits in this programme as the boundary case: what does capture mean when the source is electricity? Radigue’s answer is that the tone’s character — its instability, its grain, its slow interior life — is itself the entry condition, attended to with a field recordist’s patience.

Listen for
  • How long it takes to be sure anything has changed — then how far it has traveled when you check back
  • The grain and instability inside the tone: the synthesizer’s own noise floor and drift
  • What a “source” means when nothing was ever in front of a microphone
After — verify

How is this still about sources? Because the tone’s character IS the entry condition — the choice of oscillator, its drift, its noise are decisions of the same kind as where Westerkamp points her microphone. If the piece felt like “nothing happening”, relisten to any five minutes at low volume and track only the brightness.

Session 2 — Samples: captured vs processed

Session 2 · Deconstruction

Split one concrète work into two columns

Pick one concrète work — one of the Session 1 records, or another work built from captures. Loop a short passage and split everything you hear into two columns: what was captured (the sound as the microphone took it: source, distance, environment) and what was processed (what was done afterwards: cuts, speed, layering, filtering, level). For each element, guess the capture chain and the processing chain, in order. Finish with the sixth question for the whole passage: what path had to exist for this to exist?

Worksheet — one row per audible element
Element (e.g. train, gull, hiss bed)—
Captured: likely source, distance, environment—
Processed: likely chain, in order—
Confidencelow / mid / high
Note

You will be wrong, and being wrong in writing is the point. The hardest call in this block is deciding which column a quality belongs to — is that distance real, or is it reverb? A guess on paper can be corrected; an impression cannot.

This worksheet is a blueprint

In replication lab R2 you will re-make this passage with your own captures pushed through the chain you analyzed — and find out, empirically, which column each guess belonged in. Write each row as if someone will have to record and build from it. Someone will: you.

After — verify

Every row should have something in both columns — a capture with no processing guess, or a process with no source guess, is half a row. If a row resists the split entirely, mark it low confidence; R2 will test exactly that row first. Session 3 — Video: the hands in the field

Session 3 · Video

Field-recording documentary footage, watched twice

Find documentary footage of a field recordist working — any era, any region; the canon-breadth rule applies, so consider a recordist outside the obvious canon for this slot. Watch it twice: once as an audience member, then again watching only the hands, the headphones, and the gain. The suggestions below are searches, not curated picks — choose footage where you can actually see the rig being operated.

Search: Chris Watson field recording documentarywatch for mic placement, waiting, and headphone monitoring
Search: Hildegard Westerkamp soundwalk interviewwatch for listening as a practice separate from recording
Search: field recording with contact microphoneswatch for handling noise and how surfaces are touched
  1. Monitoring — what is on the recordist’s headphones, and when do they adjust them?
  2. Hands and gain — where are the hands? When does gain move, and why then?
  3. Waiting — how much of the work is standing still, and what are they waiting for?
  4. Loss — what is unrecoverable: which sounds, once missed or clipped in the field, can never be taken again?
Watch for the unrecoverable

The studio forgives almost everything; the field forgives almost nothing. Count the moments where a wrong gain setting or a late record button would have lost the take forever — that count is why lab 008 exists.

After — verify

Can you describe what the recordist was monitoring without rewatching? What was the longest wait you saw between arriving and recording? If you can’t answer either, rewatch ten minutes — watching only the hands and the headphones.

Deliverable — the stolen principle

File all worksheets in the logbook. Then write down one stolen principle — a structural idea, never material — before you build anything with it. It will be applied and judged at capstone C2. Naming it in advance makes it a commitment instead of a story you tell afterwards.

Pass standard
Minimum passThree sessions filed, six-question notes exist, the captured-vs-processed worksheet exists, and one stolen principle is named.
Solid passThe stolen principle is structural enough that another person could build from it without copying any sound.
Advanced transferYou can hear the same principle operating in a studio-synthesized work with no captures at all.
  • Valid: “the mic’s position is the melody” — perspective as the moving element
  • Valid: “turn the world down to make the detail loud” — gain as authorship, stolen from Westerkamp’s move, not her beach
  • Invalid: “train sounds like Schaeffer’s” — that is material, not structure
