Replicate: haze as a chosen depth

Copy one clear section of Actress — “Hubble”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: resample blur (recording your own sound back in to degrade it) applied in deliberate layers — copies stacked until the surface is out of focus, with the timing left clean underneath. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordActress — “Hubble” (2010, Splazsh)
The effecta simple loop heard as if through atmosphere — every edge smeared at a consistent depth while the pulse underneath stays precise; haze that reads as a decision, not a bad recording
The mechanismresample blur applied in deliberate layers — copies stacked until the surface is out of focus, with the timing left clean underneath

Before you start
  • Gear: all three Lab 007 paths ready — the Digitakt II’s internal resample, the USB bounce through Live, and the analog loop out through M4 3/4 and back in — plus the MicroFreak on M4 input 1 and the DT 990 Pro in the phones jack
  • New set saved as REP-HUBBLE, tempo 130 BPM — Splazsh territory
  • The analog leg is one monitor switch from feedback: Digitakt II input monitoring off while the return is patched, phones at the lab-001 mark, one hand near the input level on every first pass
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop — three paths times careful passes eats the clock; the arrangement at the end is deliberately small
  • What you will make: a short replica of Actress — “Hubble”. Focus on this effect: a simple loop heard as if through atmosphere - every edge smeared at a consistent depth while the pulse underneath stays precise.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: resample blur applied in deliberate layers - generations of copying stacked until the surface is out of focus, with the timing left clean underneath.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Actress — “Hubble”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Is this a bad recording or a decision?
    Answer

    A decision - and the proof is consistency. An accident would be uneven: some elements degraded, others escaping. This haze coats everything at the same depth, start to finish, like one lens setting held for the whole take.

  2. What stays solid underneath the blur?
    Answer

    The pulse. Tap along: the timing is clean even where the timbre is fog. Blur lives in the spectrum - softened attacks, smeared edges - while the grid underneath keeps machine time.

  3. Does anything attack cleanly, anywhere?
    Answer

    Almost nothing. Every element arrives slightly out of focus - the transients rounded the way several generations of copying rounds them.

  4. Is the haze constant, or does it breathe?
    Answer

    It breathes. There are moments where the focus briefly sharpens - an element steps toward the lens, then recedes into the atmosphere again. Those moments are the arrangement: the track moves by changing depth, not by changing material.

  5. How deep is the blur — one copy, or several?
    Answer

    Several — it sounds about three copies deep, on purpose. One pass just dulls; stacked passes change the substance, the way a photocopy of a photocopy stops being a document and becomes a texture.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same depth of field — surfaces consistently smeared, pulse precise, and at least one audible move between focal depths — at matched loudness.
  • One loop only: every layer in the piece is the same two bars at a different depth. No new material may enter after phase 1.
  • The blur must come from real passes through the three paths at duller, quieter settings — not from a filter imitating the effect. EQ can help; the path must do the work.
  • The kick’s timing stays clean at every depth: blur the spectrum, never the clock.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one clean two-bar synth loop, three resampled copies of it made through the internal, USB, and analog paths at progressively duller and quieter settings, and an arrangement that moves the listener between those four focal depths.

MicroFreak · 2-bar loop · clean print → internal resample · GEN-INT → USB bounce · GEN-USB → analog loop × 2 · GEN-ANA → arrange by depth · ≈ −12 dBFS

The four depths, drawn as one loop receding from the lens. Same two bars in every panel; only the focus changes, and the pulse line at the bottom stays equally sharp in all four. The arrangement will move between these panels the way other tracks move between sections.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 20 min

The reference and the clean loop

Phase focus: the reference and the clean loop. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename REF, drag “Hubble” in, loop one minute, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility so it peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. MicroFreak: Type → Wavetable, medium-fast attack, filter half open — a stab with a clear edge and visible top end.
  3. M4 input-1 gain on held stabs, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Record a two-bar loop at 130 BPM into a track named CLEAN: a chord stab pattern with one note that moves, sparse enough to leave air.
  5. Consolidate it (⌘ J).
  6. Add the pulse on the Digitakt II: a plain kick on quarters plus one closed hat, recorded to its own track — this layer stays clean at every depth and never enters a resample pass.
  7. Loop CLEAN over the pulse for a minute and confirm you can stand it;
  8. this loop is the entire harmonic content of the piece.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Actress — “Hubble” (2010, Splazsh) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the clean loop — as the smallest Live set that proves resample blur applied in deliberate layers — copies stacked until the surface is out of focus, with the timing left clean underneath.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects a simple loop heard as if through atmosphere — every edge smeared at a consistent depth while the pulse underneath stays precise; haze that reads as a decision, not a bad recording.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the clean loop into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates resample blur applied in deliberate layers — copies stacked until the surface is out of focus, with the timing left clean underneath, not just the surface of Actress — “Hubble” (2010, Splazsh).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, a two-bar stab loop with real edges sits consolidated as CLEAN, and a plain pulse runs underneath on its own never-to-be-blurred track.

Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

Three blurred copies — one per path, each duller and quieter

Phase focus: three blurred copies — one per path, each duller and quieter. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. GEN-INT — the near depth. Play CLEAN from the Digitakt II and resample it internally to a new slot.
  2. Print as GEN-INT.
  3. Honest expectation: nearly identical — this copy sits just one step behind the parent.
  4. GEN-USB — the middle depth. Bounce CLEAN through Live over USB with EQ Eight low-passing at 6 kHz on the bounce channel, render, and bring it back as GEN-USB.
  5. Duller by choice, conversion by path.
  6. GEN-ANA — the far depth. Send CLEAN out M4 out 3/4 and back in 3/4, return peaking only ≈ −24 dBFS, clip-gained back up after.
  7. Then run that print through the loop again — two analog passes.
  8. Low-pass the second return at 4 kHz.
  9. Print as GEN-ANA: soft edges, audible floor, real distance.
  10. Trim all four layers — CLEAN, GEN-INT, GEN-USB, GEN-ANA — to identical loop lengths, bar-counted, and stack them on four tracks aligned to the same downbeat.
  11. Audition the stack one layer at a time over the pulse and log one line per generation: what does this path’s blur sound like, in your words — not “worse,” but its character.

Arrange by depth — the track moves between focal planes

Do this

  3. Arrange 3–4 minutes in Arrangement view: two minutes deep in the haze

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • A/B your three generations — can you name what each path’s blur sounds like?
    Answer

    You should have three different descriptions, not just “more lo-fi.” Something like: the internal copy is a pane of glass — present but nearly invisible; the USB copy is dulled and slightly flat, conversion eating the top end; the analog copy is soft-edged and breathing, with audible noise floor, the only one that sounds like it traveled somewhere. If two descriptions collapse into one, redo the blind shuffle listening only to attacks and noise floor. These three sentences are Lab 007’s deliverable in miniature — carry them into the lab’s session notes.

  • Where does the record sit on your three-step depth scale?
    Answer

    Deep — around your analog generation, or past it. But the record has one thing none of your far copies have: the pulse’s precision untouched at the bottom of the haze. Relisten and place it honestly: most results land the record between the second and third depth, smeared like GEN-ANA on top but rhythmically as clean as the parent. That gap is the record’s craft — blur applied to texture but withheld from time. Noticing exactly where your scale falls short is the most useful line in today’s logbook.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 007 — this lesson is the lab’s three-path comparison, played as music: one parent, three architectures, the differences exaggerated until they became an arrangement. Choose the path like you choose an instrument — you just did, three times, on purpose.
