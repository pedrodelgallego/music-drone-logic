Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the building as instrument

Copy one clear section of Stephen Vitiello — World Trade Center Recordings: Winds After Hurricane Floyd. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: contact microphones fixed to the windows of a tower during extreme weather - the glass as membrane, the structure as resonator, the storm as the player. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordStephen Vitiello — World Trade Center Recordings: Winds After Hurricane Floyd (1999)

Before you start
  • Honest analogue, stated plainly: Vitiello had the 91st floor of a skyscraper and the tail of a hurricane. You have a window pane, a door, or a balcony rail, and whatever this week’s weather brings. The forces are smaller; the physics — a membrane driven by wind, a structure resonating — is identical, and so is the lesson
  • Gear: the Lab 008 solid path — contact mic or piezo with mounting putty and gaffer tape — into the handheld recorder; closed phones for monitoring. Back at the bench: Live 12, the M4, DT 990 Pro at the mark. The recorder’s mics stay off; air is not invited
  • New set saved as REP-WTC; safety per the Lab 008 checklist — everything mounts on the inside of the glass, nothing leans out, cables strain-relieved to the frame so a tug cannot pull the kit, and the kit stays dry: rain is the player, never the audience of your electronics
  • What you will make: a short replica of Stephen Vitiello — World Trade Center Recordings: Winds After Hurricane Floyd. Focus on this effect: a skyscraper heard as a body under stress - creaking, flexing, singing in high wind, with no airborne sound in the frame.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: contact microphones fixed to the windows of a tower during extreme weather - the glass as membrane, the structure as resonator, the storm as the player.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Stephen Vitiello — World Trade Center Recordings: Winds After Hurricane Floyd. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is physically vibrating in what you hear?
    Answer

    The building - glass, frame, steel - not the air. The contact mics are fixed to the windows, so everything in the file traveled through solid material; the wind itself is never recorded, only what it does to the structure.

  2. Does the wind sound like wind?
    Answer

    No - there is no whoosh, no rushing air, none of the sound design shorthand for “storm.” Wind heard through a solid is pressure and release: groans, ticks, long flexing creaks, a hum that swells with the gusts.

  3. Where is the room?
    Answer

    Nowhere. There is no reverb, no distance, no acoustic space at all - solid-borne sound arrives dry and inside your head, the Tsunoda effect from the Lab 008 listening.

  4. Does the building have a pitch?
    Answer

    Yes - under the creaks there are sustained tones, and they return to the same places.

  5. How do events arrive — steadily, or in surges?
    Answer

    In surges. Gusts are the storm’s phrasing: a swell, a peak of strain, a release with settling ticks.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: three minutes where your structure’s voice — creak, flex, hum — is the only material, dry and roomless, with gusts arriving clean. A solo-flip against REF lands on the same physics at a smaller scale.
  • Contact mic only. If a sound needed air to reach the file, it does not belong in the piece.
  • The weather plays; you do not. No tapping, no knocking, no helping the building along.
  • No processing — edits and level only. The structure’s pitch must be found, not added.

The piece’s machinery in one row: weather drives a membrane, the membrane carries a structure’s resonance, a contact mic taps that vibration where it lives, and a gain set for the strongest gust keeps every surge intact.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the choice of structure

Phase focus: the reference and the choice of structure. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a section of the World Trade Center Recordings into it.
  2. Live Audio Effects → Utility, trim to peak ≈ −12 dBFS, loop three or four minutes.
  3. Live One solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  4. Live Survey your structures: every window, the front door, the balcony rail, a wardrobe panel against an exterior wall.
  5. Live Press an ear or a fingertip to each during a gust and rank them by how much they carry.
  6. Live Pick the winner — largest membrane facing the weather.
  7. Live Log the choice and why.
  8. Live Check the forecast and commit to a session window with real wind or steady rain.
  9. Logbook No weather this week means this lesson waits; write that in the logbook too — the dependency is Vitiello’s, not a flaw in your plan.
  10. Recorder Test the contact mic on a known surface first, per Lab 008 — scratch the desk it is mounted on, confirm signal, then unmount.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, a chosen structure with a reason on paper, a session scheduled against the forecast, and a contact mic proven alive.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
No surface seems to carry anything by earTesting in calm conditions — the instrument is silent without its playerTest during a gust or rain shower; or run the tap test (knuckle on the frame) just to rank surfaces, then trust the weather for the take
The contact mic produces nothing on the desk testDead cable or wrong input — piezos are fragile at the solder jointSwap cable, check the recorder’s input selection; certify the chain indoors before the weather arrives
Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The mount and the gain for gusts

Phase focus: the mount and the gain for gusts. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Recorder Putty the piezo to the inside surface of the pane, a third of the way in from one corner.
  2. Recorder Tape the cable to the frame twice — strain relief first, tidiness second — so nothing tugs the mount.
  3. Live Monitor on the closed phones and listen to a gust arrive through the glass.
  4. Live Then move the mic to a second spot — nearer the middle — and compare.
  5. Live Keep the position with more voice and less rattle; log which won.
  6. Recorder Set the recorder’s gain during the strongest gust you can wait for: that gust peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  7. Recorder The storm is allowed to produce one stronger — that is what the remaining headroom is for.
  8. Recorder Confirm the lull still registers: between gusts the meter should breathe above its floor, not flatline.
  9. Recorder If the quiet is gone, the mount is weak — press the putty again.
  10. Recorder Safety pass before rolling: window stays closed, kit dry and inside, no cable crossing the floor at ankle height, nothing leaning out.
Expected result

A mounted, strain-relieved contact mic on the better of two tested positions, gain committed against the strongest observed gust, and a dry, safe rig ready for a long take.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The signal is mostly your own room — voices, footsteps, the fridgeThe pane carries the building’s interior too; the mic does not know which side mattersRecord when the home is quiet, walk softly, and accept that some interior bleed is structure-borne truth — edit around the rest in phase 4
Buzz or hum rides under everythingUnshielded piezo cable picking up mains — the solid path’s classic faultReroute the cable away from power strips and wall warts; shorter cable, different wall
Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

The long take — the weather plays

Phase focus: the long take — the weather plays. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Roll a single take of at least 30 minutes.
  2. Do not stop between gusts; the lulls are material — the settling ticks after a surge are some of the best sounds the structure makes.
  3. Stay off the floor near the mount, or leave the room entirely.
  4. Footsteps travel through the building into the pane.
  5. Listen in on the closed phones occasionally rather than constantly — log the time of each strong gust so phase 4 can find them fast.
  6. MicroFreak + Recorder If rain is the player instead of wind: same take, same rules — rain on glass through the solid path arrives as pointillist taps with the pane’s resonance under them, a different song from the same instrument.
  7. MicroFreak + Recorder Stop after 30–45 minutes or when the weather dies.
  8. MicroFreak + Recorder Note start, end, and the strongest gust’s clock time.
Expected result

One long, unbroken take containing several full gust cycles — swell, strain, release — with logged timestamps and no footsteps of yours in the frame.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The strongest gust clippedGain set against a polite gust — the weather found a stronger oneDrop gain 6 dB and keep rolling; the Lab 008 rule, relearned at the window: set for what might happen
Half an hour of takes and barely any eventsThe weather under-delivered on the forecastKeep the take — lulls and small ticks are still the structure’s voice — and schedule a second session; the dependency on weather is the lesson’s honest cost
Phase 4 · ≈ 30 min

Compose three minutes, then find the building’s note

Phase focus: compose three minutes, then find the building’s note. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Import the take into REP-WTC on a track named STRUCTURE.
  2. Live Use the gust log to audition the surges first.
  3. Live Assemble three minutes in Arrangement from the take’s best stretches: at least two full gust cycles and one long lull with settling ticks.
  4. Live Edits and level only — no EQ, no reverb; the roomlessness is the sound.
  5. Live Cut anything that needed air or people: voices, the fridge through the wall if it identifies itself, your own door.
  6. Live The structure’s voice is the only material.
  7. Live Drop Audio Effects → Spectrum on the track.
  8. Live Play a gust, then a different gust, then the lull.
  9. Live Find the peak that stays put across all three; read its frequency, log it in Hz and the nearest note name.
  10. Live That is what your building sings.
  11. Live A/B against REF at matched loudness, judging only: dry and roomless, gusts clean, and a singable pitch under the creak.
Expected result

A three-minute piece made only of the structure’s voice, and a logbook line naming your building’s resonant pitch — measured, not imagined.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
No persistent peak shows on the spectrumLooking during lulls, or the window is too wide — broadband events bury the resonanceCheck during the tail of a gust, raise Spectrum’s averaging, and watch the low half of the display; structural notes sit lower than you expect
The piece reads as “noises” rather than an instrumentBest-moments editing — surges kept, phrasing lostRebuild around whole gust cycles: swell, strain, release, settle; the phrasing is the storm’s, keep it intact
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your three minutes at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Wind through glass-as-solid versus wind through air — what is the difference?
    Answer

    Through air, wind is broadband rush — the sound of turbulence at the microphone, a whoosh with no pitch and no body. Through the solid, the rush is gone entirely: what remains is what the wind does — pressure becoming creak, flex, and hum, phrased in surges, dry as a fingertip on your skull. Your file should contain no whoosh anywhere. If it does, air leaked into the chain — a recorder mic left live, a gap whistling at the frame. Relisten to both versions of wind, yours and any film’s, and notice they share nothing but the name.

  • What pitch does your building sing?
    Answer

    It has one — every structure does — and by now it is a number in your logbook, not a guess. The test of the measurement: the peak holds still while the gusts change, because resonance belongs to the structure, not the excitation. Vitiello’s tower sang under hurricane winds; your pane sings under a breeze; both are architecture answering weather at frequencies fixed by their own dimensions. Relisten to the record’s sustained tones once more and hear them as a 415-meter answer to the same question you just asked a window.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 008 — the solid path stops being a category and becomes a method: transducer plus weather equals instrument. Every large surface you pass now has a note in it, waiting for the right day.
