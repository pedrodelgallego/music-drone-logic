Replicate: the building as instrument

Copy one clear section of Stephen Vitiello — World Trade Center Recordings: Winds After Hurricane Floyd. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core method: Vitiello pressed contact microphones (mics that pick up vibration through solid material, not air) to the windows of a tower during a hurricane. The glass acts as a membrane, the whole building resonates, and the storm is the only performer. Do only the steps that make that method audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordStephen Vitiello — World Trade Center Recordings: Winds After Hurricane Floyd (1999)
The effecta skyscraper heard as a body under stress — creaking, flexing, humming in high wind, with no ordinary air-recorded sound in the recording
The mechanismcontact microphones pressed to the tower windows during extreme weather. The glass vibrates; the whole structure resonates (vibrates at its own natural frequency); the storm drives everything

Before you start
  • Honest analogue: Vitiello had the 91st floor and a hurricane. You have a window, a door, or a balcony rail, and whatever weather arrives this week. The scale is smaller. The physics is identical: a surface driven by wind, a structure that resonates. The lesson is the same.
  • Gear: the Lab 008 solid path — a contact mic or piezo (a disc that converts vibration through solid material into an audio signal) fixed with mounting putty and gaffer tape — into the handheld recorder. Closed headphones for monitoring. Back at the bench: Live 12, the M4, DT 990 Pro at the marked level. The recorder’s built-in mics stay off. Air is not invited.
  • Save a new set as REP-WTC. Safety per the Lab 008 checklist: everything mounts on the inside of the glass, nothing leans out, cables are taped to the frame (strain-relieved) so a tug cannot pull the mic loose, and the kit stays dry. Rain drives the piece; it must not touch your electronics.
  • What you will make: a short replica of Stephen Vitiello — World Trade Center Recordings: Winds After Hurricane Floyd. Target this effect: a structure heard under stress — creaking, flexing, humming in wind — with no ordinary room sound anywhere in the recording.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the method works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same method: contact microphones on a surface during weather, the surface as membrane, the structure as resonator, the storm as the performer.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences come from your source material or rig limits, not from missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Stephen Vitiello — World Trade Center Recordings: Winds After Hurricane Floyd. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is physically vibrating in what you hear?
    Answer

    The building — glass, frame, steel — not the air. The contact mics are fixed to the windows, so everything in the recording traveled through solid material. The wind itself is never captured. Only what it does to the structure is.

  2. Does the wind sound like wind?
    Answer

    No. There is no whoosh, no rushing air. Wind heard through a solid is all pressure and release: groans, ticks, long creaks, a hum that swells with each gust. Nothing here sounds like a movie storm.

  3. Where is the room?
    Answer

    Nowhere. There is no reverb and no sense of space. Sound traveling through solid material arrives dry and close — almost inside your head. This is the quality you heard in the Lab 008 listening exercise.

  4. Does the building have a pitch?
    Answer

    Yes. Under the creaks there are sustained tones. They keep returning to the same frequency because every structure has a natural resonant pitch — a frequency at which it vibrates most easily. That frequency belongs to the building, not the wind.

  5. How do events arrive — steadily, or in surges?
    Answer

    In surges. Each gust is a complete phrase: a swell, a peak of strain, a release, then settling ticks as the structure relaxes.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: three minutes where your structure’s voice — creak, flex, hum — is the only material. Dry and roomless. Gusts arriving clean. A solo-flip against REF should land on the same physics at a smaller scale.
  • Contact mic only. If a sound needed air to reach the microphone, it does not belong in the piece.
  • The weather plays; you do not. No tapping, no knocking, no helping the building along.
  • No processing — edits and level only. The structure’s pitch must be found in what was recorded, not added afterward.

The piece in one row: weather drives a surface, the surface carries the structure’s vibration, a contact mic picks up that vibration directly, and the recorder’s gain is set for the strongest gust so no surge is lost.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the choice of structure

Phase focus: the reference and the choice of structure. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a section of the World Trade Center Recordings into it.
  2. Audio Effects → Utility, trim to peak ≈ −12 dBFS, loop three or four minutes.
  3. One solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  4. Survey your structures: every window, the front door, the balcony rail, a wardrobe panel against an exterior wall.
  5. Press an ear or a fingertip to each during a gust and rank them by how much they carry.
  6. Pick the winner — largest membrane facing the weather.
  7. Log the choice and why.
  8. Check the forecast and commit to a session window with real wind or steady rain.
  9. If there is no useful weather this week, this lesson waits. Write that in the logbook. The dependency on real weather comes from Vitiello’s method, not from a flaw in your plan.
  10. Test the contact mic on a known surface first, per Lab 008 — scratch the desk it is mounted on, confirm signal, then unmount.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The mount and the gain for gusts

Phase focus: the mount and the gain for gusts. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Putty the piezo to the inside surface of the pane, a third of the way in from one corner.
  2. Tape the cable to the frame twice — strain relief first, tidiness second — so nothing tugs the mount.
  3. Monitor on the closed phones and listen to a gust arrive through the glass.
  4. Then move the mic to a second spot — nearer the middle — and compare.
  5. Keep the position with more voice and less rattle; log which won.
  6. Set the recorder’s gain during the strongest gust you can wait for: that gust peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  7. The storm is allowed to produce one stronger — that is what the remaining headroom is for.
  8. Confirm the lull still registers: between gusts the meter should breathe above its floor, not flatline.
  9. If the quiet is gone, the mount is weak — press the putty again.
  10. Safety pass before rolling: window stays closed, kit dry and inside, no cable crossing the floor at ankle height, nothing leaning out.

Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

The long take — the weather plays

Phase focus: the long take — the weather plays. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Roll a single take of at least 30 minutes.
  2. Do not stop between gusts; the lulls are material — the settling ticks after a surge are some of the best sounds the structure makes.
  3. Stay off the floor near the mount, or leave the room entirely.
  4. Footsteps travel through the building into the pane.
  5. Listen in on the closed phones occasionally rather than constantly — log the time of each strong gust so phase 4 can find them fast.
  6. If rain is the player instead of wind: same take, same rules — rain on glass through the solid path arrives as pointillist taps with the pane’s resonance under them, a different song from the same instrument.
  7. Stop after 30–45 minutes or when the weather dies.
  8. Note start, end, and the strongest gust’s clock time.

Phase 4 · ≈ 30 min

Compose three minutes, then find the building’s note

Phase focus: compose three minutes, then find the building’s note. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the take into REP-WTC on a track named STRUCTURE.
  2. Use the gust log to audition the surges first.
  3. Assemble three minutes in Arrangement from the take’s best stretches: at least two full gust cycles and one long lull with settling ticks.
  4. Edits and level only — no EQ, no reverb; the roomlessness is the sound.
  5. Cut anything that needed air or people: voices, the fridge through the wall if it identifies itself, your own door.
  6. The structure’s voice is the only material.
  7. Drop Audio Effects → Spectrum on the track.
  8. Play a gust, then a different gust, then the lull.
  9. Find the peak that stays put across all three; read its frequency, log it in Hz and the nearest note name.
  10. That is what your building sings.
  11. A/B against REF at matched loudness, judging only: dry and roomless, gusts clean, and a singable pitch under the creak.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your three minutes at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Wind through glass-as-solid versus wind through air — what is the difference?
    Answer

    Through air, wind is a broadband rush — turbulence at the microphone, a whoosh with no pitch. Through solid material, the rush disappears entirely. What remains is what the wind does: pressure becoming creak, flex, and hum, phrased in surges, arriving dry. Your file should contain no whoosh. If it does, air leaked into the chain — a recorder mic left on, or a gap in the frame whistling. Listen to your file and to any film’s storm, and notice they share nothing but the name.

  • What pitch does your building sing?
    Answer

    It has one — every structure does — and by now it is a number in your logbook. The test: the peak holds still while the gusts change. That is because the resonant frequency belongs to the structure itself, not to the wind driving it. The wind changes; the building’s note does not. Vitiello’s tower had its own frequency. Your window does too. Both are structures answering weather at a pitch fixed by their own dimensions.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.

A transducer (any device that converts one form of energy to another — here, vibration to signal) plus weather equals an instrument. Every large surface you pass now has a note in it, waiting for the right day.
