Replicate: the narrated zoom

Copy one clear section of Hildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a narrated walk — a close, quiet voice over a wide scene — plus volume moves that follow each sentence: the city fades down, the named detail comes up. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating

The effecta whole city fades into the background while tiny barnacle clicks fill the foreground — because the narrator said so and the volume agreed

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone, held mid-chest with your mouth 20–30 cm from the mic — the voice must travel with the microphone, so the studio rig stays home; DT 990 Pro for the desk pass afterward
  • New set saved as REP-KITS-BEACH — no tempo; what matters here is your walking pace and the rhythm of your sentences
  • Field note: someone narrating quietly into a recorder draws attention — choose a time and place where you are comfortable doing this, ask before recording anyone else, and watch for traffic; monitor at a steady moderate level
  • What you will make: a short replica of Hildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk”. Focus on this effect: the city fades away and tiny sounds fill the foreground — because the narrator pointed at them and the volume agreed.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a narrated walk — close, quiet voice over a wide scene — plus volume moves that follow each sentence: the city ducked, the named detail raised.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or equipment limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Hildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Where is her voice relative to the scene?
    Answer

    Much closer — the voice is intimate, almost in your ear, while the beach and the city sit far away. Two distances coexist in one recording. That gap is what makes the voice feel like a guide rather than just another sound in the scene.

  2. What does she announce, and what follows?
    Answer

    She tells you she is turning the city down — and then the city actually goes down, while the small sounds swell. The volume move is narrated as it happens, which turns a mixing decision into part of the piece.

  3. How big are the barnacles, really?
    Answer

    Tiny — physically far too quiet to compete with a city. Their apparent size in the piece comes entirely from the volume decision that favors them over everything else.

  4. Does the scene change, or the hierarchy?
    Answer

    The hierarchy. The beach stays exactly where it was. What changes is what sounds most important. A volume move rewrote the scene’s order without moving anything in it.

  5. What is the narration doing, technically?
    Answer

    It is writing the script for the fader. Each sentence points your attention at something, then the volume move confirms what she pointed at. Voice first, level second, every time.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a 3–4 minute narrated walk where the listener’s attention moves where your voice points, and at least one volume move follows a sentence. A solo-flip against REF should reveal the same basic shape: close voice over a wide world.
  • Your voice stays close and quiet; the environment sits wide behind it — two distances in one file.
  • One physical zoom in the field (step toward a named detail, mic closer) and one volume zoom at the desk (the background ducked, the detail raised).
  • Volume is the only processing — Utility automation and clip gain; no EQ, no reverb, nothing else.

The whole mechanism in one row: one walking recorder, one quiet voice 20 cm from the mic, a physical step toward each named detail, and a desk pass where Utility automation turns the background down after the voice has told you what to listen for.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference, the location, the script

Phase focus: the reference, the location, the script. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Kits Beach Soundwalk” in, and loop the first eight minutes.
  2. Audio Effects → Utility, trim to ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Map her moves: write three timestamps where a sentence is followed by a level change, with the sentence paraphrased.
  4. Choose your location — anywhere with one broad wash (traffic, ventilation, water, a laundromat) and at least three small details buried in it.
  5. Script three quiet sentences, one per detail, in her grammar: name the wash, name the detail, say what you are about to do — “under all this traffic there is a gate latch — I want to get closer to it.”
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Hildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk” (1989) and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference, the location, the script — as the smallest Live set that proves a narrated walk — a close, quiet voice over a wide scene — plus volume moves that follow each sentence: the city ducked, the named detail raised.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects a whole city fades into the background while tiny barnacle clicks fill the foreground — because the narrator said so and the volume agreed.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference, the location, the script into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates a narrated walk — a close, quiet voice over a wide scene — plus volume moves that follow each sentence: the city ducked, the named detail raised, not just the surface of Hildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk” (1989).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, three of her voice-then-gain moves are mapped with timestamps, and three scripted sentences name your location’s wash and details.

Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

The walk — voice, step, and one take

Phase focus: the walk — voice, step, and one take. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Set the gain once, mid-chest position, for the wash plus your quiet voice — test one sentence before the take;
  2. the voice should peak well below clipping.
  3. The gain does not move again unless you narrate the move.
  4. Record 3–4 minutes in one take, walking slowly.
  5. Speak each scripted sentence quietly, then act on it: step toward the detail until the mic is close enough that the detail beats the wash.
  6. Hold each close position for at least fifteen seconds of silence after the sentence — the detail needs room to be heard without you.
  7. If your recorder has a gain knob and you raise it for a named detail, say so on tape, the way she does — an unnarrated move is a mistake;
  8. a narrated one is the technique.
  9. Log the take, lab-006 style: place · details · level.

Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The desk duck — make the level agree with the voice

Phase focus: the desk duck — make the level agree with the voice. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the take onto a track named WALK, fader at 0 dB, Audio Effects → Utility on it.
  2. Automate Utility gain: a dip of −3 to −6 dB starting as each sentence begins — the world ducks, the close voice rides on top.
  3. After each sentence, ride the gain back up into the detail passage — the recorded zoom gets a second push, and the detail arrives in front.
  4. Spread every ride across the length of the sentence or longer;
  5. a move you can hear happening is a move that needed more seconds.
  6. Render 3–4 minutes and listen end to end at the mark, no touching.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The attention test and the A/B

Phase focus: the attention test and the A/B. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play your render end to end at the mark, no touching.
  2. After each sentence, notice what you are listening to — it should be the detail, not the voice.
  3. If you have a second listener: play the piece once and ask them to name, afterward, the three things they remember hearing.
  4. Record the list.
  5. A/B one zoom of yours against one of REF’s mapped moves at matched loudness, judging two things only: is your voice equally foregrounded, and does your world step back as far?
  6. Logbook: name the single gain move that did the “zoom” in your best passage — the step, the recorder knob, or the desk ride.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does the listener’s attention move where your narration points?
    Answer

    The memory test answers this. A listener who heard the piece once should list your three details, roughly in order — the voice directed their attention and the volume kept its appointments. If they list the background wash, or the voice itself, the pointing failed. Usually this means the detail never got its fifteen silent seconds to be heard. Listen back to the record and notice how much silence she leaves after each sentence. The pointing is mostly patience.

  • Name the volume move that did the “zoom.”
    Answer

    There were three candidates: your feet (walking closer), the recorder knob (field gain), and the desk ride (Utility). In your best passage, one of them carried the weight — write which one, because that is the move you now own. On the record, the decisive move is the studio fader, and she tells you so out loud. Listen for the sentence where she names it. Naming the move is what makes it a deliberate choice rather than a happy accident.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
