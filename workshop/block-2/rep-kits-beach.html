Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the narrated zoom

Copy one clear section of Hildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a narrated walk - close, quiet voice over a wide scene - plus level moves that enact each sentence: the city ducked, the named detail raised. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordHildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk” (1989)
The effecta whole city recedes and barnacle clicks the size of a fingernail fill the foreground — because a voice said so and the gain agreed
The mechanisma narrated walk — close, quiet voice over a wide scene — plus level moves that enact each sentence: the city ducked, the named detail raised

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone, held mid-chest with your mouth 20–30 cm from the mic — the voice must travel with the microphone, so the desk rig stays home; DT 990 Pro for the desk pass afterward
  • New set saved as REP-KITS-BEACH — no tempo; the clock is your walking pace and your sentences
  • Field line: a person quietly narrating into a recorder draws looks — choose a time and place where that is comfortable, ask before recording anyone, and watch traffic; monitor at a moderate fixed level
  • What you will make: a short replica of Hildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk”. Focus on this effect: a whole city recedes and barnacle clicks the size of a fingernail fill the foreground - because a voice said so and the gain agreed.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a narrated walk - close, quiet voice over a wide scene - plus level moves that enact each sentence: the city ducked, the named detail raised.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Hildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Where is her voice relative to the scene?
    Answer

    Much closer - the voice is intimate, almost in your ear, while the beach and the city sit yards and miles away. Two perspectives coexist in one recording, and the gap between them is what makes the voice a guide rather than a participant.

  2. What does she announce, and what follows?
    Answer

    She tells you she is turning the city down - and then the city goes down, audibly, while the small sounds swell. The move is narrated as it happens, which turns a mixing decision into a teaching moment.

  3. How big are the barnacles, really?
    Answer

    Tiny - close clicks at minute acoustic level, physically incapable of competing with a city. Their hugeness in the piece is manufactured entirely by the level decision favoring them.

  4. Does the scene change, or the hierarchy?
    Answer

    The hierarchy - the beach stays exactly where it was; what changes is what counts as foreground. A level move rewrote the scene’s order of importance without moving a single object in it.

  5. What is the narration doing, technically?
    Answer

    It is the fader’s score - each sentence licenses the level move that follows, pointing your attention a half-second before the gain confirms the pointing. Voice first, level second, every time.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a 3–4 minute narrated walk in which the listener’s attention demonstrably moves where the voice points, and at least one level move audibly enacts a sentence — a solo-flip against REF lands on the same close-voice-over-wide-world geometry.
  • Voice close and quiet, the world wide behind it — two distances in one file.
  • One physical zoom in the field (step in, mic closer to a named detail) and one gain zoom at the desk (the wash ducked, the detail raised).
  • Level is the only processing — Utility automation and clip gain; no EQ, no reverb, nothing else.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one walking recorder, one quiet narrating voice twenty centimeters from it, a physical step toward each named detail, and a desk pass where Utility automation turns the world down whenever the voice has just told you what to hear.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference, the location, the script

Phase focus: the reference, the location, the script. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Kits Beach Soundwalk” in, and loop the first eight minutes.
  2. Live Audio Effects → Utility, trim to ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Logbook Map her moves: write three timestamps where a sentence is followed by a level change, with the sentence paraphrased.
  4. Logbook Choose your location — anywhere with one broad wash (traffic, ventilation, water, a laundromat) and at least three small details buried in it.
  5. Logbook Script three quiet sentences, one per detail, in her grammar: name the wash, name the detail, say what you are about to do — “under all this traffic there is a gate latch — I want to get closer to it.”
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, three of her voice-then-gain moves are mapped with timestamps, and three scripted sentences name your location’s wash and details.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
No details come to mind for your locationListening from memory instead of visitingStand in the location for five silent minutes first; the details surface one by one
The scripted sentences sound like captionsDescribing the scene instead of directing attentionRewrite each to end with an intention — what you want to hear, and what you are about to do
Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

The walk — voice, step, and one take

Phase focus: the walk — voice, step, and one take. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Set the gain once, mid-chest position, for the wash plus your quiet voice — test one sentence before the take;
  2. Live the voice should peak well below clipping.
  3. Live The gain does not move again unless you narrate the move.
  4. Live Record 3–4 minutes in one take, walking slowly.
  5. Live Speak each scripted sentence quietly, then act on it: step toward the detail until the mic is close enough that the detail beats the wash.
  6. Live Hold each close position for at least fifteen seconds of silence after the sentence — the detail needs room to be heard without you.
  7. Recorder If your recorder has a gain knob and you raise it for a named detail, say so on tape, the way she does — an unnarrated move is a mistake;
  8. Recorder a narrated one is the technique.
  9. Recorder Log the take, lab-006 style: place · details · level.
Expected result

One take containing a wide wash, three quiet sentences printed close, and three physical zooms — each sentence followed by an audible change of distance.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The voice pops and booms over everythingToo close, or speaking at full voiceQuiet voice, mouth slightly off-axis from the mic, 20–30 cm — intimate, not amplified
The named detail never reads in the takeYou pointed the mic but never moved your feetHalve the distance and hold still — the zoom is made of steps, not aim
Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The desk duck — make the level agree with the voice

Phase focus: the desk duck — make the level agree with the voice. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Import the take onto a track named WALK, fader at 0 dB, Audio Effects → Utility on it.
  2. Live Automate Utility gain: a dip of −3 to −6 dB starting as each sentence begins — the world ducks, the close voice rides on top.
  3. Live After each sentence, ride the gain back up into the detail passage — the recorded zoom gets a second push, and the detail arrives in front.
  4. Live Spread every ride across the length of the sentence or longer;
  5. Live a move you can hear happening is a move that needed more seconds.
  6. Live Render 3–4 minutes and listen end to end at the mark, no touching.
Expected result

A walk where each sentence is followed by the world stepping back and the named detail stepping forward — the field zoom and the desk zoom reading as one gesture.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The duck swallows the voice along with the worldDip too deep for the voice’s headroomKeep the dip at −3 to −6 dB — the voice is your closest sound and survives a shallow duck
The rides sound like playback faultsAutomation drawn too fastStretch each ride across the whole sentence; the move should be noticed late, not caught
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The attention test and the A/B

Phase focus: the attention test and the A/B. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play your render end to end at the mark, no touching.
  2. After each sentence, notice what you are listening to — it should be the detail, not the voice.
  3. If a second listener is available: play it once and ask them to name, afterward, the three things they remember hearing.
  4. Record the list.
  5. Live A/B one zoom of yours against one of REF’s mapped moves at matched loudness, judging two things only: is your voice equally foregrounded, and does your world step back as far?
  6. Recorder + Logbook Logbook: name the single gain move that did the “zoom” in your best passage — the step, the recorder knob, or the desk ride.
Expected result

A listener’s memory list that matches your three details in order, an A/B that comes down to voice distance and duck depth, and one logbook line naming which move carried the zoom.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The listener remembers the voice, not the detailsNarration too long — the sentences became the showCut each sentence to one breath; the voice points and then gets out of the way
Your zoom feels small next to REF’sOnly one stage of the zoom present — feet or fader, not bothStack them: the step in the field, the duck-and-rise at the desk, on the same detail
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does the listener’s attention move where your narration points?
    Answer

    The memory test answers this: a listener who heard the piece once should list your three details, roughly in order, because the voice scheduled their attention and the levels kept the appointments. If they list the wash, or the voice itself, the pointing failed — usually because the detail never got its fifteen silent seconds. Relisten to the record and notice how much silence she leaves after each sentence; the pointing is mostly patience.

  • Name the gain move that did the “zoom.”
    Answer

    There were three candidates: your feet (distance), the recorder knob (field gain), and the desk ride (Utility). In your best passage one of them carried the weight — write which, because that is the move you now own. On the record the decisive move is the studio fader, and she tells you so out loud; relisten for the sentence where she names it, and notice that naming the move is what makes it authorship instead of trickery.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2, and the attention test seeds the one-minute miniature in Lab 006.
  • Carries into the lab: gain as authorship is the étude’s central lesson — the level test in Lab 006 measures the same decision this record performs, narrated, in public.
