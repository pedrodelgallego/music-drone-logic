Replicate: one instrument, emptied out

Copy one clear section of Sarah Davachi — All My Circles Run (2017; expanded 2018) — one instrument per piece: “For Strings,” “For Voice,” “For Organ,” “For Piano.” This lesson leans on “For Voice”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill is about one technique: hold a tone for as long as one breath allows, don't clean it up, and arrange the takes by deciding only when they start and end relative to each other. Time does the transforming. The small sounds of the body — breath, waver — are the point, not problems to fix. Do only the steps that make that technique audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: a microphone — any microphone — into M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack; the MicroFreak and Digitakt II rest. You do not own a string section or a pipe organ: your voice is the honest analogue, and it is the better one, because its mechanism — breath — is the loudest of any instrument you own. No mic at all? Record voice memos on your phone and drag them in; the breath matters more than the fidelity
  • New set saved as REP-CIRCLES — tempo irrelevant; the only durations that matter are the lengths of your breath
  • Monitoring: phones stay on while recording, but software-monitor off or low — hearing your own voice doubled makes pitch holding harder. Phones at the lab-001 mark for playback; break every 15 minutes, and rest the voice too
  • What you will make: a short replica of Sarah Davachi — All My Circles Run (2017; expanded 2018) — one instrument per piece: “For Strings,” “For Voice,” “For Organ,” “For Piano.” This lesson leans on “For Voice”. Focus on this effect: a single instrument class, sustained and overlapped until its identity blurs and the physical act of producing the tone becomes the foreground.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: long held tones, uncleaned, arranged only by where their entries and exits overlap - duration does the transforming, and the mechanism noise does the speaking.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Sarah Davachi — All My Circles Run (2017; expanded 2018) — one instrument per piece: “For Strings,” “For Voice,” “For Organ,” “For Piano.” This lesson leans on “For Voice”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What instrument is this — and how long did you take to decide?
    Answer

    The title tells you. Your ear takes longer — and gets less sure the longer the piece runs.

  2. What small noises survive here that a pop record would delete?
    Answer

    Breath. The waver as a tone settles. The small instabilities of a person holding a pitch longer than is comfortable.

  3. Does the piece develop, or persist?
    Answer

    Persists — and the persistence is the form. Tones enter, hold, and leave; the overlaps thicken and thin; nothing builds or resolves. What changes is how deep into it you are, not its shape.

  4. What counts as an event in this music?
    Answer

    An entry or an exit, nothing else. Every audible change is a tone arriving or a tone ending, and the harmony shifts only as a side-effect of which tones are currently sounding.

  5. Where is the synthesizer?
    Answer

    There isn’t one. The album sets synthesis aside and sustains acoustic instruments instead — one instrument per piece.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same structure — held tones from one source, overlapping, body sounds audible, no events except entries and exits.
  • One source for the whole piece — your voice. No second instrument, no synth pad underneath to make it “fuller.”
  • No cleaning — breaths stay, wavers stay, the settling of each tone stays. No pitch correction, no EQ surgery, no gating.
  • Arrangement means deciding overlaps, nothing else — where takes start and end is the entire compositional vocabulary.

The whole system in one row: a dozen held vocal tones recorded breath-to-breath, laid across three or four tracks so their entries and exits overlap like roof shingles, levels matched once, nothing processed.

voice → mic → M4 in 1 → 10–12 held tones · printed whole → shingled overlaps · entries and exits → mix · uncleaned · ≈ −12 dBFS

The whole arrangement is this picture: takes overlapping so some voice is always sounding. The soft dot at the start of each bar is a breath — kept intentionally. The only decisions you make are where each bar starts and stops.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the input

Phase focus: the reference and the input. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “For Voice” into it.
  2. Loop three minutes from the middle.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. Plug the mic into M4 input 1 (phantom on only if the mic needs it).
  6. ⌘ T; rename: VOICE, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed, monitoring off.
  7. Sing your most comfortable pitch and hold it at the volume you intend to use;
  8. set the input-1 gain so the held tone peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  9. Sing it once more to confirm; lungs warm up and get louder.
  10. Record thirty seconds of room silence and listen back — know what your room contributes before you stack twelve takes of it.

Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

The takes — held tones, breath to breath, uncleaned

Phase focus: the takes — held tones, breath to breath, uncleaned. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Choose two pitches: your comfortable note and a second a fourth or fifth away.
  2. Two is enough harmony for five minutes; the overlaps will do the rest.
  3. Record 10–12 takes into VOICE, one breath each, alternating pitches as you like.
  4. Vowel ah or oo, steady, no vibrato on purpose — the waver that happens anyway is the material.
  5. Start each recording before the intake and stop after the air is gone — the breath and the die-away belong to the take.
  6. Expect 15–30 seconds per take; do not strain past the comfortable end of a breath.
  7. Rest a minute every few takes.
  8. Listen back to one early take in full and write one line: name the mechanism sounds you can hear — intake, settling, waver, decay.
  9. Delete nothing for being imperfect.
  10. A cracked tone is a re-take; a wavering one is a keeper — learn the difference now, in writing.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Arrange by overlap — entries and exits only

Phase focus: arrange by overlap — entries and exits only. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Make three or four audio tracks and distribute the takes across them so overlapping clips never share a track.
  2. Levels matched once — the takes were gained alike; leave the faders at 0 dB.
  3. Shingle the takes across 4–6 minutes of Arrangement: each entry lands while at least one earlier tone still holds, so some voice is always sounding.
  4. Vary the overlap depth — sometimes a few seconds, sometimes most of a take.
  5. No fades beyond a few milliseconds of de-click at clip edges — the breath is the fade-in and the emptying lungs are the fade-out;
  6. they were recorded for this.
  7. Rule: if you want a change, move an entry or an exit. That's your only tool.
  8. No volume rides, no effects, no pitch edits. When the texture feels thin, overlap more.
  9. When it feels crowded, overlap less.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Listen for the mechanism, and for the blur

Phase focus: listen for the mechanism, and for the blur. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the piece end to end, no touching, phones at the mark.
  2. Write down the moment — minutes and seconds — when you stop hearing “me, singing” and start hearing tone.
  3. Name, in one line, the mechanism sound your piece foregrounds.
  4. For most voices it is the breath at each entry; it may be your settling waver instead.
  5. One answer, committed to paper.
  6. A/B a minute of yours against REF at matched loudness, judging only two things: is your mechanism as audible as theirs, and does your instrument’s identity blur with duration the way theirs does?
  7. The counter-test: imagine the piece with every breath edited out.
  8. Write one line on what would be lost — then resist ever performing that edit.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • What mechanism sound does your piece foreground — and is it doing musical work?
    Answer

    You named it in phase 4 — for most voices, the breath. The test: imagine removing every breath. The piece loses its joints, its evidence of a body working. On the record the same is true — the small noises are not waste material; they are structural. Relisten to one entry on each piece and hear the intake as punctuation.

  • Does the record’s instrument blur with duration the way yours does?
    Answer

    Yes — and now you can hear it from the inside. Identity lives in attacks, phrases, and change. Sustain removes all three, and any instrument held long enough drifts toward tone-with-a-texture. The album shows this once per instrument. Your piece showed it with your own voice. Relisten to the opening of “For Voice” one last time and compare when the blur hits against your own timestamp.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: Lab 005 sustains a synth voice with slow modulation. This record sustains a body instead, and the “modulation” is involuntary — breath and waver. When you design the lab’s tone, ask what its small mechanical sounds are, and whether you have cleaned away the thing that gives a listener something to hold on to.
