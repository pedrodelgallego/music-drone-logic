Replicate: rave cassettes smeared into ambient

Copy one clear section of Lee Gamble — “Diversions 1994–1996”. Build a short study by smearing and time-stretching old dance recordings into ambient source material, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill is about one technique: take old rave cassettes — already lossy, hissy, second-generation — and smear and time-stretch them until the beat is gone and only an ambient haze of the original energy remains. The source is found sound resampled past its function: the dancefloor becomes memory. Do only the steps that turn the rave recording into ambient material.

Before you start
  • Gear: a hissy, second-generation source — a tape rip, an old mix, a lo-fi dance recording you have the right to work from; Live's warp and stretch engines; DT 990 Pro for judging; the lab-006 log repurposed for source and stretch ratio
  • New set saved as REP-DIVERSIONS — no tempo grid; the goal is to lose the pulse, so do not lock to a click
  • Stretching boosts hiss and can spike on transients — re-check gain after each stretch, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS; phones at the lab-001 mark
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — choose fragments, stretch and smear them, arrange one ambient passage, then file it and stop
  • What you will make: a short replica of Lee Gamble — “Diversions 1994–1996”. Focus on this effect: a rave recording smeared into ambient haze.
  • What you will turn off: the beat grid, the urge to keep it danceable, and any clean source — lo-fi and hiss are the material.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: old rave cassettes smeared and time-stretched into ambient source material (found-sound resampling).
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are which fragments you chose, not the beat surviving where it should have dissolved.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Lee Gamble — “Diversions 1994–1996”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Is there a beat?
    Answer

    Barely, or not at all — the rhythm has been stretched until it smears into a haze. You sense it was once dance music, but the pulse is gone. What remains is atmosphere and the ghost of motion.

  2. What is the source?
    Answer

    Old rave and jungle tapes — second-generation, hissy, lossy. The lo-fi quality is not a flaw to fix; it is part of the material, a marker of the cassette and the era it came from.

  3. What does the time-stretch do to it?
    Answer

    It blurs transients into washes, turns breakbeats into texture, and pulls melodic fragments into long ambient tones. Stretching far enough converts rhythm into timbre — the beat becomes a drone.

  4. Why use old dance tapes specifically?
    Answer

    For their charge. They carry the memory of a scene and a feeling, so smearing them produces nostalgia, not just texture — the ambient haze is haunted by the dancefloor it came from.

  5. Where is the composition, if it is just stretched tape?
    Answer

    In which fragments are chosen, how far each is stretched, and how the hazes are layered and sequenced. Choosing the source moments and the degree of dissolution — those decisions are the form.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Warp in Live: turn Warp on, choose the Texture or Complex Pro mode for smearing, and drag the clip's length to time-stretch far past its original duration.
  • Sound target: one 60–90 second ambient passage made from stretched, smeared rave fragments — beatless, hazy, nostalgic.
  • 4–6 fragments from a lo-fi dance source, each stretched far enough that the pulse dissolves.
  • The interest comes from degree of dissolution and layering, not from keeping the groove.
  • Warp/stretch, level and gentle filtering only — re-quantising or adding a fresh beat breaks the lesson.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the dissolve rule

Phase focus: the reference and the dissolve rule. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag the reference section in, and loop it at ≈ −12 dBFS with Audio Effects → Utility.
  2. Choose your lo-fi source — a tape rip or old mix you have the right to use — and import it; keep the hiss, do not clean it.
  3. Write the rule in full: stretch each fragment until the pulse is gone — if I can still nod to it, it is not stretched far enough.
  4. Scrub the source and mark 4–6 candidate fragments: a breakbeat, a pad, a vocal stab, a sub line — moments with character.
  5. Decide the membership test: the source should be felt as memory, not heard as a track.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Lee Gamble — “Diversions 1994–1996” and use audio clips, Warp, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — the reference and the dissolve rule — as the smallest Live set that proves old rave cassettes smeared and time-stretched into ambient source material (found-sound resampling).
  3. Keep every fragment on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects whether the beat dissolves.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: load the lo-fi source into a Flex machine, then translate the reference and the dissolve rule using time-stretch, slices, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.

  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main move in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the beat dissolves.

Phase 2 · ≈ 35 min

Stretch and smear until the beat dissolves

Phase focus: stretch and smear. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Take the first fragment, turn Warp on, choose Texture or Complex Pro, and stretch it to many times its length until transients smear into a wash.
  2. Re-check gain, then resample the stretched result to a new clip named HAZE·1.
  3. Repeat for each fragment, varying the stretch ratio — some lightly blurred, some pulled into long drones.
  4. For at least one fragment, stretch a breakbeat far enough that the kicks become a slow pulse-less throb.
  5. Keep the hiss and artefacts; a touch of gentle low/high filtering to shape the haze is allowed, nothing that cleans it.
  6. Log each clip's source and stretch ratio in the repurposed lab-006 log.

  4. Record or resample each haze to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects whether the beat dissolves.

Expected result

4–6 stretched haze clips, the pulse dissolved in each, the cassette hiss preserved, every stretch ratio logged.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The beat is still clearly thereStretch ratio too small, or a transient-preserving warp modeStretch much further and switch to a smearing mode (Texture/Complex Pro); the rhythm should melt, not just slow
The haze sounds metallic and gritty in a bad wayWarp artefacts at extreme ratiosBack off the ratio slightly, or layer two moderate stretches; you want soft blur, not digital tearing — unless that grit suits the piece
Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

The study — layer the haze

Phase focus: the study. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Lay your haze clips in Arrangement and build one 60–90 second ambient passage by layering and crossfading — no beat grid.
  2. Overlap clips so one haze melts into the next; let the hiss be continuous so the passage feels like one tape.
  3. Bring the slow pulse-less throb in and out as the closest thing to rhythm — implied, never stated.
  4. Let a recognisable fragment surface briefly, then dissolve again — the ghost of the source appearing and fading.
  5. Resist re-quantising or adding a fresh beat; the beatlessness is the point.
  6. Print the study to one audio track named PRINT.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your study at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Has the beat truly dissolved?
    Answer

    That is the pass condition. A working study has no pulse you can tap to — only an implied, smeared motion. If a listener starts nodding, the stretch was too conservative somewhere; find that clip and pull it further. The record passes by leaving only the ghost of rhythm, never the rhythm itself.

  • Does the source still haunt the haze?
    Answer

    This is the subtler test. The passage should feel charged with the memory of a dancefloor — not generic ambient. If it could be any pad anywhere, you have erased too much of the source's character. Let a fragment surface briefly so the rave reads as a memory, the way the reference keeps you half-recognising tracks you never quite hear.
