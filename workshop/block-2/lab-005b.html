The Beatless Long-Form

The point

Lab 003 showed that a loop with subtraction and drift can hold a listener for eight minutes. This lab removes the loop entirely. The harder claim: beating, slow modulation, and volume movement can carry ten minutes with nothing repeating and nothing pulsing.

1 · Overview

One patch, four layers

  • Foundation, interval, and air are three separate recordings of one saved MicroFreak patch.
  • The texture bed is that same patch sampled into the Digitakt II and degraded by resampling — recording your own sound back in to reshape it.
  • Everything is the same source tone, just at different weights. That shared origin is what holds the piece together.

Beating is the pulse

  • The interval layer is tuned slightly sharp or flat against the foundation by a chosen number of cents.
  • That tuning difference creates beating — a slow pulse caused by the two pitches interfering with each other. The speed of the pulse is determined by how far apart the pitches are in Hz.
  • Target 0.3–1.5 Hz. Write that number in the spec before you record.

Level is the arrangement

  • No mutes or sudden entrances. Four volume faders and one reverb send are the entire structure.
  • Every fade takes sixty seconds or longer.
  • The "peak" of the piece is not a louder moment — it is increased density from the Saturator (gentle overload that adds grit) at constant overall volume. More mass, not more loudness.

2 · The two specs — filled, the worked example

System Spec — filled (S1: verify against your build)
InputsOne MicroFreak patch (LAB2 VOICE) recorded three times — root, interval (detuned by a named number of cents), and +2 octaves with filtering · one Digitakt II texture bed sampled from the foundation take and resampled 1–2 generations
TransformsTwo slow unsynced modulators inside the patch (LFO → Timbre ~25, one cycle/15–20 s · cycling envelope → Cutoff ~15, 30–60 s loop) · per-layer EQ to keep each layer in its own frequency range · Hybrid Reverb return growing mid-piece · Saturator on the bed increasing in the final third, output-compensated so the meter does not rise
Feedback / variationBeating at the named rate between foundation and interval · the two modulators run free and never realign · volume choreography — which layer is loudest, minute by minute
ConstraintsOne interval for the whole piece · no grid, no synced LFOs, no events · ≤5 tracks + 1 return · every fade ≥60 s · 8–12 minutes
Human roleDesign the tone, choose the interval, set the beat rate — then be patient: long hands-off takes, and volume moves slower than feels comfortable
Engineering Spec — filled (S1: verify, correct after)
Signal pathMicroFreak → M4 in 1 → FOUNDATION/INTERVAL/AIR (one armed at a time) · Digitakt II main L/R → M4 in 3/4 → BED · master → M4 → DT 990 Pro
Gain planPeaks −15 to −12 dBFS — set on the held tone's body, not its initial swell. Sustained material holds its level, so the meter stays where it is (see the 004b crest-factor amendment). Master ≤ −3 dBFS including the reverb bloom.
Clock / syncNone. Metronome off, ruler in m:ss, every LFO free-running. Tempo exists only so the transport can record.
LatencyNothing is played in time, so buffer 512 is fine. Software monitoring is acceptable throughout.
Stereo / phaseFoundation and interval panned ≤25 % apart. Mono check at three points: the beating, the bloom, and the erosion must all survive Width 0 %.
Noise budgetHiss has nowhere to hide under a sustained tone. Run a 30 s silence test before the first take. Fix any noise at source gain — not later in the mix.
Print strategyRecord ten-minute takes in full. Editing means trimming and long crossfades only. Render as 24-bit WAV including the reverb tail.
Failure modeThe static drone (modulators too small or slow — compare minute 1 against minute 5 to diagnose) and the reverb wash (reverb masking a weak tone — mute the return for one minute to check).
SafetyThis is the most fatiguing material in Year 1. Keep phones at the mark, take them off every fifteen minutes, and don't make judgments in the first minute back on. Low frequencies are felt before they register as loud.

3 · The Held Tone Test — certified here

A recurring certification

Like the bench certification, the Held Tone Test is repeated at later points in the course: 3 minutes here · 6 minutes at C2 · 10 minutes at C5 and C7. It is the drone strand's core claim — made testable and repeatable.

Certification

One tone, three minutes, documented

  1. Design and record one single held tone — one voice, no layers, no effects — that is interesting enough to listen to for three unbroken minutes.
  2. Sit through the whole thing, eyes closed, phones at the mark. No skipping. The test is the sitting.
  3. File the take plus one paragraph saying where the life in the tone comes from — which modulator, what rate, what it moves. "It sounds nice" fails. "The cycling envelope opens Wave over 40 seconds while the LFO stirs Timbre every 15" passes.
  4. Routing sketch: one line from source to ears, with the gain setting and where you measured it (body of the tone, not the initial swell).
Pass condition

You sat through the three minutes wanting to keep listening, not checking the clock — and the paragraph shows you understand why it works, in mechanical terms. If it fails, fix it in the patch (rates, amounts, harmonic content). Do not reach for effects.

4 · Build — condensed from Lab Note 002

The full build with every knob position, volume choreography table, and decision check is in Lab Note 002 — The build. Here is the condensed version:

  1. Gridless project — turn the metronome off, set the ruler to minutes, 48 kHz / 24-bit, buffer 512, auto-warp off. Create tracks: FOUNDATION · INTERVAL · AIR · BED · return A — REVERB (8–12 s, 100 % wet).
  2. The voice — start from an init patch, commit to one engine (Harmo is the worked default), attack ≥2 s, full sustain, cutoff dark. The "aliveness" comes from two modulators: LFO → Timbre (~25, unsynced, 15–20 s/cycle) and cycling envelope → Cutoff (~15, 30–60 s loop). Listen to it for three minutes before judging. Save as LAB2 VOICE.
  3. Foundation take — ten minutes, Hold pressed on the root, hands off for two minutes, at most three slow knob moves, return to the start setting by the end. Set gain on the body of the tone, peaks −15 to −12 dBFS.
  4. Interval take — same patch at the chosen interval. Fine-tune the pitch against the looping foundation until the beating lands at the rate in your spec. Ten minutes, hands off. Pan the two takes ≤25 % apart. The beating must survive mono.
  5. Air + bed — air layer: +2 octaves, five minutes, high-pass at 800 Hz, unsynced Echo ≤30 % feedback, fader at −25 dB. Bed: sample 30–60 s of the foundation into the Digitakt II, one looped trigger, −1 octave, slowest LFO on cutoff, resample 1–2 generations, record five quiet minutes.
  6. Level choreography — the arrangement is four volume faders plus one reverb send. Plan it on paper as "which layer is loudest, per minute." Every fade ≥60 s. Use S-curves or live-ridden takes and keep whichever is calmer.
  7. Bloom and erosion — bring the reverb send up from −15 → −6 dB across the middle of the piece. Increase Saturator drive +2 → +8 dB on the bed in the final third, with output compensated so density increases but the meter does not rise.
  8. Subtract and end — fade layers out in reverse order of arrival. Last 60–90 s: foundation alone, then master fade into the reverb tail.
  9. Mix for sustain — use high-pass filters to give each layer its own frequency range (foundation owns below 200 Hz). Mono check three times. One full quiet listen. No limiter. Peaks ≤ −3 dBFS.
  10. Render and step away — normalize off. Listen somewhere else. Write a ten-line notes file. Make one change the next day and save as v2.
  • Prescribed fault — the static drone: set both modulation amounts to zero and record two minutes. Compare that against the real patch at matched volume and write one rule: what is the smallest modulation amount at which the tone sounds alive? This is the lab's failure mode, made on purpose so you can recognize it when it happens by accident.

5 · Diagnosis & report

The full report bundle — ten items, filed in the logbook
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, master peaking −3 dBFS, 8–12 minutes
  • Routing diagram — drawn before patching, corrected after; the diff is the lesson
  • Recording plan — what was printed, when, at what stage
  • Patch notes — engine, modulator rates and amounts, the interval, and the beat rate in Hz
  • Session notes
  • Reflection answers, including the listening comparison — which Lab 005 reference contributed what
  • One removal decision — what was cut and what cutting it did
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule
  • Print map — what exists as audio, automation, patch state, device memory; what is gone
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the system worked like X; after building it, I discovered it worked like Y.”
Pass standard
Minimum passThe full report bundle above, plus the Held Tone Test certified at 3 minutes.
Solid passMinute 1 and minute 5 sound audibly different; no change is catchable as it happens; the beating survives mono; the Saturator thickens the sound while the meter stays steady.
Advanced transferYou can say which Block 1 loop skills carried over and which did not — and you can build the same slowness using a different engine or a different interval.
  1. Where does this piece's slowness actually live — and is that where your hypothesis said it would be?
  2. What did the beating add that one modulated tone on its own could not?
  3. Which volume move mattered most — and was it also the slowest one?
  4. Where did the skills from Lab 003 lead you wrong here?
  5. At what minute did your attention start to drift during the full playback — and what was happening in the sound at that moment?
Worked system diff — the expected granularity

Example: “I thought the piece would work because of the layering. It actually worked because of the detune — the layers were just where the beating happened.”

Track-lab principle

A drone is slow, not static. Remove the clock and beating, modulation, and volume must do all the work.
