Voice as Material

Make a short piece using only your own voice as raw material — captured with the field kit and then transformed into instrument and texture. No other sound sources allowed. Your voice is the most personal thing you can record, and the hardest to capture cleanly. This lab proves that what you can do with it is decided at the microphone, not in the software.

Track lab · S1 · full cycle · builds on the field-recording labs · ties to the extended-vocal listening lineage

Start here
  • Apply the capture discipline from Lab 006 and Lab 008 — mic distance, level, room. Your voice follows the same physics as any field source, just at closer range.
  • Set the field kit on the table: handheld recorder, contact mic, DT 990 Pro for checking takes. Do not monitor live through headphones while you speak.
  • Create a new Live set called LAB-008B-voice and a recordings folder next to it.
  • Use the quietest room you have with the softest surfaces. Recording is the core of this lab — spend at least two-thirds of your time before you open Live.
  • Expected time: one full cycle — a capture session, a transformation session, and a build-and-print session.
The point

Your voice can sound like recognizable speech, like a wordless vocal sound (a phoneme), or like pure texture where the source is completely unidentifiable. Where you place each sound on that line is a deliberate choice, not an accident of how much you processed it. This lab removes the option of using other instruments, so the voice has to carry everything. It also proves one rule you will keep forever: no amount of processing rescues a bad recording. Garbage in, garbage forever.

1 · Listen first — the extended voice

Three artists who turned the voice into an instrument without using any other sound. As you listen to each one, notice where on the continuum they sit — and that they stay there deliberately and consistently.

01 Meredith Monk — “Gotham Lullaby” / “Dolmen Music” voice past words

Monk drops lyrics and uses vowels, leaps, and extended vocal technique instead — the voice as a wordless instrument that still carries real feeling. Meaning comes from tone and shape, not from language.

Listen for
  • How far from speech she is — mostly phonemes and pure tone, almost never a recognizable word.
  • That the absence of words takes nothing away. The voice sounds more expressive, not less.
After — verify

Where on the continuum does she sit — closer to phoneme or closer to pure texture? Mark that position. It is one you can choose for your own piece.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

02 Joan La Barbara — “Voice Is the Original Instrument” / “Circular Song” breath and multiphonics as material

La Barbara uses breath, multiphonics (producing more than one pitch at once with the voice), and circular breathing as raw material — the mechanics of the body made audible. The "instrument" is everything the throat and mouth can do, including what singers usually hide.

Listen for
  • Breath as a musical event — inhale, exhale, the texture of moving air.
  • How a single held vowel grows into a cluster of overtones — texture from one sustained sound.
After — verify

Name three distinct vocal sources she uses (sung tone, breath, multiphonic, and so on). Those are the kinds of capture your own session needs to gather.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

03 Pamela Z — “Bone Music” / live looping works processing as the second instrument

Z loops, layers, and processes her voice live in real time. Recognizable speech fragments, phonemes, and pure texture all appear in the same piece. The chain of processing tools is itself an instrument she performs.

Listen for
  • Intelligible words and pure texture in the same piece — she moves along the continuum deliberately.
  • How a fragment of speech turns into rhythm once it is looped and resampled — repeated and re-recorded — so the words blur into pulse.
After — verify

Find the exact moment a word stops being a word and becomes a sound. That crossover point is the main compositional tool this lab puts in your hands.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

The continuum — the lab's central tool

Intelligible speech (the listener understands words) → phoneme (recognizably vocal but no meaning — vowels, consonants, breath) → pure texture (the source is completely unidentifiable; it is just sound). Every clip you use in the piece sits somewhere on this line. For each clip, decide where — on purpose.

Write one sentence in your logbook before you record anything. Aim for something like: my piece will live mostly at the phoneme end, with one moment of intelligible speech to give the texture a human reference. Write it in your own words. The build will test whether you held to it.

3 · Capture the voice

Step 01

Gather a vocabulary of sources

Goal: a labelled library of clean takes that covers the full range of the continuum. You are building a stock of raw material, not performing a finished piece.

Do this
  1. Record 20 s of room silence first — your noise-floor reference for the whole lab.
  2. Capture, as separate labelled takes, at the spec's distance and angle:
    • Sung tone — one held vowel, 10–20 s, steady pitch (raw material for pads/drones).
    • Whispered text — a few sentences, intimate (the speech end of the continuum).
    • Vowel morphs — slide between ee → ah → oo slowly (formant movement to exploit later).
    • Breath — inhales, exhales, controlled air (pure texture and rhythm source).
    • Consonants / mouth percussion — clicks, t/k/p, sibilant sss (transients for rhythm).
  3. Take one source with the contact mic on the throat or jaw — a completely different, body-conducted timbre with no room in it.
  4. Check each take on phones at the mark before moving on. Log distance, angle, level, and room for each.
Decision checkpoint

Before you process anything: are your peaks in the right range and is the background noise well below the quietest take? If any take has audible plosive thumps (the blast from a "p" or "b" too close to the mic) or harsh sibilance (fizzy "s" sounds), re-record it now. This is your only chance to fix it. Everything that follows assumes clean source recordings.

Logbook — capture conditions

One row per take: source type · mic & distance · angle · peak dBFS · room. This table is part of the pass gate.

4 · Transform — at least three distinct moves

Transformation is travel along the continuum

Each transformation moves the source toward the texture end of the continuum. Document at least three. The goal is not to use as much processing as possible, but to land each clip at a chosen point on the line — sometimes barely changed, sometimes completely unrecognizable.

Step 02

Three transformation families

Do at least three of these, and document each
  1. Formant shifting — take a sung or spoken recording and shift the formants (the resonant peaks that give a voice its character) independently of pitch, using Live's pitch/formant tools or Complex Pro warp + formant. The voice sounds larger-than-human, or impossibly small, while staying recognizably vocal — the phoneme end made strange.
  2. Granular — load a vowel or breath recording into the Granulator (or a grain freeze device). Granular processing slices sound into tiny fragments ("grains") and rebuilds them. Hold one grain to make a static pad from a single instant of sound, or scatter many grains for a cloud-like texture. This is the strongest push toward pure texture.
  3. Resampling generations — bounce a transformed clip to audio, reprocess it, then bounce again. Each generation (re-recording your own output) erodes the voice a little more. Count how many generations a clip survives before it stops sounding like a voice at all.
  4. Freeze into a pad — time-stretch a held vowel to many times its original length in Complex Pro, so a 2-second tone becomes a 40-second drone with the formants smeared into something resembling harmony.
  5. Gate into rhythm — run breath or consonant sounds through a rhythmic gate, or slice them into a Drum Rack. Mouth transients become a pulse with no drum machine involved.

Step 03

Build the piece from voice-derived material only

Goal: a 2–4 minute piece where the bed, the movement, and any rhythm all come from your voice — and the continuum is used as the structure of the piece.

Do this
  1. Bed — use a frozen or stretched vowel as the sustained foundation, the role a drone would normally play.
  2. Movement — granular clouds or formant sweeps that drift over the bed. Keep them off a strict rhythmic grid.
  3. Rhythm (optional) — gated breath or sliced consonants if the piece wants a pulse. Still voice-only.
  4. Anchor — decide whether one moment of intelligible speech appears to give the listener a human reference point, or whether you stay entirely in texture. Both are valid. The choice is the composition.
  5. Mix so the layers do not fight each other (the bed owns the low frequencies, breath owns the highs). Check the mix in mono to make sure the texture survives. Confirm that every track traces back to a voice recording.
  • The crutch test: mute everything and bring tracks back one at a time. If any track sounds like a synth pad or a drum machine filling a gap, ask whether it still reads as voice-derived. If you cannot trace it to a recorded take, it does not belong.

5 · The prescribed fault

Step 06

Ruin a capture on purpose, then try to save it

Goal: show yourself that capture faults cannot be undone. Make a bad recording on purpose, then try and fail to fix it in software — so you understand why the microphone matters.

Induce the fault
  1. Record a line too close and on-axis — mouth nearly on the capsule, no angle. Hit hard plosives (p, b) and a long sibilant (sss). Push the input so the loudest plosive just clips.
  2. Now try to save it in Live: use a high-pass filter to cut the plosive thump, a de-esser to tame the sibilance, and formant-shifting to hide the proximity boom.
  3. Play the damaged take side-by-side with a clean take of the same line at matched loudness. Document what survived and what did not.

6 · Pass gate

Certification

Voice-only piece, conditions logged, three transforms documented

  1. A short piece (2–4 min) built from your voice only — every track traceable to a take, no melodic-instrument crutch.
  2. The capture conditions table filled: source, mic, distance, angle, level, room for each take used.
  3. At least three distinct transformations documented with settings and the continuum position each produced.
  4. A one-line statement of where on the continuum the piece lives, and why that was a choice.
Pass condition

A listener can tell the piece is made of voice — even where the voice is pure texture — and you can prove it: every sound traces to a logged take, and all three transformations can be reproduced from your notes. If the piece felt like it needed a synth to be finished, the problem is earlier in the chain. The captures were too thin, not the processing too weak.

Report bundle — filed in the logbook
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, 2–4 minutes, voice-derived material only
  • Capture conditions table — one row per take used
  • Transformation log — minimum three, with settings and continuum positions
  • Hypothesis sentence and the continuum-position statement
  • Fault entry — the ruined capture, the failed rescue, the “garbage in” rule
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the piece would be made in the processing; it was made at the microphone.”
Pass standard
Minimum passThe bundle above is complete: a voice-only piece, capture conditions logged, three transformations documented.
Solid passThe continuum is used as structure — clips move between speech, phoneme, and texture deliberately — and no track reads as a non-vocal stand-in.
Advanced transferYou can name the one capture decision that most shaped the piece, and re-capture a source to land a clip at a different continuum position on purpose.
  1. Where on the continuum did the piece end up, and did that match your hypothesis?
  2. Which capture decision — distance, angle, room choice, or using the contact mic — mattered most to how the final piece sounds?
  3. Which transformation pushed a clip furthest from sounding like a voice? At what point did you lose the human reference, and was that the right call?
  4. Did you include any intelligible speech? What did its presence — or absence — do to how a listener relates to the texture around it?
Worked system diff — the expected granularity

”I thought the granular cloud would be the core of the piece. It turned out to be the one whispered line — the texture only meant something because there was recognizable human voice to compare it to.”

Track-lab principle

The quality of your voice recordings is decided at the microphone. Capture cleanly, so that every transformation you make is a creative choice — not a desperate repair.
