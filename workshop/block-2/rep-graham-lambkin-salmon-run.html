Replicate: the home as concrète studio

Copy one clear section of Graham Lambkin — “Salmon Run”. Build a short study from ordinary domestic recordings used as the primary material, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill is about one decision: use ordinary domestic field recordings as the primary musical source — concrète made from the home. Not a far-off landscape, not a dramatic event, but the sink running, a door, footsteps on a stair, the room itself. The mundane recording is the material, presented as music rather than as a sample to be processed beyond recognition. Do only the steps that make the ordinary audible as the subject.

Before you start
  • Gear: a recorder or phone, or a mic into M4 input 1 with gain by the lab-001 ledger; DT 990 Pro for editing; the lab-006 capture log, with a column for which room
  • New set saved as REP-SALMON-RUN — no tempo; this is timed in seconds and follows household pace
  • Domestic sounds run quiet then spike (a tap is soft, a dropped pan is loud) — set gain for the loudest likely event, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS; phones at the lab-001 mark
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — one walk through the home recording, one edit pass, one short study, then file it and stop
  • What you will make: a short replica of Graham Lambkin — “Salmon Run”. Focus on this effect: ordinary household recordings standing as the music itself.
  • What you will turn off: the urge to “fix” or beautify the domestic sounds, and the urge to process them past recognition.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: ordinary domestic field recordings used as the primary musical source (concrète from the home).
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are which home and which sounds, not the everyday being dressed up into something else.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Graham Lambkin — “Salmon Run”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What kind of sounds are these?
    Answer

    Plainly domestic — water, dishes, doors, rooms, a household going about itself. Nothing exotic, nothing dramatic. You recognise them immediately as home sounds, and that recognition is part of the work.

  2. How much have they been processed?
    Answer

    Surprisingly little. The sounds stay recognisable; they are arranged and juxtaposed more than transformed. The composition is in the selection and placement, not in burying the source under effects.

  3. Why use such ordinary material?
    Answer

    Because attention reframes it. Recorded and placed deliberately, a dripping tap or a kettle becomes a musical event. The piece insists the home already contains music if you record and arrange it as such.

  4. What gives it a sense of place?
    Answer

    The room tone and the incidental detail — the hum that ties takes together, the sense that one person is moving through one house. The recordings are not scrubbed clean; their context is the glue.

  5. Where is the composition, if it is just home recordings?
    Answer

    In what is kept, what is cut, and the order. Choosing which mundane moments earn a place, trimming them, and sequencing them into a passage — those decisions are the form.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: one 60–90 second study built entirely from ordinary recordings of your own home, recognisable as such.
  • 8–12 domestic captures from at least two rooms: water, dishes, doors, footsteps, the room itself.
  • The interest comes from selection, juxtaposition and placement, not from processing the sounds away.
  • Editing, level and light context only — heavy effects that hide the domestic origin break the lesson.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the home rule

Phase focus: the reference and the home rule. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag the reference section in, and loop it at ≈ −12 dBFS with Audio Effects → Utility.
  2. Write the rule in full: every sound comes from my own home, recorded plainly, and stays recognisable as a home sound.
  3. List candidate sources by room — kitchen: tap, kettle, dishes, fridge hum; hall: door, stairs, footsteps; living room: chair, page, the room itself.
  4. Pick at least two rooms so the study has a sense of moving through a place.
  5. Decide the membership test: if a stranger could not tell this came from a house, it does not belong.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Graham Lambkin — “Salmon Run” and use audio clips, light editing, and clip envelopes — no synthesis.
  2. Build the phase goal — the reference and the home rule — as the smallest Live set that proves ordinary domestic field recordings used as the primary musical source (concrète from the home).
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects whether the home stays recognisable.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: capture domestic sounds into recorder buffers, then translate the reference and the home rule into Flex machines, slices, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the control path so it demonstrates ordinary domestic field recordings used as the primary musical source (concrète from the home), not just the surface of Graham Lambkin — “Salmon Run”.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main move in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the home stays recognisable.
Expected result

The reference loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, the home-only rule is on paper, and a candidate list across at least two rooms is written before any capture.

Phase 2 · ≈ 35 min

Record the household plainly

Phase focus: record the household. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Record 8–12 domestic captures, 10–30 seconds each, gain by the lab-001 ledger set for the loudest likely event, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Let the action play out naturally — fill a kettle, wash a few dishes, climb the stairs — do not perform it, just do it near the mic.
  3. Capture at least one stretch of plain room tone per room; this is the glue that ties takes to a place.
  4. Keep the incidental detail — a creak, a distant voice, the hum — do not chase a clinical recording.
  5. Log each take with its room — the lab-006 log gains a room column here.
  6. Trim each capture to the moment that earns its keep, but leave a little room tone on the edges.

Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

The study — select, place, do not disguise

Phase focus: the study. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Lay your captures in Arrangement and build one 60–90 second passage by selection and placement — keep the strong moments, cut the rest.
  2. Use room tone underneath to join takes, so the listener feels they are inside one continuous house.
  3. Juxtapose for meaning: a tap answering a clock, footsteps over a kettle — let ordinary events become musical by their order.
  4. Resist processing: a touch of level and maybe a single gentle EQ is the most; if you want reverb to “improve” it, stop.
  5. Keep at least one moment where a plain domestic sound stands alone and is clearly itself.
  6. Print the study to one audio track named PRINT.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your study at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Can a listener tell these are home sounds?
    Answer

    That is the pass condition. Play it to fresh ears and they should say “this is someone's house” without prompting. If they hear abstract texture instead, you have processed the source past recognition — back the effects off until the home returns. The record passes this test instantly; its whole charm is that you know exactly what you are hearing and yet it works as music.

  • Does the ordinary read as composed, not random?
    Answer

    In a working study the mundane sounds feel chosen and placed — there is intent in the order, a sense that this tap follows that door for a reason. If it feels like a clip dump, the selection and sequence are doing too little. Trim harder, build a through-line, and let juxtaposition carry meaning, the way the reference turns a household into a passage.
