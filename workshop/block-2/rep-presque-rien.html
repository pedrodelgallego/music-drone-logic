Replicate: the unedited frame

Copy one clear section of Luc Ferrari — “Presque Rien No. 1 (Le lever du jour au bord de la mer)”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: one fixed perspective, long takes, chronological order preserved, and every edit buried inside steady-state sound where the material cannot show a seam. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone, fully charged with storage checked — a thirty-minute take eats batteries, and a dead battery is a dawn that never existed; mic into M4 input 1 works only if your one position is within cable reach. DT 990 Pro for the edit
  • New set saved as REP-PRESQUE-RIEN — no tempo; this lesson is measured in what you leave out
  • Field line: ask before recording people within earshot of a window or balcony, secure the recorder against falls and weather, monitor later at a moderate fixed level
  • What you will make: a short replica of Luc Ferrari — “Presque Rien No. 1 (Le lever du jour au bord de la mer)”. Focus on this effect: hours of a fishing-village dawn pass as twenty-one minutes that sound untouched - documentary on the surface, composition hidden underneath.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: one fixed perspective, long takes, chronological order preserved, and every edit buried inside steady-state sound where the material cannot show a seam.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Luc Ferrari — “Presque Rien No. 1 (Le lever du jour au bord de la mer)”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many edits can you actually find?
    Answer

    Almost none — and most of what you flag as edits are just events: an engine starting, a voice arriving. The real cuts sit inside water sounds and insect hum, where nothing was changing and nothing shows.

  2. How much time passes inside the piece?
    Answer

    Far more than plays. A village goes from asleep to working, which takes hours. The piece delivers that in twenty-one minutes that never feel rushed. The compression is total and completely hidden.

  3. What is the loudest event, and who decided that?
    Answer

    Whatever you heard — an engine, a voice, a machine — Ferrari decided it would be there. Everything in the piece survived a cull; nothing is accidental.

  4. Where is the recordist?
    Answer

    Nowhere audible — no handling noise, no footsteps, no breathing, no repositioning. One fixed perspective, held for the whole piece. That is what lets the listener forget a microphone exists.

  5. What did Ferrari actually do?
    Answer

    Almost nothing audible. He chose a place, a perspective, and a starting moment, then removed hours without leaving fingerprints. The piece argues those choices alone are composition. Presque rien (“almost nothing”) names the method, not just the result.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: 4–5 minutes cut from a 30+ minute take that a listener accepts as unbroken real time. Solo-flipped against REF, both should share the same feeling of an untouched surface with invisible authorship.
  • One position, one take. The recorder does not move once started, and neither does its gain.
  • Chronological order stays intact. Compression happens only by cutting things out. Presque Rien never reorders time.
  • Every cut hidden inside steady-state sound — room tone, traffic wash, rain, fridge hum. No effects, no audible fades.

How the piece works: one fixed microphone position, one long uninterrupted take, a paper map of its events, and cuts hidden inside sounds that never change — mixed at ≈ −12 dBFS.

one place · one position · 30+ min → recorder or phone · gain set once → paper map · keep / cut decisions → joins hidden in steady sound · 4–5 min

Thirty minutes become four. The kept sections join inside steady background sound — the cuts land where nothing was changing, so nothing shows. The listener hears the short bottom bar and believes it is the full top one.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the frame

Phase focus: the reference and the frame. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag Presque Rien No.
  2. 1 in, and loop its opening minutes.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Choose your one position: a window, a balcony, a kitchen counter.
  5. The test is steady background plus occasional events — a place where something hums or washes continuously.
  6. Choose your starting moment the way Ferrari chose dawn: a span where the place is in transition — the street waking, a machine mid-cycle, dinner being cooked one room away.
  7. Write a prediction in the logbook: what you believe the place will do over thirty minutes, in five lines.
  8. The edit session will grade this prediction.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Luc Ferrari — “Presque Rien No. 1 (Le lever du jour au bord de la mer)” (1970) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.

  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.

Phase 2 · ≈ 35 min, mostly unattended

The take — press record and disappear

Phase focus: the take — press record and disappear. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Set the gain once, before the take, on the loudest thing the place is likely to do — by the lab-001 ledger if you record through the M4. It does not move again.
  2. Place the recorder, don’t hold it.
  3. A folded cloth underneath kills surface coupling; a windowsill or shelf beats your hand every time.
  4. Record 30+ minutes.
  5. Do not pause, do not ride, do not check the screen.
  6. Leave the room if you can.
  7. Log one line, lab-006 style: place · position · level · start time.
  8. While it runs, do something else entirely — the take is better off without your attention.

Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The map — every event on paper

Phase focus: the map — every event on paper. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the take onto a track named TAKE, fader at 0 dB.
  2. Scrub end to end and write the event map: timestamp · event · keep/cut, one line per event.
  3. Spot-play anything the waveform hides.
  4. Mark 6–8 keep regions totalling five to six minutes — events worth keeping plus the steady wash on both sides of each;
  5. the wash is where the joins will live.
  6. Check the map against your phase-1 prediction and write one line on the difference — what the place did that you did not expect.
  7. Order check: the keeps must be in take order.
  8. If you are tempted to move a late event earlier, resist — removal is the only tool this record permits.

Phase 4 · ≈ 30 min

The compression — hide every join

Phase focus: the compression — hide every join. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Assemble the keep regions in order on TAKE, butted end to end in Arrangement.
  2. At each join, find steady wash on both sides, match their levels with clip gain first, then draw a 1–4 second crossfade entirely inside the wash.
  3. Never fade across an event.
  4. Play each join solo at the mark with eyes closed, three times.
  5. If you can point at it, move it or lengthen it.
  6. Play the whole 4–5 minutes end to end, no touching, and write down every moment you suspect a seam — then fix only those.
  7. If a second listener is available, play it for them and ask one question: “how many edits?” Record the answer in the logbook.

Expected result

Four to five minutes that play as one unbroken stretch of real time — a morning’s worth of place with the hours silently removed, and a listener’s edit count wildly below the truth.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Can a listener find your cuts?
    Answer

    They should not be able to. The honest test is asking another person “how many edits?” and having them answer “none” or a number far below the truth. Every cut they do find goes back to phase 4: either the wash levels did not match, or the crossfade crossed an event. Relisten to the record with the same question. Its seams have stayed hidden for fifty years — that is the standard.

  • What did you keep — and what does that choice say?
    Answer

    Your keep list is a portrait — of your attention, and of what you thought the place was about. Ferrari kept engines, voices, and water and called it almost nothing. But what you keep is everything: two people at the same window would cut two different mornings from the same take. Write one line about what your keeps say the place is about. That line, not the take, is the composition.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2, and the event map seeds the one-minute miniature in Lab 006.
  • Carries into the lab: this record’s whole argument is that the composition happens at capture time — place, perspective, and starting moment were the decisions. The edit only protected them.
