Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the unedited frame

Copy one clear section of Luc Ferrari — “Presque Rien No. 1 (Le lever du jour au bord de la mer)”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: one fixed perspective, long takes, chronological order preserved, and every edit buried inside steady-state sound where the material cannot show a seam. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone, fully charged with storage checked — a thirty-minute take eats batteries, and a dead battery is a dawn that never existed; mic into M4 input 1 works only if your one position is within cable reach. DT 990 Pro for the edit
  • New set saved as REP-PRESQUE-RIEN — no tempo; this lesson is measured in what you leave out
  • Field line: ask before recording people within earshot of a window or balcony, secure the recorder against falls and weather, monitor later at a moderate fixed level
  • What you will make: a short replica of Luc Ferrari — “Presque Rien No. 1 (Le lever du jour au bord de la mer)”. Focus on this effect: hours of a fishing-village dawn pass as twenty-one minutes that sound untouched - documentary on the surface, composition hidden underneath.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: one fixed perspective, long takes, chronological order preserved, and every edit buried inside steady-state sound where the material cannot show a seam.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Luc Ferrari — “Presque Rien No. 1 (Le lever du jour au bord de la mer)”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many edits can you actually find?
    Answer

    Almost none - and most of what you flag as edits are events: an engine starting, a voice arriving. The real joins sit inside the water and the insect wash, where nothing was changing and nothing shows.

  2. How much time passes inside the piece?
    Answer

    Far more than plays - a village goes from asleep to working, which takes hours, and the piece delivers it in twenty-one minutes that never feel hurried. The compression is total and totally hidden.

  3. What is the loudest event, and who decided that?
    Answer

    Whatever it was on your listen - an engine, a voice, a machine - Ferrari decided it would be there, because everything that survived into the piece survived a cull.

  4. Where is the recordist?
    Answer

    Nowhere audible - no handling, no footsteps, no breath, no repositioning. One perspective, held for the whole piece, which is what lets the listener forget a microphone exists.

  5. What did Ferrari actually do?
    Answer

    Almost nothing audible - he chose a place, a perspective, a starting moment, and then removed hours without leaving fingerprints. The piece argues that those choices alone are composition; “presque rien” names the method, not the result.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: 4–5 minutes cut from a 30+ minute take that a listener accepts as unbroken real time — a solo-flip against REF lands on the same untouched surface and the same invisible authorship.
  • One position, one take — the recorder does not move once started, and neither does its gain.
  • Chronological order preserved; compression happens only by removal. Presque Rien never reorders time.
  • Every cut hidden inside steady-state sound — room tone, traffic wash, rain, fridge hum. No effects, no audible fades.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one fixed microphone position, one long untouched take, a paper map of its events, and crossfades buried inside the sounds that never change, mixed at ≈ −12 dBFS.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the frame

Phase focus: the reference and the frame. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag Presque Rien No.
  2. Live 1 in, and loop its opening minutes.
  3. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Live Choose your one position: a window, a balcony, a kitchen counter.
  5. Live The test is steady background plus occasional events — a place where something hums or washes continuously.
  6. Live Choose your starting moment the way Ferrari chose dawn: a span where the place is in transition — the street waking, a machine mid-cycle, dinner being cooked one room away.
  7. Logbook Write a prediction in the logbook: what you believe the place will do over thirty minutes, in five lines.
  8. Logbook The edit session will grade this prediction.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one position and one start time are chosen and written down, and a five-line prediction of the take exists on paper.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
No position seems “interesting enough”Hunting for events instead of a framePick the steadiest place, not the busiest — the wash is what will hide your cuts later
REF feels richer than your room ever couldUntrimmed level conflated with placeTrim to ≈ −12 dBFS and judge again; half the richness was loudness
Phase 2 · ≈ 35 min, mostly unattended

The take — press record and disappear

Phase focus: the take — press record and disappear. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Set the gain once, before the take, on the loudest thing the place is likely to do — by the lab-001 ledger if you record through the M4. It does not move again.
  2. Recorder Place the recorder, don’t hold it.
  3. Recorder A folded cloth underneath kills surface coupling; a windowsill or shelf beats your hand every time.
  4. Recorder Record 30+ minutes.
  5. Recorder Do not pause, do not ride, do not check the screen.
  6. Recorder Leave the room if you can.
  7. Recorder Log one line, lab-006 style: place · position · level · start time.
  8. Recorder While it runs, do something else entirely — the take is better off without your attention.
Expected result

One unbroken 30+ minute file with no handling, no gain moves, and no trace of you — a slab of real time, captured at a level decided once.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Thumps and rustle through the takeThe recorder was held, or coupled to a live surfaceSet it down on cloth and step away; your body is the loudest thing you own
A loud event clipped mid-takeGain set on the quiet morning, not the loud oneField clipping is forever — re-run the take with gain set for the place’s loudest plausible event
Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The map — every event on paper

Phase focus: the map — every event on paper. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Import the take onto a track named TAKE, fader at 0 dB.
  2. Logbook Scrub end to end and write the event map: timestamp · event · keep/cut, one line per event.
  3. Logbook Spot-play anything the waveform hides.
  4. Logbook Mark 6–8 keep regions totalling five to six minutes — events worth keeping plus the steady wash on both sides of each;
  5. Live the wash is where the joins will live.
  6. Logbook Check the map against your phase-1 prediction and write one line on the difference — what the place did that you did not expect.
  7. Logbook Order check: the keeps must be in take order.
  8. Logbook If you are tempted to move a late event earlier, resist — removal is the only tool this record permits.
Expected result

A complete event map, 6–8 keep regions chosen with wash handles around each, and one logbook line about the gap between prediction and place.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Everything seems worth keepingJudging events alone, not the piece’s lengthThe target is four minutes — rank the keeps and cut from the bottom until the math works
The keeps have no steady sound around themRegions drawn tight to the eventsWiden every region until it includes wash on both sides; joins need somewhere to hide
Phase 4 · ≈ 30 min

The compression — hide every join

Phase focus: the compression — hide every join. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Assemble the keep regions in order on TAKE, butted end to end in Arrangement.
  2. Live At each join, find steady wash on both sides, match their levels with clip gain first, then draw a 1–4 second crossfade entirely inside the wash.
  3. Live Never fade across an event.
  4. Live Play each join solo at the mark with eyes closed, three times.
  5. Live If you can point at it, move it or lengthen it.
  6. Logbook Play the whole 4–5 minutes end to end, no touching, and write down every moment you suspect a seam — then fix only those.
  7. Logbook If a second listener is available, play it for them and ask one question: “how many edits?” Record the answer in the logbook.
Expected result

Four to five minutes that play as one unbroken stretch of real time — a morning’s worth of place with the hours silently removed, and a listener’s edit count wildly below the truth.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A join is audible as a soft “bump” in the washLevels not matched before the crossfadeClip-gain both sides to the same wash level first; the fade only hides what already matches
The piece feels like a highlight reel, not real timeToo many events kept, too little wash between themPut wash back — the boring stretches are what make the events believable
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Can a listener find your cuts?
    Answer

    They should not be able to — the honest test is another person and the question “how many edits?”, and the right wrong answer is “none” or a number far below the truth. Every join they do find goes back to phase 4: the wash did not match, or the fade crossed an event. Relisten to the record with the same question; its seams have stayed hidden for fifty years, which is the standard.

  • What did you keep — and what does that choice say?
    Answer

    Read your keep list as a portrait, because it is one: of your attention, and of what you believed the place was about. Ferrari kept engines, voices, and water and called it almost nothing — but the keeping was everything; two recordists at the same window would cut two different mornings from the same take. Write one line naming what your keeps say the place is about; that line, not the take, is the composition.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2, and the event map seeds the one-minute miniature in Lab 006.
  • Carries into the lab: the étude’s rule that the writing happens at capture time is this record’s whole argument — place, perspective, and starting moment were the composition, and the edit only protected them.
