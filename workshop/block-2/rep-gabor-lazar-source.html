Replicate: the generator as the source

Copy one clear section of Gábor Lázár — “Source”. Build a short study from purely synthetic material — no acoustic recording anywhere — and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill is about one decision: build a whole piece from purely synthetic source material, with no acoustic recording anywhere in the chain. The raw generator — an oscillator, a noise source, a feedback loop — is foregrounded as the source rather than hidden behind samples of the world. Do only the steps that make that foregrounded generator audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: one synth voice you trust — MicroFreak, an Operator/Wavetable instance, or a feedback patch; DT 990 Pro for judging; the lab-006 capture log, repurposed with a column for which generator and which setting rather than place and distance
  • New set saved as REP-SOURCE — a tempo grid is optional; the study is timed in seconds, and the pulse can come from the generator itself
  • Synthetic peaks are stable but can run hot fast — set gain on the loudest single tone you intend to use, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS; phones at the lab-001 mark, because pure tones invite the knob up
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — one generator round, one shaping pass, one short study, then file it and stop
  • What you will make: a short replica of Gábor Lázár — “Source”. Focus on this effect: a piece built entirely from synthetic material where the generator itself is the subject.
  • What you will turn off: every sample of the world, every field recording, every disguise that makes a synth sound “organic.”
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a whole piece from purely synthetic source material, no acoustic recording — the raw generator is foregrounded as the source.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are generator choices or rig limits, not a recording sneaking back in.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Gábor Lázár — “Source”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Can you hear a single recording of the physical world anywhere?
    Answer

    No — and that absence is the point. There is no room, no air, no object being struck. Everything has the seamless, edgeless quality of a signal that was generated, not captured. The ear keeps reaching for a cause and finds only the machine.

  2. What is the “instrument,” if there is no recording?
    Answer

    The generator itself — an oscillator, a noise source, a feedback network. It is not pretending to be a flute or a drum. The raw output of the synthesis process is the material, presented plainly.

  3. How does the sound change over time?
    Answer

    By moving a parameter, not by editing clips. Pitch, timbre, density, or filter cutoff sweeps; the same generator is heard from many angles. The composition lives in how the controls are driven, not in arranging recorded fragments.

  4. Why keep it synthetic on purpose?
    Answer

    Because the constraint forces honesty. With no recording to lean on, every interesting moment has to be earned from the generator. The piece becomes a study of what one synthetic source can do when nothing is allowed to hide it.

  5. Where is the composition, if it is one machine?
    Answer

    In the parameter path and the cuts between states. Choosing the generator, choosing which controls move, deciding how fast and how far, and where one state hands to the next — those decisions are the form.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: turn the oscillator Type to choose the engine, hold Shift for the second function of each knob, and use the recorded mod sequence to drive a parameter hands-free during the take.
  • Sound target: one 60-second study where every sound is synthetic and the generator is plainly the subject.
  • One generator, three or four distinct states reached by moving parameters — no samples, no field recordings, no acoustic layers.
  • The interest comes from driving the controls, never from arranging recorded clips.
  • Level and the parameter moves only — if you reach for a sampler of the world, you have left the lesson.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the one-generator rule

Phase focus: the reference and the one-generator rule. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag the reference section in, and loop it at ≈ −12 dBFS with Audio Effects → Utility.
  2. Pick exactly one generator — MicroFreak oscillator, a single Operator/Wavetable patch, or a feedback loop — and write it at the top of the logbook page.
  3. Write the rule in full: no acoustic recording enters this set — no field clips, no sampled objects, no “organic” layers.
  4. List the parameters you are allowed to move: usually pitch, timbre/wavetable position, filter cutoff, and one density or modulation amount.
  5. Mute everything else on the device — one voice, plainly heard.
  6. Decide the membership test: if I cannot trace a sound back to this generator, it does not belong in the study.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Gábor Lázár — “Source” and use one instrument — Operator or Wavetable — plus MIDI clips and parameter automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — the reference and the one-generator rule — as the smallest Live set that proves a whole piece from purely synthetic source material, no acoustic recording — the raw generator is foregrounded as the source.
  3. Keep the single generator on a named track, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects whether the generator stays foregrounded.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: with no acoustic source, build from the internal test tones, white/pink noise, or a single resampled synth pass, then drive Flex/Static machines, scenes, and the crossfader.
  2. Program the control path so it demonstrates a whole piece from purely synthetic source material, no acoustic recording — the raw generator is foregrounded as the source, not just the surface of Gábor Lázár — “Source”.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main parameter move in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the synthetic source stays foregrounded.
Expected result

The reference loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one generator is chosen and named, and the no-recording rule plus the allowed-parameters list are on paper before any sound is shaped.

Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

Find the states — drive the generator, not the keyboard

Phase focus: find the states. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Hold one note and sweep each allowed parameter slowly from minimum to maximum, listening for where the timbre changes character — those edges are your states.
  2. Mark three or four distinct states: e.g. pure tone, gritted/folded tone, noise-edge, and a fast-modulated state.
  3. For each state, find the exact knob positions and write them in the log next to the generator name.
  4. Record a 10–15 second pass of each state alone, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, into named clips: STATE·1, STATE·2.
  5. Each clip must be the generator only — no reverb tail standing in for a room, no sample underneath.
  6. If a state sounds “recorded,” find what is faking it and remove it; the synthetic origin should be obvious.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Gábor Lázár — “Source” and use the single instrument plus clip envelopes and resampling.

  4. Record or resample each state to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.

Three or four named state clips, each the generator alone, each clearly different from its neighbors, with knob positions logged so any state is repeatable.

Phase 3 · ≈ 35 min

The study — compose the parameter path

Phase focus: the study. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Build one 60-second study in Arrangement that moves through your states in an order you choose.
  2. Prefer driving the live parameter (record automation or the MicroFreak mod sequence) over butting state clips end to end — motion beats montage here.
  3. Decide each transition: a hard cut between states, or a slow morph as a knob crosses the boundary; both are valid, choose on purpose.
  4. Keep one parameter as the “lead” the listener follows — pitch, or cutoff, or density — so the study has a through-line.
  5. Resist adding anything from outside the generator; if the study needs more, find it in an unused corner of the same source.
  6. Print the study to one audio track named PRINT.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your study at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Can a listener tell every sound is synthetic?
    Answer

    That is the pass condition. Play the study to fresh ears and ask “does any of this sound recorded?” A working version gets a clear “no — it is all machine.” If something reads as a room, an object, or a field, find it and cut it; one stray recording undoes the whole premise. The record passes this test from the first second — there is nothing to mistake for the world.

  • Is the generator the subject, or just a means?
    Answer

    In a working study the listener is plainly hearing a generator being explored, not a synth pretending to be an instrument. The interest is in the timbre and its motion, not in melody or a recognizable patch. If your study leans on a tune to stay interesting, the source is not foregrounded enough — strip the melody and make the parameter motion carry it, the way the reference does.
