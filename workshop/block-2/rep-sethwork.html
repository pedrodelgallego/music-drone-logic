Replicate: thickness from detune

Copy one clear section of Phill Niblock — “Sethwork” (Touch Three, 2006); Seth Josel, e-bowed acoustic guitar. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches how the record works: the same sustained pitch stacked many times, with each copy tuned slightly apart (measured in cents — hundredths of a semitone). Every pair of near-identical pitches beats (pulses) at its own rate, and the sum of all those unsynchronized beatings is the churn you hear. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring; the Digitakt II rests. You do not own an e-bowed guitar: one steady MicroFreak tone, printed once and duplicated with clip detune, is the honest analogue — the mechanism is the cents, not the strings
  • New set saved as REP-SETHWORK — tempo irrelevant; the only clock in this piece is the beat rates the detunes make
  • Eight stacked copies of one tone sum well above one copy’s level — set gain for the full stack, not the single layer, and keep phones at the lab-001 mark; break every 15 minutes
  • What you will make: a short replica of Phill Niblock — “Sethwork” (Touch Three, 2006); Seth Josel, e-bowed acoustic guitar. Focus on this effect: many close-pitched sustained guitar tones fuse into one massive cluster that churns from the inside, though no player adds vibrato or movement.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: the same sustained pitch stacked many times, each copy cents apart - every pair of near-unisons beats at its own rate, and the sum of unsynced beatings is the churn.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Phill Niblock — “Sethwork” (Touch Three, 2006); Seth Josel, e-bowed acoustic guitar. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many layers are sounding?
    Answer

    More than you can count, by design. Your count will keep changing as the beatings bring different layers to the front.

  2. Is anyone playing vibrato?
    Answer

    No — and that is the question the piece is built around. The wavering you hear belongs to no single layer. It is interference between layers, pulsing at rates set by their pitch differences. Each guitar tone, heard alone, would be completely still.

  3. Does the pitch of the whole cluster move?
    Answer

    Barely, and slowly. The center holds while the inside churns. What sounds like a harmonic change is usually the beating speeding up or slowing down as layers drift slightly relative to each other. The form is texture changing, not notes changing.

  4. What did the e-bow remove from the guitar?
    Answer

    The attack. An e-bow (a small electromagnetic device) sustains the string continuously, so the guitar loses its pluck and becomes a pure held tone. That is why it stops sounding like a guitar at all.

  5. Why does it sound like an organ when it is one guitar?
    Answer

    Because an instrument's identity lives in its attack and in being one single voice — and both are gone here. Strip the pluck, stack many copies, and what remains is a mass of sustained tone with the steadiness of organ pipes. The instrument dissolves into spectrum.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: solo-flipped against REF at matched loudness, both should land on the same quality — a mass that churns continuously even though every layer in it is perfectly still.
  • One pitch only. Every layer is the same note, tuned slightly apart in cents. A second note turns this into a chord lesson, not this one.
  • No modulation anywhere — no LFO (automatic knob-mover), no vibrato, no automation. If you add motion by hand, the experiment is void.
  • Equal levels across all layers. Raising one layer makes the cluster collapse back toward a single loud tone with quieter companions.

How the piece works: one steady tone recorded once, duplicated to seven or eight tracks, each copy tuned a different number of cents apart, all faders equal. The beating between these near-identical pitches does all the moving.

MicroFreak · one held tone · no modulation → printed once · duplicated ×7–8 → each clip detuned · −11 to +13 cents → equal faders · the churn · ≈ −12 dBFS

The same tone shown three ways. One copy is a flat line — nothing moves. Two copies a few cents apart beat at one steady rate. Eight copies beat at every rate their pairs produce, and the sum is a texture that churns without any single layer moving. The motion is created by the number of copies.

The master tone — one take, completely still

Do this

  3. Filter halfway open — audible harmonics, no buzz.

Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The stack — seven copies, a cents plan, equal faders

Phase focus: the stack — seven copies, a cents plan, equal faders. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Write the cents plan in the logbook first.
  2. A working spread: −11, −7, −3, 0, +4, +8, +13 — uneven gaps on purpose, so no two pairs beat at the same rate.
  3. Duplicate LAYER (⌘ D) until you have 7–8 tracks, each holding a copy of the same clip, all starting together at 1.1.1.
  4. On each clip, set Detune in the clip’s Audio tab to its planned cents value.
  5. (Printing 7–8 separate MicroFreak takes nudged by the fine-tune works too and is closer to Niblock’s practice — but the clip route keeps every layer provably identical except in pitch, which is the cleaner experiment.)

  7. Play the stack and check the sum: peaks ≈ −12 dBFS on the master.
  8. Trim all faders together if it runs hot — together, always;

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The solo test — prove where the motion lives

Phase focus: the solo test — prove where the motion lives. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the full stack for one minute.
  2. Then solo any single layer and hold it for thirty seconds: dead still.
  3. Unsolo: the churn returns instantly.
  4. Do this three times with different layers; write what the switch is switching.
  5. Mute layers one at a time, listening to the churn thin out — each removal deletes that layer’s pairs.
  6. Note the count at which the texture stops feeling massive;
  7. that number is your version of the record’s density decision.
  8. A/B the full stack against REF at matched loudness, judging only the internal motion: are your beat rates as varied, as layered, as theirs?
  9. Revise your phase-1 beat-rate count for the record now that you know what to listen for.
  10. Print the full stack, 4–5 minutes, as one stereo file — the piece exists in the sum, and the sum deserves a take.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your stack at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Solo one layer, then unmute all. Where did the motion come from?
    Answer

    From interference between layers, not from modulation. Each layer is provably still — you listened to the master take alone and wrote nothing moves — yet the stack churns, because every pair of detuned copies beats at a rate set by their pitch gap. Nothing in the session creates motion directly. The motion is what happens when near-identical waves are added together. Relisten to the record’s cluster knowing this: every wobble is two specific layers disagreeing, even when you cannot name which two.

  • Could you have made this churn with an LFO instead?
    Answer

    Not this churn. An LFO (automatic knob-mover) imposes one rate from outside. The stack produces dozens of rates from inside, none synchronized, drifting in and out of step. That is why the texture never repeats and never settles. It is the audible difference between modulated and interfering sound, and it is why the record stays alive for twenty minutes with no events. Relisten to one minute of your stack and try to find a moment that repeats exactly. Then try the same on “Sethwork.” You will fail at both.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
