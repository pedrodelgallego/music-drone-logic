Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: thickness from detune

Copy one clear section of Phill Niblock — “Sethwork” (Touch Three, 2006); Seth Josel, e-bowed acoustic guitar. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: the same sustained pitch stacked many times, each copy cents apart - every pair of near-unisons beats at its own rate, and the sum of unsynced beatings is the churn. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring; the Digitakt II rests. You do not own an e-bowed guitar: one steady MicroFreak tone, printed once and duplicated with clip detune, is the honest analogue — the mechanism is the cents, not the strings
  • New set saved as REP-SETHWORK — tempo irrelevant; the only clock in this piece is the beat rates the detunes make
  • Eight stacked copies of one tone sum well above one copy’s level — set gain for the full stack, not the single layer, and keep phones at the lab-001 mark; break every 15 minutes
  • What you will make: a short replica of Phill Niblock — “Sethwork” (Touch Three, 2006); Seth Josel, e-bowed acoustic guitar. Focus on this effect: many close-pitched sustained guitar tones fuse into one massive cluster that churns from the inside, though no player adds vibrato or movement.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: the same sustained pitch stacked many times, each copy cents apart - every pair of near-unisons beats at its own rate, and the sum of unsynced beatings is the churn.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Phill Niblock — “Sethwork” (Touch Three, 2006); Seth Josel, e-bowed acoustic guitar. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many layers are sounding?
    Answer

    More than you can count, by design - and your count will change as the beatings bring different layers forward.

  2. Is anyone playing vibrato?
    Answer

    No - and this is the question the piece exists to ask. The waver you hear belongs to no single layer; it is interference between layers, pulsing at rates set by their pitch differences. Each guitar tone, alone, would be still.

  3. Does the pitch of the whole cluster move?
    Answer

    Barely, and slowly - the center holds while the inside churns. What reads as harmonic event is usually a beating speeding up or slowing down as layers drift relative to each other. The form is thickness changing, not notes changing.

  4. What did the e-bow remove from the guitar?
    Answer

    The attack. An e-bow sustains the string magnetically, so the guitar loses its pluck and becomes a pure held tone - which is why the instrument stops sounding like a guitar at all.

  5. Why does it sound like an organ when it is one guitar?
    Answer

    Because identity lives in attacks and in singularity, and both are gone. Strip the pluck, stack the copies, and what remains is a mass with the steadiness of pipes - instrument identity dissolves into spectrum.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your stack lands on the same paradox — a mass that churns continuously though every layer in it is dead still.
  • One pitch only — every layer is the same note, offset in cents. A second note is a chord lesson, not this one.
  • No modulation anywhere — no LFO, no vibrato, no automation. If you add motion by hand, the experiment is void.
  • Equal levels across all layers — favoring one collapses the cluster back toward a single tone with company.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one steady tone printed once, duplicated to seven or eight tracks, each clip offset by a different handful of cents, all faders equal — and the beating between near-unisons doing every bit of the moving.

MicroFreak · one held tone · no modulation → printed once · duplicated ×7–8 → each clip detuned · −11 to +13 cents → equal faders · the churn · ≈ −12 dBFS

The same tone three ways. One copy is a flat line — nothing moves. Two copies a few cents apart beat at one steady rate. Eight copies beat at every rate their pairs make, and the sum is a band that churns without any layer moving. The motion enters with the count.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Sethwork” into it.
  2. Live Loop two minutes from the middle of the piece, where the cluster is fully assembled.
  3. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Live REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. Live ⌘ T once; rename: LAYER, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
  6. Logbook Write in the logbook: how many distinct beat rates — slow wobbles of different speeds — you can pick out in the loop.
  7. Logbook Two minutes of honest counting; the number will be revised.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one named, routed, armed track waits for the master tone, and a beat-rate count is on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The master tone — one take, completely still

Phase focus: the master tone — one take, completely still. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, one note in the cello register — around A2 wears well.
  2. MicroFreak No paraphony, no chord.
  3. MicroFreak Filter halfway open — audible harmonics, no buzz.
  4. MicroFreak Zero LFO, zero filter movement, envelope all sustain: slow attack, full hold, nothing moving anywhere in the patch.
  5. MicroFreak Set the M4 input-1 gain on the held tone so peaks sit ≈ −20 dBFS — deliberately low, because eight summed copies will raise the stack toward the usual ≈ −12 dBFS.
  6. MicroFreak Print 4–5 minutes of the steady tone into LAYER using Hold.
  7. MicroFreak One take; this single recording is the entire raw material of the piece.
  8. Logbook Listen back to one minute of it, alone, and write the confirmation line in the logbook: nothing moves.
  9. Logbook If anything moves, find it and re-print now — phase 3 multiplies whatever this take contains.
Expected result

One 4–5 minute take of a harmonically present, provably motionless tone at ≈ −20 dBFS — boring alone, and correct precisely because of it.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The solo take already shimmers or wobblesAn LFO or cycling envelope still routed in the patchZero every modulation slot and re-print; stillness is the control condition
The take is pure and dull, like a test toneFilter closed too far — no partials to beat laterOpen the cutoff until harmonics are plainly audible, re-print
Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The stack — seven copies, a cents plan, equal faders

Phase focus: the stack — seven copies, a cents plan, equal faders. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Logbook Write the cents plan in the logbook first.
  2. Logbook A working spread: −11, −7, −3, 0, +4, +8, +13 — uneven gaps on purpose, so no two pairs beat at the same rate.
  3. Live Duplicate LAYER (⌘ D) until you have 7–8 tracks, each holding a copy of the same clip, all starting together at 1.1.1.
  4. Live On each clip, set Detune in the clip’s Audio tab to its planned cents value.
  5. MicroFreak + Live (Printing 7–8 separate MicroFreak takes nudged by the fine-tune works too and is closer to Niblock’s practice — but the clip route keeps every layer provably identical except in pitch, which is the cleaner experiment.)
  6. MicroFreak + Live All faders equal, all at the same level; pan the layers gently across the field — a few notches left and right — so the cluster has width without any layer dominating.
  7. MicroFreak + Live Play the stack and check the sum: peaks ≈ −12 dBFS on the master.
  8. MicroFreak + Live Trim all faders together if it runs hot — together, always;
  9. MicroFreak + Live equality is a rule of the piece.
Expected result

Seven or eight identical clips at different cents offsets, equal faders, summing to ≈ −12 dBFS — and a sound that visibly came from nowhere: the churn is already there, uninvited, the moment you press play.

If it fails

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The solo test — prove where the motion lives

Phase focus: the solo test — prove where the motion lives. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the full stack for one minute.
  2. Then solo any single layer and hold it for thirty seconds: dead still.
  3. Live Unsolo: the churn returns instantly.
  4. Logbook Do this three times with different layers; write what the switch is switching.
  5. Logbook Mute layers one at a time, listening to the churn thin out — each removal deletes that layer’s pairs.
  6. Logbook Note the count at which the texture stops feeling massive;
  7. Logbook that number is your version of the record’s density decision.
  8. Live A/B the full stack against REF at matched loudness, judging only the internal motion: are your beat rates as varied, as layered, as theirs?
  9. Live Revise your phase-1 beat-rate count for the record now that you know what to listen for.
  10. Live Print the full stack, 4–5 minutes, as one stereo file — the piece exists in the sum, and the sum deserves a take.
Expected result

A demonstrated on/off proof that the churn lives between layers, a written density threshold, a sharpened hearing of the record’s beat rates, and one printed cluster.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your stack at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Solo one layer, then unmute all. Where did the motion come from?
    Answer

    Interference, not modulation. Each layer is provably still — you listened to the master take alone and wrote nothing moves — yet the stack churns, because every pair of detuned copies beats at the rate of its frequency gap. Nothing in the session generates motion; the motion is what addition does to near-identical waves. Relisten to the record’s cluster knowing this: every wobble you hear is two specific layers disagreeing, even when you cannot name which two.

  • Could you have made this churn with an LFO instead?
    Answer

    Not this churn. An LFO imposes one rate from outside; the stack produces dozens of rates from inside, none synced, drifting in and out of step — which is why the texture never loops and never settles. That inexhaustibility is the audible difference between modulated and interfering sound, and it is the reason the record stays alive for twenty minutes with no events. Relisten to one minute of your stack hunting for a repeat; then try the same hunt on “Sethwork” and fail at both.
