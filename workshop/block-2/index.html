Block 2 — Sources

A source is an entry condition, not just content

This block covers synthesis, capture, and resampling. Every sound that enters your system brings conditions with it — things like impedance (how easily signal flows), noise floor, level, distance, and environment. This block teaches you to make those choices on purpose instead of accepting whatever you get by default.

Engineering focus

A source brings physical conditions into your system: impedance, noise, handling, level, distance, and environment. Two required comparisons in this block: record the same source too quiet, at the right level, and too hot (lab 006); then capture the same object with an air mic, a contact mic, and an electromagnetic pickup (lab 008).

Ableton Suite synthesis tour

Synthesized sound is a source just like a microphone recording. You already have the tools for it in Ableton Suite. Before buying new plugins, spend time with the devices that often go unused: Operator (FM synthesis and classic subtractive synthesis — building sound from oscillators and filters), Wavetable (smoothly morphing between different wave shapes), Sampler (playing your own recordings as an instrument), Granulator (slicing any audio file into tiny grains and rearranging them), and Corpus (adding resonance that mimics physical objects). Labs 006b, 007b, and 008b all use these devices. Build the sound yourself.

Listening programme L2 — concrète & field

Session 1 · Records

Four capture philosophies

Four different approaches to recording the world and bringing it into a system. Analyze each one using the six-question frame, then compare what each recordist decided to do.

Pierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer”the founding capture
Hildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk”the microphone as narrator
Chris Watson — El Tren Fantasmathe field composed
Éliane Radigue — “Kyema”capture’s opposite pole
Listen for

What was recorded, and where? What did each recordist choose to keep from the real world — and what did they leave out or remove?

Session 2 · Samples

Deconstruction — captured vs processed

Pick one concrète (music made from real-world recordings) work and divide everything you hear into two columns: what was captured in the field, and what was processed in the studio. That worksheet becomes your plan for replication lab R2.

Session 3 · Video

Field-recording documentary footage

Watch this for technique, not atmosphere. Pay attention to what the recordist is hearing in their headphones, where their hands are on the gain controls, and what decisions cannot be undone once a moment in the field is gone.

Replication lessons — one per reference

  • Forty lessons, one per reference. Every listening reference in labs 005–008 and programme L2 has its own 60–120 minute step-by-step replication lesson, spread across each lab. Use one when a reference shows you a gap in your listening or capture technique.

Lab 005 Replicate: change below perceptionÉliane Radigue — “Kyema” Changes happen so slowly you only notice them in memory — you never catch the moment of change as it happens
Lab 005 Replicate: sustain as practicePauline Oliveros — “Horse Sings from Cloud” Hold a tone still and wait until a change feels necessary. The small wavering and tension before each move is where the music lives
Lab 005 Replicate: the erased attackStars of the Lid — “Requiem for Dying Mothers” Remove every attack (the sharp start of a note) by fading it out. No note ever clearly begins — the sound just appears already present
Lab 005 Replicate: tuning as the motionKali Malone — “No Sun to Burn” Two voices move according to a strict tuning rule, very slowly. The pulsing you hear when two slightly out-of-tune pitches meet (called beating) is the only motion in the piece
Lab 005 Replicate: spectrum as melodyFolke Rabe — “What??” One pitch stays fixed. Its overtones (the higher frequencies naturally present in any sound) are brought forward one at a time. The order in which they appear is the melody
Lab 005 Replicate: sustain from repetitionCharlemagne Palestine — “Strumming Music” Notes are repeated so fast they stop sounding like individual notes and blur into a continuous tone. The buildup of resonance in the room becomes the texture
Lab 005 Replicate: erosion over a still coreTim Hecker — “Chimeras” A chord stays fixed while automated processing slowly degrades the surface of the sound. The harmony never changes — only the texture does
Lab 005 Replicate: thickness from detunePhill Niblock — “Sethwork” One pitch is layered many times, each copy tuned a few cents (tiny fractions of a semitone) apart. The interference between layers creates movement — no single layer moves on its own
Lab 005 Replicate: one instrument, emptied outSarah Davachi — All My Circles Run One instrument plays a sustained sound, arranged through overlapping entries. The physical details of the instrument — breath, slight wavering — are kept clearly audible rather than smoothed away
Lab 005 Replicate: the system plays itselfAlvin Lucier — “Music on a Long Thin Wire” Set up the system, let it run unattended for ten minutes, then keep sounds you can honestly say you did not compose
Lab 006 Replicate: repetition makes the objectPierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer” Playing a recording on exact repeat removes its identity as a real-world object. It becomes a musical element you hear in its own right
Lab 006 Replicate: the unedited frameLuc Ferrari — “Presque Rien No. 1” The composition is made from four decisions: where you record, what angle you face, when you start, and where you quietly fade out. Every edit is hidden inside continuous, steady sound
Lab 006 Replicate: the narrated zoomHildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk” The spoken narration and the volume fader work together: a sentence directs your attention to a sound, then the gain rises or falls to match what was just described
Lab 006 Replicate: the microphone as cameraChris Watson — El Tren Fantasma Where you place the microphone works like a camera angle. Recording one subject from five different positions tells it as a journey through space
Lab 006 Replicate: form as geographyAnnea Lockwood — A Sound Map of the Hudson River One subject is recorded at a series of locations in order, all at the same level. The form of the piece comes from the physical route — not from editing
Lab 006 Replicate: capture as provocationMatmos — “Lipostudio (And So On…)” The original sound (medical fat removal procedures) is the point — the groove is just the delivery method. Processing must never disguise what the source actually was
Lab 006 Replicate: montage before tapeWalter Ruttmann — “Weekend” The edit cut is the only rhythmic instrument. Rhythm is built from how long each piece of audio is and where each cut falls — there are no crossfades or transitions
Lab 006 Replicate: one object, a whole cataloguePierre Henry — “Variations pour une porte et un soupir” Find every sound one object can make before you record anything else. Use a single source until you have nothing left to discover from it
Lab 006 Replicate: capture sorted by physicsBernard Parmegiani — De Natura Sonorum Group recordings by how they behave acoustically — sharp, smooth, grainy — not by what physical object made them. The categories you create become the structure of the piece
Lab 006 Replicate: the house as drum kitMatthew Herbert — Around the House Write a rule limiting what you can sample (Herbert only sampled sounds from his own home). Stick to it strictly — the restriction that feels hardest to keep is the one that defines the work
Lab 007 Replicate: the orphaned voiceBurial — “Archangel” Pitch-shift and time-stretch a vocal sample until the recognizable person is gone but an emotional quality remains. Lay it off the beat grid over crackling texture
Lab 007 Replicate: the loop point as instrumentOneohtrix Point Never — “Replica” A two-second sample can be looped from many different start and end points, each giving a different feel. Try them all — keep the loop that sounds slightly awkward or unexpected
Lab 007 Replicate: wear as the subjectThe Caretaker — An Empty Bliss Beyond This World Run a sample through many generations of resampling so it degrades — adds hiss, warps, loses clarity. Push it until it sounds like a distant memory rather than a clean recording
Lab 007 Replicate: capture at syllable scaleAkufen — “Deck the House” When a sample is shorter than about 400 ms, the brain stops reading it as language or melody and hears it as a percussive hit. Cut radio fragments that short and sequence them as rhythm and pitch
Lab 007 Replicate: haze as a chosen depthActress — “Hubble” Blurring a sound through resampling is a deliberate choice, not a mistake. Take one loop through several stages of resampling and arrange the results from clearest to most degraded, like a camera pulling focus
Lab 007 Replicate: one machine gluing many worldsDJ Shadow — “Building Steam with a Grain of Salt” Very different sounds from different places feel like they belong together when they all pass through the same sampler’s clock and gain chain. Shared processing is the glue
Lab 007 Replicate: acoustic grains looped into a bandFour Tet — “Unspoken” Find the loop point at the edge of a gesture (the moment just before or after an action) rather than inside it. Looping natural human timing keeps the sample sounding alive
Lab 007 Replicate: the song under the staticFennesz — “Endless Summer” Heavy processing covers the harmony but does not erase it. Even at the most degraded point, a listener should still be able to feel the underlying chords and sing along
Lab 007 Replicate: loops from the in-betweenJan Jelinek — Loop-Finding-Jazz-Records Sample the parts of a jazz record that are not the music — room noise, decay tails, hiss between notes. Loop only those scraps and the feel of jazz remains without quoting any actual melody
Lab 007 Replicate: dub smeared past the bar lineVladislav Delay — Multila A delay set to a length that does not fit the bar line blurs the rhythm while the pulse (the underlying beat) continues. Resampling the result captures that blur as a new stable sound
Lab 008 Replicate: the transducer below the surfaceJana Winderen — Energy Field Some environments are silent to an air microphone but full of sound to the right sensor. A hydrophone (underwater microphone) reveals an entire sonic world that a regular mic cannot hear
Lab 008 Replicate: the coil where ears report silenceChristina Kubisch — Five Electrical Walks Every electrical circuit emits an electromagnetic field that an induction coil can pick up as sound. Walking a planned route past different electrical sources turns the journey into an arrangement
Lab 008 Replicate: the unedited frameFrancisco López — La Selva Deliberately not shaping or directing the listener is itself a technique. Set the gain once, do not edit the recording, and give it an uninformative title — the absence of decisions is the decision
Lab 008 Replicate: the surface as filterToshiya Tsunoda — Extract from Field Recording Archive Every solid surface transmits vibration differently. Place one contact mic on five different surfaces and you get five distinct-sounding recordings of the same source — each surface acts as its own filter
Lab 008 Replicate: the planet as oscillatorJacob Kirkegaard — Eldfjall A building has its own slow mechanical vibrations — heating systems, structural stress, distant traffic. Make long contact mic recordings of these until the building stops sounding like machinery and starts sounding like a place
Lab 008 Replicate: the city as ensembleBJ Nilsen — The Invisible City Layer recordings of different urban systems (transport, wind, crowds) like instruments in a band. Volume is the only mixing tool — raising one layer makes it the focus; the city you build does not match any real place
Lab 008 Replicate: duration as a level decisionChris Watson — Stepping into the Dark Set your gain for the unexpected, then stay still long enough that the environment stops reacting to your presence. Waiting without intervening is a deliberate technique, not passivity
Lab 008 Replicate: the building as instrumentStephen Vitiello — World Trade Center Recordings Attach a contact mic to the largest flat surface near you (a window, a wall) during windy or rainy weather. Every structure has a natural resonant pitch that weather excites — find yours
Lab 008 Replicate: the sky on a wireAlvin Lucier — “Sferics” Atmospheric electricity (lightning, ionospheric activity) creates signals an antenna can pick up as audio. These signals are irregular by nature — if your recording starts to groove or repeat, you have introduced something artificial
Lab 008 Replicate: the expedition as formGeir Jenssen — Cho Oyu 8201m Record at fixed points along a real journey in strict order. The physical route determines the form of the piece — the difficulty and conditions of travel are part of the material
