Block 2 — Sources

A source is an entry condition, not just content

Synthesis, capture, and resampling. Every sound that enters your system arrives with conditions attached — impedance, noise, handling, level, distance, environment — and this block teaches you to own those conditions instead of inheriting them by accident.

Year 1 · Domain A · Labs 005–008 · Replication R2 · Programme L2 · closes into C2

Engineering focus

A source is an entry condition into a system: impedance, noise, handling, level, distance, environment. Required comparisons — the same source recorded too quiet, correct, and too hot (lab 006); the same object captured by air mic, contact mic, and electromagnetic pickup (lab 008).

Block claim

Block 1 proved the path; Block 2 decides what enters it. The transferable skill is hearing what a microphone, a synthesizer voice, or a resampling loop adds to a sound before any processing begins — because by the time you can hear it in the mix, it is already part of the material.

Working order

Do these in this order
  1. Programme L2 — run the three listening sessions first and name one stolen principle
  2. Lab 005 — sustain a tone and keep it alive with slow modulation
  3. Lab 005b — build the worked beatless long-form and certify the Held Tone Test
  4. Lab 006 — capture the world and own the gain decision
  5. Lab 007 — resample the same source through three architectures
  6. Lab 008 — certify the field kit, with the safety and ethics checklist
  7. R2 — rebuild one concrète gesture and verify the L2 analysis
  • No gate capstone here. Block 2 closes into C2, the Year 1 capstone, after Block 3 — captured sources organized by probabilistic and polymetric time, sources × time as one system. C2 also renews the Held Tone Test at six minutes.
  • Deep reference: Lab Note 002 — the beatless long-form course behind lab 005b, the drone counterpart to Lab Note 001.

Listening programme L2 — concrète & field

Session 1 · Records

Four capture philosophies

Four ways of deciding what the world sounds like when it enters a system. Analyze each with the six-question frame, then compare the philosophies.

Pierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer”the founding capture
Hildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk”the microphone as narrator
Chris Watson — El Tren Fantasmathe field composed
Éliane Radigue — “Kyema”capture’s opposite pole
Listen for

What was captured, and from where? What does each recordist let the world keep — and what do they take away from it?

Session 2 · Samples

Deconstruction — captured vs processed

Pick one concrète work and split everything you hear into two columns: what was captured, and what was processed. The worksheet becomes the blueprint for replication lab R2.

Session 3 · Video

Field-recording documentary footage

Watched for monitoring, not scenery: what is on the recordist’s headphones, where the hands and the gain are, and what is unrecoverable once the moment in the field has passed.

  • Deliverable: written system analyses (six questions per reference) + one stolen principle — a structural idea, never material, named in advance and evaluated at C2. Full briefs and worksheets: programme L2 scaffold
