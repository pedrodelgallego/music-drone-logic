Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the expedition as form

Copy one clear section of Geir Jenssen — Cho Oyu 8201m. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: recordings made en route and presented in expedition order - base camps, prayer flags, wind, breathing at altitude - chronology as the only structure. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Honest analogue, stated plainly: Jenssen climbed an 8000-meter peak; you will not. The reachable version is a real walk with a real gradient — your door to the highest or quietest point you can get to on foot: a hilltop, the top of a long stairwell, the far edge of a park where the city thins. The form survives the change of scale completely
  • Gear: the handheld recorder with foam fitted, closed phones, spare batteries and card — the Lab 008 air path, carried. Back at the bench: Live 12, the M4, DT 990 Pro at the mark
  • New set saved as REP-CHO; the Lab 008 safety checklist walks with you — weather checked, one ear free near roads, the route through places you are allowed to be, and someone knows where you went if the high point is remote
  • What you will make: a short replica of Geir Jenssen — Cho Oyu 8201m. Focus on this effect: an ascent you can hear - recordings that thin as the altitude rises, with the recordist’s body and condition audibly inside them.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: recordings made en route and presented in expedition order - base camps, prayer flags, wind, breathing at altitude - chronology as the only structure.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Geir Jenssen — Cho Oyu 8201m. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What orders the tracks?
    Answer

    The climb. The sequence is the expedition’s own chronology - villages and base camp before high camps, high camps before the summit push - and the titles read like a logbook. No musical logic overrides the route’s logic anywhere.

  2. Is the recordist hidden?
    Answer

    No - and that is the radical part, coming off Watson’s absence. Footsteps crunch, fabric moves, and the breathing is right there in the frame, getting heavier as the air gets thinner.

  3. What thins as the album rises?
    Answer

    Almost everything. Low on the mountain: voices, animals, prayer flags, camp life - many layers. Higher: wind, rope, boots, breath - few. The density of the world falls with altitude, and you can hear the gradient between any two tracks.

  4. What do you know without a single word of narration?
    Answer

    Where he is, roughly how high, how hard the body is working, what the weather is doing - condition and place, read entirely from sound. The breathing is a barometer, the wind is an altimeter, the missing layers are a map.

  5. Could the tracks be reordered?
    Answer

    Not without destroying the piece. Shuffle them and you have a collection of nice recordings; in order, they are an ascent.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: five to seven fixed-length stations assembled in strict route order, where a listener can feel the gradient — busier to thinner, lower to higher — and can hear you walking it. The flip against REF compares shapes, not mountains.
  • Strict chronology: no reordering, ever. The rule is absolute because the form is the route — break it once and the piece becomes a playlist.
  • Your presence stays in frame: arrival footsteps, breath after the climb, the recorder handled honestly. Chosen presence, not accidental handling.
  • Fixed station length — two minutes each, decided before leaving. Equal frames make the gradient measurable.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a route with a real gradient, equal two-minute frames hung on it like stations of a survey, a body audibly carrying the recorder between them, and an assembly that adds nothing but the order the route already chose.

route · door → highest / quietest point → 5–7 stations · 2 min each · in order → body in frame · breath + footsteps → assembly · strict chronology · untreated

The route as the piece sees it. Each dot is a station; the bar under it is how many layers of world that station contains. The line climbs, the bars shrink — that crossing of curves is the entire form, and it only exists if the order is kept.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the route design

Phase focus: the reference and the route design. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag in your three sample tracks from Cho Oyu 8201m — early, middle, late — in order.
  2. Live Audio Effects → Utility, trim to peak ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Logbook Design the route in the logbook: start at your door, end at the highest or quietest reachable point.
  4. Logbook Mark five to seven stations where the world should audibly change — the street, the courtyard, the path, the slope, the top.
  5. Recorder Fix the frame: two minutes per station, recorded standing or with the recorder set down, you arriving on mic — start recording a few steps before you stop walking.
  6. Recorder Predict the gradient: next to each station, guess its layer count — how many distinct sound sources you expect.
  7. Recorder The walk will grade the guesses.
  8. Recorder Check weather, card space, batteries; count the kit out per Lab 008.
Expected result

The reference trimmed and in order, a route with five to seven named stations and predicted layer counts, a fixed two-minute frame, and a counted kit.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
No route nearby seems to have a gradientThinking in altitude onlyAny monotonic change works: into a park, away from the main road, up a stairwell, toward the edge of town — quieter is higher for this form
The route needs a bus or a car in the middleAmbition beyond the timeboxShrink it — the form needs a continuous walked line, not distance; Jenssen’s scale is his, yours is yours
Phase 2 · ≈ 45 min

The expedition — walk it, station by station

Phase focus: the expedition — walk it, station by station. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Walk the route in order.
  2. No skipping, no doubling back — the chronology you record is the chronology you keep.
  3. At each station: set gain for that station’s loudest plausible event (a fresh Lab 008 decision every time — log the knob position), start recording a few steps out, arrive, settle, and hold the full two minutes.
  4. Your arrival breath stays in.
  5. Recorder Between stations, either stop the recorder or let it roll on the move — but be consistent, and if you roll, those transit stretches are material too: footsteps, gates, your breathing on the climb.
  6. Recorder At the high point: same two-minute frame, plus one extra minute if the place asks for it — the summit station may be the emptiest recording you have ever made on purpose;
  7. Recorder let it be.
  8. Recorder Log per station: time, what the place did, and your own state — out of breath, cold hands, calm.
  9. Recorder Jenssen’s liner notes are exactly this log.
Expected result

Five to seven station recordings in strict route order, each with a logged gain decision and a logged body state, the kit counted back at the door.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Wind blast ruins the exposed upper stationsFoam insufficient where the gradient ends — the high point is the windy pointDead-cat on, body as windbreak, or tuck the recorder at chest height in the lee of your jacket; re-record that station before descending
A station has nothing in it but hissGain left at the street’s setting in a quiet place — the kit’s self-noise exposedEach station is a fresh decision: raise gain until the place’s floor clears the kit’s, and redo the frame
Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The assembly — chronology does the composing

Phase focus: the assembly — chronology does the composing. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import every station into REP-CHO, one track, named EXPEDITION.
  2. Live Lay them in Arrangement in strict route order — the rule is absolute: no reordering, no matter how much better a swap would “flow.”
  3. Live Trim each station to its two-minute frame, keeping the arrival — footsteps and breath at the head of each is the connective tissue.
  4. Live Joins: a short crossfade or a hard cut, nothing longer; the seams are allowed to be audible, because the cut from street to slope is the route speaking.
  5. Live One Utility on the track if a station’s level needs civilizing — but resist matching them flat: the quiet stations are quiet because the world up there is, and that is the data.
  6. Live Title each station in the clip name the way Jenssen titles tracks: place and time — S4 · the slope · 17:25 · out of breath.
Expected result

A 10–15 minute assembly in unbroken route order, arrivals kept, seams honest, stations titled like a logbook — and no compositional decision made at the desk beyond the joins.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The urge to swap two stations is overwhelmingA playlist instinct — ordering by interest instead of by routeThe rule holds. Log the urge as the lesson’s finding: the form is doing its work precisely where it refuses you
The piece feels flat played end to endThe route lacked a real gradient — the stations are siblingsThat is a route problem, not an edit problem; redesign with a steeper change and walk it again another day
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The gradient audit — grade the climb

Phase focus: the gradient audit — grade the climb. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the assembly end to end at the mark, no touching.
  2. Listen as a stranger: can you feel the world thinning?
  3. Logbook Count layers per station — distinct simultaneous sources — and write the numbers next to phase 1’s predictions.
  4. Logbook The two columns should both fall; where they disagree, the world surprised you, and that is logbook material.
  5. Logbook Map your body: mark every spot where you are audible — arrival breath, footsteps, fabric, the heavier breathing late in the route — and next to each, write what it tells a listener.
  6. Logbook The drop-in test: place the playhead blind somewhere in the middle and listen for twenty seconds.
  7. Logbook If you can tell early from late by sound alone, the gradient is real.
  8. Live A/B one early and one late station against the record’s early and late tracks at matched loudness, comparing shape only: many layers to few, calm body to working body.
Expected result

Two falling columns of layer counts, a map of your body in the file with a meaning at each mark, and a drop-in test you pass blind.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Early and late stations are indistinguishable blindThe gradient was social, not sonic — the route changed on the map but not in the airKeep the assembly as a draft and re-route: more vertical, more distance from the road, later in the day
Your presence reads as mistakes, not as materialAccidental handling rather than chosen presence — thumps and rustle instead of breath and stepsThe difference is intention and steadiness: re-record the worst station holding still, letting only the honest sounds — arrival, breath — stay in frame
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your assembly at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Can a listener feel the gradient?
    Answer

    They should feel it twice: busier to thinner in the layer counts, lower to higher in what the wind and the spaces do. The proof is the blind drop-in — early and late must be tellable apart in twenty seconds, the way any twenty seconds of Cho Oyu places itself on the mountain. If yours fails, the route lacked gradient or the assembly hid it with level matching; the order was never the problem, because the order was never touched. Relisten to the record’s first and last tracks and notice the gradient is audible even at album-length distance.

  • Where is your body audible, and what does it add?
    Answer

    At the arrivals, in the footsteps, and — if the route earned it — in breathing that gets heavier late. What it adds is authenticity of a measurable kind: effort as data. Jenssen’s breathing at altitude is not noise that slipped past the engineer; it is the altimeter, the proof the recordist is inside the document rather than behind glass. Your version is smaller but the same instrument: the breath at station five says the slope was real. Relisten to your map of body-moments and ask of each — does this tell the listener something about the route? Keep the ones that do; they are the difference between a walk documented and a walk faked.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.

  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 008 — the kit’s handling rules now have an exception with a name: presence is a fault when accidental and material when chosen. And the route joins gain and duration as a capture decision made before pressing record — the third thing the field does not let you fix later.
