Replicate: the expedition as form

Copy one clear section of Geir Jenssen — Cho Oyu 8201m. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill is about one technique: record as you go, then play back in the exact order you recorded — no reordering, no extra structure. The route is the composition. Do only the steps that make that technique audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordGeir Jenssen — Cho Oyu 8201m (2006)
The effectan ascent you can hear — recordings that thin as the altitude rises, with the recordist’s body and condition audibly inside them
The mechanismrecordings made en route and presented in expedition order — base camps, prayer flags, wind, breathing at altitude — chronology as the only structure

Before you start
  • You are not climbing an 8000-meter peak. That's fine. Walk somewhere nearby with a real gradient — from busy to quiet, or from low to high. A hilltop, the top of a long stairwell, the far edge of a park. The form works at any scale.
  • Gear: the handheld recorder with foam fitted, closed phones, spare batteries and card — the Lab 008 air path, carried. Back at the bench: Live 12, the M4, DT 990 Pro at the mark
  • New set saved as REP-CHO; the Lab 008 safety checklist walks with you — weather checked, one ear free near roads, the route through places you are allowed to be, and someone knows where you went if the high point is remote
  • What you will make: a short replica of Geir Jenssen — Cho Oyu 8201m. Focus on this effect: an ascent you can hear - recordings that thin as the altitude rises, with the recordist’s body and condition audibly inside them.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: recordings made en route and presented in expedition order - base camps, prayer flags, wind, breathing at altitude - chronology as the only structure.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Geir Jenssen — Cho Oyu 8201m. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What orders the tracks?
    Answer

    The climb. The sequence follows the expedition’s chronology — villages and base camp before high camps, high camps before the summit push. The track titles read like a logbook. No musical logic overrides the route’s logic anywhere.

  2. Is the recordist hidden?
    Answer

    No — and that’s deliberate. Footsteps crunch, fabric moves, and the breathing is audible in the recording, getting heavier as the altitude rises.

  3. What thins as the album rises?
    Answer

    Almost everything. Low on the mountain: voices, animals, camp life — many overlapping sound sources. Higher: wind, boots, breath — very few. You can hear the change in density between any two tracks.

  4. What do you know without a single word of narration?
    Answer

    Where he is, roughly how high, how hard his body is working, and what the weather is doing — all from sound alone. The breathing tells you the effort level, the wind tells you the exposure, and the fewer sound sources tell you the altitude.

  5. Could the tracks be reordered?
    Answer

    Not without destroying the piece. Shuffle them and you have a collection of nice recordings. In order, they are an ascent.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: five to seven fixed-length stations played in strict route order. A listener should feel the gradient — busier to thinner, lower to higher — and hear you walking through it. The comparison against REF is about shape and structure, not scale.
  • Strict chronology: no reordering, ever. The route is the form. Swap two stations and the piece becomes a playlist.
  • Your presence stays in the recording: arrival footsteps, breath after the climb. Keep these intentionally — not accidental handling noise.
  • Fixed station length — two minutes each, decided before you leave. Equal frames let you measure the gradient.

The whole system in one row: a route with a real gradient, equal two-minute recording frames at each station, your body audibly moving through them, and an assembly that adds nothing except the order the route already decided.

route · door → highest / quietest point → 5–7 stations · 2 min each · in order → body in frame · breath + footsteps → assembly · strict chronology · untreated

The route as the piece sees it. Each dot is a station; the bar below it shows how many sound sources that station contains. The line climbs and the bars shrink — that's the entire form. It only works if you keep the order.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the route design

Phase focus: the reference and the route design. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag in your three sample tracks from Cho Oyu 8201m — early, middle, late — in order.
  2. Audio Effects → Utility, trim to peak ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Design the route in the logbook: start at your door, end at the highest or quietest reachable point.
  4. Mark five to seven stations where the world should audibly change — the street, the courtyard, the path, the slope, the top.
  5. Fix the frame: two minutes per station, recorded standing or with the recorder set down, you arriving on mic — start recording a few steps before you stop walking.
  6. Predict the gradient: next to each station, guess its layer count — how many distinct sound sources you expect.
  7. The walk will grade the guesses.
  8. Check weather, card space, batteries; count the kit out per Lab 008.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Geir Jenssen — Cho Oyu 8201m (2006) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the route design — as the smallest Live set that proves recordings made en route and presented in expedition order — base camps, prayer flags, wind, breathing at altitude — chronology as the only structure.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects an ascent you can hear — recordings that thin as the altitude rises, with the recordist’s body and condition audibly inside them.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the route design into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates recordings made en route and presented in expedition order — base camps, prayer flags, wind, breathing at altitude — chronology as the only structure, not just the surface of Geir Jenssen — Cho Oyu 8201m (2006).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The reference trimmed and in order, a route with five to seven named stations and predicted layer counts, a fixed two-minute frame, and a counted kit.

Phase 2 · ≈ 45 min

The expedition — walk it, station by station

Phase focus: the expedition — walk it, station by station. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Walk the route in order.
  2. No skipping, no doubling back — the chronology you record is the chronology you keep.
  3. At each station: set gain for that station’s loudest plausible event (a fresh Lab 008 decision every time — log the knob position), start recording a few steps out, arrive, settle, and hold the full two minutes.
  4. Your arrival breath stays in.
  5. Between stations, either stop the recorder or let it roll on the move — but be consistent, and if you roll, those transit stretches are material too: footsteps, gates, your breathing on the climb.
  6. At the high point: same two-minute frame, plus one extra minute if the place asks for it — the summit station may be the emptiest recording you have ever made on purpose;
  7. let it be.
  8. Log per station: time, what the place did, and your own state — out of breath, cold hands, calm.
  9. Jenssen’s liner notes are exactly this log.

Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The assembly — chronology does the composing

Phase focus: the assembly — chronology does the composing. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import every station into REP-CHO, one track, named EXPEDITION.
  2. Lay them in Arrangement in strict route order — the rule is absolute: no reordering, no matter how much better a swap would “flow.”
  3. Trim each station to its two-minute frame, keeping the arrival — footsteps and breath at the head of each is the connective tissue.
  4. Joins: a short crossfade or a hard cut, nothing longer; the seams are allowed to be audible, because the cut from street to slope is the route speaking.
  5. Use one Utility on the track if a station’s level needs adjusting — but don’t match all stations to the same volume. The quiet stations are quiet because that place was quiet, and that difference is the data.
  6. Title each station in the clip name the way Jenssen titles tracks: place and time — S4 · the slope · 17:25 · out of breath.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The gradient audit — grade the climb

Phase focus: the gradient audit — grade the climb. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the assembly end to end at the mark, no touching.
  2. Listen as a stranger: can you feel the world thinning?
  3. Count layers per station — distinct simultaneous sources — and write the numbers next to phase 1’s predictions.
  4. The two columns should both fall; where they disagree, the world surprised you, and that is logbook material.
  5. Map your body: mark every spot where you are audible — arrival breath, footsteps, fabric, the heavier breathing late in the route — and next to each, write what it tells a listener.
  6. The drop-in test: place the playhead blind somewhere in the middle and listen for twenty seconds.
  7. If you can tell early from late by sound alone, the gradient is real.
  8. A/B one early and one late station against the record’s early and late tracks at matched loudness, comparing shape only: many layers to few, calm body to working body.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Early and late stations are indistinguishable blindThe gradient was social, not sonic — the route changed on the map but not in the airKeep the assembly as a draft and re-route: more vertical, more distance from the road, later in the day
Your presence reads as mistakes, not as materialAccidental handling rather than chosen presence — thumps and rustle instead of breath and stepsThe difference is intention and steadiness: re-record the worst station holding still, letting only the honest sounds — arrival, breath — stay in frame
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your assembly at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Can a listener feel the gradient?
    Answer

    They should feel it two ways: busier to thinner in the number of sound sources, and a change in what wind and spaces sound like. The test is the blind drop-in — can you tell early from late in twenty seconds? If not, the route lacked a real gradient, or level-matching at the desk hid it. The order was never the problem if you kept it. Relisten to the record’s first and last tracks and notice the gradient is obvious even at that distance.

  • Where is your body audible, and what does it add?
    Answer

    At the arrivals, in the footsteps, and — if the route earned it — in breathing that gets heavier late on. What it adds is evidence: the effort is audible, which means the place was real. Jenssen’s breathing at altitude is not noise that slipped through; it tells you how high he is. Your version is smaller but the same idea: the breath at station five says the slope was real. For each body-sound on your map, ask — does this tell a listener something about the route? Keep the ones that do.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
