Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the unedited frame

Copy one clear section of Francisco López — La Selva. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: full-bandwidth capture with every decision made before the take - position, hour, gain - and none made during or after; no edits, no species labels. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordFrancisco López — La Selva (1997)

Before you start
  • Gear: the Lab 008 air path — the handheld recorder with foam fitted over every capsule, spare batteries and card space checked, closed phones for monitoring; Live 12 only hosts the excerpt at the end
  • Say it plainly: López stood in a rainforest, you will stand in whatever is densest within reach. The replication is the frame — one position, one gain, no moves — and that transfers to a boiling kitchen exactly as it does to La Selva
  • Field checklist applies: if the take is a street, identifiable voices mean you ask or you delete; one ear free near traffic; weather and the kit do not negotiate
  • New set saved as REP-LOPEZ — it will hold exactly one audio file and no devices
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — scouting, one fifteen-minute take, and listening that takes as long as the take
  • What you will make: a short replica of Francisco López — La Selva. Focus on this effect: an environment heard as one broadband mass - strata at every distance, form with no arranger, nothing foregrounded, nothing named.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: full-bandwidth capture with every decision made before the take - position, hour, gain - and none made during or after; no edits, no species labels.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Francisco López — La Selva. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Where is the recordist?
    Answer

    Nowhere audible. No zoom toward an animal, no fade flattering an event, no gain ride softening a downpour - the artistry all happened before the file existed: where to stand, which hours to keep, what level to hold.

  2. How many sounds are there?
    Answer

    Wrong unit. Counting sources sends you toward species lists, which the record refuses; count strata instead - close insect grain, mid-field movement, far weather - and the number becomes small and useful.

  3. Does it develop, with nobody editing development into it?
    Answer

    Yes - density rises and falls, registers trade places, sections seem to begin and end. Choosing when and where is composition enough; the forest supplies the arrangement and the recordist supplies the frame.

  4. Why no species names anywhere?
    Answer

    Because a label changes the listening. The moment something is “a howler monkey,” you hear documentation; unnamed, you hear sound - pitch, grain, distance, weight. López strips the names so the matter itself carries the piece.

  5. What is the gain doing through the loud and quiet passages?
    Answer

    Holding still. The downpours and the near-silences sit at their true relationship because one level was set for the loudest thing that might happen and then defended for the whole take - the lab’s field rule at full length.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a four-minute excerpt of one unbroken take — hard in, hard out, zero edits, zero fades — that holds attention as a mass with at least three audible strata, under a title that names nothing. Closeness to the record’s discipline is the measure, and the discipline is hearable as an absence.
  • One take, one position, one gain — no moves of any kind during the fifteen-plus minutes.
  • No edits inside the excerpt, no fades at its edges, no processing after it.
  • The title stays blank — a number, an hour, nothing that points.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a position chosen before the take, one gain set for the loudest thing that might happen, fifteen unbroken minutes with the hands in pockets, and a four-minute excerpt framed — not edited — out of it.

What the unmoving frame does to your hearing, in three snapshots. The recording never changes its behavior — the gain line under all three panels is the same flat line. Only the listening develops: the wall becomes strata, and the strata become form.

Phase 1 · ≈ 20 min

The reference and the mass

Phase focus: the reference and the mass. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live In Live: ⌘ T, rename REF, drag in a section of La Selva, loop three minutes, trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Live List your three densest reachable candidates — rain on a roof or skylight, a kitchen at full boil (kettle, extractor, pans, fridge), a rush-hour street from a doorway or balcony.
  3. Live Audition each, ears only, for two minutes of standing still: can you hear at least three strata by distance?
  4. Live If not, it is an event list, not a mass — move on.
  5. Recorder + Logbook Choose one candidate and one exact position — where the recorder will sit, not be held — and commit in the logbook: location, position, hour, and predicted loudest event.
  6. Recorder + Logbook Schedule the take for the candidate’s busiest hour.
  7. Recorder + Logbook Waiting for the right hour is composition; López kept the hours that performed.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, and the logbook holds a signed commitment: one place, one position, one hour, one predicted loudest event. Nothing else remains to decide.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Every candidate sounds like separate events with gapsNot dense enough — the mass needs simultaneityStack the kitchen on purpose (kettle + extractor + a pan of water at full boil) or wait for real rain
You cannot pick a position and keep changing your mindHunting the “best” spot — which is foregrounding before the takePick the spot where no single source dominates and commit; evenness, not drama, is the criterion
Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The gain rehearsal — one level, defensible for fifteen minutes

Phase focus: the gain rehearsal — one level, defensible for fifteen minutes. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Recorder Place the recorder at the committed position — set down or mounted, never hand-held for fifteen minutes.
  2. Recorder Foam fitted if any air moves.
  3. Recorder Provoke or wait for the loudest plausible event and set gain so it peaks ≈ −12 dBFS with headroom to spare.
  4. Live Now monitor the quietest stretch at that gain: the mass must still sit clearly above the hiss.
  5. Recorder If it does not, the position is too far from the mass — move the recorder closer and re-rehearse the peak.
  6. Recorder Position, not gain, resolves the conflict.
  7. Recorder Rehearse your own stillness: phones on, hands off, sixty seconds without touching anything.
  8. Recorder Any rustle you make now would be printed forever later.
  9. Logbook Write the final gain number in the logbook.
  10. Logbook It is now law for the take.
Expected result

One gain setting that survives both extremes — the predicted peak clears, the quiet floor clears the hiss — written down and frozen, with the recorder resting on something that is not a hand.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The quiet passages drown in hiss at the safe gainSelf-noise — the kit’s own performer, exposed by distanceMove the recorder into the mass instead of raising gain; proximity buys signal for free
Low blasts on the rehearsal even indoorsAir moving across bare capsules — the extractor or a window draftFoam on, and angle the recorder out of the airflow; wind is not “the sound of wind”
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

The take — fifteen minutes, no moves

Phase focus: the take — fifteen minutes, no moves. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Press record.
  2. Step back from the rig and stay still — sit if you can.
  3. The take is 15 minutes minimum; longer is better.
  4. Touch nothing.
  5. No gain ride when the kettle screams, no repositioning when the rain eases, no pause when “nothing is happening.” Nothing happening is material.
  6. While it runs, keep a pencil list titled moves I wanted to make — every urge to adjust, zoom, or rescue, with its timestamp.
  7. The urges are the lesson; the list is its record.
  8. When time is up, stop, and immediately log the take: location, hour, gain, weather or stove-state, and what the place did that the plan did not predict.
  9. Back up the file before leaving the position — per the field checklist.
Expected result

One unbroken fifteen-plus-minute file at one gain, a field log, and a pencil list of resisted moves — the audible absence of every item on that list is the technique, captured.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A peak clipped mid-takeThe loudest thing that might happen was louder than predictedThe take is dead — log the miss, reset gain for the new peak, and run it again; this is the field’s price
Thumps or rustle from your own body in the fileYou moved — shifted weight, touched the table, leaned in to check the meterRestart, sitting farther from the rig; the meter does not need watching, that is what the rehearsal was for
Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

The excerpt — framed, not edited

Phase focus: the excerpt — framed, not edited. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the take into REP-LOPEZ and listen end to end once, at the mark, before choosing anything.
  2. Choose four contiguous minutes — the stretch where the strata are busiest trading places.
  3. Live Consolidate that region to one clip: two hard cuts, head and tail.
  4. Live No fades, no edits inside, no Utility, no EQ.
  5. Live If the head cut feels brutal, good — a fade-in would be the recordist appearing at the door.
  6. Live Title it blank, in López’s idiom: untitled #1, or the hour — 17:40–17:44.
  7. Live Nothing that names the kitchen, the rain, or the street.
  8. Live Play it for yourself end to end, then A/B a minute against REF at matched loudness — judge only the discipline: does yours also refuse to point?
Expected result

A four-minute clip with two hard edges and a blank name that holds attention as a mass — and an A/B where the only audible difference is whose environment it was.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The excerpt feels boring rather than absorbingWrong four minutes — strata static, nothing entering or leavingRe-frame around an entrance or exit the place performed on its own; the frame chooses, the scissors don’t
You keep wanting a fade-in “just to be polite”Presentation habit — softening is pointingKeep the hard cut and listen ten more times; the brutality fades, the frame remains
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your excerpt at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Did you resist pointing at anything?
    Answer

    Audit the evidence: zero gain moves in the file, zero repositionings, zero edits inside the excerpt, zero fades at its edges, a title that names nothing — and a pencil list of every urge you did not act on. The discipline is hearable only as an absence, which is why the list matters: it proves the absence was chosen, not lucky. If any item failed the audit, the lesson is which urge won, and the take is worth rerunning just to beat it.

  • How many strata can you count inside the mass?
    Answer

    Three or more, counted by distance and register — a near grain, a mid layer of movement, a far floor — and the count should hold for the record too. If yours has fewer, the environment was too thin or the position too polite; the fix is density or microphone position, decided before the next take. Relisten to one minute of each, counting strata not sources, and write both numbers side by side — the gap between them is your next location’s brief.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 008 — “set for the loudest thing that might happen” is the lab’s rule for a moment; this record is that rule defended for fifteen minutes straight. The field log you wrote is the lab’s field log, already in the right shape.
