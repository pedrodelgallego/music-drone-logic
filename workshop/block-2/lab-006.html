Block 2 · Lab 006 · Étude

Capturing the World

The sampling path: your first recordings of the world, with the gain decisions owned. Three ways into the interface, one level test run in the field, and a one-minute concrète miniature made only of what you captured.

Étude · S1 · D2 E2 M3 F1 · concrète · half-cycle — pair with a listening session or a review week

listen → hypothesis → draw the path → calibrate → build → fault → diagnose → print → delta
Start here
  • Run the seven listening references below first — they are recordists making capture decisions, which is exactly the skill this étude trains.
  • Save a new set as LAB-006-etude.
  • Decide your three capture paths before leaving the desk: which mic, which phone, which line or contact source.
  • Expected time: one focused half-cycle — one capture outing plus one edit session.
The point

A recording is never just the sound — it is the sound plus a distance, a room, and a noise floor, all chosen at capture time.

How this lab works

Choose and capture

  • Three paths into the M4: mic, line, phone — three doors, three different results.
  • One capture through each door, of sounds you chose on purpose.
  • Every capture logged: place, distance, level.

The level test

  • The same source captured too quiet, correct, and too hot — in the field, not at the desk.
  • Listen back at home and hear what each mistake costs.
  • This is the block’s required comparison; own the gain decision where it actually happens.

A one-minute miniature

  • One minute of concrète from your own captures — nothing borrowed.
  • Selection and editing only: no effects, no synthesis.
  • The writing happened at capture time; the edit just reveals it.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
The capture chainMic → preamp → recorder: three stages in a row, and each one shapes the result. The mic decides what is heard, the preamp decides how loud and how clean, the recorder decides what survives. A weak link anywhere is in every capture forever.
Line vs mic vs phoneThree entry doors into the interface, with different noise and character. A line signal is already strong and clean; a mic signal is tiny and needs the preamp’s help (and inherits its hiss); a phone is a complete chain in your pocket — convenient, opinionated, and already processed.
PerspectiveWhere the mic stands is a writing decision. One step closer and the source dominates; one step back and the room takes over. You compose the recording with your feet before you press record.
Handling noiseThe recordist’s own body in the recording: fingers shifting on the recorder, the cable brushing a sleeve, footsteps through the floor. The mic does not know your touch is not the subject.
The noise floor of a placeEvery location has a hiss of its own — ventilation, distant traffic, electricity in the walls — audible the moment you listen for it. It is part of every capture you make there, whether you noticed it or not.

Listening — the hypothesis source

Seven recordists, seven capture strategies. Listen for the decisions made before and during recording — where to stand, how hot to run, when to start — because those are exactly the decisions this étude hands to you.

01 Pierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer” the founding act

The recorded world becomes material — the first time captured sound was treated as something to compose with, not document.

Listen for
  • Train sounds treated as rhythm and pitch, not as scenery.
  • The same fragment returning — repetition imposed on the world.
  • The moment you stop hearing “train” and start hearing “pattern.”
After — verify

When did the trains stop being trains for you? Usually at the first exact repeat — repetition strips the label off a sound and leaves only its shape. If they stayed trains throughout, relisten following one short fragment and count its returns.

02 Luc Ferrari — “Presque Rien No. 1” capture as composition

A morning by the sea, barely edited — the compositional choices are where to stand and when to start.

Listen for
  • How few edits you can actually detect — hunt for one.
  • What holds your attention when nothing is arranged.
  • How the scene changes over twenty minutes — and who chose that arc.
After — verify

What did Ferrari actually do? Almost nothing audible — he chose a place, a perspective, a starting moment, and compressed hours into twenty minutes. The piece argues that those choices alone are composition. If it felt like “just a recording,” relisten asking who decided what you would hear next.

03 Hildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk” gain as authorship

She turns the city down to make barnacles loud, and tells you so — the gain move is narrated as it happens.

Listen for
  • The moment she announces she is turning the city down — and the change that follows.
  • How big the tiny barnacle clicks become once the level decision favors them.
  • The distance of her voice versus the distance of the scene — two perspectives in one recording.
After — verify

What did the gain move change — the beach, or the hierarchy of the beach? The place stayed the same; the level decision rewrote what counted as foreground. That is gain as authorship, and it is the étude’s central lesson. If you missed the move, relisten for the sentence where she names it.

04 Chris Watson — El Tren Fantasma the composed field

Places joined into a journey that never happened — field recordings assembled with a composer’s hand.

Listen for
  • The joins between locations — can you find a single edit?
  • The perspectives: inside the train, beside the track, under it.
  • How clean the captures are — no handling, no wind thumps, no accidents.
After — verify

Did this train journey ever happen? Not as one ride — it is many captures, from many days and positions, joined into a continuous fiction. The seams you could not find are craft at the capture stage, not the edit. Relisten to one transition and notice how a shared sound carries you across it.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

05 Annea Lockwood — A Sound Map of the Hudson River one subject, many positions

The same river captured along its whole length — one subject, dozens of perspectives.

Listen for
  • What changes from site to site: the river, or everything around it?
  • Near water versus far water — how distance reads in a recording.
  • What stays recognizably “river” across every position.
After — verify

What was actually changing between sections? Mostly the position and the place — the subject held still while the perspective moved. The piece is a catalogue of what mic placement alone can say. If two sections sounded like different subjects, relisten: it is the same water at a different distance.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

06 Matmos — “Lipostudio (And So On…)” captured objects as dance music

Surgery recordings made groove — capture feeding sequencing, the recorded world pushed all the way to the dancefloor.

Listen for
  • Which sounds are recognizably surgical, and which have lost their origin entirely.
  • How a wet, irregular capture becomes a drum hit — what was kept, what was trimmed.
  • The grid imposed on material that had none.
After — verify

Could you name the sources without being told? Mostly not — the groove hides the origin, which is the point: a capture is raw material, not a subject it must stay loyal to. Relisten once knowing it is liposuction, and notice how the knowledge changes nothing about the beat.

07 Toshiya Tsunoda — Extract from Field Recording Archive the tiny made audible

Vibrations and small spaces — pipes, bottles, the inside of things; mic placement as the entire art.

Listen for
  • How small the captured spaces are — what could the mic be touching or inside?
  • The noise floor presented as part of the picture, not a flaw in it.
  • What a microphone hears in these positions that an ear never could.
After — verify

Where was the microphone? Against, inside, or coupled to the object — positions no listener occupies, which is why the recordings sound like nothing you have heard live. Placement is the whole composition here. Relisten to one track and write a one-line guess at the exact mic position.

Before you start — five minutes, then the field
  • Recorder or phone charged, storage checked — a dead battery is a capture that never existed.
  • If using a mic into the M4: gain set by the lab-001 ledger before the first take, not by ear in the moment.
  • Capture log template ready: place · distance · level, one line per capture.
  • Headphones for field monitoring at a moderate, fixed level.
  • Timebox written down: one outing, one edit session, done.

Brief & system

The brief

Capture the world through three different doors — one air mic, one phone, one line or contact source if available — run the level test on one source in the field, then edit a one-minute concrète miniature from your own captures. Selection and editing only.

System spec — provided (S1: verify it against what you actually capture)
Inputsthree captures of the world — one air mic, one phone, one line or contact if available
Transformsselection and editing only — no effects, no synthesis
Variationperspective and level — where you stood and how hot you ran, across captures
Constraintsone minute · own captures only · edited, never processed
Human rolecapture decisions — place, distance, level — plus the edit
  • Mini engineering note — the paths: recorder or phone → file → Live (the gain decision already happened in the field; import changes nothing) · or mic → M4 in 1, gain by the lab-001 ledger. Whichever door you use, the level is decided at capture, not at import — that is what “owning the gain decision” means.
  • Prescribed fault: record one capture deliberately too hot in the field, then listen back at the desk. Write one rule: field clipping is forever — there is no re-take of a moment that has passed, and no fader downstream repairs a clipped converter. No full write-up; the rule is the deliverable.
