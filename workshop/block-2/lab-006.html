Capturing the World

Record the world and own every volume decision you make. You will try three different ways to get sound into your interface, run a level test in the field, and edit a one-minute piece using only what you captured. Concrète is music built from recordings of real-world sounds — that is what this lab makes.

Start here
  • Run the ten listening references below first — they are recordists making capture decisions, which is exactly the skill this étude trains.
  • Save a new set as LAB-006-etude.
  • Decide your three capture paths before leaving the desk: which mic, which phone, which line or contact source.
  • Expected time: one focused half-cycle — one capture outing plus one edit session.
The point

A recording is the sound plus the distance you stood from it, the room around it, and the background hiss of the place — and you choose all three before you press record.

How this lab works

Choose and capture

  • Three ways into the MOTU M4: mic input, line input, or phone — each gives a different sound and noise level.
  • One recording through each path, of sounds you chose deliberately.
  • Log every capture: place, distance, and input level.

The level test

  • Record the same source three times: too quiet, correct, and too loud (clipping). Do this in the field, not back at your desk.
  • Listen to all three at home. Hear exactly what each mistake sounds like.
  • This is the block’s required comparison. You learn to set gain (input volume) in the place where it matters.

A one-minute miniature

  • One minute of concrète built entirely from your own recordings — nothing borrowed.
  • Selection and editing only: no effects, no synthesis.
  • The creative decisions happen when you record. Editing just chooses what to keep.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
The capture chainMic → preamp → recorder: three stages, each shaping the result. The mic picks up the sound, the preamp (a circuit that amplifies the tiny mic signal) sets how loud and how clean, and the recorder saves whatever arrives. A problem at any stage is baked into every recording you make.
Line vs mic vs phoneThree ways into the interface, each with different noise and character. A line-level signal is already strong and clean. A mic-level signal is tiny and needs the preamp to boost it — that boosting also adds a little hiss. A phone is a complete recording chain in your pocket: convenient, but it processes the sound before you even hear it.
PerspectiveWhere you place the mic is a creative decision. Move one step closer and the source fills the recording. Step back and the room takes over. You shape the recording with your feet, before you press record.
Handling noiseAny sound your body makes while holding the recorder ends up in the recording too: fingers shifting grip, a cable brushing your sleeve, footsteps through the floor. The mic cannot tell your touch apart from the subject.
The noise floor of a placeEvery location has its own background hiss — ventilation, distant traffic, electrical hum from the walls. It is in every recording you make there, whether you noticed it at the time or not.

Listening — the hypothesis source

Ten artists, ten capture strategies. As you listen, notice the decisions made before, during, and right after recording: where to stand, how loud to record, when to start, where to cut, how far a single object can be pushed. Those are the exact decisions this étude asks you to make.

01 Pierre Schaeffer — “Étude aux chemins de fer” the founding act

Schaeffer was the first person to treat a recording as raw material for composition rather than as a document. Here he uses train sounds as rhythm and pitch.

Listen for
  • Train sounds treated as rhythm and pitch, not as scenery.
  • The same fragment returning — repetition imposed on the world.
  • The moment you stop hearing “train” and start hearing “pattern.”
After — verify

When did the trains stop sounding like trains? For most listeners it happens at the first exact repeat — hearing the same fragment twice tells your brain it is a pattern, not a place. If they stayed trains throughout, try again and follow one short fragment, counting each time it comes back.

02 Luc Ferrari — “Presque Rien No. 1” capture as composition

A morning at the seaside, barely edited. Ferrari's main decisions were where to stand, when to press record, and how to compress twenty minutes of time into twenty minutes of listening.

Listen for
  • How few edits you can actually detect — hunt for one.
  • What holds your attention when nothing is arranged.
  • How the scene changes over twenty minutes — and who chose that arc.
After — verify

What did Ferrari actually do? Almost nothing you can hear. He chose a place, a perspective, and a starting moment. The piece argues that those decisions are enough to count as composition. If it felt like “just a recording,” relisten and ask: who decided what came next?

03 Hildegard Westerkamp — “Kits Beach Soundwalk” gain as authorship

Westerkamp turns her recorder's gain down to quiet the city noise, and turns it up to make tiny barnacle clicks fill the speakers — and she narrates each move as she does it.

Listen for
  • The moment she announces she is turning the city down — and the change that follows.
  • How big the tiny barnacle clicks become once the level decision favors them.
  • The distance of her voice versus the distance of the scene — two perspectives in one recording.
After — verify

What did the gain move change — the beach itself, or which sounds felt most important? The beach stayed the same. The level decision changed what you heard as the foreground. Adjusting gain is a creative choice, not just a technical one. If you missed the move, relisten and wait for the sentence where she announces it.

04 Chris Watson — El Tren Fantasma the composed field

Watson recorded trains across Mexico over many days and positions, then joined them into a single continuous journey that never actually happened.

Listen for
  • The joins between locations — can you find a single edit?
  • The perspectives: inside the train, beside the track, under it.
  • How clean the captures are — no handling, no wind thumps, no accidents.
After — verify

Did this journey happen? Not as one continuous ride. It is many separate recordings, from different days and positions, edited into a seamless whole. The invisible joins come from skilled capture, not just clever editing. Relisten to one transition and notice how a shared sound bridges the cut.

05 Annea Lockwood — A Sound Map of the Hudson River one subject, many positions

Lockwood recorded the Hudson River at many points along its length — the same subject, but dozens of different positions and distances.

Listen for
  • What changes from site to site: the river, or everything around it?
  • Near water versus far water — how distance reads in a recording.
  • What stays recognizably “river” across every position.
After — verify

What changed between sections? Mostly your position and the surroundings — the river itself stayed the same while the perspective moved. The piece shows how much mic placement alone can say. If two sections sounded like completely different subjects, relisten: it is the same water, just from a different distance.

06 Matmos — “Lipostudio (And So On…)” captured objects as dance music

Matmos recorded sounds from a liposuction surgery and turned them into dance music — the captured sounds are cut, looped, and sequenced into a groove.

Listen for
  • Which sounds are recognizably surgical, and which have lost their origin entirely.
  • How a wet, irregular capture becomes a drum hit — what was kept, what was trimmed.
  • The grid imposed on material that had none.
After — verify

Could you name the sources without being told? Probably not — the groove hides where the sounds came from. That is the point: a recording is raw material. You do not have to stay loyal to its original meaning. Relisten knowing the source, and notice that the knowledge changes nothing about how the beat feels.

07 Walter Ruttmann — “Weekend” montage before tape

Ruttmann recorded a Berlin weekend onto optical film in 1930 and cut the sounds together — the first piece made entirely of edited sound, two decades before Schaeffer had tape to work with.

Listen for
  • The cut rhythm — edits arriving like drum hits, the splice itself as percussion.
  • Recognizable sources — saws, bells, voices, traffic — turning abstract purely through the speed of the montage.
  • How short each cell is: most sounds get a second or less before the next cut takes over.
After — verify

What instrument is playing this piece? The splice itself. The sounds are ordinary; the rhythm lives entirely in how fast they are cut. If it felt like a documentary, relisten while tapping along with the edits — you will find yourself tapping a tempo.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

08 Pierre Henry — “Variations pour une porte et un soupir” one object, a whole catalogue

The whole album uses one creaking door and a human sigh — nothing else. It shows that a single recorded object contains enough variety to fill an entire work, if you explore it carefully enough.

Listen for
  • How many distinct voices one door has — creak, groan, slam, squeal — each treated as a separate instrument.
  • The sigh as the door’s counterpart: breath against hinge, the soft answering the mechanical.
  • The variation logic — each movement isolates one behaviour of the object and stays on it.
After — verify

How much source material is there? A door, a sigh, and almost nothing else — sounds you could capture in one afternoon. That is the point: focused attention on one object can replace a whole library of sources. If the album felt too varied to be just two objects, pick one movement and name the single door behaviour it is built on.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

09 Bernard Parmegiani — De Natura Sonorum capture sorted by physics

Parmegiani organised this album not by what sounds are, but by what they do — striking, bouncing, resonating, breathing. Recorded sounds and synthesized sounds sit side by side because they behave the same way.

Listen for
  • Movements built on one physical behaviour — striking, bouncing, resonating — rather than on one source.
  • Captured sounds and synthesized ones sitting in the same bin because they behave the same way.
  • How a pairing like “Incidences/Résonances” is audible: the hit, then everything the hit excites.
After — verify

What is the sorting key — what a sound is, or what it does? What it does. A field recording and a synthesizer patch that behave the same way are placed together. Where the sound came from stops mattering. If you found yourself sorting by source, relisten to one movement and try filing each sound by behaviour instead.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

10 Matthew Herbert — Around the House the house as drum kit

Herbert recorded his own kitchen, bathroom, doors, and dishes, then sampled and sequenced them into full dance tracks. It is the concrète method applied to house music, fifty years after Schaeffer's train recordings.

Listen for
  • Drum hits that are audibly domestic — crockery, water, a latch — once you listen past the groove.
  • The grid doing what repetition did for Schaeffer: a kitchen sound looped until it is simply a drum.
  • How few layers each groove needs when every hit carries a real object’s character.
After — verify

Where does the concrète end and the house track begin? They are the same thing: capture, trim, repeat. A sequencer grid is just repetition with a fixed tempo. If the sources stayed hidden, relisten to one track and try to find a single hit you could re-record in your own kitchen tonight.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start — five minutes, then the field
  • Recorder or phone charged, storage checked — a dead battery is a capture that never existed.
  • If using a mic into the M4: set gain using the lab-001 ledger before your first take — do not adjust by ear in the moment.
  • Capture log template ready: place · distance · level, one line per capture.
  • Headphones for field monitoring at a moderate, fixed level.
  • Timebox written down: one outing, one edit session, done.

Brief & system

The brief

Record the world using three different input paths: one air mic, one phone, one line or contact source if available. Run the level test on one source while you are in the field. Then edit a one-minute concrète piece from your own recordings. Selection and editing only — no effects, no synthesis.

System spec — provided (S1: verify it against what you actually capture)
Inputsthree captures of the world — one air mic, one phone, one line or contact if available
Transformsselection and editing only — no effects, no synthesis
Variationperspective and level — where you stood and how hot you ran, across captures
Constraintsone minute · own captures only · edited, never processed
Human rolecapture decisions — place, distance, level — plus the edit
  • Mini engineering note — the paths: recorder or phone → file → Live (the gain decision already happened in the field; importing does not change the level) · or mic → M4 input 1, gain set by the lab-001 ledger. Either way, the level is locked at capture time, not at import. That is what “owning the gain decision” means.
  • Prescribed fault: record one capture deliberately too hot in the field so it clips (the signal is too loud and the top gets cut off, adding a harsh distortion). Listen back at the desk. Write one rule: clipping in the field is permanent — the moment is gone and no fader downstream can fix a clipped recording. One sentence is enough; that rule is the deliverable.
