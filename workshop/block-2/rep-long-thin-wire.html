Replicate: the system plays itself

Copy one clear section of Alvin Lucier — Music on a Long Thin Wire. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record’s core mechanism: a system set near self-resonance (the threshold where a filter or circuit starts to sing on its own) and then left alone. Tiny changes in tension, temperature, and air move the sound. The composer’s last act is starting it. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Safety first, and not as a formality: this lesson runs a filter at the edge of self-oscillation — the point where a filter starts to ring on its own, close to uncontrolled feedback. It can jump in level without warning. Before any sound: a Limiter on Live’s master, ceiling −6 dB; phones at the lab-001 mark or lower, never raised during the print; and the one-hand-on-the-fader rule — whenever the system is running, one hand rests on the master fader, ready to pull it down, not the phones volume
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack; the Digitakt II rests. You do not own a fifty-foot wire: a resonant filter pushed to where it sings on its own, nudged by a slow LFO at minimum depth, is the honest stand-in — a circuit on the edge instead of a wire under tension, drifting for reasons you likewise cannot control
  • New set saved as REP-WIRE — tempo does not matter; the print is ten unbroken minutes of clock time
  • What you will make: a short replica of Alvin Lucier — Music on a Long Thin Wire. Focus on this effect: drifting pitch and blooming harmonics with no performer — the system doing everything after being started.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a system set near self-resonance and then left alone — tiny changes in tension, temperature, and air move the sound; the composer’s last act is starting it.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or equipment limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Alvin Lucier — Music on a Long Thin Wire. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Who is playing?
    Answer

    No one. An oscillator drives a long wire through a magnet; the wire resonates, the room responds, and everything you hear follows from that arrangement. The composer’s performance ended when the system started.

  2. The pitch drifts. What is changing it?
    Answer

    Physics — tension, temperature, the air in the room, the wire’s own modes feeding back on themselves. Changes that small would be inaudible in most music. Here they are the entire foreground, because nothing louder competes.

  3. Are there events — and if so, who wrote them?
    Answer

    Yes — swells, harmonics blooming out of nowhere, sudden jumps where the wire flips to a different resonance. Nobody wrote them. They are the system passing through its own thresholds.

  4. Could the performer make the wire do any of that again?
    Answer

    No — and that is the biggest difference from everything else in this block. The composer controls conditions: wire length, drive frequency, room. The events within those conditions are not repeatable.

  5. What gives the piece its form, if no one is composing?
    Answer

    Duration and the room. The piece is however long the system runs — the recording is a window cut from days of installation — and its “sections” are the system’s slow wandering between stable states.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your print lands on the same quality — drifting pitch, blooming overtones, events with no fingerprints on them.
  • You may set the system, then you must leave it alone — the ten-minute print is recorded with hands off the gear.
  • The only modulation is one slow LFO (a slow automatic knob-mover) at minimum depth — a stand-in for temperature drift, not a performance.
  • The limiter stays on the master from the first sound to the last; its ceiling is non-negotiable.

The whole mechanism in one row: a filter on the edge of self-oscillation, almost nothing feeding it, one barely-moving LFO nudging the cutoff like changing temperature, a limiter standing guard, and ten minutes printed with no one playing.

MicroFreak filter · resonance at the singing edge → slow LFO · minimum depth · the “temperature” → 10 unattended minutes · printed → master · Limiter at −6 dB · always

Three panels: you set three conditions and start the system; the system runs alone, feeding on itself; what comes out is a drifting line with blooms and jumps you never asked for. Your role ends at the first panel’s edge.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The safety rig, then the reference

Phase focus: the safety rig, then the reference. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. On the master: Audio Effects → Limiter, ceiling −6 dB.
  2. This device is the first thing in the set and the last thing removed — confirm it is on before anything is armed.
  3. Phones at the lab-001 mark or lower.
  4. Write the mark in the logbook with this rule beside it: the phones level does not move during this lesson, no matter how quiet the system gets.
  5. ⌘ T, rename REF (⌘ R), drag Music on a Long Thin Wire in, loop four minutes, and Utility-trim so it peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  6. ⌘ T once; rename: WIRE, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed, software monitoring on at a low level.
  7. Rehearse the one-hand rule once, dry: hand on the master fader, pull to silence in under a second.
  8. It will likely never be needed; rehearse it anyway.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Alvin Lucier — Music on a Long Thin Wire (1977) and use MIDI effects, Random, LFO/Expression Control, and resampling.

  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.

Phase 2 · ≈ 20 min

Find the edge — a filter that almost sings alone

Phase focus: find the edge — a filter that almost sings alone. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, oscillator volume far down — the filter needs almost nothing to chew on, a whisper of input, not a tone.
  2. Raise filter Resonance slowly, listening at the mark, hand near the master fader.
  3. Somewhere high on the dial the filter begins to sing at its cutoff pitch. Find that threshold, then ease back slightly — to where the singing comes and goes on its own.
  4. Set Cutoff so the sung pitch sits somewhere mid-low — around A2–A3 is comfortable to listen to for ten minutes.
  5. This is your equivalent of the wire length.
  6. Check the level of the singing at its loudest: peaks ≈ −12 dBFS on the input meter.
  7. If it runs hotter, lower the M4 gain — never compensate at the phones.
  8. Sit with the bare edge for two minutes, hands off, before adding anything.
  9. If it already drifts and sputters on its own, you have found the right spot;
  10. note the resonance position in the logbook.

Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

Add the weather, then print ten minutes hands-off

Phase focus: add the weather, then print ten minutes hands-off. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak LFO: sine, sync off, rate at the slowest the dial offers.
  2. In the mod matrix, route LFO → cutoff at the smallest depth that noticeably changes the system’s behavior over a minute — start at nearly nothing and creep up. The LFO must never sound like an audible sweep.
  3. Listen for five minutes, hands off, one hand resting on the master fader. The system should wander: singing, faltering, finding a different overtone. If it settles into one steady state, nudge the depth one step — then hands off again.
  4. Record-arm WIRE, press record, and print 10:00 by the clock.
  5. Hands off the MicroFreak entirely. Stay within reach, re-read the reference-pass notes, watch the limiter do nothing.
  6. The rule: nothing is touched during the print. A boring stretch is not a malfunction — it is the system in a stable state. The wire had those too.

Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

Listen to what it decided

Phase focus: listen to what it decided. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the full ten minutes end to end, no skipping, phones at the mark.
  2. Log every event with a timestamp — every bloom, drop, drift, and jump, in plain words.
  3. Against each event, write who made it. The honest answer, every line: not you. The LFO you set is too slow and too shallow to account for any single event’s timing.
  4. A/B three minutes of your print against REF at matched loudness, judging one thing: do both feel found rather than made — incident without intention?
  5. Choose the best two minutes of the print and mark them with locators.
  6. That choice — a window cut from the system’s run — is the only composing in this lesson. It is exactly the composing Lucier kept for himself.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your print at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Did you compose any event in the print?
    Answer

    No — and the session can prove it. Your inputs were three conditions set before recording began: a resonance position, a barely-moving LFO, a level under a limiter. Every event in the log happened at a time you did not choose, in a shape you did not draw. What you composed was the situation; the music composed itself. Listen to your chosen two minutes knowing you could not make them happen again. That is exactly what you wrote about the wire in the reference pass.

  • Describe two behaviors the system produced that you did not ask for.
    Answer

    Your log has them — typically a slow pitch drift with no LFO to blame, and a sudden bloom or jump where the filter found a different resonance. Whatever your two were, the test is the same: no setting you touched specifies them, so they belong to the system. Keep this in mind: a patch is not just a sound — set near its edges, it behaves like a collaborator with its own ideas. Listen to the record one last time and count those ideas. The wire had many.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: Lab 005 says your role is mostly to listen, moving a hand only when the tone stops breathing. This record takes that to its limit: a system set well needs no hand at all. When your drone is built, ask how much of it could run without you — and whether the parts that need you are the parts worth keeping.
