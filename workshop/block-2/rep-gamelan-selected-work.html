Replicate: kotekan and ombak

Steal two mechanisms from Balinese Gong Kebyar — e.g. Kebyar Ding: a fast line that no single player performs, built by interlocking two parts (polos and sangsih) so each plays only fragments and the melody lives between them; and the ensemble's shimmer, made by tuning instrument pairs slightly apart so they beat against each other (ombak, meaning "waves"). Build a short take and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

You are not copying the Balinese melody, the bronze keys, or the ceremonial form. You are copying two mechanisms: hocket (interlocking), where the tune only exists in the gaps between two parts; and paired detuning, where a small pitch difference turns a held note into a pulsing shimmer. Build both on the rig. The melody between the parts and the beating between the layers — that is the whole brief.

The system you are replicating
Source recordBalinese Gamelan Gong Kebyar — e.g. Kebyar Ding
The effectA figuration too fast and too even to be one human, sitting in a bright, restless shimmer that seems to breathe even when the pitch holds still
The mechanismTwo parts hocket — each plays only part of the line, neither plays the tune alone — and paired instruments are tuned a hair apart so identical notes beat against each other

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Digitakt II synced to Live, with at least two free tracks loaded with the same bright, short-decay metallic sound
  • MicroFreak on Live's input — you will use it (and/or a second Digitakt voice) as the detuned partner for the ombak layer
  • A short pitched figure of your own invention written out — five or six notes is plenty. Never transcribe the Balinese melody; bring your own line
  • What you will make: a 60–90 second study where one fast line is performed by two interlocking parts that each play only half the notes, layered with a paired-detuned voice that shimmers. Focus on this effect: a line faster than your hands could play, inside a pulsing brightness.
  • What you will turn off: chords, song structure, a full kit, any second idea. One interlocked line, one shimmer.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanisms — the tune lives between two parts, and the brightness is acoustic beating between detuned pairs.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about two minutes of the Gong Kebyar recording. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Take the fastest figuration. Could one player physically perform every note of it at that speed and evenness?
    Answer

    No — and that impossibility is the design, not a recording trick. The figure is shared between two players (polos and sangsih). Each plays only some of the notes and rests on the others, so each hand moves at half the apparent speed. Together, the two streams fill each other's gaps and the result is faster and more even than one pair of hands could manage. The blur you hear is two people, not one virtuoso.

  2. If you tried to hum the fast line, could you point to one part that is "the tune"?
    Answer

    Not really. Neither polos nor sangsih is the melody on its own — solo, each sounds like a stuttering, incomplete fragment. The tune only exists in the combination, living in the gaps where one part rests and the other plays. That is the heart of hocket: the line is between the players, not inside either of them.

  3. When a note is held or repeated, is the pitch dead still, or does it pulse and shimmer?
    Answer

    It pulses. Gamelan instruments are tuned in pairs — one slightly above a target pitch, one slightly below. When both play the "same" note, the small difference in frequency produces acoustic beating: the two sound waves drift in and out of sync and the volume swells and dips at a steady rate. That is ombak, "waves." A perfectly in-tune ensemble would sound flat by comparison; the shimmer is deliberate.

  4. What is the attack character — soft and rounded, or bright and metallic with a fast bite?
    Answer

    Bright and metallic, with a hard percussive bite and a quick decay. The bronze keys give a sharp transient and a ringing tail that is damped quickly for the next note. That short, bright attack is what keeps the fast interlock sounding like distinct strikes rather than a blur — each note has a clear edge, so the hocket stays readable even at speed.

Now build the two mechanisms you just described. Do not transcribe their melody — build your own line through their machinery.

  • Sound target: a fast figure split across two interlocking parts, sitting inside a steady shimmer from a paired-detuned layer.
  • The line: your own five- or six-note figure, never the Balinese melody — it is only the raw material the hocket runs on.
  • The interlock: two parts (polos and sangsih) that each play only alternate or interlocking strikes; neither plays the whole line; together they are one continuous run.
  • The shimmer: a held or repeated voice doubled by a copy detuned a few cents (or a small Hz offset), so the pair beats at an audible, steady rate.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Split one line into two interlocking parts

Phase focus: build the hocket so the tune lives between the parts, not inside either one. Compare against REF before moving on.

one fast line (your figure) → polos (odd strikes) + sangsih (even strikes) → one continuous interlocked run
Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track and gain-match it with a Utility so A/Bs are honest.
  2. Load the same bright sound on two tracks — call them polos and sangsih. Same sample, same tuning, so the only difference is which notes each one plays.
  3. Program polos: place your figure's notes on alternate steps only — e.g. steps 1, 3, 5, 7 — leaving the in-between steps empty.
  4. Program sangsih to fill the gaps: the notes polos left out land on steps 2, 4, 6, 8. Solo each track: each should sound like a holey fragment, not a tune.
  5. Unmute both. The two halves should fuse into one fast, even line at double the apparent speed of either part alone.
  6. A/B the interlocked run against REF for evenness — no part should stick out as louder or early.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Balinese Gamelan Gong Kebyar — e.g. Kebyar Ding and use Live audio/MIDI tracks, Utility, automation, and resampling.

Phase 2 — ~25 minutes

Make the shimmer with a detuned pair

Phase focus: turn a steady note into a pulsing one by setting two layers a hair apart so they beat.

Do this
  1. Hold one steady note at a pitch from your figure — a clean, sustaining voice you can layer.
  2. Make its partner: a second voice at the same pitch, then detune it a few cents sharp (or set a small Hz offset) so it sits fractionally above its twin.
  3. Layer the pair and listen for the beat. When both play the same note, you should hear the loudness swell and dip at a steady rate — that pulsing is the acoustic beating (the ombak).
  4. Adjust the beat rate by changing the offset: a tiny difference beats slowly (a gentle breathing shimmer); a wider one beats faster (a buzzing, rough edge). Set it to a slow, musical pulse, not a vibrato.
  5. A/B the shimmer against REF: held notes in the gamelan should pulse at a comparable, living rate — neither dead-still nor seasick.

Phase 3 — ~25 minutes

Combine and perform: interlock inside the shimmer

Phase focus: run the hocketed line on top of the detuned shimmer and record one continuous take. This is the whole piece.

Do this
  1. Write the plan first: a short note of where the bare shimmer holds, where the interlocked line enters, and where both run together — your score for the take.
  2. Set the detuned pair as a sustained bed and let the shimmer establish before the figuration arrives.
  3. Bring in the interlock — polos and sangsih together — over the shimmer, so the fast line sits inside the pulsing brightness.
  4. Record the journey in one pass: shimmer alone, then interlock enters, then both, then thin back. No chords, no kit — the two mechanisms carry it.
  5. Listen back with eyes closed: can a stranger hear that the fast line is impossible for one player, and that the held sound is alive rather than static?

Verify against the record

A/B your take against a comparable stretch of the Gong Kebyar recording — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

If you solo each of your two parts, is each one plainly incomplete — and only the combination a real line?

Answer

That is the test for genuine hocket. If one part is hummable alone, you wrote a melody plus a decoration, not an interlock. Each soloed part should sound stuttering and incomplete; the tune should only appear when both play. If yours fails this, go back to phase 1 and re-deal the notes strike by strike.

Hold one note in yours and one in theirs at matched loudness — does yours pulse like theirs, and is the rate in the same ballpark?

Answer

It should pulse, and the rate is set by your detune amount. If yours is dead-still, the layers are perfectly in tune — nudge one off centre until the swell appears. If yours wobbles wildly while theirs breathes slowly, your offset is too wide; narrow it. The gamelan's brightness comes from this beating, not from EQ or reverb. Matching the pulse proves you copied the mechanism, not just the timbre.
