Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the loop point as instrument

Copy one clear section of Oneohtrix Point Never — “Replica”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: fragments sampled from TV-advertisement compilations, looped at the phrase’s least stable point - mid-gesture, mid-word - under a sparse piano-like figure. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: any banal audio source — radio, a podcast, an ad playing from a browser — plus the MicroFreak into M4 input 1 for the piano-ish layer, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack; the Digitakt II is the loop-point bench if you prefer knobs to markers
  • New set saved as REP-REPLICA — no tempo worth setting yet; the loop will dictate its own
  • You will hear one two-second fragment several hundred times this session — phones at the lab-001 mark, and take a real break at the half hour before judging meaning
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop — most of the time goes into the audition, and that is correct
  • What you will make: a short replica of Oneohtrix Point Never — “Replica”. Focus on this effect: a short orphan loop, cut from material too banal to mean anything, repeating until it carries a grief the source never contained.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: fragments sampled from TV-advertisement compilations, looped at the phrase’s least stable point - mid-gesture, mid-word - under a sparse piano-like figure.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Oneohtrix Point Never — “Replica”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How long are the actual source moments?
    Answer

    A second or two - and the piece dwells on them for minutes. The ratio is the form: almost no material, almost all repetition. What feels like development is mostly your own attention deepening on an unchanged fragment.

  2. Where does each loop point fall — at a stable moment or a stumbling one?
    Answer

    Stumbling. The loops turn around mid-gesture - inside a word, halfway through a chord change, just before a phrase resolves - so every repeat reaches for a resolution that never arrives.

  3. Could the source material have sounded like this in its original context?
    Answer

    No - and that is the test the record passes. Nothing in an advertisement sounds like grief; the feeling lives entirely in the treatment.

  4. What turns the seam into a rhythm?
    Answer

    Repetition at a fixed interval. The loop point is a small audible event - a bump, a swallowed consonant, a truncated decay - and once it recurs every couple of seconds it stops being a flaw and becomes the pulse.

  5. What is the second layer doing under the loops?
    Answer

    Almost nothing, on purpose - a sparse piano-like figure, a few notes that frame the loop without competing with it. It gives the orphan fragment a harmonic home so the stumble reads as longing rather than malfunction.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same dwelling — one stumbling loop carrying the weight, one sparse layer framing it — at matched loudness.
  • One source fragment, 2–3 seconds, from something genuinely banal — if the source already sounds like music you are proud of, it is the wrong source.
  • The loop point is chosen by audition, not by zoom: at least ten candidates on the same material before any is kept.
  • Two layers total: the loop and one piano-ish figure. Nothing else gets in.

The piece’s machinery in one row: two seconds of banal capture, a dozen loop points auditioned on it, the most unstable one kept and repeated for two minutes under a few framing notes, mixed at ≈ −12 dBFS.

2–3 s banal capture · radio / ad → 10+ loop points · same material → keep the stumble → loop + piano figure · ≈ −12 dBFS

Three loop points on the same two seconds of audio, drawn as three brackets. The material never changes; only where the bracket closes changes, and each bracket is a different piece of music. The third one — closing mid-gesture — is the record’s choice.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the banal capture

Phase focus: the reference and the banal capture. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename REF, drag “Replica” in, loop a characteristic minute, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility so it peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Live Find a banal source: a talk-radio stream, a podcast ad break, a shopping channel.
  3. Live Record 30–60 seconds of it into a track named CAPTURE — set the input or resample gain so it peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Live Listen back once and cut a 2–3 second region that contains a complete small gesture: a sentence fragment with a pitch rise, two chords of muzak, a slogan.
  5. Live Consolidate it (⌘ J) and name the clip ORPHAN.
  6. Logbook Write one logbook line: what the fragment is selling or saying.
  7. Logbook Phase 4 checks whether that meaning survived.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS and one consolidated 2–3 second fragment of genuinely banal audio sits on its own track, its original meaning noted on paper.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Nothing in the capture feels usableHunting for “good” material — the wrong criterion for this lessonTake the most ordinary complete gesture instead; the loop point will do the work, not the source
The capture is distorted or buried in noiseResample/input gain set carelessly — banal does not mean carelessRe-record with peaks ≈ −12 dBFS; the fragment must be clean enough that the stumble you add later is the only flaw
Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

The audition — ten loop points on one fragment

Phase focus: the audition — ten loop points on one fragment. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Drop ORPHAN into a Simpler on a new track named LOOP; set Mode → Classic, loop on.
  2. Digitakt II Digitakt II alternative: load it to a track, press [SRC], turn on LOOP, and adjust STA/LEN while it plays — knobs make this audition faster.
  3. Digitakt II Audition bracket one: the “correct” loop — start at the gesture’s beginning, end at its natural rest.
  4. Digitakt II Hold a note and listen for 30 seconds.
  5. Digitakt II Log one word for what it feels like.
  6. Digitakt II Now move the end point earlier in steps — cut before the resolution, then mid-word, then mid-syllable.
  7. Digitakt II Then move the start point into the gesture’s middle.
  8. Digitakt II At least ten distinct brackets, 30 seconds of held listening each, one logged word per bracket.
  9. Digitakt II You are listening for the bracket that reaches — the one where every repeat leans toward a resolution that never comes.
  10. Digitakt II It usually ends just before something: the chord change, the stressed syllable, the cadence.
  11. Digitakt II Keep the most unstable, most yearning bracket.
  12. Logbook Write down its exact start and length numbers — you will need them for the verify step.
Expected result

A logbook list of ten-plus brackets with one word each, and one kept loop that stumbles forward every pass — the same audio as bracket one, and a completely different piece of music.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Every bracket sounds the sameMoves too small, or judging in two-second sipsMove the point by a tenth of the fragment at a time, and hold each bracket a full 30 seconds — instability needs repeats to show
The seam clicks hard on every passLoop points landing mid-waveform at full levelNudge to a zero crossing or add a few ms of crossfade — keep the stumble, lose the spike
Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The frame — one piano-ish layer, almost nothing

Phase focus: the frame — one piano-ish layer, almost nothing. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak MicroFreak: a soft keys patch — Type → Wavetable or V.Analog, gentle attack, medium release, filter closed to mellow.
  2. MicroFreak It should suggest a piano remembered, not a piano present.
  3. Live Set the M4 input-1 gain on held notes, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, and record onto a track named FRAME.
  4. Live Find two or three notes that sit under the loop’s pitch center — play along with the loop until your hand finds them;
  5. Live the right ones make the loop’s lean ache more, not resolve.
  6. Live Record two minutes of the figure played sparsely — a note or two per bar at most, with bars of rest.
  7. Live If you play a melody, you have played too much; this layer frames, it does not speak.
Expected result

Two minutes of sparse, soft pitched notes printed at ≈ −12 dBFS that make the kept loop lean harder — a frame that deepens the stumble without explaining it.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The layer resolves the loop — everything suddenly sounds settledNotes chosen that complete the loop’s implied cadenceMove one note a step away from comfort; the frame should hold the question open, not answer it
The frame competes with the loop for attentionToo many notes, or the patch too brightHalve the note count and close the filter further — the loop is the subject, the frame is the wall behind it
Phase 4 · ≈ 25 min

Build two minutes, then move the cut and listen to the meaning move

Phase focus: build two minutes, then move the cut and listen to the meaning move. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Arrange two minutes: the loop alone for the first 20–30 seconds — let the listener learn its stumble — then the frame entering underneath.
  2. Live One pause where the loop stops for a bar and returns.
  3. Live Nothing else.
  4. Live Balance: loop in front, frame behind, mix peaking ≈ −12 dBFS.
  5. Live Play it end to end without touching anything.
  6. Live A/B against REF at matched loudness, judging only two things: does your loop reach the way the record’s loops reach, and is your piece as patient about repeating?
  7. Live The cut test: duplicate the LOOP track, and on the copy move the loop’s end point 100 ms — one number, one nudge.
  8. Live Solo-flip between the two for a minute.
  9. Live Log one line: what changed in the meaning, not the sound.
  10. Logbook Reread your phase 1 logbook line about what the fragment was selling.
  11. Logbook Ask whether any of that survived.
  12. Logbook It should not have.
Expected result

A two-minute piece built from one stumbling loop and a sparse frame, plus a logbook line proving you heard a 100 ms edit change what the music is about.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The two minutes feel boring rather than hypnoticThe kept bracket was stable after all — boredom is the symptom of a loop that resolvesReturn to the phase 2 list and take the runner-up bracket; the right loop makes repetition feel like waiting, not like wallpaper
The 100 ms move changes nothing you can hearThe nudge landed inside silence or sustained tone — dead territoryMove the point toward the nearest consonant, attack, or chord change; meaning lives at the edges of events
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Move your loop point 100 ms — did the meaning change?
    Answer

    It should have, and audibly: a loop that ended reaching now ends shrugging, or a swallowed word becomes a completed one and the longing drains out. If both versions feel interchangeable, your fragment carries its character in a sustained middle rather than at its edges — usable, but it makes the loop point a volume knob instead of an instrument. The record’s whole method depends on cuts landing where events live. Keep the version that aches more, and write down why in one line.

  • Which loop point does the record choose — stable or stumbling?
    Answer

    Stumbling, nearly every time. Relisten to two or three loops on “Replica” and sing past each cut: the phrase almost always wanted to go somewhere the loop refuses to take it. That refusal, metronomically repeated, is what converts advertisement residue into elegy — the loop mourns its own missing resolution every couple of seconds. A stable cut would have nothing to mourn. If you heard the record’s loops as tidy, relisten following only the turnarounds; the stumble is sitting in plain sight.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 007 — the lab teaches that the resample path is an authorship decision; this record shows the loop point is one too. Capture, path, and cut are three places where the meaning of a sample gets written, and none of them is the source.
