Replicate: the loop point as instrument

Copy one clear section of Oneohtrix Point Never — “Replica”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: fragments sampled from TV-advertisement compilations, looped at the phrase’s least stable point - mid-gesture, mid-word - under a sparse piano-like figure. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordOneohtrix Point Never — “Replica” (2011, Replica)
The effecta short loop cut from ordinary, meaningless source material that, through repetition, ends up feeling full of longing the original never had
The mechanismfragments sampled from TV-advertisement compilations, looped at the phrase’s least stable point — mid-gesture, mid-word — under a sparse piano-like figure

Before you start
  • Gear: any banal audio source — radio, a podcast, an ad playing from a browser — plus the MicroFreak into M4 input 1 for the piano-ish layer, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack; the Digitakt II is the loop-point bench if you prefer knobs to markers
  • New set saved as REP-REPLICA — no tempo worth setting yet; the loop will dictate its own
  • You will hear one two-second fragment several hundred times this session — phones at the lab-001 mark, and take a real break at the half hour before judging meaning
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop — most of the time goes into the audition, and that is correct
  • What you will make: a short replica of Oneohtrix Point Never — “Replica”. Focus on this effect: a short orphan loop, cut from material too banal to mean anything, repeating until it carries a grief the source never contained.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: fragments sampled from TV-advertisement compilations, looped at the phrase’s least stable point - mid-gesture, mid-word - under a sparse piano-like figure.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Oneohtrix Point Never — “Replica”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How long are the actual source moments?
    Answer

    A second or two — and the piece dwells on them for minutes. Almost no material, almost all repetition. What feels like development is mostly your own attention deepening on an unchanged fragment.

  2. Where does each loop point fall — at a stable moment or a stumbling one?
    Answer

    Stumbling. The loops turn around mid-gesture — inside a word, halfway through a chord change, just before a phrase resolves — so every repeat reaches for a conclusion that never arrives.

  3. Could the source material have sounded like this in its original context?
    Answer

    No — and that is the test this record passes. Nothing in an advertisement sounds like longing. The feeling lives entirely in how the fragment is cut and repeated.

  4. What turns the seam into a rhythm?
    Answer

    Repetition at a fixed interval. The loop point is a small audible event — a bump, a clipped consonant, a cut-off decay. Once it recurs every couple of seconds, it stops being a flaw and becomes the pulse.

  5. What is the second layer doing under the loops?
    Answer

    Almost nothing, on purpose — a sparse piano-like figure, a few notes that frame the loop without competing. It gives the fragment a harmonic home so the stumble reads as longing rather than malfunction.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: when solo-flipped against REF at matched loudness, both should share the same quality — one stumbling loop carrying the weight, one sparse layer framing it.
  • One source fragment, 2–3 seconds, from something genuinely ordinary. If the source already sounds like music you are proud of, it is the wrong source.
  • The loop point is chosen by listening, not by zooming in on a waveform. Try at least ten positions on the same material before keeping any.
  • Two layers total: the loop and one piano-like figure. Nothing else gets in.

How the piece works: two seconds of ordinary audio, a dozen loop point positions auditioned on it, the most unstable one kept and repeated for two minutes under a few framing notes, mixed at ≈ −12 dBFS.

2–3 s banal capture · radio / ad → 10+ loop points · same material → keep the stumble → loop + piano figure · ≈ −12 dBFS

Three different loop points on the same two seconds of audio. The material never changes; only where the loop ends changes. Each bracket creates a different piece of music. The third one — closing mid-gesture — is the record’s choice.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the banal capture

Phase focus: the reference and the banal capture. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename REF, drag “Replica” in, loop a characteristic minute, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility so it peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Find a banal source: a talk-radio stream, a podcast ad break, a shopping channel.
  3. Record 30–60 seconds of it into a track named CAPTURE — set the input or resample gain so it peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Listen back once and cut a 2–3 second region that contains a complete small gesture: a sentence fragment with a pitch rise, two chords of muzak, a slogan.
  5. Consolidate it (⌘ J) and name the clip ORPHAN.
  6. Write one logbook line: what the fragment is selling or saying.
  7. Phase 4 checks whether that meaning survived.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Oneohtrix Point Never — “Replica” (2011, Replica) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.

  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects a short loop cut from ordinary, meaningless source material that, through repetition, ends up feeling full of longing the original never had.

  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS and one consolidated 2–3 second fragment of genuinely banal audio sits on its own track, its original meaning noted on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

The audition — ten loop points on one fragment

Phase focus: the audition — ten loop points on one fragment. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this

  3. Audition bracket one: the “correct” loop — start at the gesture’s beginning, end at its natural rest.
  4. Hold a note and listen for 30 seconds.
  5. Log one word for what it feels like.

  8. At least ten distinct brackets, 30 seconds of held listening each, one logged word per bracket.
  9. You are listening for the bracket that reaches — the one where every repeat leans toward a resolution that never comes.
  10. It usually ends just before something: the chord change, the stressed syllable, the cadence.
  11. Keep the most unstable, most yearning bracket.
  12. Write down its exact start and length numbers — you will need them for the verify step.

Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The frame — one piano-ish layer, almost nothing

Phase focus: the frame — one piano-ish layer, almost nothing. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: a soft keys patch — Type → Wavetable or V.Analog, gentle attack, medium release, filter closed to mellow.
  2. It should suggest the memory of a piano, not an actual piano.
  3. Set the M4 input-1 gain on held notes, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, and record onto a track named FRAME.
  4. Find two or three notes that sit under the loop’s pitch center — play along with the loop until your hand finds them;
  5. the right ones make the loop’s lean ache more, not resolve.
  6. Record two minutes of the figure played sparsely — a note or two per bar at most, with bars of rest.
  7. If you play a melody, you have played too much; this layer frames, it does not speak.

Phase 4 · ≈ 25 min

Build two minutes, then move the cut and listen to the meaning move

Phase focus: build two minutes, then move the cut and listen to the meaning move. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Arrange two minutes: the loop alone for the first 20–30 seconds — let the listener learn its stumble — then the frame entering underneath.
  2. One pause where the loop stops for a bar and returns.
  3. Nothing else.
  4. Balance: loop in front, frame behind, mix peaking ≈ −12 dBFS.
  5. Play it end to end without touching anything.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Move your loop point 100 ms — did the meaning change?
    Answer

    It should have, and audibly. A loop that ended mid-reach now ends with a shrug, or a clipped word becomes a completed one and the feeling drains out. If both versions sound the same, your fragment carries its character in the middle rather than at its edges — usable, but it turns the loop point into a volume knob rather than an instrument. The record’s whole method depends on cuts landing where events live. Keep the version that reaches more, and write down why in one line.

  • Which loop point does the record choose — stable or stumbling?
    Answer

    Stumbling, nearly every time. Relisten to two or three loops on “Replica” and hum past each cut: the phrase almost always wants to go somewhere the loop refuses to take it. That refusal, repeated at regular intervals, is what turns advertisement audio into something that feels like loss. A stable cut would have nothing to long for. If the record’s loops sounded tidy to you, relisten following only the turning points. The stumble is in plain sight.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 007 — the lab teaches that the resample path is an authorship decision. This record shows the loop point is one too. Capture, path, and cut are three places where the meaning of a sample gets set, and none of them is the original source.
