Replicate: loops from the in-between

Copy one clear section of Jan Jelinek — Loop-Finding-Jazz-Records. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: sub-second slivers cut from the gaps inside vinyl jazz — room tone, breath, decay, the surface noise itself — layered as one-bar loops over a quiet kick. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: Live alone does the work this lesson — DT 990 Pro in the M4’s phones jack; the Digitakt II and the MicroFreak rest. Bring one jazz recording, any era — vinyl rips and older masters give the most surface noise to work with
  • New set saved as REP-JELINEK, tempo around 118 BPM — the pulse should feel like a walk, not a run
  • You will spend most of the hour zoomed in on quiet material with the temptation to turn up — set phones at the lab-001 mark before the harvest and leave them there; hiss at high volume is still loud
  • Timebox: 90 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into hunting slivers, not into devices
  • What you will make: a short replica of Jan Jelinek — Loop-Finding-Jazz-Records. Focus on this effect: soft, pulsing machine-funk that feels soaked in jazz while containing nothing a listener could quote.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: sub-second slivers cut from the gaps in vinyl jazz — room tone, breath, decay, the surface noise itself — layered as one-bar loops over a quiet kick.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or equipment limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Jan Jelinek — Loop-Finding-Jazz-Records. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Can you name a single tune being sampled?
    Answer

    No — not because the sources are obscure, but because nothing quotable was taken. There is no lick, no melody, no bars of anyone’s solo. The title tells you the method; the music still keeps the secret.

  2. What is actually looping?
    Answer

    The in-between: room tone, the breath before a phrase, a cymbal’s decay, the crackle of the vinyl itself. Slivers too short and too quiet to count as “music” in their original context, promoted to instruments.

  3. Where does the groove come from?
    Answer

    From the cut, not the player. Loop lengths and offsets rubbing against each other over a steady kick. No drummer is playing this groove — the slivers are placed and repeated until repetition becomes rhythm.

  4. Why does it still read as jazz?
    Answer

    The surface carries the genre even when the notes are gone: vinyl warmth, brushed metal decaying, wood resonance, a room with people in it. Genre lives in the grain as much as in the harmony.

  5. How loud is the kick?
    Answer

    Soft — felt under the loops rather than played on top of them. It organizes without announcing itself, which is why the record pulses rather than bangs.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same quality — unmistakably jazz-flavored, with nothing quotable in it, pulsing softly at matched loudness.
  • Every sliver comes from the gaps — silences, decays, breaths, surface noise. Under one second each.
  • Absolute rule: no recognizable lick. If a sliver can be traced to a specific phrase, it is a quote and it goes.
  • One soft kick under 4–6 one-bar loops; 2–3 minutes; the arrangement is entries and mutes.

The whole mechanism in one row: one jazz recording mined for its unwanted moments, four to six sub-second slivers looped at one bar each, and a quiet kick underneath holding the offsets together.

Where to cut, drawn as three snapshots. The phrase — the part anyone else would sample — is off-limits. The gap after it, the decay under it, the surface hiss around it: that is the harvest. Looped over a quiet kick, the sliver becomes rhythm.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the source

Phase focus: the reference and the source. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename REF (⌘ R), drag in a track from Loop-Finding-Jazz-Records, loop its most characteristic minute, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. ⌘ T, rename MINE, and drag in your jazz recording — a full track or album side.
  3. Older and dustier is better; the surface is material.
  4. Warp off on MINE for now — you are about to hunt in its real time, not its grid.
  5. Logbook, one line: what your jazz source’s most famous moment is.
  6. That moment is now banned.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Jan Jelinek — Loop-Finding-Jazz-Records (2001) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the source — as the smallest Live set that proves sub-second slivers cut from the gaps inside vinyl jazz — room tone, breath, decay, the surface noise itself — layered as one-bar loops over a quiet kick.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects soft, pulsing machine-funk that feels soaked in jazz while containing nothing a listener could quote.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the source into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates sub-second slivers cut from the gaps inside vinyl jazz — room tone, breath, decay, the surface noise itself — layered as one-bar loops over a quiet kick, not just the surface of Jan Jelinek — Loop-Finding-Jazz-Records (2001).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The reference loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, the jazz source sits whole and unwarped on its own track, and its best moment is named in the logbook as off-limits.

Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

The harvest — deliberately take the wrong moments

Phase focus: the harvest — deliberately take the wrong moments. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play through MINE with the waveform tall and your hand on stop.
  2. Hunt for: the silence between two phrases, a ride cymbal’s decay after the band drops out, an inhale before a solo, a chord’s release, thirty seconds of pure surface hiss from the run-in or fade.
  3. Cut 6–8 slivers, each under one second — ⌘ E to split, drag each to its own Session clip.
  4. Name them for what they are: GAP, RIDE-TAIL, BREATH, RELEASE, HISS…
  5. Rule at the cut: if you can name the phrase a sliver came from, it is a quote — discard it and cut earlier or later.
  6. Audition each sliver looped continuously (set the clip loop to its full length). Keep the ones that develop a personality on repetition; a sliver that stays boring after twenty repeats goes back.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

The loops — one bar each, layered into haze

Phase focus: the loops — one bar each, layered into haze. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Pick 4–6 survivors and give each its own track and a one-bar clip.
  2. Two treatments: texture — the sliver looped eight or sixteen times per bar until it reads as a surface; event — the sliver once or twice per bar, a soft accent coming around.
  3. Retune freely within ±3 semitones (clip Transpose) so the slivers agree on a loose tonal center. More than that and the vinyl grain starts to bend audibly.
  4. If the stacked hiss builds a harsh high end, add a gentle low-pass (EQ Eight, one band) per offending loop — darken it, do not clean it. The surface noise stays.
  5. Play all loops together and adjust clip gains until no single loop dominates — the target is a soft, even haze with accents circulating inside it, every layer at comparable loudness.

Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

The pulse — a kick that is felt, then the piece

Phase focus: the pulse — a kick that is felt, then the piece. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. New MIDI track, a soft kick from Live’s Core Library in a Drum Rack — round, no click.
  2. Four-on-the-floor at your project tempo, velocity modest.
  3. Balance it under the loops: pull the kick fader down until you stop hearing it, then raise it one or two dB — felt, barely heard.
  4. Check the master still peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  5. Arrange 2–3 minutes in Arrangement view using entries and mutes — haze first or kick first, either way. One loop dropping out mid-piece is an event worth a whole minute.
  6. Play it end to end at the mark, then A/B a minute against REF at matched loudness, judging softness of pulse and density of haze.
  7. Logbook, one line: which sliver turned out to be the groove — it is rarely the one you expected.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does yours pass the quote test?
    Answer

    Play it for a listener — imagined or real — and ask two questions: what genre is this made of, and hum me the part it borrows. The piece passes when the first answer is “jazz, obviously” and the second is silence. If anything is hummable back to a source phrase, a quote slipped through the harvest. Find it, cut from the gap nearby instead, and re-render. Listen to the record applying the same two questions. It passes both, on every track.

  • Find one sliver in the record you’d swear is just surface noise doing rhythm.
    Answer

    Listen carefully: somewhere in the loop stack is crackle or hiss placed and repeated so deliberately that it functions as a hi-hat or a shaker — the medium’s own surface noise, promoted to percussion. Finding it changes how you hear the whole album. The noise floor stops being below the music and becomes a layer of it. Write the timestamp. That one identification is this lesson’s listening exam.
