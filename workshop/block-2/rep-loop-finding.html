Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: loops from the in-between

Copy one clear section of Jan Jelinek — Loop-Finding-Jazz-Records. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: sub-second slivers cut from the in-between of vinyl jazz - room tone, breath, decay, the surface itself - layered as one-bar loops over a quiet pulse. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: Live alone does the work this lesson — DT 990 Pro in the M4’s phones jack; the Digitakt II and the MicroFreak rest. Bring one jazz recording, any era — vinyl rips and older masters give the best surface
  • New set saved as REP-JELINEK, tempo around 118 BPM — the pulse should walk, not run
  • You will spend most of the hour zoomed in on quiet material with the volume tempted upward — set phones at the lab-001 mark before the harvest and leave them there; hiss at high gain is still level
  • Timebox: 90 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into hunting slivers, not into devices
  • What you will make: a short replica of Jan Jelinek — Loop-Finding-Jazz-Records. Focus on this effect: soft, pulsing machine-funk that feels saturated with jazz while containing nothing a listener could quote.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: sub-second slivers cut from the in-between of vinyl jazz - room tone, breath, decay, the surface itself - layered as one-bar loops over a quiet pulse.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Jan Jelinek — Loop-Finding-Jazz-Records. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Can you name a single tune being sampled?
    Answer

    No - and not because the sources are obscure, but because nothing quotable was taken. There is no lick, no head, no eight bars of anyone’s solo; the title confesses the method and the music still keeps the secret.

  2. What is actually looping?
    Answer

    The in-between: room tone, the breath before a phrase, a cymbal’s decay, the crackle of the vinyl itself. Slivers too short and too quiet to be “music” in their original context, promoted to instruments.

  3. Where does the funk come from?
    Answer

    From the cut, not the player: loop lengths and offsets rubbing against each other over a steady pulse. No drummer is playing this groove - the slivers themselves are placed and repeated until repetition becomes rhythm.

  4. Why does it still read as jazz?
    Answer

    The surface carries the genre even when the notes are gone: vinyl warmth, brushed metal decaying, wood resonance, a room with people in it. Genre, it turns out, lives in the grain as much as in the harmony.

  5. How loud is the pulse?
    Answer

    Soft - a kick that is felt under the loops rather than played on top of them. It organizes without announcing, which is why the record pulses rather than bangs.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same paradox — unmistakably jazz-soaked, with nothing quotable in it, pulsing softly at matched loudness.
  • Every sliver comes from the in-between — gaps, decays, breaths, surface. Under one second each.
  • The rule, absolute: no recognizable lick. If a sliver can be traced to a phrase, it is a quote, and it goes.
  • One soft kick under 4–6 one-bar loops; 2–3 minutes; the arrangement is entries and mutes.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one jazz recording mined for its wrong moments, four to six sub-second slivers looped at one bar each, and a quiet pulse underneath holding the offsets together.

one jazz recording · any era → harvest the in-between · 6–8 slivers · < 1 s → 4–6 one-bar loops · layered → soft kick under · 2–3 min · ≈ −12 dBFS

Where to cut, drawn as three snapshots. The phrase — the part anyone would sample — is banned. The gap after it, the decay under it, the hiss around it: that is the harvest. Looped over a quiet kick, the sliver becomes rhythm.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the source

Phase focus: the reference and the source. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename REF (⌘ R), drag in a track from Loop-Finding-Jazz-Records, loop its most characteristic minute, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Live ⌘ T, rename MINE, and drag in your jazz recording — a full track or album side.
  3. Live Older and dustier is better; the surface is material.
  4. Live Warp off on MINE for now — you are about to hunt in its real time, not its grid.
  5. Logbook Logbook, one line: what your jazz source’s most famous moment is.
  6. Logbook That moment is now banned.
Expected result

The reference loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, the jazz source sits whole and unwarped on its own track, and its best moment is named in the logbook as off-limits.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Your jazz source is too clean to give surfaceA modern remaster — the hiss was scrubbed outFind an older transfer or vinyl rip; the dust is half the harvest
REF later out-hazes everything you buildUtility never trimmed — loudness reading as densityTrim to ≈ −12 dBFS before any comparison
Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

The harvest — deliberately take the wrong moments

Phase focus: the harvest — deliberately take the wrong moments. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play through MINE with the waveform tall and your hand on stop.
  2. Hunt for: the silence between two phrases, a ride cymbal’s decay after the band drops out, an inhale before a solo, a chord’s release, and thirty seconds of pure surface hiss from the run-in or fade.
  3. Live Cut 6–8 slivers, each under one second — ⌘ E to split, drag each to its own Session clip.
  4. Live Name them for what they are: GAP, RIDE-TAIL, BREATH, RELEASE, HISS…
  5. Live The rule, enforced at the cut: if you can name the phrase a sliver came from, it is a quote — discard it and cut earlier or later.
  6. Live Audition each sliver looped fast (set the clip loop to its full length).
  7. Live Keep the ones that develop a personality on repetition; a sliver that stays boring after twenty repeats goes back.
Expected result

Six to eight named sub-second slivers, none traceable to a phrase, several already turning into rhythm under repetition — a pantry stocked entirely from the in-between.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Every sliver still sounds like the band playingCuts too long — a second of jazz is a phraseHalve the cuts; at a few hundred milliseconds the music becomes material
The slivers are inaudibly quietThe in-between sits far below the music’s levelNormalize or clip-gain each sliver up to working level — this is the one place gain rescue is correct, and note it in the ledger
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

The loops — one bar each, layered into haze

Phase focus: the loops — one bar each, layered into haze. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Pick 4–6 survivors and give each its own track and a one-bar clip.
  2. Live Two treatments: texture — the sliver looped eight or sixteen times per bar until it reads as a surface;
  3. Live event — the sliver once or twice per bar, a soft accent coming around.
  4. Live Retune freely within ±3 semitones (clip Transpose) so the slivers agree on a loose tonal center;
  5. Live more than that and the vinyl grain starts to bend audibly.
  6. Live If the stacked hiss builds a harsh top, a gentle low-pass (EQ Eight, one band) per offending loop — darkening, not cleaning.
  7. Live The dust stays.
  8. Live Play all loops together and adjust clip gains until no loop wins — the target is a soft, even haze with accents circulating inside it, every layer at comparable loudness.
Expected result

Four to six one-bar loops breathing together — textures underneath, events circulating on top — already pulsing faintly before any kick exists, the offsets doing the drumming.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The stack is mud — no accents surviveEverything treated as texture, nothing as eventThin two loops to one or two repeats per bar so accents can circulate
One loop’s click at the cycle point dominatesSliver edges cut mid-waveformAdd a few-millisecond fade at the clip’s start and end — the standard de-click
Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

The pulse — a kick that is felt, then the piece

Phase focus: the pulse — a kick that is felt, then the piece. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live New MIDI track, a soft kick from Live’s Core Library in a Drum Rack — round, no click.
  2. Live Four-on-the-floor at your project tempo, velocity modest.
  3. Live Balance it under the loops: pull the kick down until you stop hearing it, then bring it up one or two dB — felt, barely heard.
  4. Live Check the master still peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  5. Live Arrange 2–3 minutes in Arrangement view from entries and mutes: haze first or kick first — steal the record’s patience either way;
  6. Live one loop dropping out mid-piece is an event worth a whole minute.
  7. Live Play it end to end at the mark, then A/B a minute against REF at matched loudness, judging softness of pulse and density of haze.
  8. Live + Logbook Logbook, one line: which sliver turned out to be the groove — it is rarely the one you expected.
Expected result

A 2–3 minute piece of soft machine-funk — jazz in every pore, quotable nowhere, with a kick you feel before you hear and one sliver unexpectedly running the show.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
It bangs instead of pulsingKick balanced like a techno kick — on top, not underneathDrop the kick until it disappears, then raise it two dB and stop
It reads as abstract noise, not as jazzThe harvest leaned all hiss, no instrument tailsSwap one texture loop for a ride decay or chord release — the genre lives in the decaying metal and wood
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does yours pass the quote test?
    Answer

    Play it for an imagined listener — or a real one — and ask two questions: what genre is this made of, and hum me the part it borrows. The piece passes when the first answer is “jazz, obviously” and the second is silence. If anything is hummable back to a source phrase, a quote slipped through the harvest; find it, cut a gap from near it instead, and re-render. Relisten to the record applying the same two questions; it passes both, every track.

  • Find one sliver in the record you’d swear is just surface noise doing rhythm.
    Answer

    Relisten with the question held: somewhere in the loop stack is crackle or hiss placed and repeated so deliberately that it functions as a hi-hat or a shaker — the medium itself, promoted to percussion. Finding it changes how you hear the whole album: the noise floor stops being below the music and becomes a layer of it. Write the timestamp; that one identification is this lesson’s listening exam.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
