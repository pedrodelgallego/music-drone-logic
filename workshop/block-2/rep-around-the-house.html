Replicate: the house as drum kit

Copy one clear section of Matthew Herbert — Around the House. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: percussion and texture sampled from household objects under self-imposed sampling rules - no drum machines, no other records. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone, or a mic into M4 input 1 with gain by the lab-001 ledger; the Digitakt II awake and connected for the sequencing half; DT 990 Pro; the lab-006 capture log
  • New set saved as REP-AROUND-THE-HOUSE, tempo 122 BPM — and the same tempo on the Digitakt II
  • Cupboard thumps and slammed drawers are real transients near your mic and your ears — gain set on the hardest hit, phones at the lab-001 mark, and the furniture treated kindly enough to survive the session
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — contract, kit session, chop-and-load, groove; when the timer ends, the groove ships as is
  • What you will make: a short replica of Matthew Herbert — Around the House. Focus on this effect: house music that hits hard while every drum sound is recognisably from your home.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: percussion and texture sampled from household objects under self-imposed sampling rules - no drum machines, no other records.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Matthew Herbert — Around the House. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is the kick?
    Answer

    Not a drum machine — a recorded thump from a real object: softer attack than a 909, a decay shaped by the object itself and a little of the room it was recorded in. It still holds the low end.

  2. Why does it still bang?
    Answer

    Because hitting hard is about sequencing, not the sound itself — tight trims, strict placement, careful levels, the off-beat in the right place. The groove structure is standard house music. Only the sound sources changed.

  3. Where do you hear the house — the building, not the genre?
    Answer

    In the tails and the imperfections: hits ring like real objects, no two strikes are identical, and small household resonances cling to the sounds where a drum machine would leave silence. The home is audible in those tiny details.

  4. What did the constraint buy?
    Answer

    A signature. Anyone can buy the same drum machine; nobody else can record your kitchen.

  5. Is the constraint itself audible?
    Answer

    Yes — as absence. No 909 hat, no borrowed break, no familiar preset anywhere. Once you notice what is missing, you are hearing the constraint directly. Rules show up as the holes where the defaults used to be.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a two-minute house groove at ≈ 122 BPM, sequenced on the Digitakt II, that bangs while every hit stays faintly domestic.
  • A written three-line sampling contract, signed and dated, honored absolutely — zero violations, however the groove suffers.
  • The full kit recorded today from your own home: kick, snare, hats, plus one texture.
  • Trim, tune, and level shape the hits; nothing synthesized, nothing borrowed, nothing from before today.

The record in one row: a written contract before any sound is made, a home recorded as a drum kit, a sampler running the sequence, and a groove whose character comes from following the contract to the end.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the contract

Phase focus: the reference and the contract. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag the grooviest track of Around the House in, loop a minute, Audio Effects → Utility, trim to ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Write your three-line contract.
  3. The shape: a source boundary, a prohibition, a freshness rule — e.g.
  4. 1 · kitchen only / 2 · no synthesized drums / 3 · every hit recorded today.
  5. Vary it to fit your home, not to dodge difficulty.
  6. Read each clause and ask: will this hurt by phase 4? At least one must — likely the boundary clause.
  7. Sign and date it in the logbook.
  8. From this line down, the contract outranks your taste.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Matthew Herbert — Around the House (1998) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the contract — as the smallest Live set that proves percussion and texture sampled from household objects under self-imposed sampling rules — no drum machines, no other records — discipline Herbert later formalized as his personal contract for composition.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects house music that hits hard while every drum sound is recognisably from a real home — a kit that no machine can copy because the source is one person’s own space.

  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, and a signed, dated, three-line contract sits in the logbook with at least one clause you already resent.

Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

Record the kit — the house played by hand

Phase focus: record the kit — the house played by hand. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Gain by the lab-001 ledger, set on your hardest hit, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — transients fool meters, so test with full force, not politeness.
  2. Kick candidates: cupboard thump, palm on a hollow door, a heavy book on a sofa.
  3. Record 8–10 strikes of each, varying force.
  4. Snare candidates: cutlery on a wooden board, a slammed drawer, a slapped cushion with a jar of rice on it.
  5. 8–10 each.
  6. Hat candidates: key-ring shakes, crumpled foil, scissor snips.
  7. 8–10 each, plus one longer shake for an open hat.
  8. One texture: the kettle rising, a fridge hum, a tap running — 20–30 seconds.
  9. Log everything (place · distance · level), and check each candidate against the contract before recording it.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Chop and load the Digitakt II

Phase focus: chop and load the Digitakt II. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. In Live, audition each candidate family and keep the best: one kick, one snare, one closed hat, one open hat, the texture.
  2. Trim each to its transient with a short natural tail.
  3. Export the keepers as one-shots and transfer them to the Digitakt II over USB (or sample the M4’s outputs into the Digitakt II’s inputs — either path honors the contract; the hits were recorded today either way).
  4. Assign one sample per track: T1 kick, T2 snare, T3 closed hat, T4 open hat, T5 texture.
  5. Per track: set start point exactly at the transient, shorten LEN until the hit is tight, tune the kick down a few semitones if it needs weight — and stop there.
  6. Filter sweeps and overdrive are disguises; the contract’s spirit forbids them even where its letter forgets.
  7. Press [TEMPO] and turn the tempo encoder until the Digitakt II reads 122 BPM to match the set.

Phase 4 · ≈ 30 min

Sequence the groove — and honor the contract when it hurts

Phase focus: sequence the groove — and honor the contract when it hurts. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Program a 16-step pattern: kick on every quarter, closed hat on the off-beats, snare on 2 and 4. Plain first;
  2. personality comes from the samples.
  3. Use velocity and a micro-timing nudge or two for feel; add the open hat sparingly and bring the texture in and out as a bed.
  4. Build two minutes of arrangement — pattern variations, drops, the texture carrying the empty bars.
  5. Record the Digitakt II into Live through the M4 for the printed version.
  6. When a hit feels weak, the contract allows exactly two fixes: re-trim, or re-record from the house.
  7. Note in the logbook every time you wanted a third option, and which clause blocked it.
  8. Zero violations.
  9. If a borrowed clap or a synthesized sub sneaks in “just to check,” delete it before A/B-ing — the comparison only counts on contract-legal material.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your printed groove at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does the groove bang while every hit stays faintly domestic?
    Answer

    Both at once is the pass condition, and each failure has its own fix. If it hits hard but sounds anonymous, you over-trimmed and over-tuned until the home character washed off — go back to phase 3 with lighter hands. If it sounds domestic but feels limp, the sequencing or balance is too cautious — go back to phase 4’s grid and levels. Relisten to REF and notice the tails — the domestic character lives in the last 50 milliseconds of every hit, which is exactly what aggressive trimming removes.

  • Which contract clause hurt most?
    Answer

    Check the phase-4 list — the clause you wanted to break most often is the answer, and that clause is where your sound becomes yours. Every moment of frustration was a moment you could not reach for a default, which means every moment the result was different from what anyone else would have made. Herbert’s whole approach is built on that trade: the contract limits you exactly where it makes you distinctive. Write one line naming your clause and what it forced into existence — then decide whether your next track keeps it. Keeping it is how a rule becomes a voice.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
