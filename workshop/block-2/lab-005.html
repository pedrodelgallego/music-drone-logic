Block 2 · Lab 005 · Track lab

Sustained Tone & Slow Modulation

Sustaining interest with zero rhythm: one tone, held, with modulation slower than attention. No drums, no events, no drops — six to eight minutes carried entirely by what lives inside the sound and how slowly it moves.

Track lab · S1 · D2 E2 M1 F2 · ambient drone · full cycle

listen → hypothesis → draw the path → calibrate → build → fault → diagnose → print → delta
Start here
  • Run the ten listening references below first; the hypothesis they produce is the start of the build.
  • Save a new set as LAB-005-drone.
  • Read both provided specs against your actual bench before any sound — this is an S1 lab: verify, don’t author.
  • Expected time: one full cycle — two to three weeks of part-time sessions.
The point

When nothing repeats and nothing pulses, the ear listens into the sound — a drone is not static, it is slow.

How this lab works

Design a tone worth living in

  • One MicroFreak voice with a rich inner life — the listener will spend eight minutes inside it.
  • Long attack, long release: the note has no edges.
  • Test: does the bare held tone reward a full minute of attention?

Make it move slower than attention

  • LFO cycles measured in minutes, not beats — change the listener feels but cannot catch.
  • Two held pitches a chosen interval apart, so their beating supplies the only pulse.
  • Test: A/B minute 1 against minute 5 — they must differ, audibly.

Hold the room

  • 6–8 minutes with no events — only weather.
  • The human role is patience: a hand moves only when the tone stops breathing.
  • Print the held takes in full; a drone is a performance, not a loop.
Pass standard
Minimum passAudible original piece, routing diagram, fault entry, print map, and system diff exist.
Solid passMinute 1 and minute 5 are audibly different states, and the listener cannot say when the change happened.
Advanced transferYou can rebuild the same slowness with a different engine or a different tuning interval and keep it alive.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
DroneA held tone long enough that pitch stops being the news. Once the note no longer changes, your ear gives up waiting for the next one and starts examining the one that is there.
Partials / overtonesThe quiet family of tones inside every tone — softer pitches stacked above the one you name. In ordinary music they pass unnoticed; in a drone they are what you actually listen to.
BeatingTwo near-identical pitches pulsing against each other — a slow wah-wah-wah made by the tones themselves. Rhythm without a drummer: the closer the pitches, the slower the pulse.
Slow modulationChange spread so thin in time that it is felt as breathing, not heard as an event. An LFO with a five-minute cycle still moves constantly — you just never catch it in the act.
Tuning intervalThe distance between two held pitches. In a piece with no melody and no chords, this one distance is the drone’s entire harmony — choose it like you would choose a key.

Listening — the hypothesis source

Ten drones, none of them static. Each one solves the same problem — sustaining interest with zero rhythm — with a different kind of slowness. Each gets a short brief: what to listen for before you press play, and how to check yourself after.

01 Éliane Radigue — “Kyema” modulation at the speed of weather

ARP 2500 tones changing so slowly that noticing the change is the piece.

Listen for
  • Try to catch a change in the act — pick one layer and watch it.
  • Compare the sound at minute one with the sound ten minutes in: how different are they?
  • Whether anything ever “arrives” — an entrance, an event, a downbeat.
After — verify

Did you ever catch a change as it happened? Almost certainly not — you only noticed afterwards that things were different. That delay is the technique: the modulation runs below the speed of attention. Relisten to any five-minute span and compare its first and last thirty seconds instead of hunting for events.

02 Pauline Oliveros — “Horse Sings from Cloud” sustain as practice

Held accordion and voice tones; the instruction behind the piece was not to change a tone until the change asks for itself.

Listen for
  • How long each tone holds before it moves — time it once.
  • The small instabilities just before a change: wavering, breath, a swelling edge.
  • The difference between a tone being held and a tone being sustained by a machine — the human effort is audible.
After — verify

Could you predict when a tone was about to change? The wavering that precedes each move is the cue — the change “asks for itself” audibly before it happens. If every change surprised you, relisten following one voice only and wait for its edges to soften.

03 Stars of the Lid — “Requiem for Dying Mothers” the swell as event

Orchestral drones where the only event is a slow rise and fall — the swell does the work that a drum fill does elsewhere.

Listen for
  • The shape of one swell, start to finish — how long is the rise, how long the decay?
  • How many layers are sounding at any moment (fewer than it feels).
  • What marks a “section” when there are no drums and no bar lines.
After — verify

What was the only kind of event you heard? A level rising and falling — the swell is the bar line, the phrase, and the climax all at once. If you heard “melodies,” relisten: most of them are one layer getting louder while another retreats.

04 Kali Malone — “No Sun to Burn” tuning as the motion

Organ intervals whose beating does the moving; nothing else needs to.

Listen for
  • The pulse inside the held chord — no one is playing it; the tuning is.
  • How the pulse speed changes when the interval changes.
  • Which intervals beat hard and which sit almost still.
After — verify

Where did the pulsing come from? Two pipes tuned close enough to interfere — the rhythm is a property of the interval, not a performance. If you heard it as tremolo or vibrato, relisten to one chord change and notice the pulse rate jump the instant the interval does.

05 Folke Rabe — “What??” the overtones surface

One tone held until its inner family becomes the melody.

Listen for
  • The high, faint tones above the fundamental — when do you first notice them?
  • Whether those high tones are new arrivals or were always there.
  • How your attention climbs the spectrum as the minutes pass.
After — verify

Did new sounds enter the piece? Mostly no — the overtones were present from the first second; the holding is what made them audible. If you are unsure, relisten to the opening minute knowing what arrives later, and you will already hear it waiting.

06 Charlemagne Palestine — “Strumming Music” sustain built from repetition

Rapid strumming fuses into a drone — sustain made of attacks.

Listen for
  • The moment the individual piano attacks blur into a continuous tone.
  • The cloud of harmonics that grows above the strummed notes — sound no single keystroke contains.
  • What changes over twenty minutes when the player’s hands never stop doing the same thing.
After — verify

Can you still hear single notes? At speed the attacks fuse, and the real music happens in the overtone cloud the piano builds in the room above them. If the piece read as “just repetition,” relisten with your attention an octave higher than the playing.

07 Tim Hecker — “Chimeras” drone under erosion

The held tone survives inside distortion and damage.

Listen for
  • The core pitch under the grit — find it early and hold on to it.
  • What the distortion contributes: texture and motion, while the tone supplies the stillness.
  • Whether the damage moves at the same speed as the tone, or faster.
After — verify

Did the distortion destroy the drone? No — the pitch holds through all of it; the erosion is a surface running at a different speed than the core. If you lost the pitch, relisten at low volume: the grit recedes and the held tone is plainly still there.

08 Phill Niblock — “Sethwork” thickness from detune

A massive sustained cluster built by stacking the same pitch many times a few cents apart — the beating between near-unisons is the sound; the motion comes from interference, not modulation.

Listen for
  • The churn inside what is nominally one note — pick a register and stay with it.
  • That no individual layer ever moves; every layer holds still.
  • How the cluster’s width reads as loudness — thickness doing the work of level.
After — verify

Is anything modulating? No — nothing sweeps, nothing drifts; the motion is interference between still layers, near-unisons beating against each other. If it felt like the sound was moving, relisten to one passage and try to catch a single layer in the act of changing — you will not, and that failure is the finding.

09 Sarah Davachi — All My Circles Run one instrument, emptied out

Each piece sustains a single instrument class — strings, voice, organ — until its mechanism, the bow noise, the breath, the reed beat, becomes the foreground.

Listen for
  • The mechanism sounds surfacing — breath under the voice, bow grain under the strings.
  • Entries and exits hidden in overlaps — voices arrive and leave under cover of each other.
  • How instrument identity blurs with duration — held long enough, a string section stops being one.
After — verify

Name the mechanism sound foregrounded in the piece you chose — breath, bow grain, a reed’s beat. And when did a “violin” stop sounding like a violin? Somewhere a few minutes in, the label fell away and only the sustained material remained. If you missed it, relisten for the first entry you can actually catch — most are hidden in the overlaps, and finding even one takes full attention.

10 Alvin Lucier — “Music on a Long Thin Wire” the system plays itself

A wire driven by an oscillator self-resonates — the composer sets the conditions and does not touch; the room and the physics make all the variation.

Listen for
  • Drift nobody performed — the pitch and timbre wandering with no hand on anything.
  • Timbre changes arriving without gestures — no attack, no fade, just a different state.
  • The difference between an event and a tendency — the wire has only tendencies.
After — verify

Who made the change you just heard? Nobody — the conditions did; the wire, the room, and the physics are the only performers. Relisten and try to attribute one single event to a hand — you cannot, and that failure is the lesson.

Requirement

Your written hypothesis names where this drone’s slowness will live — weather-speed modulation, the change that asks for itself, the swell, the beating interval, surfacing overtones, fused attacks, erosion over a still core, thickness from interference, the instrument’s mechanism surfacing, or conditions left to play themselves — and what was stolen from the comparison. The target idea across all ten: a drone is slow, not static — the variation lives inside the tone. What you steal is a structure, never a sound.

1 · The two specs — provided

System Spec — provided (S1: verify against your build)
InputsOne MicroFreak voice with long attack and long release — the tone has no edges · a second held pitch a chosen interval away, by paraphony or a second printed take
TransformsMicroFreak analog filter moved by one very slow LFO (cycle measured in minutes) · gentle space on the master — nothing that creates events
Feedback / variationBeating between the two pitches · LFO drift on cutoff or timbre · all variation lives inside the tone, never as a new element
ConstraintsNo rhythm, no events, no breakdown · ≤4 tracks · one tuning interval for the whole piece · 6–8 minutes
Human roleChoose the interval · design the tone · then mostly listen — a hand moves only when the tone stops breathing
Engineering Spec — provided (S1: verify against your build, correct after)
Signal pathMicroFreak → M4 in 1 → Live track 1 · second pitch by paraphony on the same path, or printed as a second take to Live track 2 · Live master → M4 → phones/monitors
Gain planPeaks −12 dBFS while tracking — a sustained tone has no transients to hide behind; the meter sits where it sits · master −3 dBFS for the render
Clock / syncNo grid to keep — tempo is irrelevant; Live’s transport exists only to record · LFO free-running, never synced
LatencyNothing is played in time — a large, safe buffer is fine; software monitoring acceptable throughout
Stereo / phaseMicroFreak is mono · width comes from panning the two takes apart · one mono check: the beating must survive the collapse
Noise budgetMicroFreak output hiss is fully exposed under a sustained tone — set preamp gain by the lab-001 ledger, then record and audit thirty seconds of “silence” before the first take
Print strategyPrint each held take in full as audio — a drone is a performance of patience, not a loop to repeat · render at the end, 24-bit WAV
Failure modeA drone that is actually static — LFO too slow or too small to matter, the interval beat-free; diagnosed by A/B-ing minute 1 against minute 5 at matched level
SafetyLong exposure at sustained level is this session’s main hazard — phones at the lab-001 mark, and take them off every fifteen minutes; loudness creep is invisible inside a drone

2 · Bench preflight

Pre-flight — before the first note
  • Gain ledger from lab 001 applied at M4 in 1 — set by the number, not by ear
  • Thirty seconds of noise floor recorded and listened back — know your hiss before the take
  • Monitoring mode declared per source, from lab 002’s table
  • Both spec tables read against the actual session; mark every row reality disagrees with
  • LFO rate written down in minutes per cycle — “slow” is not a measurement
  • Phones at the mark · a break planned every fifteen minutes

4 · Diagnosis & report

The full report bundle — ten items, filed in the logbook
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, master peaking −3 dBFS, 6–8 minutes
  • Routing diagram — drawn before patching, corrected after; the diff is the lesson
  • Recording plan — what was printed, when, at what stage
  • Patch notes — the settings that mattered, including the LFO rate in minutes and the tuning interval by name
  • Session notes
  • Reflection answers, including the listening comparison — which record contributed what to the hypothesis
  • One removal decision — what was cut from the finished piece and what cutting it did
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule
  • Print map — what now exists as audio, automation, patch state, or device memory; what is reproducible and what is gone
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the system worked like X; after building it, I discovered it worked like Y.”
  • Minimum pass gate: audible output · routing diagram · fault entry · print map · system diff. Without these five, the lab is incomplete regardless of audio quality — a musically strong piece cannot bypass the engineering spine.
Track-lab principle

A drone is slow, not static — when nothing repeats and nothing pulses, the variation must live inside the tone.
