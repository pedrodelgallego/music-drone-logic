Sustained Tone & Slow Modulation

How do you hold a listener's attention with no beat, no melody, and no events? This lab answers that question. You hold one tone for six to eight minutes, and all the interest comes from slow changes happening inside the sound itself.

The point

When nothing repeats and nothing pulses, your ear starts examining the sound itself. A drone — a tone held for a very long time — is not static. It is slow. The interest lives inside it.

How this lab works

Design a tone worth listening to

  • One MicroFreak voice with enough internal movement to stay interesting for eight minutes.
  • Long attack, long release: the note fades in and out slowly, with no sharp edges.
  • Test: does the bare held tone hold your attention for a full minute?

Make it change slowly

  • An LFO (a slow automatic knob-mover) with a cycle measured in minutes, not beats. The listener feels the change but cannot catch it happening.
  • Two held pitches a chosen distance apart. Their slight difference in tuning creates a slow pulsing effect called beating — the only rhythm in the piece.
  • Test: compare minute 1 against minute 5. They must sound audibly different.

Hold it for the full duration

  • 6–8 minutes with no events. The piece is the slow change, not a build-up to something.
  • Your job is patience. Only touch a knob if the tone has gone flat and lifeless.
  • Record each held take in full as audio. A drone is a performance — not a short loop you repeat.
Pass standard
Minimum passAudible original piece, routing diagram, fault entry, print map, and system diff exist.
Solid passMinute 1 and minute 5 are audibly different states, and the listener cannot say when the change happened.
Advanced transferYou can rebuild the same slowness with a different engine or a different tuning interval and keep it alive.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
DroneA tone held long enough that your ear stops waiting for the next note and starts listening to what is already there. Pitch is no longer the point — the inner life of the sound is.
Partials / overtonesEvery musical note contains quieter pitches stacked above it. In most music these go unnoticed. In a drone, held long enough, they become what you actually hear.
BeatingWhen two pitches are very close but not identical, they interfere with each other and produce a slow pulse — a gentle wah-wah-wah. No drummer needed: the rhythm comes from the tuning itself. The closer the pitches, the slower the pulse.
Slow modulationAutomatic change happening so gradually that you feel it rather than hear it as a moment. An LFO with a five-minute cycle is always moving — you just can never catch it in the act.
Tuning intervalThe gap between two held pitches. With no melody and no chords, this single gap is the entire harmony of the piece. Choose it carefully — it is as important as choosing a key.

Listening — the hypothesis source

Ten drones, none of them static. Each one holds attention with no rhythm, but uses a different type of slowness to do it. For each, you get a brief: what to focus on before you press play, and a check to run afterwards.

01 Éliane Radigue — “Kyema” modulation at the speed of weather

Tones made on an ARP 2500 synthesizer that change so slowly that noticing the change is the whole experience.

Listen for
  • Try to catch a change in the act — pick one layer and watch it.
  • Compare the sound at minute one with the sound ten minutes in: how different are they?
  • Whether anything ever “arrives” — an entrance, an event, a downbeat.
After — verify

Did you catch a change as it happened? Probably not — you noticed later that things were different. That delay is the technique. The change happens below the speed you can track. Try relisting to any five-minute stretch and compare its first thirty seconds against its last thirty seconds instead of hunting for moments.

02 Pauline Oliveros — “Horse Sings from Cloud” sustain as practice

Held accordion and voice tones. The performer's rule: don't change a tone until the change feels necessary on its own.

Listen for
  • How long each tone holds before it moves — time it once.
  • The small instabilities just before a change: wavering, breath, a swelling edge.
  • The difference between a tone being held and a tone being sustained by a machine — the human effort is audible.
After — verify

Could you predict when a tone was about to change? The slight wavering just before each move is the cue — the sound signals the change before it happens. If every change surprised you, relisten while following one voice only, and wait for its edges to loosen.

03 Stars of the Lid — “Requiem for Dying Mothers” the swell as event

Orchestral drones where the only event is a slow rise and fall in volume. That swell does the structural work that a drum fill or chord change would do in other music.

Listen for
  • The shape of one swell, start to finish — how long is the rise, how long the decay?
  • How many layers are sounding at any moment (fewer than it feels).
  • What marks a “section” when there are no drums and no bar lines.
After — verify

What was the only kind of event? A level rising and falling. The swell is the phrase, the climax, and the structure all in one. If you heard “melodies,” relisten: most of them are just one layer getting louder while another gets quieter.

04 Kali Malone — “No Sun to Burn” tuning as the motion

Organ chords whose beating — the pulsing caused by two slightly different pitches — supplies all the movement. Nothing else needs to.

Listen for
  • The pulse inside the held chord — no one is playing it; the tuning is.
  • How the pulse speed changes when the interval changes.
  • Which intervals beat hard and which sit almost still.
After — verify

Where did the pulsing come from? Two pipes tuned close enough to interfere with each other. The rhythm is built into the tuning — no one performed it. If you heard it as tremolo or vibrato, relisten to one chord change and notice the pulse rate jump the moment the interval changes.

05 Folke Rabe — “What??” the overtones surface

One tone held long enough that its overtones — the quieter pitches naturally present inside it — gradually become what you hear.

Listen for
  • The high, faint tones above the fundamental — when do you first notice them?
  • Whether those high tones are new arrivals or were always there.
  • How your attention climbs the spectrum as the minutes pass.
After — verify

Did new sounds enter the piece? Mostly no. The overtones were already there from the first second — the holding simply made them audible. If you are unsure, go back to the opening minute knowing what you heard later. You will hear those tones waiting there already.

06 Charlemagne Palestine — “Strumming Music” sustain built from repetition

Rapid piano strumming played so fast and continuously that the individual attacks fuse into a sustained tone. Sustain built entirely from attacks.

Listen for
  • The moment the individual piano attacks blur into a continuous tone.
  • The cloud of harmonics that grows above the strummed notes — sound no single keystroke contains.
  • What changes over twenty minutes when the player’s hands never stop doing the same thing.
After — verify

Can you still hear individual notes? At that speed the attacks blur together, and the real interest happens in the cloud of overtones building above them. If it sounded like “just repetition,” relisten while directing your attention to the higher frequencies above the playing.

07 Tim Hecker — “Chimeras” drone under erosion

A sustained tone treated with heavy distortion and digital damage. The core pitch stays intact throughout.

Listen for
  • The core pitch under the grit — find it early and hold on to it.
  • What the distortion contributes: texture and motion, while the tone supplies the stillness.
  • Whether the damage moves at the same speed as the tone, or faster.
After — verify

Did the distortion destroy the drone? No. The pitch holds all the way through. The grit moves at a different speed than the core. If you lost track of the pitch, relisten at lower volume — the distortion recedes and the held tone is clearly still there.

08 Phill Niblock — “Sethwork” thickness from detune

A dense sustained sound built by recording the same pitch dozens of times, each slightly out of tune with the others. The beating between those near-identical pitches is the entire sound. The movement comes from interference — not from any modulator.

Listen for
  • The churn inside what is nominally one note — pick a register and stay with it.
  • That no individual layer ever moves; every layer holds still.
  • How the cluster’s width reads as loudness — thickness doing the work of level.
After — verify

Is anything modulating? No. Nothing sweeps or drifts. The motion is purely interference between layers that are each completely still. If it felt like the sound was moving, relisten to one passage and try to catch a single layer in the act of changing. You will not catch one — and that is the finding.

09 Sarah Davachi — All My Circles Run one instrument, emptied out

Each piece sustains a single instrument type — strings, voice, or organ — for long enough that the mechanical sounds of playing it (bow noise, breath, reed vibration) move to the foreground.

Listen for
  • The mechanism sounds surfacing — breath under the voice, bow grain under the strings.
  • Entries and exits hidden in overlaps — voices arrive and leave under cover of each other.
  • How instrument identity blurs with duration — held long enough, a string section stops being one.
After — verify

Name the physical playing sound that came to the foreground — breath, bow noise, a reed’s vibration. And at what point did a “violin” stop sounding like a violin? Somewhere a few minutes in, the instrument identity fades and only the sustained sound remains. If you missed it, go back and listen for the first moment you can actually catch. Most entries are hidden under overlapping tones, and finding even one takes real attention.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

10 Alvin Lucier — “Music on a Long Thin Wire” the system plays itself

A long wire is driven by an oscillator and left to vibrate on its own. The composer sets the conditions and then steps away. The room, the temperature, and the physics of the wire produce all the variation.

Listen for
  • Drift nobody performed — the pitch and timbre wandering with no hand on anything.
  • Timbre changes arriving without gestures — no attack, no fade, just a different state.
  • The difference between an event and a tendency — the wire has only tendencies.
After — verify

Who made the change you just heard? Nobody. The wire, the room, and the physics did it. Relisten and try to point to one event caused by a human hand. You cannot — and that is the lesson.

Requirement

Your written hypothesis must name where this drone’s slowness will live — for example: weather-speed modulation, a change that asks for itself, a swell, a beating interval, surfacing overtones, fused attacks, erosion over a still core, thickness from interference, an instrument’s mechanism, or conditions left to run themselves. It also names what you borrowed from the listening. The core idea across all ten references: a drone is slow, not static — the variation lives inside the tone. What you borrow is a structure, never a specific sound.

1 · The two specs — provided

System Spec — provided (S1: verify against your build)
InputsOne MicroFreak voice with long attack and long release so the tone has no sharp edges · a second held pitch at a chosen interval, either by paraphony or a second printed take
TransformsMicroFreak analog filter moved by one very slow LFO (cycle measured in minutes) · light reverb on the master — nothing that creates rhythmic events
Feedback / variationBeating between the two pitches · LFO drift on cutoff or timbre · all variation stays inside the tone, never introduced as a new element
ConstraintsNo rhythm, no events, no breakdown · ≤4 tracks · one tuning interval for the whole piece · 6–8 minutes
Human roleChoose the interval · design the tone · then mostly listen — only touch a knob when the tone goes flat and lifeless
Engineering Spec — provided (S1: verify against your build, correct after)
Signal pathMicroFreak → M4 in 1 → Live track 1 · second pitch by paraphony on the same path, or printed as a second take to Live track 2 · Live master → M4 → phones/monitors
Gain planPeaks −12 dBFS while recording. A sustained tone has no short bursts to hide behind — the meter stays where it is. Master at −3 dBFS for the render.
Clock / syncNo grid needed. Tempo is irrelevant; Live’s transport is only there to record. LFO runs free, never synced to the clock.
LatencyNothing is played in time, so a larger buffer is fine. Software monitoring is acceptable throughout.
Stereo / phaseMicroFreak is mono. Width comes from panning the two takes apart. One mono check: the beating must survive when left and right are collapsed to mono.
Noise budgetMicroFreak hiss is fully exposed under a sustained tone — nothing masks it. Set preamp gain from the lab-001 ledger, then record and listen back to thirty seconds of silence before the first take.
Print strategyRecord each held take in full as audio. A drone is a performance — not a short loop you repeat. Render at the end, 24-bit WAV.
Failure modeA drone that is actually static — LFO too slow or too small to hear, the interval producing no beating. Diagnose by comparing minute 1 against minute 5 at matched volume.
SafetyLong exposure at sustained volume is this session’s main hazard. Keep phones at the lab-001 mark and take them off every fifteen minutes. Volume creep is hard to notice inside a drone.

2 · Bench preflight

Pre-flight — before the first note
  • Gain ledger from lab 001 applied at M4 in 1 — set it by the number, not by ear
  • Thirty seconds of silence recorded and listened back — know what your background hiss sounds like before you record any tone
  • Monitoring mode declared per source, from lab 002’s table
  • Both spec tables checked against your actual session; mark any row where your setup differs
  • LFO rate written down in minutes per cycle — “slow” is not a measurement
  • Phones at the mark · a break planned every fifteen minutes

4 · Diagnosis & report

The full report bundle — ten items, filed in the logbook
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, master peaking −3 dBFS, 6–8 minutes
  • Routing diagram — drawn before patching, corrected after; the diff is the lesson
  • Recording plan — what was printed, when, at what stage
  • Patch notes — the settings that mattered, including the LFO rate in minutes and the tuning interval by name
  • Session notes
  • Reflection answers, including the listening comparison — which record contributed what to the hypothesis
  • One removal decision — what was cut from the finished piece and what cutting it did
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule
  • Print map — what now exists as audio, automation, patch state, or device memory; what is reproducible and what is gone
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the system worked like X; after building it, I discovered it worked like Y.”
  • Minimum pass gate: audible output · routing diagram · fault entry · print map · system diff. Without all five, the lab is not complete — even a great-sounding piece does not pass without the engineering record.
Track-lab principle

A drone is slow, not static. When nothing repeats and nothing pulses, all the variation must live inside the tone.
