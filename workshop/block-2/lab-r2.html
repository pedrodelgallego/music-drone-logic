Rebuild the Gesture

Take one concrète passage you analyzed in L2 and re-make it using your own recordings pushed through the processing chain you wrote down. Your material will be different — your train, your beach — so the target is the gesture: the recording perspective, the edit rhythm, the processing chain. Where the gesture matches, your analysis was right. Where it doesn't, you have found something you couldn't hear yet.

The point

You cannot re-record Schaeffer's train — and that is exactly why this lab works. Because the material is different, everything left to compare is the approach: where the mic stood, how the edits flow, what the chain did. The gesture is your analysis made audible.

What “close” means here

In R1 you matched sounds, notes, and levels. Here the material is different, so the comparison moves up a level. You are matching three things: capture perspective (the same distance and relationship between foreground and background), edit rhythm (cuts of the same length and density, moving at the same pace), and processing chain (the same operations, in the same order). A stranger hearing both passages should hear the same approach at work, applied to different worlds.

How this lab works

Capture your equivalents

  • One own capture per row of the worksheet’s captured column
  • Match the perspective: distance, environment, noise floor
  • The capture session is part of the lab, not preparation for it

Push them through the chain

  • Every processed-column guess becomes a chain you wire
  • In the worksheet’s order — order is part of the claim
  • A row too vague to build is itself a finding

Name every miss

  • A/B the gesture, not the material
  • File each miss under the layer it lives in
  • Correct the worksheet — that is what leaves the room

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
The gestureEverything about a passage that isn't the material itself: where the mic stood, how long the cuts are, what the processing chain did and in what order. Two recordings of entirely different sounds can carry the same gesture. That sameness is what you are rebuilding.
Capture perspectiveThe relationship a recording fixes between the subject and its surroundings: how close, how much room sound, how loud the background noise. Matching perspective means your recording sits in its world the way theirs sits in its world.
Edit rhythmThe timing of the cuts: how long each fragment lasts, how abrupt or smooth each join is, how dense the edits become and when. Listen to the passage the way you would listen to a drummer — the edits are the beat.
Gear miss vs hearing missA gear miss means "my equipment can't capture that" — note it for later. A hearing miss means "I didn't even notice that quality until I tried to build it" — that is the real finding: the exact edge of your current ear.

1 · Target & analysis

The target is the concrète passage you took apart in L2's Samples session. Your captured-vs-processed worksheet is the blueprint: each element, your guess at its recording (source, distance, environment), and your guess at its processing chain in order. If a row is too vague to record or build from, sharpen it before you start. Discovering that a row was unbuildable is itself a finding — that is the lab working.

  • The firewall. Open a new project and name it R2-study. This replica is study evidence, not portfolio output. It is never published, never "finished," and nothing from this session — no recording, no clip, no resample — moves into portfolio work. The corrected worksheet is what leaves this room; the audio does not.
  • Worksheet only. No tutorials, no breakdown videos, no interviews about how the work was made. Someone else's analysis would only test someone else's hearing. If you get stuck, go back to the record with the six questions — not to a search bar.
  • Your captures, their chain. You may not use any audio from the reference recording. Every sound in the rebuild is one you recorded yourself, using the lab 008 kit or whatever recording setup you have.
Before you start
  • The Samples-session worksheet from L2 on the desk, captured and processed columns filled
  • The reference passage available as a local audio file — a stream cannot be dragged onto a track
  • A capture session planned: where you will record your equivalents, with what, at what gain — the lab 008 field checklist applies
  • A new Live set saved as R2-study — the firewall starts at the project name
  • Phones at the lab-001 mark
  • Timebox: two sessions, three at most — capture counts as a session; write the end date down
Before building

Pre-commit the predictions

In the logbook, before you record any sound: which parts of the gesture do you expect to match — the perspective, the edit rhythm, or the chain? Which do you expect to miss, and why? Writing the prediction down first is the only honest way to find out what you actually believed. The misses you failed to predict are the most valuable ones.

2 · Rebuild & verdict

Setup

The A/B rig

Same setup as R1: the reference on a track named REF, Warp off, gain-matched with a Utility; your rebuild tracks grouped as REBUILD. One solo press switches between them — always one at a time, never layered. The loop covers the passage the worksheet describes. The full step-by-step setup for this block — including how to A/B a passage with no grid to loop against — is forthcoming; R1's setup procedure covers you until then.

Expected result

You can solo REF, then solo REBUILD, at the same perceived loudness, over the same passage, hands-free.

REF (Utility, gain-matched) → solo → master → phones REBUILD group (your captures + chain) → solo → master → phones

Only one version plays at a time. Playing them together hides perspective and timing differences.

One worksheet row = one capture = one track = one guess the build will prove or break.

Worksheet rowYour captureLive trackFirst move
Foreground element — captured columnYour equivalent, recorded at the same distance and environmentRB-FOREMatch the distance and surroundings first. Re-record rather than trying to "fix" perspective with reverb later.
World / background elementYour room, your street, your background noiseRB-WORLDSet its level against the foreground based on what the worksheet claims — not by taste.
Processed element — processed columnAny recording above, run through the processing chainRB-PROCAdd processing devices in the worksheet's order. The order is part of the guess being tested.
  1. Timebox first. Two sessions maximum; three at the absolute most, and the recording trip counts as one. The replica is finished when the timebox ends, not when it is perfect. The end date is already in the logbook. When it arrives, render and stop — even mid-miss.
  2. Capture your equivalents. One recording per captured-column row. Match the worksheet's claimed perspective: distance, environment, handling. Write down your gain decisions — this is lab 006 and 008 technique under test.
  3. Build the chain. Each processed-column guess becomes a device in Live, added in the worksheet's order. Cut your material to match the reference's edit rhythm — fragment lengths and join styles, transcribed by ear the way R1 transcribed notes.
  4. A/B at matched loudness. Thirty seconds REF solo, thirty seconds REBUILD solo, back and forth — never both at once. Focus on one dimension per pass: perspective first, then edit rhythm, then the chain's effect, then levels.
  5. Write the miss list. Note every audible difference in the gesture and assign each to the layer it belongs to — use the table below. The layer matters more than the individual miss. It tells you which worksheet question you answered wrong.
  6. Correct the worksheet. Go back to the rows the rebuild proved wrong. Strike the old claim and write the corrected one beside it, including any quality that turned out to belong in the other column. The corrected analysis is the deliverable that outlives the rebuild.
  7. Close and quarantine. Render the passage into the logbook as study evidence, close the project, and don't reopen it for portfolio work. Carry the miss list forward — it is your sharpened hypothesis heading into capstone C2.

Detailed step-by-step recipes — capture-session procedure, edit-rhythm transcription method, chain-matching moves — are forthcoming. The rules above are final; only their expanded detail is pending.

The difference is…LayerExample
A different kind of object entirelyMaterialTheir foreground is metallic and resonant; mine is soft and damped — wrong choice of subject, not wrong recording technique
Same kind of object, different place or sizeSignalTheirs sits deep in a hall; mine is close and dry — the perspective guess was wrong
Same sound, moves differentlyControlTheir texture opens slowly under a filter; mine is static — a processed-column row was missing
Same sound, lands differently in timeTimeTheir cuts move in long uneven phrases; mine chop on a regular grid — the edit rhythm was transcribed wrong
Decision check — gear miss or hearing miss?

For each item on the miss list: did the gesture fail because your equipment can't capture it — no long-reach mic, no quiet enough preamp — or because you didn't notice that quality until you tried to rebuild it? A gear miss is a note for the gear list. A hearing miss is the valuable finding — it is the precise edge of your current ear, located.

Pass gate — R2 complete when
  • The replica render — the rebuilt passage, filed in the logbook as study
  • Your own captures, filed with their gain and perspective notes
  • The Samples-session worksheet it was rebuilt from, with corrections marked — including column moves
  • A/B notes at matched loudness
  • The miss list — each miss assigned to its layer, gear or hearing named
  • One sentence: what can you hear now that you couldn’t before?

No fault ritual, no full bundle — this is a half-cycle. If your rebuild sounds nothing like the reference material, the lab can still pass. The gesture, not the material, is what is being judged.

Replication principle

The material was never yours to copy. The gesture always was. Rebuild the approach, not the world it was applied to.
