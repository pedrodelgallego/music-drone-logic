Block 2 · Replication R2 · Study

Rebuild the Gesture

Take one concrète passage you analyzed in L2 and re-make it with your own captures pushed through the chain you analyzed. Your material is necessarily different — your train, your beach — so closeness here means the gesture: the capture perspective, the edit rhythm, the processing chain. Where the gesture lands, your analysis was right; where it doesn’t, you have found what you can’t hear yet.

Replication lab · S2 · E2 M3 F1 · half-cycle — requires the L2 Samples session · filed as study, never portfolio · scaffold

listen → hypothesis → draw the path → calibrate → build → fault → diagnose → print → delta
Start here
  • Put the L2 Samples-session worksheet beside the computer; no worksheet, no R2.
  • Save a new set as R2-study.
  • Capture your own material per the worksheet’s captured column, then process it per the processed column.
  • Stop at the timebox. The corrected worksheet is the deliverable; the replica is evidence.
The point

You cannot re-record Schaeffer’s train — and that is exactly why this lab works. With the material removed from the comparison, everything left to match is the system: where the mic stood, how the edits breathe, what the chain did. The gesture is the analysis, made audible.

What “close” means here

In R1 you matched sounds, notes, and levels. Here the material is necessarily different, so closeness moves up a level: the capture perspective (same distance, same relationship between foreground and world), the edit rhythm (cuts of the same length and density, breathing at the same rate), and the processing chain (the same operations, in the worksheet’s order). A stranger hearing both passages should recognize the same hand at work, on different worlds.

How this lab works

Capture your equivalents

  • One own capture per row of the worksheet’s captured column
  • Match the perspective: distance, environment, noise floor
  • The capture session is part of the lab, not preparation for it

Push them through the chain

  • Every processed-column guess becomes a chain you wire
  • In the worksheet’s order — order is part of the claim
  • A row too vague to build is itself a finding

Name every miss

  • A/B the gesture, not the material
  • File each miss under the layer it lives in
  • Correct the worksheet — that is what leaves the room

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
The gestureEverything about a passage that isn’t the material itself: where the mic stood, how long the cuts are, what the chain did and in what order. Two recordings of different worlds can carry the same gesture — that sameness is what you are rebuilding.
Capture perspectiveThe relationship the recording fixes between the subject and its world: how close, how much room, how loud the noise floor. Matching perspective means your capture sits in its world the way theirs sits in its world.
Edit rhythmThe timing of the cuts: how long each fragment lasts, how abrupt or smoothed each join is, how dense the edits get and when. Listen to the passage as a drummer would — the edits are the beat.
Gear miss vs hearing missA gear miss is “my equipment can’t make that sound” — a note for the shopping list. A hearing miss is “I didn’t even notice that until I tried to build it” — the real finding: the exact edge of your current ear.

1 · Target & analysis

The target is the concrète passage you took apart in L2’s Samples session. Your captured-vs-processed worksheet is the blueprint: each element, your guess at its capture (source, distance, environment), your guess at its chain, in order. If a row is too vague to record or build from, sharpen it before you start. Finding out a row was unbuildable already counts — that is the lab working.

  • The firewall. Open a new project and name it R2-study. The replica is study evidence, never portfolio output: it is not published, never “finished,” and nothing from this session — no capture, no clip, no resample — moves into portfolio work. The corrected worksheet is what leaves this room; the audio does not.
  • Worksheet only. No tutorials, no breakdown videos, no interviews about how the work was made. Someone else’s analysis would test someone else’s hearing. If you get stuck, go back to the record with the six questions — not to a search bar.
  • Your captures, their chain. The material restriction runs the other way here: you may not lift audio from the reference at all. Every sound in the rebuild is one you recorded yourself, on the lab 008 kit or whatever capture path you own.
Before you start
  • The Samples-session worksheet from L2 on the desk, captured and processed columns filled

  • A capture session planned: where you will record your equivalents, with what, at what gain — the lab 008 field checklist applies

Before building

Pre-commit the predictions

In the logbook, before you record any sound: which parts of the gesture do you expect to nail — the perspective, the edit rhythm, or the chain — which do you expect to miss, and why? Writing the prediction down first is the only honest way to find out what you actually believed. The misses you failed to predict are worth double.

2 · Rebuild & verdict

Setup

The A/B rig

Same rig as R1: the reference on a track named REF, Warp off, gain-matched with a Utility; your rebuild tracks grouped as REBUILD; one solo press flips between them, serial, never layered. The comparison loop covers the passage the worksheet describes. The full step-by-step setup recipe for this block — including how to A/B a passage with no grid to loop against — is forthcoming; R1’s setup procedure carries you until then.

Expected result

You can solo REF, then solo REBUILD, at the same perceived loudness, over the same passage, hands-free.

REF (Utility, gain-matched) → solo → master → phones REBUILD group (your captures + chain) → solo → master → phones

Only one is ever audible at a time — the comparison is serial, never layered, because layering hides perspective and timing misses.

One worksheet row = one capture = one track = one guess the build will prove or break.

Worksheet rowYour captureLive trackFirst move
Foreground element — captured columnYour equivalent, recorded at the same perspectiveRB-FOREMatch the distance and environment before matching anything else — re-record rather than “fix” perspective with reverb.
World / background elementYour room, your street, your noise floorRB-WORLDSet its level against the foreground by the worksheet’s claim, not by taste.
Processed element — processed columnAny capture above, re-entered into the chainRB-PROCWire the chain in the worksheet’s order; the order is part of the guess.
  1. Timebox first. Two sessions; three at the absolute most, and the capture trip counts as one. The replica is finished when the timebox ends, not when it is perfect. The end date is already in the logbook: when it arrives, render and stop, mid-miss if necessary.
  2. Capture your equivalents. One recording per captured-column row, matching the worksheet’s claimed perspective: distance, environment, handling. Gain decisions are yours and get written down — this is lab 006 and 008 technique under test.
  3. Build the chain. Processed-column guesses become devices, in the worksheet’s order. Cut your material to the reference’s edit rhythm — fragment lengths and join styles transcribed by ear, the way R1 transcribed notes.
  4. A/B at matched loudness. Thirty seconds REF solo, thirty seconds REBUILD solo, back and forth — never both at once. Listen for one dimension per pass: perspective only, then edit rhythm, then the chain’s fingerprint, then levels.
  5. Write the miss list. Every audible difference in the gesture, each assigned to the layer where it lives — use the table below. The layer matters more than the miss: it tells you which worksheet question you answered wrong.
  6. Correct the worksheet. Re-answer the rows the rebuild proved wrong — strike the old claim, write the corrected one beside it, including every quality that turned out to live in the other column. The corrected analysis is the deliverable that outlives the replica.
  7. Close and quarantine. Render the passage into the logbook as study evidence, close the project, and don’t reopen it for portfolio work. Carry the miss list forward: it is C2’s sharpened hypothesis about what capture decisions actually sound like.

The detailed per-step recipes — capture-session procedure, edit-rhythm transcription method, chain-matching moves — are forthcoming with Block 2 authoring. The rules above are final; only their expansion is pending.

A different kind of object entirelyMaterialTheir foreground is metallic and resonant; my equivalent is soft and damped — wrong choice of subject, not wrong recording

Decision check — gear miss or hearing miss?

For each item on the miss list: did the gesture fail because the rig can’t capture that way — no long-reach mic, no quiet enough preamp — or because you didn’t notice that quality until you tried to rebuild it? A gear miss is a note for the gear roadmap. A hearing miss is the gold — it is the precise edge of your current ear, located.

Pass gate — R2 complete when
  • The replica render — the rebuilt passage, filed in the logbook as study
  • Your own captures, filed with their gain and perspective notes
  • The Samples-session worksheet it was rebuilt from, with corrections marked — including column moves
  • A/B notes at matched loudness
  • The miss list — each miss assigned to its layer, gear or hearing named
  • One sentence: what can you hear now that you couldn’t before?

No fault ritual, no full bundle — this is a half-cycle. If the replica’s world sounds nothing like the record’s, the lab can still pass: the gesture, not the material, is what is being judged.

Replication principle

The material was never yours to copy; the gesture always was. Rebuild the hand, not the world it touched.
