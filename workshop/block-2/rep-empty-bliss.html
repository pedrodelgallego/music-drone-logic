Replicate: wear as the subject

Copy one clear section of The Caretaker — An Empty Bliss Beyond This World. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record’s core mechanism: loops cut from old 78 rpm ballroom records with the surface damage left in — crackle kept, dropouts kept, the medium’s wear made into the subject of the music rather than something to remove. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: the full analog loop from Lab 007’s path C — Digitakt II outs → M4 in 3/4 → Live → M4 out 3/4 → Digitakt II inputs — patched and verified, MicroFreak on M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack
  • New set saved as REP-EMPTY-BLISS, tempo around 80 BPM — slow ballroom time
  • The analog loop can feed back on itself with one wrong monitor switch. Keep Digitakt II input monitoring off while the return is patched. Phones at the lab-001 mark, one hand near the input level on every first pass.
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop. Each generation takes real time to print, and that waiting is part of the work.
  • What you will make: a short replica of The Caretaker — An Empty Bliss Beyond This World. Focus on this effect: a sweet old phrase heard through its own decay - the wear louder than some of the music.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: loops cut from 78 rpm ballroom records with the wear left audible - surface noise kept, dropouts kept, the medium’s failure promoted to subject.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of The Caretaker — An Empty Bliss Beyond This World. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Is the crackle a flaw or an instrument?
    Answer

    An instrument. Remove it and the piece loses its meaning, not just its texture.

  2. What does the degradation hide, and what can it not hide?
    Answer

    It hides the band. The room sound, the players, the arrangement details — all of that disappears behind the wear. But the melody stays findable. The damage takes the detail and leaves the feeling.

  3. Where do the loops end?
    Answer

    Mid-phrase, and at the same spot every time — like a memory that cuts off at the same place no matter how often you reach for it. The repetition of the cutoff is what makes it feel like forgetting rather than editing.

  4. How loud is the surface relative to the music?
    Answer

    Sometimes louder — and that is a deliberate mixing choice, not an accident. A restorer would have pushed the noise down. This record turns it up until the crackle and the music swap places as foreground and background.

  5. What is the album about — the band, or the wear?
    Answer

    The wear. The ballroom bands are the material, not the subject. The album is about what repetition, copying, and time do to something remembered. The music about forgetting is performed by the act of forgetting.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same quality — a sweet phrase audibly aged, crackle in the foreground, one dropout that stings — at matched loudness.
  • One musical phrase only, played as sentimentally as you can. The sweetness is the raw material. Restrained, tasteful material will not feel like a loss when the wear takes hold.
  • The aging must be real: actual passes through the actual analog loop at low level, generation by generation. No lo-fi plugin is allowed anywhere in this lesson.
  • One dropout, placed deliberately, landing at the same spot every loop pass — a recurring loss, not a random glitch.

The piece in one row: record a clean sentimental phrase, send it through the M4 analog loop at low level several times (shaving the top frequencies each pass), add a crackle bed, then loop four bars with one deliberate dropout.

MicroFreak · soft chords · too sweet → analog loop × 3–5 generations · low level → top filtered · crackle added → 4-bar loop · one dropout · ≈ −12 dBFS

What the generations do, drawn as three snapshots of the same phrase. The melody line survives all three panels. Above it, detail disappears. Below it, the noise floor rises. Between the second and third panel, the phrase shifts from something played to something half-remembered.

Phase 1 · ≈ 20 min

The reference and the too-sweet phrase

Phase focus: the reference and the too-sweet phrase. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

Age it — generations through the analog loop, low and honest

Phase focus: age it — generations through the analog loop, low and honest. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Route GEN-0 out the established loop: Live → M4 out 3/4 → Digitakt II inputs, or simply M4 out 3/4 cabled back to M4 in 3/4 with Live recording the return — the converters and cable are the path either way.
  2. Digitakt II input monitoring stays off.
  3. Set the send level low: the return should peak only ≈ −24 dBFS.
  4. Record the pass to a new track named GEN-1, then bring its clip gain back up to match GEN-0 by ear. The noise floor rises with it — that is the whole point, not a mistake.
  5. Put EQ Eight on the return path with a low-pass at 8 kHz for the first pass.
  6. Repeat the whole move for GEN-2 through GEN-4 or GEN-5, lowering the filter each pass — 8 k, 6 k, 4.5 k, 3.5 k — always sending the previous generation, never GEN-0.
  7. Logbook After printing each generation, listen to it alone for thirty seconds. Write one line: “still playing” or “starting to remember” — your words for when the phrase crosses from performance to recollection.
  8. Logbook Note the generation number where that shift happens.
  9. Logbook Stop one generation after the shift.
  10. Live Too few passes and it is just a dull synth. Too many and the melody disappears entirely. The record lives exactly one step past the point where it stopped sounding like it was played.

Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The surface and the wound — crackle, swell, one dropout

Phase focus: the surface and the wound — crackle, swell, one dropout. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename SURFACE — a vinyl-crackle sample looped end to end, or your own: the M4 trim raised on a silent input, printed.
  2. Mix it boldly, just under the worn phrase — this record lets the surface speak;
  3. ≈ 6 dB below the music, not 20.
  4. Take your chosen generation print and loop its four bars on a track named MEMORY.
  5. Add a slow 1–2 dB volume swell across each loop pass with clip envelopes. Old recordings breathe slightly. Perfectly flat sustain sounds digital by comparison.
  6. Cut the dropout: find the moment where the phrase leans hardest toward its resolution and delete 200–400 ms — a clean hole, no fade-in or fade-out around it.
  7. The crackle bed keeps running through the hole. The music disappears; the medium does not.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Which generation number started “remembering” instead of “playing”?
    Answer

    For most rigs it is around generation three or four — the pass where the accumulated noise floor becomes a constant presence and the filtered high end stops sounding like EQ and starts sounding like distance. The exact number matters less than the fact that you caught the shift and logged it: you heard generational character arrive, which is Lab 007’s measurement done with a sentimental phrase instead of a test render. If you never heard a shift, your passes were recorded at too healthy a level — the path was preserving when it should have been aging.

  • Does your dropout land as loss, not glitch?
    Answer

    The test is in your own body. By the third pass you should brace slightly before the hole. By the tenth you should feel the missing note as something taken rather than something broken. A glitch surprises; a loss is anticipated. What converts one into the other is the identical spot, every pass, without fail — and keeping the crackle running through the hole tells the ear that the medium survived even where the music did not. Relisten to the record’s mid-phrase cutoffs: same spot, every pass, surface unbroken. If yours still reads as error, it is landing on a note nobody cared about — move it onto the note you liked most.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
