Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: wear as the subject

Copy one clear section of The Caretaker — An Empty Bliss Beyond This World. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: loops cut from 78 rpm ballroom records with the wear left audible - surface noise kept, dropouts kept, the medium’s failure promoted to subject. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: the full analog loop from Lab 007’s path C — Digitakt II outs → M4 in 3/4 → Live → M4 out 3/4 → Digitakt II inputs — patched and verified, MicroFreak on M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack
  • New set saved as REP-EMPTY-BLISS, tempo around 80 BPM — old ballroom time, slow enough to sway
  • The analog loop is one monitor switch from feedback: Digitakt II input monitoring off while the return is patched, phones at the lab-001 mark, one hand near the input level on every first pass
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop — the generations take real time to print, and that waiting is part of the material
  • What you will make: a short replica of The Caretaker — An Empty Bliss Beyond This World. Focus on this effect: a sweet old phrase heard through its own decay - the wear louder than some of the music.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: loops cut from 78 rpm ballroom records with the wear left audible - surface noise kept, dropouts kept, the medium’s failure promoted to subject.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of The Caretaker — An Empty Bliss Beyond This World. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Is the crackle a flaw or an instrument?
    Answer

    An instrument - remove it and the piece loses its meaning, not just its texture.

  2. What does the degradation hide, and what can it not hide?
    Answer

    It hides the band and cannot hide the melody. The room, the players, the arrangement details - all of that recedes behind the wear; but the tune stays reachable, the way a forgotten afternoon still has a feeling attached.

  3. Where do the loops end?
    Answer

    Mid-phrase, and at the same spot every time - like a memory that cuts off at the same place no matter how often you reach for it. The repetition of the failure is what makes it read as forgetting rather than editing.

  4. How loud is the surface relative to the music?
    Answer

    Sometimes louder - and that is a mixing decision, not an accident of transfer. A restorer would have pushed the noise down; this producer turned it up until the medium and the music trade places as foreground.

  5. What is the album about — the band, or the wear?
    Answer

    The wear. The ballroom bands are the occasion, not the subject: what the album examines is what repetition, copying, and time do to a remembered thing. It is music about memory failing, performed by the failure itself.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same worn tenderness — a sweet phrase audibly aged, wear in the foreground, one dropout that hurts — at matched loudness.
  • One musical phrase only, played deliberately schmaltzy — the sweetness is fuel; dignified material will not miss its youth.
  • The aging must be honest: real passes through the real analog loop at low level, generation by generation — no lo-fi plugin allowed anywhere in this lesson.
  • One dropout, placed deliberately, at the same spot every loop pass — a wound, not a glitch.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a soft sentimental phrase printed clean, sent through the M4’s analog loop at low level for several generations with the top shaved each pass, a crackle bed over it, and a four-bar loop with one deliberate dropout.

MicroFreak · soft chords · too sweet → analog loop × 3–5 generations · low level → top filtered · crackle added → 4-bar loop · one dropout · ≈ −12 dBFS

What the generations do, drawn as three snapshots of the same phrase. The melody line persists in all three panels; the detail above it shrinks, and the floor underneath it rises. Somewhere between the second and third panel, the phrase stops being played and starts being remembered.

Phase 1 · ≈ 20 min

The reference and the too-sweet phrase

Phase focus: the reference and the too-sweet phrase. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename REF, drag a track from An Empty Bliss Beyond This World in, loop one minute, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility so it peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. MicroFreak MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, Paraphonic on, soft attack, warm filter setting — a parlor organ, not a synth.
  3. MicroFreak M4 input-1 gain set on held chords, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. MicroFreak Play a four-bar phrase at ≈ 80 BPM: simple major-key chords with one sentimental move — a ii–V leaning home, a melody note held over the change.
  5. MicroFreak Schmaltz is the brief.
  6. Live Print three takes onto a track named GEN-0 and keep the sweetest.
  7. Live Consolidate the keeper (⌘ J).
  8. Live This clean print is generation zero — the photograph before the years get at it.
  9. Live Keep it safe; you will need it for the verify A/B.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS and one clean, deliberately sentimental four-bar phrase sits consolidated as GEN-0 — pretty, a little embarrassing, and full of things to lose.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The phrase sounds cool, modern, restrainedTaste got in the way — you wrote for this decadeAdd the cliché you just avoided: the held melody note, the obvious resolution. The wear needs sugar to work on
Chords clip or pump on the way inGain set on single notes, then paraphony stacked voices on topReset the M4 trim on the densest held chord, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS
Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

Age it — generations through the analog loop, low and honest

Phase focus: age it — generations through the analog loop, low and honest. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Digitakt II + Live Route GEN-0 out the established loop: Live → M4 out 3/4 → Digitakt II inputs, or simply M4 out 3/4 cabled back to M4 in 3/4 with Live recording the return — the converters and cable are the path either way.
  2. Digitakt II + Live Digitakt II input monitoring stays off.
  3. Live Set the send level low: the return should peak only ≈ −24 dBFS.
  4. Live Record the pass to a new track named GEN-1, then bring its clip gain back up to match GEN-0 by ear.
  5. Live The floor rides up with it — that is the point, not a mistake.
  6. Live Put EQ Eight on the return path with a low-pass at 8 kHz for the first pass.
  7. Live Repeat the whole move for GEN-2 through GEN-4 or GEN-5, lowering the filter each pass — 8 k, 6 k, 4.5 k, 3.5 k — always sending the previous generation, never GEN-0.
  8. Logbook After printing each generation, listen to it alone for thirty seconds and write one logbook line: “still playing” or “starting to remember” — your words for when the phrase stops being a performance and becomes a recollection.
  9. Logbook Note the generation number where the flip happens.
  10. Logbook Stop one generation after the flip.
  11. Live Too few passes and it is just a dull synth; too many and the melody drowns — the album lives exactly one step past remembering.
Expected result

A stack of generation prints, GEN-1 through GEN-4/5, each duller and hissier than the last, a logbook line per generation, and a circled number: the generation where playing became remembering.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A howl or doubling builds the moment you arm the passInput monitoring open somewhere — the loop is hearing itself liveKill the return’s monitor, re-check the Lab 007 safety row, and re-arm with one hand on the input level
Generation 4 sounds barely different from generation 1Passes printed at healthy level — unity gain is preservation, and preservation is the wrong job todayDrop the send so the return peaks ≈ −24 dBFS and lift the clip gain after; the floor only accumulates if you make it work for its dinner
Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The surface and the wound — crackle, swell, one dropout

Phase focus: the surface and the wound — crackle, swell, one dropout. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename SURFACE — a vinyl-crackle sample looped end to end, or your own: the M4 trim raised on a silent input, printed.
  2. Live Mix it boldly, just under the worn phrase — this record lets the surface speak;
  3. Live ≈ 6 dB below the music, not 20.
  4. Live Take your chosen generation print and loop its four bars on a track named MEMORY.
  5. Live Ride 1–2 dB of slow volume swell across each pass with clip envelopes — old discs breathe;
  6. Live flat sustain sounds digital.
  7. Live Cut the dropout: pick the spot where the phrase leans hardest toward resolution and delete 200–400 ms — a clean hole, no fade tricks.
  8. Live The crackle bed keeps running through the hole; the music vanishes, the medium does not.
  9. Live Listen to four full passes.
  10. Live The dropout should land at the identical place each time, and by the third pass you should catch yourself bracing for it.
Expected result

A four-bar worn loop under an audible surface, breathing slightly, with one recurring hole where the sweetest moment used to be — and your own ear already flinching before it arrives.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The dropout reads as a playback error, not a lossIt cuts the crackle too, or lands on unimportant materialKeep the surface running through the hole, and move the hole onto the phrase’s most-wanted note — losses only hurt where something was loved
The crackle sits on top like a stickerBed added at full bandwidth over a dull phrase — two different eras collidingLow-pass the crackle to roughly match the phrase’s ceiling so surface and music share one old medium
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Sit with it, then A/B against the clean ghost

Phase focus: sit with it, then A/B against the clean ghost. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Arrange 2–3 minutes: the loop runs; the only events allowed are the surface swelling slightly, one pass where the music drops 2 dB further behind the crackle, and the ending — the loop simply stopping mid-phrase, surface continuing two bars alone.
  2. Play it end to end at the mark, no touching.
  3. Notice the moment the dropout stops surprising you and starts being mourned — log roughly when.
  4. Live A/B against GEN-0 at matched loudness: thirty seconds of the clean phrase, then thirty of the finished loop.
  5. Live Log one line on what the generations removed and one on what they could not remove.
  6. MicroFreak + Live Solo-flip against REF and judge two things only: is your wear as foregrounded as the record’s, and does your dropout carry the same kind of loss as its mid-phrase cutoffs?
Expected result

A 2–3 minute piece in which the medium audibly outlives the music, plus two logbook lines: what aging took, and what it failed to take.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The piece reads as a lo-fi effect demo, not a feelingThe phrase underneath was never sweet enough to mournGo back to phase 1 and play it more sentimental — the lesson’s emotional arithmetic is sweetness minus fidelity
Three minutes feels like too longJudging at reading speed, not listening speed — or the loop truly lacks a woundSit through it once more without multitasking; if it still sags, deepen the dropout or push the music another 2 dB behind the surface
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Which generation number started “remembering” instead of “playing”?
    Answer

    For most rigs it lands around generation three or four — the pass where the accumulated floor becomes a constant presence and the filtered top stops sounding like EQ and starts sounding like distance. The exact number matters less than the fact that you caught the flip and logged it: that is you hearing generational character arrive, which is Lab 007’s entire measurement done with a sentimental phrase instead of a test render. If you never heard a flip, your passes were printed too healthy — the path was preserving when it should have been aging.

  • Does your dropout land as loss, not glitch?
    Answer

    The test is in your own body: by the third pass you should brace slightly before the hole, and by the tenth you should feel the phrase’s missing note as something taken rather than something broken. Glitches surprise; losses are anticipated — recurrence at the identical spot is what converts one into the other, and keeping the crackle running through the hole is what tells the ear the medium survived even where the music did not. Relisten to the record’s mid-phrase cutoffs: same spot, every pass, surface unbroken. If yours still reads as error, it is landing on material nobody would miss — move it onto the note you loved most.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
