Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the field as found score

Steal one structural idea from Christina Kubisch's Electrical Walks: reveal a layer of sound that was always present but inaudible, then let a map of where that layer lives decide your arrangement. Build a short take and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

You are not copying Kubisch's specific sounds or her city. You are copying the technique: reveal a layer that was inaudible, draw a map of where its events are strongest, and let the route through that map decide the form — not your taste. The piece is discovered, not composed. Everything else is decoration.

The system you are replicating
Source recordChristina Kubisch — Electrical Walks (ongoing since 2003)
The effectA hidden electromagnetic world — ATMs, lights, security gates, transit lines — suddenly becomes a vivid, composed-sounding piece, even though nobody wrote a note
The mechanismInduction-coil listening makes an inaudible field audible; the map of where the field's events occur becomes the found score, and the route through it is the form

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • One piece of your own raw material that hides a quiet layer — a noisy field recording, a buzzy synth take, a Digitakt II sample with audible noise floor, or a MicroFreak patch with raw oscillator/noise content
  • MicroFreak ready on Live's input — its plain oscillators and noise are your stand-in for electromagnetic buzz if you want to generate the hidden layer rather than excavate it
  • Pen and paper, or a clip overview in Live, for the map — it is drawn from what you uncover, before any arranging happens
  • Optional: a real telephone/induction pickup coil into Live's input will literally capture the EMI from a phone charger, laptop, or screen — use it if you have one, but the build works without it
  • What you will make: a 60–90 second study built entirely from a layer you exposed that was inaudible in your source, arranged by walking a map of that layer. Focus on this effect: a hidden world is revealed and its own geography becomes the music.
  • What you will turn off: your composing instinct, your taste, and the urge to tidy the discovered layer into something pretty. You curate the route; you do not write the notes.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: an inaudible layer made audible, and a map of it used as the score.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to a few minutes of Electrical Walks documentation. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. None of these sounds were "made" by a musician — so where does the music come from?
    Answer

    From revealing, not making. The electromagnetic emissions of lights, ATMs, and transit systems were always there; the induction coil just converts them into audible signal. The composer's act is choosing the coil and the route, not authoring the sound. The "music" is a layer of the ordinary world that was always below the threshold of hearing.

  2. Why does it feel composed — phrased, sectional, full of arrivals — when nobody wrote it?
    Answer

    Because the city's electromagnetic geography is already sectional: a quiet stretch of pavement, a loud bank of fluorescent tubes, the rhythmic pulse of a ticket gate. As you move, you cut from one source to the next. That sequence reads as phrasing. The route through the field is the arrangement — the city does the composing and the walker does the editing.

  3. What is the map for — why guide the walk instead of wandering?
    Answer

    The map turns wandering into a score. It marks where the strong sources are, so the walk becomes a repeatable, intentional path through known events rather than chance. That's the key transfer for you: a map of where the hidden events live, used as the thing that decides order and duration. Authorship shifts from "what notes" to "which path."

  4. What would be lost if Kubisch cleaned up the hum into smooth, pleasant tones?
    Answer

    The evidence that it's real. The grit, the ugly buzz, the lurching levels prove this is a real field and not a designed synth patch. If you smooth the discovered layer into something tasteful, you've stopped discovering and started composing. Present the layer as found — keep the warts.

Now build the mechanism you just described. Do not imitate her sounds — reveal your own hidden layer and let its map score it.

  • Sound target: a take made only of a layer that was inaudible in your source, presented as found, with its order and timing taken from a map of where its events occur.
  • The hidden layer: the noise floor, buried hum, sub rumble, digital artefacts, or raw EMI-style buzz you exposed — never the obvious sound that was already in front.
  • The map: a drawn or listed chart of where in the material the strong events sit, made before you arrange anything.
  • The form: the route you walk through that map — nothing else decides the order.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Make the inaudible layer audible

Phase focus: excavate a layer that is present but below the threshold of hearing, so there is something to map. Compare against REF before moving on.

source material (or MicroFreak / pickup coil) → extreme boost + EQ (expose the buried layer) → the revealed field, as found
Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track and gain-match it with a Utility so A/Bs are honest.
  2. Choose your source: load a sample that you suspect hides a quiet layer — one with audible noise floor, hum, or sub. Or use a noisy field recording on a Live track.
  3. Or generate the hidden layer: set a raw oscillator or the noise engine very low, the kind of buzz you would normally filter out. The optional pickup coil into Live's input does the same job by capturing real device EMI.
  4. Reveal it: drop a Utility with heavy gain and an EQ Eight, then carve away the loud, obvious content and boost the quiet residue — the hiss, the 50/60 Hz hum, the digital fizz, the sub rumble — until the buried layer is the loudest thing in the room.
  5. Audition the revealed layer alone. It should sound like a world you did not know was there, not like your original sample with treble on it.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Christina Kubisch — Electrical Walks (ongoing since 2003) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — Make the inaudible layer audible — as the smallest Live set that proves Induction-coil listening makes an inaudible field audible; the map of where the field's events occur becomes the found score, and the route through it is the form.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects A hidden electromagnetic world — ATMs, lights, security gates, transit lines — suddenly becomes a vivid, composed-sounding piece, even though nobody wrote a note.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate Make the inaudible layer audible into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates Induction-coil listening makes an inaudible field audible; the map of where the field's events occur becomes the found score, and the route through it is the form, not just the surface of Christina Kubisch — Electrical Walks (ongoing since 2003).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.

A track that plays only the previously inaudible layer of your material — grit, hum, and all — clearly a hidden domain dragged into earshot.

Phase 2 — ~30 minutes

Draw the map of where the events are

Phase focus: chart the geography of the revealed layer — where it surges, where it goes quiet, where it pulses — so you have a found score to walk.

Do this
  1. Scan the revealed layer end to end, listening only for where its character changes — a surge of hum here, a silence there, a rhythmic flutter of digital artefact, a low drone.
  2. Mark every distinct emitter as a point on a map: name it (e.g. buzz-zone, dead-air, tick-pulse, sub-bloom) and note where it sits in the source and how strong it is.
  3. Treat it as a real map, not a timeline: sketch the strong zones as places you could visit in any order, the way Kubisch's city map marks emitters, not a fixed sequence.
  4. Mark the single strongest emitter and the quietest dead zone — the extremes of your field. These anchor the walk.

Phase 3 — ~25 minutes

Walk the map and let the route be the form

Phase focus: choose a path through the map and record the walk. The route is the whole arrangement — your authorship is in the curation of the path, not the sound.

Do this
  1. Plot one route across the map: a list of emitters in the order you will visit them and how long you linger at each, e.g. dead-air → buzz-zone (long) → tick-pulse → sub-bloom → dead-air. This list is your found score.
  2. Walk it in one pass: move through your revealed layer in the route's order — by jumping the playhead, muting and unmuting the mapped zones, or re-triggering the regions — holding each emitter as long as the route says.
  3. If the layer lives in samples, place each emitter as a slice and trigger them in route order so the walk plays as a sequence of arrivals at known places.
  4. Record the walk with no metre and no tidying — let the durations and the grit fall as the field gives them. You decided the path; you do not get to fix the sounds along it.
  5. Listen back: a stranger should feel they are being led somewhere through a real place, not hearing a tune you wrote.

Verify against the record

A/B your take against a comparable stretch of Electrical Walks — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Does yours sound discovered rather than composed — like a hidden layer revealed, not a patch you designed?

Answer

If it sounds like a deliberate musical idea, you smoothed the field into taste and lost the technique. The proof is the grit: a found layer keeps the lurches, the ugly buzz, the uneven levels that mark it as real. If yours is too clean and pleasant, go back and present the revealed layer as found — Kubisch's work gets its authority from not tidying it.

Did the map decide the form — could you point to the route as the reason the events fall in this order?

Answer

It should. That's the whole claim of this technique: a map of where the hidden events live can be the score. If you can't trace your arrangement back to a step on the map — if the order came from "what sounded good" — you replaced the found score with composition. Check that every arrival in the take matches a step on your route. In Kubisch, the geography is always the reason.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: "Matched: …" · "Missed: … (best guess why)" · "Change next: …"
  • Name the one emitter on your map that did the most work — the place on the route that sold the whole walk.
  • Replica audio stays in the logbook as study evidence; it is not a release, and never reuses Kubisch's own recordings.
