Replicate: the invented sound-tool as voice

Copy the working method behind Joaquín Orellana's Humanofonía: build a sound-making tool that nobody else has, then feed it your own voice or vernacular material treated with musique concrète technique (cutting, looping, and re-speeding recorded sound as raw material). The tool's distinctiveness becomes the music. Make a short take and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

You are not copying Orellana's marimba-derived útiles sonoros (his handmade sound-tools), his Guatemalan crowd voices, or his 1971 tape sound. You are copying the mechanism: invent a custom instrument or processing chain you play as one thing with its own gestures and limits, then run found or voice material through it and chop, splice, loop, and re-speed it concrète-style until its origin is transformed but still faintly recognizable. The tool and the treated source are the whole piece.

Before you start
  • One source recording of your own that carries a place or a tradition — a voice, a crowd, a folk fragment, a vernacular instrument, a market, a chant; a few seconds is enough, loaded on a Digitakt II track
  • MicroFreak ready on Live's input, with the goal of building one distinctive patch rather than using presets
  • A way to capture and re-import audio (Digitakt II sampling or a phone) — concrète technique means recording sound as raw material and feeding it back in, changed
  • What you will make: a 60–90 second study driven by one invented sound-tool you built, playing back your own vernacular or voice material that you have cut, looped, and re-pitched concrète-style. Focus on this effect: an instrument that exists nowhere else, voicing a source you can almost but not quite name.
  • What you will turn off: presets, standard instrument sounds, a steady beat, and any second tool. One invented tool, one source family.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a custom tool's distinctiveness plus concrète-treated vernacular material carry the whole piece.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or equipment limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about two minutes of Humanofonía. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Can you name the instrument making any given sound — and what does it tell you that you cannot?
    Answer

    Mostly you cannot, and that is the signature of an invented tool. Orellana's útiles sonoros are instruments he built himself — many descended from the marimba, Guatemala's national instrument — so they have a wooden, struck, sliding character you half-recognize but cannot match to any catalogue instrument. The unfamiliarity is not a flaw; it is the composer's own voice, built into a tool nobody else owns.

  2. Where does the human voice appear, and is it left whole or taken apart?
    Answer

    It appears as crowd and individual voice material, and it is taken apart. Orellana works concrète-style: the voice is treated as raw material — spliced, layered, looped, sped up and slowed — so you catch a syllable or a crowd surge and then lose it. The source stays faintly recognizable as "human," but it is detached from any specific speaker or sentence. That detachment of a sound from its source is the core of the acousmatic approach: listening to the sound itself, not to what made it.

  3. What does the marimba lineage give the piece even when no marimba is literally playing?
    Answer

    It gives a vernacular fingerprint — a wooden, resonant, pitched-percussion grammar of attacks and decays that reads as Guatemalan even when abstracted. Orellana did not quote the marimba; he let its physical behavior shape the tools he built and the gestures he played. That is the lesson: a tradition can enter the work through how an invented instrument is built and handled, not through a recognizable tune.

  4. How much steady pulse or hummable melody is there to hold onto?
    Answer

    Very little. The form is carried by timbre, gesture, and montage — the character of the invented tool and the way treated voices are cut against it. With pulse and tune absent, the distinctiveness of the instrument and the transformation of the source become the only events. They have to carry everything. That is exactly what makes the mechanism reproducible without copying a single sound.

Now build the tool and treatment you just described. Do not build from memory of the sounds — build from the method.

  • Sound target: one invented tool with two or three idiosyncratic gestures of its own, voicing your concrète-treated source.
  • The tool: a hand-built MicroFreak patch with deliberately unusual controls, or a custom Digitakt II sample-plus-effects chain / Live rack you play as one single instrument with fixed gestures and real limits.
  • The source: your own vernacular, voice, or crowd recording, cut and looped and re-pitched so its origin is transformed but still faintly recognizable.
  • The form: the back-and-forth between the tool's character and the treated source — montage and gesture — and nothing else.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~30 minutes

Build the sound-tool

Phase focus: construct one custom instrument whose constraints — not your taste in the moment — will shape everything later. Compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track and gain-match it with a Utility so A/Bs are honest.
  2. Option A — build a patch as a tool: pick one oscillator engine and build a single voice around two or three unusual, deliberately chosen moves — e.g. one knob that slides pitch like a struck wooden bar, one that opens a resonant ring. Decide its limits and do not exceed them later.
  3. Option B — build a chain as a tool: take one short sample and wrap it in a fixed effects chain (sample start / pitch / filter / send) so the whole thing behaves as one playable instrument with a handful of gestures. The chain, not any preset, is the tool.
  4. Name the tool and write its rules: its three gestures and what it cannot do. An instrument is defined as much by its limits as its range — the constraint is what will make the music distinctively yours.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Joaquín Orellana — Humanofonía (1971) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.

Phase 2 — ~35 minutes

Treat the vernacular source, concrète-style

Phase focus: turn your recorded vernacular or voice material into raw sound — pulled away from its origin but still faintly recognizable.

recorded vernacular / voice source → chop · splice · loop · re-speed · re-pitch · montage → abstracted source, origin faintly legible
Do this
  1. Treat the source as material, not a sample to play in tune: chop it into fragments, loop a single grain, and pitch or speed it up and down so the same syllable or crowd surge appears at several sizes.
  2. Montage the fragments on a track — splice pieces against each other out of their original order so a sentence becomes a texture and a crowd becomes a pulseless mass.
  3. Tune the recognizability: push the treatment until a stranger could not name the words or the place, then ease back one notch so the human or vernacular origin is still faintly audible. That just-recognizable edge is the sweet spot.
  4. Mark the one fragment that best holds the tension between "I know what that was" and "but not quite." That is your strongest treated material.

Phase 3 — ~25 minutes

Let the tool voice the source — and record it

Phase focus: play your invented tool against the treated material in one pass. The back-and-forth between them is the whole piece.

Do this
  1. Write the gesture score first: a short ordered list of what the tool does and which treated fragment answers it, e.g. tool slides up → crowd grain loops under → tool rings → voice splice answers → silence. That list is your score.
  2. Play the tool within its written limits from phase 1 against the fragments from phase 2 — call and response, the invented instrument leading, the vernacular material answering.
  3. Record the back-and-forth in one pass. No metronome, no added instruments — let durations breathe and let the montage, not a beat, carry time.
  4. Listen back with your eyes closed: can a stranger hear one unusual instrument in conversation with a half-familiar human source, without being told what either is?

Verify against the record

A/B your take against a comparable stretch of Humanofonía — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Could a listener name the instrument in yours — and if so, has the invented-tool mechanism really happened?

Answer

If they can name it from a catalogue, the tool is not truly invented — you played a known instrument and the mechanism did not happen. Orellana's signature is that you hear an instrument that exists nowhere else. If yours is instantly identifiable, go back to phase 1, strip the preset character, and build limits that force an unfamiliar voice. The proof is that your tool is unrecognizable as anything off the shelf yet consistent enough to read as one instrument.

Is your source pulled away from its origin yet still faintly recognizable — and does the form survive with no beat and no tune?

Answer

Both must hold. If the source plays as a clear quote, you skipped the concrète treatment. If it is unrecognizable mush, you went too far and lost the human trace that gives the abstraction its tension. And if your take only works because you added a pulse or a melody, you replaced the mechanism instead of testing it — strip the addition and check whether the tool-and-treated-source back-and-forth still carries the piece. In Orellana it does.

  • Name the one gesture of your invented tool that did the most work — the move that made the instrument feel like nobody else's.
