Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: erosion over a still core

Copy one clear section of Tim Hecker — “Chimeras”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: an unchanging tonal bed pushed through degradation whose amount is the moving part - the writing happens in how much erosion reaches the sound, not in the sound. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring; the Digitakt II rests. The erosion happens inside Live: Saturator, Redux, and a reverb on one return — no plugin shopping
  • New set saved as REP-CHIMERAS — tempo irrelevant; the automation is drawn in minutes, not bars
  • Distortion adds level and high-frequency energy without warning — set the return up at low send first, phones at the lab-001 mark, a real break every 15 minutes
  • What you will make: a short replica of Tim Hecker — “Chimeras”. Focus on this effect: a stable harmonic center heard through shifting distortion and grain - the damage moves like weather while the chord underneath holds still.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: an unchanging tonal bed pushed through degradation whose amount is the moving part - the writing happens in how much erosion reaches the sound, not in the sound.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Tim Hecker — “Chimeras”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Find the core pitch early. Does it ever move?
    Answer

    Hold on to it and it holds on to you - the harmonic center stays put while everything around it churns. What feels like harmonic movement is mostly the surface revealing and concealing parts of the same chord.

  2. What is the distortion actually contributing?
    Answer

    Texture and motion - all of it. The tone supplies the stillness; the damage supplies every event. Strip the grit away in your imagination and what remains is a chord with almost nothing to say.

  3. Do the damage and the tone move at the same speed?
    Answer

    No - and the gap between their speeds is the style. The grain shifts in seconds, like gusts; the core moves in minutes or not at all, like ground. Two clocks in one sound.

  4. Is the distortion on the chord, or around it?
    Answer

    It reads as weathering - damage done to the sound itself, not an effect beside it.

  5. At low volume, what survives?
    Answer

    The chord. Turn the record down and the grit recedes into a halo while the held center stands plainly there - proof that the core was intact all along, under, not in, the damage.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same two-speed split — a chord you could hum unchanged from end to end, under a surface that never sits still.
  • The dry chord never changes — no automation on its track, no clip envelopes, no edits. It is the ground.
  • Only three things may move: the send amount, the Saturator drive, and the Redux depth — all of them slowly, over minutes.
  • The erosion lives on a return, never as an insert — the piece is the amount of damage, and amounts need a send to be ridden.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one printed chord that never changes, a return chain that saturates, crushes, and diffuses whatever is fed to it, and four minutes of automation deciding — slowly — how much of the chord gets fed.

printed chord bed · never edited → Send A · the only fader that writes → Return A · Saturator → Redux → Reverb → mix · core + weather · ≈ −12 dBFS

The whole arrangement on one timeline. The core is a flat band from first second to last; above it the erosion swells, recedes, and swells again — and the dotted line underneath is the only thing you actually draw: the automation deciding how much weather reaches the chord.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Chimeras” into it.
  2. Live Loop the stretch you found most weathered in the reference pass — two minutes is enough.
  3. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Live REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. Live ⌘ T once; rename: BED, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
  6. Logbook Write in the logbook, one line: which parts of the loop you believe are the chord and which are the damage.
  7. Logbook Phase 4 re-asks this about your own piece.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one named, routed, armed track waits for the chord, and your surface-versus-core guess is on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The bed — one chord, printed, then frozen

Phase focus: the bed — one chord, printed, then frozen. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, Paraphony on, and hold a simple chord — a root, a fifth, and an octave wears well under damage;
  2. MicroFreak thirds get muddier.
  3. MicroFreak Pick one and stay.
  4. MicroFreak Slow attack, full sustain, zero LFO, zero filter movement — the bed must contain no events of its own.
  5. MicroFreak Some harmonic content is good: erosion needs edges to chew on.
  6. MicroFreak Set the M4 input-1 gain on the held chord, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, checked after a full minute of holding.
  7. MicroFreak Print 4–5 minutes into BED using the MicroFreak’s Hold.
  8. MicroFreak One take, no punch-ins.
  9. Live Disarm the track and declare the clip frozen: no clip envelopes, no fades, no edits, no automation on this track — for the rest of the lesson, the only thing leaving BED that varies is the send.
Expected result

A 4–5 minute chord bed at ≈ −12 dBFS that is audibly inert on its own — slightly boring is correct; the surface has not arrived yet.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The bare bed already swells and shiftsAn LFO or envelope still moving in the patchZero all modulation and re-print; stillness is the bed’s one job
The chord sounds thin and the damage later has nothing to gripFilter closed too far — no harmonics to erodeOpen the cutoff until the chord has audible edges, re-print
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

The weather — a return chain, written in automation

Phase focus: the weather — a return chain, written in automation. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live On Return A, build the chain in this order: Saturator (drive to taste, start ≈ 12 dB) → Redux (start ≈ 8 bit, light downsample) → Reverb (large, 100 % wet — it is on a return).
  2. Live Set Send A on BED low, around −25 dB, and listen before going further.
  3. Live In Arrangement view, draw the Send A automation across the full 4–5 minutes: long swells and recessions, each leg taking 30–60 seconds, from nearly dry to plainly weathered and back.
  4. Live Never a step, never a jump.
  5. Live Add two more lanes, sparser: Saturator Drive drifting a few dB up where the send peaks, and Redux bit depth dipping toward 6–7 bit once or twice.
  6. Live These deepen the weather’s character; the send remains the main wind.
  7. Live The rule, enforced without mercy: nothing on BED moves.
  8. Live If you catch yourself wanting to automate the chord, that urge is the old habit this lesson exists to break.
Expected result

A 4–5 minute arrangement where the dry chord plays untouched throughout and three slow automation curves do all the composing — erosion that arrives, deepens, and withdraws like fronts passing.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The damage reads as an effect switching on, not weather arrivingAutomation legs too fast — seconds where minutes were neededStretch every swell past the 30-second mark and re-listen
At high send the chord disappears entirelyReturn level too hot — the weather buries the groundPull Return A’s fader down until the core stays audible at the deepest erosion
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Split your hearing, then A/B the split

Phase focus: split your hearing, then A/B the split. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play your 4–5 minutes end to end, no touching.
  2. Logbook In the logbook, two columns: moved and held still.
  3. Logbook Every audible event goes in one column.
  4. Logbook Check the columns against the session: everything in moved should trace to the three automation lanes;
  5. Live everything in held still to the frozen clip.
  6. Live Any event you cannot assign is a leak — find it.
  7. Live A/B one minute of yours against REF at matched loudness, judging only the split: is your core as still as theirs, and does your surface move without ever implying the chord changed?
  8. Live The low-volume test, on both pieces: turn down well below the mark and listen for what survives.
  9. Live The chord should stand clean out of the receding grit — yours and theirs alike.
Expected result

A two-column logbook page where every event has an owner, an A/B that comes down to the quality of the split, and a low-volume test both pieces pass the same way.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
At low volume your chord wobbles where the record’s holdsPitch or filter movement leaked into the bedRe-print the bed with all modulation at zero — the core must be inert
The piece fatigues within a minute at the markSaturator and Redux both running hot throughout — no recessionsDeepen the automation valleys; weather needs clear spells to read as weather
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • In your own piece, what moved and what didn’t — and can you prove it?
    Answer

    The surface moved — send, drive, depth, the three lanes you drew — and the core didn’t, because a frozen clip cannot. That proof is the point of the method: in your session the split is checkable, and checking it trains the ear to make the same split where there is no session to consult. Relisten to your piece once more and try to hear the chord as landscape and the grit as weather; once the metaphor locks, it does not unlock.

  • Does the record make the same split?
    Answer

    Yes — and the low-volume test is the cleanest evidence: turn “Chimeras” down and the held center stands intact while the damage recedes to a halo, exactly as your frozen bed does. The construction differs — Hecker’s sources and tools are his own — but the architecture is the one you just built: stillness underneath, motion on top, and all the composing done in the amount of erosion. Relisten quiet, then at the mark, and hear the same piece twice.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: Lab 005 wants variation that lives inside the tone — this record shows it can also live on the tone, as a surface running at its own speed while the core supplies the stillness. Erosion over a still core is a slowness your hypothesis can name.
