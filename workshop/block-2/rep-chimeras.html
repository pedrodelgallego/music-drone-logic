Replicate: erosion over a still core

Copy one clear section of Tim Hecker — “Chimeras”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill is about one technique: hold a chord completely still while distortion and noise move across it. The chord never changes — only how much damage reaches it changes. All the composing happens in that amount of damage. Do only the steps that make that technique audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordTim Hecker — “Chimeras” (Harmony in Ultraviolet, 2006)
The effecta stable harmonic center heard through shifting distortion and grain — the damage moves like weather while the chord underneath holds still
The mechanisman unchanging tonal bed pushed through degradation whose amount is the moving part — the writing happens in how much erosion reaches the sound, not in the sound

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring; the Digitakt II rests. The erosion happens inside Live: Saturator, Redux, and a reverb on one return — no plugin shopping
  • New set saved as REP-CHIMERAS — tempo irrelevant; the automation is drawn in minutes, not bars
  • Distortion adds level and high-frequency energy without warning — set the return up at low send first, phones at the lab-001 mark, a real break every 15 minutes
  • What you will make: a short replica of Tim Hecker — “Chimeras”. Focus on this effect: a stable harmonic center heard through shifting distortion and grain - the damage moves like weather while the chord underneath holds still.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: an unchanging tonal bed pushed through degradation whose amount is the moving part - the writing happens in how much erosion reaches the sound, not in the sound.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Tim Hecker — “Chimeras”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Find the core pitch early. Does it ever move?
    Answer

    The harmonic center stays put while everything around it churns. What feels like harmonic movement is mostly the surface revealing and hiding parts of the same chord.

  2. What is the distortion actually contributing?
    Answer

    All the movement. The chord supplies the stillness; the distortion and grain supply every event. Strip the grit away in your imagination and what remains is a chord with almost nothing happening.

  3. Do the damage and the tone move at the same speed?
    Answer

    No — and that gap is the whole style. The grit shifts in seconds; the chord moves in minutes or not at all. Two different speeds running at once.

  4. Is the distortion on the chord, or around it?
    Answer

    It feels like damage done to the sound itself, not an effect added beside it.

  5. At low volume, what survives?
    Answer

    The chord. Turn the record down and the grit fades while the held center stays clear — proof the core was always intact underneath the damage, not mixed into it.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same two-speed split — a chord that stays the same from start to finish, with a surface of noise and distortion moving on top of it.
  • The dry chord never changes — no automation on its track, no clip envelopes, no edits. It is the fixed ground.
  • Only three things may move: the send amount, the Saturator drive, and the Redux depth — and all of them move slowly, over minutes.
  • The distortion chain lives on a return track, not as an insert directly on the chord. You need a send so you can automate (ride) how much of the chord gets routed through the damage.

The whole system in one row: one recorded chord that never changes, a return chain that saturates, crushes, and reverbs whatever is sent to it, and four minutes of automation slowly deciding how much of the chord gets sent.

printed chord bed · never edited → Send A · the only fader that writes → Return A · Saturator → Redux → Reverb → mix · core + weather · ≈ −12 dBFS

The whole arrangement on one timeline. The core is a flat band from start to finish. Above it the distortion swells, drops back, and swells again. The dotted line is the only thing you draw: the automation curve that controls how much distortion reaches the chord.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Chimeras” into it.
  2. Loop the stretch you found most weathered in the reference pass — two minutes is enough.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. ⌘ T once; rename: BED, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
  6. Write in the logbook, one line: which parts of the loop you believe are the chord and which are the damage.
  7. Phase 4 re-asks this about your own piece.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Tim Hecker — “Chimeras” (Harmony in Ultraviolet, 2006) and use Live audio/MIDI tracks, Utility, automation, and resampling.

  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects a stable harmonic center heard through shifting distortion and grain — the damage moves like weather while the chord underneath holds still.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The bed — one chord, printed, then frozen

Phase focus: the bed — one chord, printed, then frozen. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this

  2. thirds get muddier.
  3. Pick one and stay.
  4. Slow attack, full sustain, zero LFO, zero filter movement — the bed must contain no events of its own.
  5. Some harmonic content is good: erosion needs edges to chew on.
  6. Set the M4 input-1 gain on the held chord, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, checked after a full minute of holding.
  7. Print 4–5 minutes into BED using the MicroFreak’s Hold.
  8. One take, no punch-ins.
  9. Disarm the track and declare the clip frozen: no clip envelopes, no fades, no edits, no automation on this track — for the rest of the lesson, the only thing leaving BED that varies is the send.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

The weather — a return chain, written in automation

Phase focus: the weather — a return chain, written in automation. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. On Return A, build the chain in this order: Saturator (drive to taste, start ≈ 12 dB) → Redux (start ≈ 8 bit, light downsample) → Reverb (large, 100 % wet — it is on a return).
  2. Set Send A on BED low, around −25 dB, and listen before going further.
  3. In Arrangement view, draw the Send A automation across the full 4–5 minutes: long swells and recessions, each leg taking 30–60 seconds, from nearly dry to plainly weathered and back.
  4. Never a step, never a jump.
  5. Add two more lanes, sparser: Saturator Drive drifting a few dB up where the send peaks, and Redux bit depth dipping toward 6–7 bit once or twice.
  6. These deepen the weather’s character; the send remains the main wind.
  7. Rule: nothing on BED moves.
  8. If you want to automate the chord, stop. This lesson exists to break that habit — all the composing goes into the damage, not the chord.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Split your hearing, then A/B the split

Phase focus: split your hearing, then A/B the split. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play your 4–5 minutes end to end, no touching.
  2. In the logbook, two columns: moved and held still.
  3. Every audible event goes in one column.
  4. Check the columns against the session: everything in moved should trace to the three automation lanes;
  5. everything in held still to the frozen clip.
  6. Any event you cannot assign is a leak — find it.
  7. A/B one minute of yours against REF at matched loudness, judging only the split: is your core as still as theirs, and does your surface move without ever implying the chord changed?
  8. The low-volume test, on both pieces: turn down well below the mark and listen for what survives.
  9. The chord should stand clean out of the receding grit — yours and theirs alike.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • In your own piece, what moved and what didn’t — and can you prove it?
    Answer

    The surface moved — send, drive, depth, the three automation lanes you drew — and the core didn’t, because a frozen clip can’t change. That’s the point of this method: in your session the split is checkable. Relisten to your piece and try to hear the chord as the fixed ground and the grit as weather passing over it.

  • Does the record make the same split?
    Answer

    Yes — and the low-volume test is the cleanest evidence. Turn “Chimeras” down and the held center stays intact while the damage fades to a halo, exactly as your frozen bed does. The construction differs (Hecker’s sources and tools are his own) but the structure is the same: stillness underneath, motion on top, and all the composing done through the amount of damage. Relisten quiet, then at the mark, and hear the same piece twice.
