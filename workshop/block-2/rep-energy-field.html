Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the transducer below the surface

Copy one clear section of Jana Winderen — Energy Field. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: hydrophones lowered below the surface - the transducer placed where ears cannot go, set before the first sound was captured. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordJana Winderen — Energy Field (2010)
The effecta world an air microphone reports as silence, heard in full detail — creaks, crackle, and depth you can hear with no reverb to explain it
The mechanismhydrophones lowered below the surface — the transducer placed where ears cannot go, set before the first sound was captured

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Gear: the Lab 008 solid path — contact mic / piezo (AKG C411 class or the DIY piezo) into the handheld recorder, or into M4 input 1; closed phones for monitoring; Live 12 waits at the end for assembly. The Digitakt II rests this lesson
  • Say it plainly: Winderen’s water is arctic and deep, yours comes out of a tap. That is fine — you are replicating the capture system, a transducer below the surface, on water you can reach: a filled sink, a stock pot, a bathtub, the radiator’s plumbing
  • Water and electronics, per the field safety checklist: the piezo gets sealed (a balloon or knotted freezer bag, taped at the cable) before it touches water; the recorder stays an arm’s length from the sink edge; hands dry before they touch anything powered. Nothing mains-powered comes near the water
  • New set saved as REP-WINDEREN — no tempo worth setting; the takes are the clock
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop — sealing, matched takes, a two-minute piece, and the A/B
  • What you will make: a short replica of Jana Winderen — Energy Field. Focus on this effect: a world an air microphone reports as silence, heard in full detail - creaks, crackle, and depth you can hear with no reverb to explain it.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: hydrophones lowered below the surface - the transducer placed where ears cannot go, set before the first sound was captured.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Jana Winderen — Energy Field. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Pick three sounds and name their sources.
    Answer

    Mostly you cannot, and that is the medium’s signature - ice under pressure, crustaceans, water moving against itself, none of it carrying an airborne label.

  2. Where is the room — the reverb that tells you how big the space is?
    Answer

    There is no room you recognize. Water carries sound differently than air - farther, faster, with detail intact - so the space reads as enormous without ever echoing.

  3. What would an air microphone, held above the ice at the same moment, have recorded?
    Answer

    Wind and near-silence. The orchestra exists only for the sensor below the surface - which means the decisive compositional act happened before recording began, when someone chose a transducer that lives where ears cannot.

  4. How much of this strangeness is processing?
    Answer

    Almost none of it needs to be. Nothing behaves like a plugin - everything behaves like a place: events have bodies, distances, and consequences. The strangeness was captured, not added.

  5. Nothing here was played — so why does it feel composed?
    Answer

    Selection, sequence, and patience. Winderen chose where to lower the hydrophones, when to keep listening, which stretches to keep, and in what order they unfold - composition done entirely with decisions, not notes.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a two-minute piece built only from submerged takes that a blind listener would not pin to a kitchen — events with body, crackle with detail, distance read as texture, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS. Closeness to the record’s otherworldliness is the measure of your transducer discipline.
  • Submerged takes only — nothing recorded in air enters the piece.
  • The matched pairs must exist: the same event captured from the air and from below, both takes kept and labeled.
  • Level moves and fades only — no reverb. Depth is the sensor’s job, not a plugin’s.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a sealed piezo below the waterline, gain set for the loudest crack, the same events captured twice — air and submerged — and a two-minute piece assembled from the submerged side only.

sealed piezo · below the surface → recorder gain · set for the loudest crack → matched pairs · AIR + SUB per event → Live · 2-min piece · submerged only

One event, two sensors. The air mic above the waterline and the sealed piezo below it witness the same ice cracking — and return two different worlds. Neither file is “the real sound”; each sensor invents and erases.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the seal

Phase focus: the reference and the seal. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live In Live: ⌘ T, rename the track REF, drag in a track from Energy Field, loop its most characteristic three minutes, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Live REF stays one solo-press away all lesson.
  3. Recorder Seal the piezo: work the disc to the bottom of a balloon or a knotted freezer bag, press the skin flat against the disc face, and tape the bag shut around the cable with gaffer — water must never reach the cable entry.
  4. Recorder Test dry: piezo into the recorder (or M4 input 1), phones on, scratch the sealed disc lightly — signal confirms the seal didn’t break a connection.
  5. Recorder Test wet, small: lower it into a drinking glass of water and stir with a teaspoon.
  6. Recorder You should hear the spoon enormously — close, dry, with body.
  7. Logbook Log the seal recipe in the logbook; it joins the kit list as a capability.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, and a sealed, tested piezo hears a teaspoon in a glass like an event — the underwater path is open.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Signal is dull and distant in the glass testAir gap between disc and seal skin — a second boundary muting the detailRe-wrap with the skin pressed flat against the disc face; squeeze the air out before taping
Hum or crackle when the cable movesStrained or broken connection at the disc — the seal stressed the solderRe-seat the cable, tape strain relief at the bag mouth so the cable, not the joint, takes the pull
Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

Matched pairs — the same event, two sensors

Phase focus: matched pairs — the same event, two sensors. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Fill the sink or a stock pot.
  2. Choose three events: a handful of ice cubes dropped in warm water and left to crack;
  3. the tap dripping from height; objects stirred and knocked together below the surface.
  4. Recorder Rig both sensors: the recorder’s own mics in air a hand above the surface, the sealed piezo fully submerged and resting still.
  5. Recorder If the recorder takes the piezo as an input, capture both paths in one take;
  6. Recorder otherwise run each event twice and keep your gestures identical.
  7. Recorder Set gain on a rehearsal crack — drop two cubes, watch the meter, set for that peak.
  8. Recorder Then leave the gain alone.
  9. Recorder Record each event 1–2 minutes.
  10. Recorder Hands off everything during takes; ice does its own performing as it melts.
  11. Recorder Label immediately: AIR-01/SUB-01 through 03.
  12. Recorder Bonus pair if you have a radiator: piezo sealed against the pipe while the heating fires — the building’s own water system.
Expected result

Three matched pairs on the card, no clipping on the loudest crack, and a first shock already logged: the submerged takes are full of events the air takes barely admit happened.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The submerged take is mostly rumble and thumpsThe piezo is touching the basin and hearing the building through it — handling, not waterSuspend it mid-water from its cable, taped to the tap; it should touch nothing solid
Cracks clip even at modest gainUnderwater events are far louder at the sensor than they sound to you in airRe-rehearse gain on the loudest event, not the average one — the field rule, verbatim
Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

Compose two minutes from the submerged world only

Phase focus: compose two minutes from the submerged world only. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import only the SUB takes into REP-WINDEREN, one track per take.
  2. Listen through once and mark the keepers — the stretches where something unidentifiable happens.
  3. Trust the unidentifiable; it is the record’s currency.
  4. Arrange two minutes: open on the densest texture, let one sparse stretch sit long in the middle, end on a single event.
  5. Sequence is your only syntax.
  6. Live Balance with clip gain and fades only — peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, no EQ, and no reverb under any argument: depth must come from the sensor or not at all.
  7. Live Keep the crackle — do not low-pass the takes “to clean them.” The high detail is where the underwater signature lives.
Expected result

A two-minute arrangement of submerged material that already refuses to sound like a sink — composed entirely by selection, order, and level.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The piece sounds like “a recording of a sink”You kept the explicable stretches — taps and stirs that carry their household labelRecut around the unidentifiable passages; the cracks and groans, not the gestures that caused them
It feels like clips in a row, not a pieceNo level relationships — every clip presented at one importancePick a front sound and a floor for each section; let one texture sit underneath throughout
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The A/B — what each sensor invented and erased

Phase focus: the A/B — what each sensor invented and erased. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Put AIR-01 and SUB-01 on adjacent tracks, trimmed to the same event, levels matched.
  2. Flip between them with solo.
  3. Logbook Logbook, two columns per sensor: invented (what it added that the other lacks) and erased (what it lost).
  4. Logbook Four cells, filled in plain words.
  5. Live Play your two-minute piece end to end at the mark, no touching, then A/B one minute against REF at matched loudness.
  6. Live Judge one thing: does yours also refuse to name its sources?
  7. Logbook Write where the record’s sense of depth comes from, now that you have made some yourself.
Expected result

Four filled cells, a piece that holds two minutes without a household label, and a logbook line about depth-as-texture — the transducer lesson in your own handwriting.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The A/B columns read “air = normal, water = weird”Judging by familiarity instead of content — air is not neutral, just habitualList three concrete sounds per take; the air take’s “room” is an invention too, write it down
Your piece reads flat next to REFEvery event at the same apparent distance — no near/far planRe-balance: one close element loud and dry, one texture small and far underneath
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • A/B the same event, air against submerged — what did each sensor invent and erase?
    Answer

    The air mic invented a room — your kitchen’s reflections wrapped around a faint tick — and erased nearly everything happening below the surface. The piezo erased the room entirely and invented body: cracks with mass, grain, and consequence that the air never carried. Neither file is “the real sound”; each boundary tells its own version, and the recordist’s first decision is which version exists at all. If your columns are thin, relisten to the pair once more and force three entries per cell.

  • Where does the record’s depth cue come from?
    Answer

    Texture, not reverb. Distance underwater reads as detail thinning and crackle density shifting — far events arrive intact but small, with no echo announcing a hall. Relisten to a far event on Energy Field and notice nothing rings; then check your own takes for the same cue, near stir against far drip. If your piece needed reverb to feel deep, the depth was fake and the plugin comes off.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 008 — the kit chooses what the world can say, and you have now heard one event exist in one medium and barely exist in another. The lab’s three-path comparison is this lesson run across air, solid, and electromagnetic at once.
