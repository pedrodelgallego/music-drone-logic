Replicate: the transducer below the surface

Copy one clear section of Jana Winderen — Energy Field. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: hydrophones (underwater microphones) lowered below the surface — the sensor placed where human ears cannot go, positioned before a single sound was captured. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordJana Winderen — Energy Field (2010)

Before you start
  • Gear: the Lab 008 solid path — contact mic / piezo (AKG C411 class or the DIY piezo) into the handheld recorder, or into M4 input 1; closed phones for monitoring; Live 12 at the end for assembly. The Digitakt II is not used this lesson.
  • Winderen’s water is arctic and deep; yours comes out of a tap. That is fine — you are replicating the capture system (a sensor below the surface) on water you can reach: a filled sink, a stock pot, a bathtub, or the radiator’s plumbing.
  • Water and electronics safety, per the field checklist: seal the piezo (a balloon or knotted freezer bag, taped at the cable) before it touches water; keep the recorder an arm’s length from the sink edge; dry your hands before touching anything powered. Nothing mains-powered comes near the water.
  • New set saved as REP-WINDEREN — no tempo worth setting; the takes are the clock
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop — sealing, matched takes, a two-minute piece, and the A/B
  • What you will make: a short replica of Jana Winderen — Energy Field. Focus on this effect: a world an air microphone reports as silence, heard in full detail - creaks, crackle, and depth you can hear with no reverb to explain it.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: hydrophones lowered below the surface - the transducer placed where ears cannot go, set before the first sound was captured.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Jana Winderen — Energy Field. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Pick three sounds and name their sources.
    Answer

    Mostly you cannot. That is the medium’s signature — ice under pressure, small crustaceans, water moving against itself. None of it carries the kind of label you would attach to an airborne sound.

  2. Where is the room — the reverb that tells you how big the space is?
    Answer

    There is none. Water carries sound differently than air — farther, faster, with detail intact — so the space reads as enormous without any echoing to explain it.

  3. What would an air microphone, held above the ice at the same moment, have recorded?
    Answer

    Wind and near-silence. The whole orchestra only exists for the sensor below the surface. The decisive compositional act happened before recording began, when someone chose a sensor that goes where ears cannot.

  4. How much of this strangeness is processing?
    Answer

    Almost none. Nothing behaves like a plugin — everything behaves like a place: events have weight, distance, and consequence. The strangeness was captured, not added afterward.

  5. Nothing here was played — so why does it feel composed?
    Answer

    Selection, sequence, and patience. Winderen chose where to lower the hydrophones, when to keep listening, which sections to keep, and in what order they unfold. Composition done entirely with decisions, not notes.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a two-minute piece built only from submerged takes, where a blind listener would not identify it as a kitchen — events with body, crackle with detail, a sense of depth that comes from the recording itself, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS. How close you get to the record’s strangeness is the measure of how well you used the sensor.
  • Submerged takes only — nothing recorded in air enters the piece.
  • Keep the matched pairs: the same event captured from above the waterline and from below, both files kept and labeled.
  • Level adjustments and fades only — no reverb. Depth comes from the sensor’s placement, not from a plugin.

The piece in one row: seal the piezo, lower it below the waterline, set gain for the loudest crack, capture each event both from air and from below, then build a two-minute piece from the submerged recordings only.

sealed piezo · below the surface → recorder gain · set for the loudest crack → matched pairs · AIR + SUB per event → Live · 2-min piece · submerged only

One event, two sensors. The air mic above the waterline and the sealed piezo below witness the same ice cracking — and return two completely different recordings. Neither is “the real sound.” Each sensor picks up some things and misses others.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the seal

Phase focus: the reference and the seal. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. In Live: ⌘ T, rename the track REF, drag in a track from Energy Field, loop its most characteristic three minutes, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. REF stays one solo-press away all lesson.
  3. Seal the piezo: work the disc to the bottom of a balloon or a knotted freezer bag, press the skin flat against the disc face, and tape the bag shut around the cable with gaffer — water must never reach the cable entry.
  4. Test dry: piezo into the recorder (or M4 input 1), phones on, scratch the sealed disc lightly — signal confirms the seal didn’t break a connection.
  5. Test wet, small: lower it into a drinking glass of water and stir with a teaspoon.
  6. You should hear the spoon enormously — close, dry, with body.
  7. Log the seal recipe in the logbook; it joins the kit list as a capability.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Jana Winderen — Energy Field (2010) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the seal — as the smallest Live set that proves hydrophones (underwater microphones) lowered below the surface — the sensor placed where human ears cannot go, positioned before recording begins.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects a world that an air microphone would report as near-silence, now heard in full detail — creaks, crackle, and a sense of depth with no reverb to explain it.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the seal into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates hydrophones (underwater microphones) lowered below the surface — the sensor placed where human ears cannot go, positioned before recording begins, not just the surface of Jana Winderen — Energy Field (2010).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, and a sealed, tested piezo hears a teaspoon in a glass like an event — the underwater path is open.

Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

Matched pairs — the same event, two sensors

Phase focus: matched pairs — the same event, two sensors. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Fill the sink or a stock pot.
  2. Choose three events: ice cubes dropped into warm water and left to crack; the tap dripping from height; objects stirred and knocked together below the surface.
  3. Set up both sensors: the recorder’s own mics in air a hand above the surface, the sealed piezo fully submerged and resting still.
  4. If the recorder can take the piezo as an input, capture both paths in one take. Otherwise run each event twice with identical gestures.
  5. Set gain on a rehearsal crack — drop two cubes, watch the meter, set for that peak.
  6. Then leave the gain alone.
  7. Record each event 1–2 minutes.
  8. Hands off everything during takes; ice does its own performing as it melts.
  9. Label immediately: AIR-01/SUB-01 through 03.
  10. Bonus pair if you have a radiator: piezo sealed against the pipe while the heating fires — the building’s own water system.

Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

Compose two minutes from the submerged world only

Phase focus: compose two minutes from the submerged world only. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import only the SUB takes into REP-WINDEREN, one track per take.
  2. Listen through once and mark the keepers — the stretches where something unidentifiable happens. Trust those moments; they are the record’s currency.
  3. Arrange two minutes: open on the densest texture, let one sparse stretch hold in the middle, end on a single event. Sequence is your only tool here.
  4. Balance with clip gain and fades only — peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, no EQ, and no reverb under any argument: depth must come from the sensor or not at all.
  5. Keep the crackle — do not low-pass the takes “to clean them.” The high detail is where the underwater signature lives.

The A/B — what each sensor invented and erased

Phase focus: the A/B — what each sensor invented and erased. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • A/B the same event, air against submerged — what did each sensor invent and erase?
    Answer

    The air mic invented a room — your kitchen’s reflections around a faint tick — and erased nearly everything happening below the surface. The piezo erased the room entirely and picked up body: cracks with weight and detail that the air version never captured. Neither file is “the real sound.” Each sensor tells its own version. The recordist’s first decision is which version gets to exist at all. If your columns are thin, relisten and force three entries per cell.

  • Where does the record’s depth cue come from?
    Answer

    Texture, not reverb. Underwater distance reads as detail thinning and crackle density shifting — far events arrive complete but small, with no echo to suggest a hall. Relisten to a far event on Energy Field and notice nothing rings. Then check your own takes for the same cue, near stir against far drip. If your piece needed reverb to feel deep, the depth was fake and the plugin comes off.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
