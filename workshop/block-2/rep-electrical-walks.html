Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the coil where ears report silence

Copy one clear section of Christina Kubisch — Five Electrical Walks. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: electromagnetic induction - a coil translates the fields that circuits leak into audio, and the walk’s route orders the material. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordChristina Kubisch — Five Electrical Walks (2007)

Before you start
  • Gear: the Lab 008 electromagnetic path — induction coil (LOM Elektrosluch class, or a telephone pickup coil) into the handheld recorder or M4 input 1; closed phones to monitor, one ear slipped free whenever you are moving
  • Safety, per the field checklist and without exception: the coil listens from a distance — nothing gets touched, opened, or probed. Distribution boxes, meter cupboards, substations, and anything with exposed wiring are off the route. Household devices are surveyed from the outside, as built
  • If the walk leaves the flat — a tram platform, a shop doorway — public-space awareness applies: one ear free, eyes up, and you stop walking before you start judging a recording
  • New set saved as REP-KUBISCH — the walk provides the order; Live only assembles it
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes — survey, walk, assembly, and the source map
  • What you will make: a short replica of Christina Kubisch — Five Electrical Walks. Focus on this effect: a city of rhythms and drones where ears report silence - machines performing sequenced music nobody wrote, with no room and no air around it.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: electromagnetic induction - a coil translates the fields that circuits leak into audio, and the walk’s route orders the material.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Christina Kubisch — Five Electrical Walks. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is missing from these recordings that every microphone recording has?
    Answer

    Space. No reverb, no distance, no room tone, no footsteps - electromagnetic fields do not echo, so this world has no acoustics at all. Relisten and try to place any single sound in a room; the impossibility is the answer.

  2. Who composed the rhythms?
    Answer

    Nobody - and everybody who designed a power supply. Switching supplies pulse at regular rates because regularity is what circuits do, and the coil turns that engineering into sequenced music with no author.

  3. How differently do two “silent” machines sound?
    Answer

    Completely - a security gate, a tram line, and an ATM share nothing but voltage. Every circuit leaks its own signature: pitch, pulse rate, grain.

  4. Where did “near” and “far” go?
    Answer

    Fields fall off fast, so presence is nearly binary - a source is there or it is gone within a step or two. Every fade you hear is Kubisch’s body moving; her position is the only fader this medium has.

  5. What gives the piece its form, if no one is playing?
    Answer

    The route. The walk decides what follows what, how long each station holds, and where the silences fall - the city is the sequencer and the itinerary is the score. Relisten to one track as a journey and notice you can almost draw the map.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a walked sequence of six to eight electromagnetic stations, assembled in route order at matched levels — rhythms, whines, and chatter with no acoustics around them, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS. Closeness to the record’s dry, airless strangeness is the measure.
  • Coil material only — no microphone takes, no room tone bridging the stations.
  • The walked order is the arrangement; no reordering in the edit.
  • No effects, and especially no reverb — this world has no acoustics, and adding some would be a lie about the medium.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a coil tested on a known device, a surveyed route of six to eight leaking circuits, one station recorded at a time with gain set for the loudest field, and an assembly that keeps the walk’s own order.

coil · tested on a known device → survey · 10+ candidates · pick 6–8 → the walk · one take per station → Live · walked order · matched levels

The walk as a score. Each station is a circuit with its own voice — the charger pulses, the router chatters, the hob roars — and the dashed line between them is the only composition you do: choosing what follows what.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the known device

Phase focus: the reference and the known device. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live In Live: ⌘ T, rename REF, drag in a track from Five Electrical Walks, loop two minutes, trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Recorder Coil into the recorder (or M4 input 1), phones on, and hold it against a charging laptop charger — the known emitter.
  3. Recorder You should hear it immediately: a whine, a pulse, or both.
  4. Recorder Learn the fall-off: move the coil away in steps — touching, 5 cm, 20 cm, arm’s length — and log the level at each distance.
  5. Recorder This is the medium’s “acoustics” lesson: presence is nearly binary.
  6. Recorder Rotate the coil at the sweet spot and note how orientation changes the voice — the coil hears the field’s direction, not just its strength.
  7. Logbook Set gain on the loudest position you found and write the setting down.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, the coil speaks on a known device, and the logbook holds a fall-off table — the rig and your expectations are both calibrated.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Nothing but faint hiss at any distanceThe device is idle — many supplies barely leak until they workPut the laptop under load (charging, screen bright) and sweep again, coil touching the brick
Constant 50/60 Hz hum drowning everythingThe coil is hearing mains wiring in the wall, not the deviceStep away from walls and extension strips; use the hum as a landmark, not a take
Phase 2 · ≈ 20 min

The survey — listen to the whole home once

Phase focus: the survey — listen to the whole home once. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Walk the home with coil and phones, one ear free.
  2. Recorder Candidates: laptop charger, phone while charging, router, induction hob, LED dimmer, fridge electronics, washing machine mid-cycle, TV front panel, toothbrush charger, doorbell transformer — and the tram or train platform if one is near.
  3. Recorder Log at least ten finds, one line each: device, the sweet spot, and the voice in plain words — “pulses like a sequencer,” “rising whine,” “rough chatter.”
  4. Recorder Mark the two safety exclusions explicitly: anything unknown or exposed is logged as not approached, per the field checklist.
  5. Recorder The coil reads from a distance or not at all.
  6. Logbook Choose 6–8 stations for contrast — at least one pulse, one drone, one chatter — and write the route order in the logbook.
  7. Logbook That order is now fixed.
Expected result

A survey list of ten-plus circuits with their voices named, a fixed route of 6–8 stations, and two honest not approached entries proving the checklist traveled with you.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Every device sounds like the same buzzCoil never reached each device’s sweet spot — you swept past at one heightSlow down: trace each device’s surface, corners and cables included, and rotate the coil at each stop
Fewer than six usable stations foundIdle devices — circuits leak most when workingTurn things on: charge the phone, boil the hob, start a wash cycle, stream video through the router
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Record the walk

Phase focus: record the walk. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Set gain once, on the loudest station from the survey (usually the hob or the washing machine), and leave it for the whole walk — stations must keep their true level relationships.
  2. Walk the route in order.
  3. At each station: approach, find the sweet spot, hold the coil still for 30–60 seconds, withdraw.
  4. The approach and withdrawal are part of the take — they are this medium’s only fades.
  5. Hold the cable still while recording — coil handling prints as thumps and crackle, the field-kit lesson in miniature.
  6. One continuous take if your arm allows it, or one file per station;
  7. either way, no station gets re-recorded out of order.
  8. The walk happened once.
  9. Log each station as recorded: device, duration, anything unexpected.
Expected result

Six to eight stations on the card in walked order, true level relationships intact, with audible approaches and withdrawals — a performed route, not a sample pack.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Quiet stations are buried in hissGain set for the loud station leaves faint emitters near the floor — real, but unmusicalGet closer instead of hotter: the sweet spot is the gain knob in this medium
Thumps and scrapes through the takesCable and coil handling — your grip is in the signal pathWrap the cable once around your hand, move slower, hold the coil by its body not its lead
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Assemble in walked order and map the sources

Phase focus: assemble in walked order and map the sources. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Import the takes into REP-KUBISCH and lay them on the Arrangement timeline in walked order — trims at the heads and tails only.
  2. Live Level-match the stations toward ≈ −12 dBFS peaks with clip gain, keeping the loud stations clearly louder — matched is not flattened.
  3. Live No effects of any kind.
  4. Live If a transition feels abrupt, that is the medium telling the truth.
  5. Live Listen end to end at the mark, then A/B a minute against REF — judge dryness, strangeness, and whether your machines also have grooves.
  6. Logbook Write the source map in the logbook: every audible sound assigned to its device — and every sound you cannot assign marked unidentified, honestly.
Expected result

A six-to-eight-station walk that plays as a piece, an A/B that comes down to dryness and rhythm, and a source map with honest gaps in it.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The sequence feels like a demo reel, not a pieceEvery station given equal time — no pacingTrim toward contrast: let the best station run long, cut the weakest to one gesture; order stays fixed
Yours sounds tame next to REFSurvey settled for the easy emitters — chargers only, no heavyweightsRe-walk with the hob, the washing machine, or the tram platform on the route
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Which of your devices had a groove?
    Answer

    At least one should have — switching power supplies pulse at audible rates, so the steadiest rhythm on your walk probably came from the cheapest wall-wart in the flat. That groove is the record’s central joke made local: regularity is what machines do, and the coil turns engineering into sequencing. If nothing grooved, relisten to your charger take at the sweet spot; if it still doesn’t pulse, the coil never got close enough, and the station deserves a retake on tomorrow’s walk.

  • Map each sound to its source — what stays unidentifiable?
    Answer

    Something should, and the honest map admits it. Fields overlap — near any wall the coil hears a neighborhood of circuits, not one device — so a clean one-to-one map usually means you stopped listening early. The unidentified entries are the same currency as Winderen’s unnamed crackle: evidence that you crossed into a medium your ears don’t live in. Relisten to your most confident station and ask whether everything in it really belongs to that device.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.

  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 008 — the electromagnetic leg of the three-path comparison is now certified, fall-off table and all. When the lab asks you to capture one object through the coil, you already know its only fader is your position.
