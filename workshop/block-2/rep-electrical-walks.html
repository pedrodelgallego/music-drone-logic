Replicate: the coil where ears report silence

Copy one clear section of Christina Kubisch — Five Electrical Walks. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record’s core mechanism: electromagnetic induction — every electrical circuit leaks an invisible field, and a pickup coil (like those used in electric guitars) translates that field into audio you can record. The walk’s route decides what order you hear things in. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: the Lab 008 electromagnetic path — induction coil (LOM Elektrosluch class, or a telephone pickup coil — a small sensor that hears electrical fields rather than air pressure) into the handheld recorder or M4 input 1; closed phones to monitor, one ear slipped free whenever you are moving
  • Safety, per the field checklist and without exception: the coil works at a distance — nothing gets touched, opened, or probed. Distribution boxes, meter cupboards, substations, and anything with exposed wiring are off the route. Survey household devices from the outside only.
  • If the walk leaves the flat — a tram platform, a shop doorway — stay aware: one ear free, eyes up, and stop walking before you judge a recording
  • New set saved as REP-KUBISCH — the walk provides the order; Live only assembles it
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes — survey, walk, assembly, and the source map
  • What you will make: a short replica of Christina Kubisch — Five Electrical Walks. Focus on this effect: a city of rhythms and drones where ears report silence - machines performing sequenced music nobody wrote, with no room and no air around it.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: electromagnetic induction - a coil translates the fields that circuits leak into audio, and the walk’s route orders the material.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Christina Kubisch — Five Electrical Walks. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is missing from these recordings that every microphone recording has?
    Answer

    Room sound. No reverb, no distance, no room tone, no footsteps — electromagnetic fields do not echo, so this world has no acoustics at all. Relisten and try to place any single sound in a room. You cannot; that is the answer.

  2. Who composed the rhythms?
    Answer

    Nobody composed them — and everyone who designed a power supply. Switching power supplies pulse at regular rates because regularity is what circuits do. The coil turns that engineering into patterned sound with no author.

  3. How differently do two “silent” machines sound?
    Answer

    Completely differently. A security gate, a tram line, and an ATM share nothing but voltage. Each circuit leaks its own signature: its own pitch, pulse rate, and texture.

  4. Where did “near” and “far” go?
    Answer

    Fields fall off very quickly, so presence is almost binary — a source is loud or it is gone within a step or two. Every fade you hear is Kubisch moving her body. Her position is the only volume control this medium has.

  5. What gives the piece its form, if no one is playing?
    Answer

    The route. The walk decides what follows what, how long each station lasts, and where the silences fall. The city is the sequencer and the itinerary is the score. Relisten to one track as a journey and notice you can almost draw the map.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a walked sequence of six to eight electromagnetic stations, assembled in route order at matched levels — rhythms, whines, and chatter with no room sound around them, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS. How close you get to the record’s dry, airless quality is the measure.
  • Coil material only — no microphone takes, no room tone bridging the stations.
  • The order you walked is the arrangement. Do not reorder in the edit.
  • No effects, and especially no reverb. This world has no room sound. Adding reverb would misrepresent what the coil actually captures.

The piece in one row: test the coil on a known device, survey a route of six to eight electrical circuits, record each station with gain set for the loudest field, then assemble in the order you walked.

coil · tested on a known device → survey · 10+ candidates · pick 6–8 → the walk · one take per station → Live · walked order · matched levels

The walk as a score. Each station is a circuit with its own sound — the charger pulses, the router chatters, the hob roars — and the dashed line between them is the only composing you do: deciding what comes next.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the known device

Phase focus: the reference and the known device. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. In Live: ⌘ T, rename REF, drag in a track from Five Electrical Walks, loop two minutes, trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Coil into the recorder (or M4 input 1), phones on, and hold it against a charging laptop charger — the known emitter.
  3. You should hear it immediately: a whine, a pulse, or both.
  4. Learn the fall-off: move the coil away in steps — touching, 5 cm, 20 cm, arm’s length — and log the level at each distance.
  5. Recorder This teaches you the medium’s key behavior: a source is loud or it is gone. There is very little in between.
  6. Recorder Rotate the coil at the sweet spot and notice how orientation changes the sound — the coil responds to the direction of the field, not just its strength.
  7. Set gain on the loudest position you found and write the setting down.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Christina Kubisch — Five Electrical Walks (2007) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the known device — as the smallest Live set that proves electromagnetic induction — a pickup coil translates the invisible fields that electrical circuits leak into audio; the walk’s route decides what order you hear it in.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects rhythms and drones in places your ears would call silent — machines producing patterned sound nobody composed, with no room or air around it.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Record the walk

Phase focus: record the walk. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Set gain once, on the loudest station from the survey (usually the hob or the washing machine), and leave it for the whole walk — stations must keep their true level relationships.
  2. Walk the route in order.
  3. At each station: approach, find the sweet spot, hold the coil still for 30–60 seconds, withdraw.
  4. The approach and withdrawal are part of the take — they are the only natural fades this medium produces.
  5. Hold the cable still while recording. Coil handling prints as thumps and crackle — the same lesson as the field kit, in miniature.
  6. Record as one continuous take if your arm allows it, or one file per station. Either way, no station gets re-recorded out of order. The walk happened once.
  7. Log each station as recorded: device, duration, anything unexpected.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Assemble in walked order and map the sources

Phase focus: assemble in walked order and map the sources. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the takes into REP-KUBISCH and lay them on the Arrangement timeline in walked order — trims at the heads and tails only.
  2. Level-match the stations toward ≈ −12 dBFS peaks with clip gain, keeping the loud stations clearly louder — matched is not flattened.
  3. No effects of any kind.
  4. If a transition feels abrupt, that is correct — fields drop off steeply, and abruptness is honest here.
  5. Listen end to end at the mark, then A/B a minute against REF — judge dryness, strangeness, and whether your machines also have grooves.
  6. Write the source map in the logbook: every audible sound assigned to its device — and every sound you cannot assign marked unidentified, honestly.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Which of your devices had a groove?
    Answer

    At least one should. Switching power supplies pulse at audible rates because regularity is what circuits do, and the coil turns that engineering into a repeating rhythm. The steadiest pulse on your walk probably came from the cheapest charger in the flat. If nothing grooved, relisten to your charger take at the sweet spot; if it still has no pulse, the coil never got close enough — that station deserves a retake on the next walk.

  • Map each sound to its source — what stays unidentifiable?
    Answer

    Something should stay unidentifiable, and an honest map admits it. Fields overlap — near any wall the coil picks up several circuits at once, not one device. A perfectly clean one-to-one map usually means you stopped listening carefully. The unidentified entries are the same thing as Winderen’s unnamed crackle: evidence that you entered a medium your ears do not normally live in. Relisten to your most confident station and ask whether everything in it really belongs to that one device.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
