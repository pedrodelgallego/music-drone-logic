Replicate: form as geography

Copy one clear section of Annea Lockwood — A Sound Map of the Hudson River. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: recording sites along one continuous system, presented in geographic order at a steady level — no narration, no processing, the route as the form. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone at one fixed gain setting for every station. Use a mic into M4 input 1 only if all five stations are within reach of the desk, which they usually aren’t. DT 990 Pro for the edit.
  • New set saved as REP-SOUND-MAP — no tempo; the route is the only structure this piece has.
  • Safety: keep water and electronics apart — hold the recorder, don’t rest it near the sink. At street corners, take headphones off while traffic is moving. Monitor at a moderate, fixed level.
  • What you will make: a short replica of Annea Lockwood — A Sound Map of the Hudson River. The goal is this effect: one river from the mountains to the sea, station after station. Every location sounds different, but you always recognize the same water.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and anything that hides whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism — recording sites along one continuous system, presented in geographic order at a steady level, with no narration and no processing. The route is the form.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences come from your source choices or gear limits, not from missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Annea Lockwood — A Sound Map of the Hudson River. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What changes from site to site?
    Answer

    Almost everything: the birds, the boats, the wind, the human activity, how close the water sounds, and how large the surrounding space feels. Each station is a complete scene.

  2. What never changes?
    Answer

    The subject — it is recognizably the same water everywhere, from mountain stream to harbor. One continuous thing runs through the whole record.

  3. How are the sections joined?
    Answer

    Plainly — steady levels, no dramatic transitions, no effect smoothing the join. The cuts feel almost routine, because the order is doing all the expressive work.

  4. Could you sequence the sites yourself?
    Answer

    Roughly, yes. Upstream sounds small, close, and fast. Downstream sounds wide, slow, and inhabited. Geography is audible, and that is what allows the route to serve as the form.

  5. Where is the composer?
    Answer

    In the route and the choice of stations, never in the foreground. Lockwood decided which river, which stops, what order, and how long each lasts. Everything you hear was the river’s own doing.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: five stations of one continuous system, equal-length takes assembled in route order at a constant level. A solo-flip between your piece and REF should land on the same plain joins and the same audible sense of geography.
  • One fixed gain setting across all stations — the differences between stations should come from the places, not from adjusting the knob.
  • Joins plain and identical: hard cuts (butt cuts), or one short crossfade length used at every seam.
  • The subject audible at every station. If a station loses the thread, it is not on the route.

The piece in one row: one continuous system, five stations planned before you record, five equal-length takes at one gain setting, and an assembly whose every decision was made by the map.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the route

Phase focus: the reference and the route. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag a section of A Sound Map of the Hudson River in, and loop it.
  2. Audio Effects → Utility, trim to ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Choose your continuous system.
  4. Water through the home: the main or meter, the kitchen tap, the bathroom tap, the cistern refilling, the drain.
  5. One street: five corners walked in a line.
  6. The test: one substance or sound runs through all five stops.
  7. Draw the route: five stations in the order the system itself dictates, not the order that is most convenient for you.
  8. Write a predicted sound for each station, one line each — phase 4 grades these predictions.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Annea Lockwood — A Sound Map of the Hudson River (1982) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the route — as the smallest Live set that proves successive recording sites along one continuous system, presented in geographic order at steady level — no narration, no processing, the route as the form.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects one river from the mountains to the sea, station after station — every location sounds different, but you always recognize the same water running through all of them.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the route into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates successive recording sites along one continuous system, presented in geographic order at steady level — no narration, no processing, the route as the form, not just the surface of Annea Lockwood — A Sound Map of the Hudson River (1982).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one continuous system is chosen, and a five-station route exists on paper in the world’s own order, with a prediction per stop.

Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

The stations — matched takes, one gain

Phase focus: the stations — matched takes, one gain. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Set the gain once, at the loudest station — by the lab-001 ledger if you go through the M4. It does not move for the rest of the route.
  2. Fix a distance rule and keep it: one hand-span from each source, or recorder held mid-chest at each corner.
  3. The rule travels; the knob doesn’t.
  4. Record 60–90 seconds per station — the same length at every stop, decided now and written down.
  5. Walk the route in order, recording station by station, the system running at each (taps actually open, the street actually moving).
  6. Log each take, lab-006 style: station · distance · level.
  7. Note any station where the subject was hard to find — that note matters in phase 3.

Phase 3 · ≈ 15 min

The assembly — obey the map

Phase focus: the assembly — obey the map. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the five takes onto one track named MAP, fader at 0 dB, in route order — first station first, no exceptions.
  2. Constant level: do not adjust the clip gain between stations.
  3. If one station is louder than the others, that is because the route says so.
  4. Joins plain and identical: hard cuts, or one crossfade length (under a second) used at every seam.
  5. The joins should feel routine, not dramatic.
  6. As urges arrive — to reorder by interest, to cut the dull station short, to add a background layer — resist them.
  7. Write each resisted urge in the logbook. That list is part of the deliverable.
  8. Render the 5–7 minutes and listen end to end at the mark, no touching.

Five stations in route order at constant level with identical joins. The form was set by the plumbing or the street, not by taste. The logbook has a list of the edits you refused to make.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The assembly sounds like five unrelated filesSeams are all different, or levels have been rebalancedMake every join identical and set the gain back to constant. Uniform seams are what lets the subject connect all five stations.
One station feels painfully dull in sequenceIt probably is dull — but the route may still need itKeep it at full length at least once before deciding. Dull stations are the distances on a map.
Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

The shuffle test and the invariant

Phase focus: the shuffle test and the invariant. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the assembled piece end to end at the mark, no touching, route map in hand.
  2. Check each station against its phase-1 prediction and mark hits and misses.
  3. The shuffle test: play the five takes in a scrambled order — to yourself after a day’s gap, or to a second listener cold — and ask for the route order back.
  4. Record the guessed sequence.
  5. Write the invariant in one line: what stayed constant through all five stations — the pitch band of moving water, the street’s traffic voice.
  6. Name it precisely.
  7. A/B one of your joins against one of REF’s at matched loudness, judging only plainness: does yours stay as administrative, or does it perform?

A graded prediction list, a shuffle-test sequence that lands close to the true route, a one-line invariant, and an A/B that comes down to how little your joins do.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The shuffle test comes back randomStations too similar — the route is not audible in themSpread the stations further along the system and re-record the two most alike
No invariant can be namedThe subject got lost at capture — five rooms got recorded instead of one systemReturn to the stations where the subject is faint and retake closer to the flow
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Could a listener sequence your stations?
    Answer

    If the route is real, mostly yes. Small-close-fast naturally comes before wide-open-inhabited, on a street as on a river. The shuffle test should produce a guessed sequence that beats random chance. Where it doesn’t, the stations were too similar — spread the stops further apart. That is a capture decision, not an editing problem.

  • What stayed constant while everything changed?
    Answer

    The subject. Your one-line invariant is the proof you found it. On the record it is the water’s voice persisting from mountain stream to harbor. In your piece it is whatever connected the five stations into one system. That constant is what makes five separate recordings into one piece. Naming it precisely is the skill this record teaches.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2, and the route discipline feeds the one-minute miniature in Lab 006.
  • Carries into the lab: the étude asks for perspective and level decisions across captures. This record shows that an entire form can be built from those decisions alone.
