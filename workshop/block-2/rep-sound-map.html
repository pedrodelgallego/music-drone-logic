Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: form as geography

Copy one clear section of Annea Lockwood — A Sound Map of the Hudson River. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: successive recording sites along one continuous system, presented in geographic order at steady level - no narration, no processing, the route as the form. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone at one fixed gain for every station; mic into M4 input 1 only if all five stations reach the desk, which they usually don’t. DT 990 Pro for the edit
  • New set saved as REP-SOUND-MAP — no tempo; the route is the only structure this piece has
  • Field line: water and electronics keep their distance — hold the recorder, don’t balance it on the sink; on street corners, headphones stay off while traffic moves; monitor at a moderate fixed level
  • What you will make: a short replica of Annea Lockwood — A Sound Map of the Hudson River. Focus on this effect: one river from the mountains to the sea, station after station - everything local changes while the subject stays unmistakably the same water.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: successive recording sites along one continuous system, presented in geographic order at steady level - no narration, no processing, the route as the form.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Annea Lockwood — A Sound Map of the Hudson River. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What changes from site to site?
    Answer

    Almost everything local - the birds, the boats, the wind, the human traffic, how close the water sounds and how big the space around it feels. Each station is a complete scene with its own cast.

  2. What never changes?
    Answer

    The subject - it is recognizably the same water everywhere, from mountain stream to harbor, and the ear holds onto it through every scene change. One continuous thing threads the whole record.

  3. How are the sections joined?
    Answer

    Plainly - steady levels, no drama at the seams, no effect smoothing the transition. The joins are almost administrative, because the order is doing the expressive work and the edit refuses to compete with it.

  4. Could you sequence the sites yourself?
    Answer

    Roughly, yes - upstream sounds small, close, and fast; downstream sounds wide, slow, and inhabited. Geography is audible, which is what lets the route serve as form at all.

  5. Where is the composer?
    Answer

    In the route and the choice of stations - never in the foreground. Lockwood’s decisions are which river, which stops, what order, how long each stays; everything audible was the river’s own doing.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: five stations of one continuous system, matched-length takes assembled in route order at constant level — a solo-flip against REF lands on the same plain joins and the same audible geography.
  • One fixed gain across all stations — the differences must be the places, not the knob.
  • Joins plain and identical: butt cuts or one short even crossfade length used at every seam.
  • The subject audible at every station — a station that loses the subject is not on the route.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one continuous system, a route of five stations written before recording, five matched-length takes at one gain, and an assembly whose every decision was already made by the map.

The route as the form. Five stations along one continuous system, recorded in order and presented in order — everything local changes at each stop, while the subject runs through all of them unbroken.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the route

Phase focus: the reference and the route. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag a section of A Sound Map of the Hudson River in, and loop it.
  2. Live Audio Effects → Utility, trim to ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Live Choose your continuous system.
  4. Live Water through the home: the main or meter, the kitchen tap, the bathroom tap, the cistern refilling, the drain.
  5. Live One street: five corners walked in a line.
  6. Live The test — one substance or voice runs through all five stops.
  7. Live Draw the route: five stations in the order the system itself imposes, not the order of convenience.
  8. Logbook Write a predicted sound for each station, one line each — phase 4 grades these predictions.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one continuous system is chosen, and a five-station route exists on paper in the world’s own order, with a prediction per stop.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The five stations feel arbitrary, swappableThe system chosen is not actually continuousPick a system where something physically flows from stop 1 to stop 5 — pipes and streets qualify; “five sounds I like” does not
Two stations are the same sound in two roomsStops too close together on the systemSpread them — first and last stations should be as unlike as the system allows
Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

The stations — matched takes, one gain

Phase focus: the stations — matched takes, one gain. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Set the gain once, at the loudest station — by the lab-001 ledger if you go through the M4. It does not move for the rest of the route.
  2. Recorder Fix a distance rule and keep it: one hand-span from each source, or recorder held mid-chest at each corner.
  3. Recorder The rule travels; the knob doesn’t.
  4. Recorder Record 60–90 seconds per station — the same length at every stop, decided now and written down.
  5. Recorder Walk the route in order, recording station by station, the system running at each (taps actually open, the street actually moving).
  6. Recorder Log each take, lab-006 style: station · distance · level.
  7. Recorder Note any station where the subject was hard to find — that note matters in phase 3.
Expected result

Five matched-length takes at one gain and one distance rule, recorded in route order, each with a log line — five measurements of one system.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Stations differ wildly in levelNothing — that difference is informationKeep it; only retake if a station clipped, and then by moving back, not by touching the gain
The subject vanishes at one stationThe room or street won the perspectiveMove the mic until the system, not the surroundings, dominates — or strike the station from the route
Phase 3 · ≈ 15 min

The assembly — obey the map

Phase focus: the assembly — obey the map. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Import the five takes onto one track named MAP, fader at 0 dB, in route order — first station first, no exceptions.
  2. Live Constant level: no clip-gain balancing between stations.
  3. Live A loud station is loud because the route says so.
  4. Live Joins plain and identical: butt cuts, or one crossfade length (under a second) used at every seam.
  5. Live The seams should feel administrative.
  6. Live Resist the urges as they arrive — to reorder by interest, to trim the dull station, to lay a bed underneath.
  7. Live + Logbook Write each resisted urge in the logbook; the list is part of the deliverable.
  8. Live + Logbook Render the 5–7 minutes and listen end to end at the mark, no touching.
Expected result

Five stations in route order at constant level with identical joins — a piece whose form was decided by plumbing or pavement, and a logbook list of the edits you refused to make.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The assembly sounds like five unrelated filesSeams all different, or levels rebalancedMake every join identical and put the gain back to constant — uniformity at the seams is what lets the subject thread through
One station feels unbearably dull in sequenceIt probably is — and the route may still need itKeep it at full length once before deciding; dull stations are the distances on a map
Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

The shuffle test and the invariant

Phase focus: the shuffle test and the invariant. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the assembled piece end to end at the mark, no touching, route map in hand.
  2. Check each station against its phase-1 prediction and mark hits and misses.
  3. The shuffle test: play the five takes in a scrambled order — to yourself after a day’s gap, or to a second listener cold — and ask for the route order back.
  4. Record the guessed sequence.
  5. Logbook Write the invariant in one line: what stayed constant through all five stations — the pitch band of moving water, the street’s traffic voice.
  6. Logbook Name it precisely.
  7. Live A/B one of your joins against one of REF’s at matched loudness, judging only plainness: does yours stay as administrative, or does it perform?
Expected result

A graded prediction list, a shuffle-test sequence that lands close to the true route, a one-line invariant, and an A/B that comes down to how little your joins do.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The shuffle test comes back randomStations too similar — the route is not audible in themSpread the stations further along the system and re-record the two most alike
No invariant can be namedThe subject got lost at capture — five rooms got recorded instead of one systemReturn to the stations where the subject is faint and retake closer to the flow
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Could a listener sequence your stations?
    Answer

    If the route is real, mostly yes — small-close-fast orders itself before wide-open-inhabited, on a street as on a river, and the shuffle test’s guessed sequence should beat chance clearly. Where it doesn’t, the stations were too alike, which is a capture decision, not an edit problem: spread the stops. Relisten to the record jumping between an early and a late section and feel how confidently your ear files them upstream and downstream — that confidence is the target.

  • What stayed constant while everything changed?
    Answer

    The subject — and your one-line invariant is the proof you found it. On the record it is the water’s voice persisting from mountain stream to harbor; in your piece it is whatever threaded the five stations into one system. That invariant is what makes five recordings one piece, and naming it precisely is the skill this record teaches: form needs a constant before geography can compose anything. Relisten to your own five stations listening only for the invariant, and notice the route assembling itself around it.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2, and the route discipline seeds the one-minute miniature in Lab 006.
  • Carries into the lab: the étude asks for perspective and level decisions across captures — this record proves an entire form can be built from those decisions and nothing else.
