Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the orphaned voice

Copy one clear section of Burial — “Archangel”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a pitched, time-stretched vocal fragment orphaned from its source, placed by ear - not by grid - over a noise bed and a sparse, swung two-step drum skeleton. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: a mic — any mic — into M4 input 1, Digitakt II connected for microtiming work, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack. No mic on the desk? Record the phrase on your phone and drag the file in; the orphaning does not care how clean the capture was
  • New set saved as REP-ARCHANGEL, tempo 138 BPM — Burial’s home neighborhood; two-step lives between 130 and 140
  • You will hear your own processed voice on repeat for an hour — phones at the lab-001 mark, and mute the monitor path while the mic is live so the recording stays howl-free
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into the stretching and the placing, not into more layers
  • What you will make: a short replica of Burial — “Archangel”. Focus on this effect: a voice that is nobody’s carrying an ache that is everybody’s, drifting slightly off the beat over crackle that sits like weather.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a pitched, time-stretched vocal fragment orphaned from its source, placed by ear - not by grid - over a noise bed and a sparse, swung two-step drum skeleton.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Burial — “Archangel”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Is the singer a man or a woman?
    Answer

    You hesitated - and the hesitation is the design. The pitching and regeneration have cut the voice loose from its body: it sits in a register no untreated throat produces, neither one thing nor the other.

  2. What survived the processing?
    Answer

    The melody and the ache - not the fidelity, not the identity. Hum the vocal line back right now: it comes out intact, even though you could not say who sang it. The tune passed through every generation untouched while the person dissolved.

  3. Where do the drum hits land relative to a strict grid?
    Answer

    Slightly off - and not by one consistent amount. The two-step skeleton is swung and hand-placed: hits arrive a touch late or early, the way a played thing does and a quantized thing never does.

  4. What is the crackle doing?
    Answer

    It is weather, not a fault. The crackle and hiss sit around the voice - a continuous bed that puts every element in the same worn room.

  5. What makes the voice sound so far away?
    Answer

    Distance that no reverb alone produces - the voice sounds like a recording of a recording. Reverb puts a sound in a room; generational blur puts it in the past.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same displacement — a voice nobody could claim, hits that breathe against the grid, crackle holding it all in one room — at matched loudness.
  • Four elements and nothing else: one orphaned vocal phrase, one noise bed, kick and snare with a single hat. No pads, no bass yet — bareness is what makes the displacement legible.
  • The voice must stop being yours before it goes in the arrangement — if a friend could name the singer, keep processing.
  • Nothing snaps to the grid: the drums are placed by ear or nudged by microtiming, and the vocal phrases land where your hand drops them.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference, the bed, and one clean phrase

Phase focus: the reference, the bed, and one clean phrase. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Archangel” in, and loop the first vocal minute.
  2. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility and trim so it peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Live ⌘ T, rename BED — drag in any vinyl-crackle sample, or print two minutes of very low-level noise (an unplugged input’s floor, rain from a window, the M4 trim raised on silence).
  4. Live Loop it, fader so it just reads on the meter, ≈ −40 dBFS.
  5. Live ⌘ T, rename VOICE-RAW, Ext. In 1, arm it.
  6. Live Mic on M4 input 1, monitoring muted, gain set so your held notes peak ≈ −12 dBFS.
  7. Live Sing one plain phrase — four to eight seconds, any words, a real melody with a leap in it.
  8. Live Sing it sincerely; irony will not survive the processing, but feeling will.
  9. Live Print three takes and keep the most committed one.
  10. Logbook Write in the logbook one line: what the phrase is and what it means to you.
  11. Logbook Phase 4 checks whether that meaning is still attached.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, a continuous crackle bed runs underneath everything at ≈ −40 dBFS, and one committed, clean vocal phrase sits printed on its own track.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A thin whine or howl builds while the mic is armedThe mic is hearing the phones or monitors — a feedback path is openMute the track’s monitoring while recording; judge takes on playback only
The crackle reads as an event, not a bedLevel too high, or the sample has its own loud popsDrop it until it disappears when other elements play and is missed when muted
Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

Orphan the voice — pitch and stretch until the person leaves

Phase focus: orphan the voice — pitch and stretch until the person leaves. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Duplicate the kept take to a new track named VOICE.
  2. Live Set the clip to Warp → Complex Pro.
  3. Live Transpose in whole semitones and listen after each move: start at +4, then try +5, +7, and −5 for comparison.
  4. Live You are listening for the moment the voice stops being yours and is not yet a cartoon — usually between +3 and +7.
  5. Live Stretch it: drag the clip’s end so it plays at roughly 115–130% of its length.
  6. Live The consonants should soften and the vowels should start to glow.
  7. Live Too far and the words dissolve entirely — the line still has to ache.
  8. Live The test, enforced without mercy: play the result and ask “could anyone who knows me say this is me?” If yes, push the transpose one more semitone or the stretch ten more percent.
  9. Live If the melody is gone too, you went one step too far — back up once.
  10. Live Cut the phrase into two or three shorter fragments — a held vowel, the leap, the ending — and consolidate each (⌘ J).
  11. Live These fragments are your vocabulary; the full phrase appears rarely.
Expected result

A voice in a register no untreated throat produces, words half-dissolved, melody fully intact — plus two or three cut fragments of it. Played to a friend, it is recognizably a person and recognizably not you.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The pitched voice sounds comic — chipmunk, not angelTranspose too far with formants tracking up, or the stretch is too short to blur the edgesCome back to +4/+5 and add stretch instead; the blur does what more semitones cannot
It is still obviously youConsonants surviving — identity rides on the attacksMore stretch, or low-pass the top gently (EQ Eight, ≈ 6 kHz shelf down); the sibilants carry your name
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

The skeleton — two-step placed by ear

Phase focus: the skeleton — two-step placed by ear. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Digitakt II Press [TEMPO] and set the pattern to 138 BPM, then press [RECORD] and place kick trigs on step 1 and step 11 — not step 9.
  2. Digitakt II landing the second kick off the half-bar is what makes it two-step — snare on steps 5 and 13, one closed hat scattered sparsely.
  3. Digitakt II Now un-machine it: hold each snare step and turn microtiming — push the snares +8/384 to +16/384 late, drag one hat early.
  4. Live Working in Live instead?
  5. Live Turn the grid off (⌘ 4) and drag the hits by hand; same lesson, different fingers.
  6. Digitakt II + Live Press [TEMPO], turn DATA ENTRY knob D to start around 55–58%, then adjust by feel against REF, not by the number.
  7. Digitakt II + Live Record the pattern into Live through the established path, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, onto a track named SKELETON.
  8. Digitakt II + Live Keep it sparse: if every step is busy, two-step collapses into drum and bass.
  9. Live A/B the groove against the record’s drums: tap along to both.
  10. Live Yours should make your shoulders move the same direction.
Expected result

A sparse, swung two-step pattern printed at ≈ −12 dBFS, with snares audibly breathing behind the grid — a skeleton that sways rather than marches.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The groove marches — correct but stiffEverything still on the grid; microtiming moves too small to hearDouble the nudges; the late snare should be almost uncomfortable, then settle
The pattern feels like it is falling apartToo many hits moved, or moved in conflicting directionsReturn the kick to the grid — anchor one element dead-on and let the snares and hats do the leaning
Phase 4 · ≈ 25 min

Assemble the displacement, then test what holds it together

Phase focus: assemble the displacement, then test what holds it together. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Arrange two minutes: BED runs unbroken end to end; SKELETON enters after one bar of bed alone;
  2. the VOICE fragments enter by the third bar.
  3. Live Place every vocal fragment by hand with the grid off (⌘ 4).
  4. Live Drag each one until it feels like an answer to the drums, not an agreement with them — usually a breath late.
  5. Live If a fragment locks onto a downbeat, move it.
  6. Live Repeat fragments unevenly: the held vowel might come four times, the full phrase once.
  7. Live The record dwells; it does not chorus.
  8. Live Balance: voice just in front, drums behind it, bed underneath everything — mix peaking ≈ −12 dBFS.
  9. Live Then play the two minutes end to end without touching anything.
  10. Live The mute test: solo-flip against REF, then mute BED for eight bars and unmute it.
  11. Logbook Write one logbook line on what left the room when the crackle did.
Expected result

Two minutes where a nobody’s voice drifts against a swaying skeleton inside one shared weather — and a logbook line about what the bed was secretly doing.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The voice sounds pasted on top of the trackIt is the only element without the bed’s room — too clean against the weatherSend a touch of the voice to a short dark reverb, or duck it 1–2 dB; it should sit in the crackle, not on it
The arrangement feels random rather than displacedFragments placed without listening — off-grid is not the same as anywhereRe-place each fragment while the loop plays, moving it until it answers the snare; keep only placements you can defend
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Is the voice still anybody’s?
    Answer

    It should not be. Play your two minutes to someone who knows your speaking voice — or imagine doing it honestly — and ask whether they could name the singer, or even commit to a sex. If they can, the orphaning is incomplete: go back to phase 2 and push one more semitone or ten more percent of stretch. The melody you wrote in the phase 1 logbook line should still be hummable; the meaning you wrote should now feel like it belongs to the listener, not to you. That transfer is the record’s whole move.

  • Turn the crackle bed off — what dies?
    Answer

    The room dies. With the bed muted, the voice and the drums fall back into being separate files on separate tracks — the voice floats free as an obvious sample, the hits sit on a black silence that makes their off-grid placement read as error instead of sway. The crackle was doing the gluing: one continuous noise that every element shares is heard as one continuous place that every element occupies. Relisten to the record imagining its surface noise stripped out, and notice you are imagining a worse, smaller song.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 007 — what pitch and stretch did to your voice is what generations do to any source: the identity goes first and the shape goes last. Choosing how far to push a pass is choosing what survives it — that is the lab’s “choose your generation’s parents,” heard from inside one voice.
