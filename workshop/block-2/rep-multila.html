Replicate: dub smeared past the bar line

Copy one clear section of Vladislav Delay — Multila. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches how the record works: a few sounds fed through long, unpredictable, self-regenerating delays (delays that keep feeding back into themselves). Fragments of the resulting wash then re-enter against the pulse. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordVladislav Delay — Multila (2000, Chain Reaction)
The effectchords and clicks dissolved into a wash that ignores the grid, while the groove is kept by feel — dub with its bar line smeared away

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak line out → Digitakt II inputs L/R for one sampling job, then the Digitakt II works alone — main out → M4 in 3/4 for the final print, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack
  • New Digitakt II project and a Live set saved as REP-DELAY, both at 120 BPM — the grid you are about to smear must exist first
  • High delay feedback can swell without warning — phones at the lab-001 mark, one hand near the delay’s volume on every feedback change, and back the feedback off before stopping the sequencer
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into the smear and the re-placing, not into more material
  • What you will make: a short replica of Vladislav Delay — Multila. Focus on this effect: chords and clicks dissolved into a wash that ignores the grid, while the groove is kept by feel - dub with its bar line smeared away.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: sparse material fed through long, irregular, regenerating delays; fragments of the resulting wash re-entering against the pulse.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Vladislav Delay — Multila. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Where is “one”?
    Answer

    Just barely findable — by feel, not by hearing a clear downbeat. Your body settles into a count, but your ear keeps losing the evidence for it.

  2. How long is the loop?
    Answer

    You cannot tell — and that is deliberate. The echoes keep feeding back past the loop’s end, so each repetition already overlaps the last one’s tail. The seam never falls in the same place twice.

  3. What is the source material before the delays touch it?
    Answer

    Almost nothing: a chord stab, some clicks and crackle, a hiss of room. The density you hear is one or two events multiplied by the delay repeating them. The delay is doing the work; the pattern is just a seed.

  4. Do the echoes land on the grid?
    Answer

    No — they land somewhere new each time, cycling against the pulse instead of reinforcing it. A delay set to a grid-aligned time stacks echoes on the beat and becomes invisible. This one scatters them between the beats, and that scattering is the texture.

  5. Is this beatless music?
    Answer

    No — the pulse is still there inside the wash. This is dance music heard through fog, not ambient with percussion added. The groove is held by feel: a few anchor clicks stay dry and untouched while everything around them drifts.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: when you solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness, both should have the same quality — a wash that ignores the grid, with a pulse you can only find by feel.
  • The seed stays sparse: one chord stab and a few clicks in two bars — the delay supplies the density.
  • The delay time must not divide the bar evenly — the echoes have to land somewhere new each repeat.
  • At least one anchor sound stays dry and on the grid throughout; the feel needs something to hold.

How the piece works: a two-bar seed pattern goes into a long delay set to an off-grid time with high feedback (so echoes keep returning). You then record (resample) a chunk of that wash, and place fragments of the frozen wash back onto the grid.

Digitakt II · 2-bar seed · stab + clicks → delay · off-grid time · high feedback → internal resample · the wash frozen → fragments re-placed on the grid · 2–3 min

Three snapshots showing what the off-grid delay does to the bar. The seed sits on the grid. The echoes land somewhere new each repeat until the bar line blurs. Then frozen pieces of that wash are placed back on the grid — and “one” returns, but only findable by feel.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the grid you will smear

Phase focus: the reference and the grid you will smear. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. In Live: ⌘ T, rename REF (⌘ R), drag in a track from Multila, loop three or four characteristic minutes, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Tap along to REF and write in the logbook where you believe “one” is, and how confident you are, in one line.
  3. Start a new Digitakt II project, press [TEMPO] and set 120 BPM, then set the pattern length to 2 bars: 32 steps at 16th resolution.
  4. Sample one chord from the MicroFreak through the Digitakt II’s inputs L/R: a dark minor chord, Paraphonic on, filter half closed, struck short — one second is plenty.
  5. Trim it to a stab.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Vladislav Delay — Multila (2000, Chain Reaction) and use Drum Rack, MIDI clips, Groove Pool, and clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the grid you will smear — as the smallest Live set that proves sparse material fed through long, irregular, regenerating delays; fragments of the resulting wash re-entering against the pulse.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects chords and clicks dissolved into a wash that ignores the grid, while the groove is kept by feel — dub with its bar line smeared away.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the grid you will smear into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates sparse material fed through long, irregular, regenerating delays; fragments of the resulting wash re-entering against the pulse, not just the surface of Vladislav Delay — Multila (2000, Chain Reaction).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS with your guessed “one” on paper. The Digitakt II holds a two-bar grid and one dark stab — everything in order before you start blurring it.

Phase 2 · ≈ 20 min

The seed — two bars of almost nothing

Phase focus: the seed — two bars of almost nothing. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. T1 — the stab: one trig on the one of bar 1, one syncopated trig late in bar 2 (step 27 or 28 of 32 — let your ear pick between them).
  2. T2 — clicks: a rim, click or short tick from the factory pool, three or four trigs scattered across the two bars, low level, no two on adjacent steps.
  3. T3 — the anchor: one click on every quarter note, dry forever.
  4. This track never touches the delay; it is the pulse the feel will hold onto.
  5. Balance the three tracks on the Digitakt II until the anchor is quietest, then the clicks, then the stab.
  6. Logbook: total trig count — it should be under a dozen.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

The smear — a delay that refuses the bar

Phase focus: the smear — a delay that refuses the bar. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Open the Digitakt II’s delay.
  2. Set TIME to 40 — five sixteenths of the bar, ≈ 625 ms at 120 BPM;
  3. each echo lands a sixteenth later in the count than the last, cycling through the whole bar before repeating.
  4. (Alternative flavor: TIME 25, ≈ 390 ms — just past the dotted eighth, never on the grid at all.)
  5. Feedback high — start around halfway and push until a stab’s tail audibly outlives the two-bar pattern, then stop.
  6. One hand stays near the delay volume; this setting can swell.
  7. If the delay offers tone or width controls, darken the repeats slightly — each echo a little duller than the last, the dub habit.
  8. Send T1 hard into the delay and one of T2’s clicks about half as hard.
  9. T3 sends nothing — the anchor stays dry.
  10. Let it run for two minutes and listen for the moment you lose the bar.
  11. Logbook, one line: how many pattern cycles that took.

Phase 4 · ≈ 25 min

Freeze the wash, place the fragments

Phase focus: freeze the wash, place the fragments. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. With the pattern running and the wash at its best, internally resample four bars of the Digitakt II’s output into a new sample slot — two full pattern cycles of pure smear.
  2. Load the resample onto T4.
  3. Audition through it and mark two or three good moments — a thick overlap, a darkening tail, an accent the delay happened to create.
  4. Place fragments against the original grid: one trig per chosen moment, some on the quarter notes, at least one deliberately off.
  5. Now a frozen piece of the wash plays in time — a captured accident, placed on purpose.
  6. Pull T1’s send and level back until the live stab is a ghost;
  7. let the resampled fragments carry the body.
  8. Keep the dry anchor exactly as it was.
  9. Arrange 2–3 minutes with mutes and a second pattern variation, and record the Digitakt II’s main out through M4 in 3/4 into Live at ≈ −12 dBFS.
  10. A/B against REF at matched loudness.

Expected result

A 2–3 minute recording where live delay and frozen fragments alternate over a pulse you find by feel. The bar line gets lost and quietly returns.

If it fails

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your print at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Where is “one” — can you still find the bar line?
    Answer

    Barely, and by feel — like the record. Tap along to your print the way you tapped along to REF in phase 1. You should be able to hold a count, but the evidence for it keeps dissolving. If “one” is instantly obvious, the delay time landed on a grid division or the anchor is too loud. If “one” has vanished completely, you smeared the anchor too — this is dance music heard through fog, not just fog. Relisten to the record and notice it stays on that same edge throughout.

  • What did resampling the wash let you do that the live delay couldn’t?
    Answer

    Freeze a moment of the wash and reuse it. The live delay is one-way — its best overlap happens once and fades. Resampling captures that moment and gives it a fixed position: you placed it on the grid, repeated it, made it answer itself two bars later. That is the difference between an effect and a source. It is Lab 007’s main idea in one move: the system’s output became its input, and composition happened in between. Relisten to your print and find one fragment that recurs; the live wash could never have guaranteed that.
