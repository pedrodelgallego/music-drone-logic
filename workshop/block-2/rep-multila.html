Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: dub smeared past the bar line

Copy one clear section of Vladislav Delay — Multila. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: sparse material fed through long, irregular, regenerating delays; fragments of the resulting wash re-entering against the pulse. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak line out → Digitakt II inputs L/R for one sampling job, then the Digitakt II works alone — main out → M4 in 3/4 for the final print, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack
  • New Digitakt II project and a Live set saved as REP-DELAY, both at 120 BPM — the grid you are about to smear must exist first
  • High delay feedback can swell without warning — phones at the lab-001 mark, one hand near the delay’s volume on every feedback change, and back the feedback off before stopping the sequencer
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into the smear and the re-placing, not into more material
  • What you will make: a short replica of Vladislav Delay — Multila. Focus on this effect: chords and clicks dissolved into a wash that ignores the grid, while the groove is kept by feel - dub with its bar line smeared away.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: sparse material fed through long, irregular, regenerating delays; fragments of the resulting wash re-entering against the pulse.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Vladislav Delay — Multila. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Where is “one”?
    Answer

    Findable - barely, and by feel rather than by hearing a downbeat announced. Your body settles into a count while your ear keeps losing the evidence for it.

  2. How long is the loop?
    Answer

    You cannot tell - and that is engineered. The echoes regenerate past the loop’s edges, so every repetition arrives already overlapped by the last one’s tail, and the seam never falls in the same place twice to the ear.

  3. What is the source material before the delays touch it?
    Answer

    Almost nothing: a chord stab, some clicks and crackle, a hiss of room. The density you hear is one or two events multiplied by regeneration - the delay is the orchestra, the pattern is a seed.

  4. Do the echoes land on the grid?
    Answer

    No - they land somewhere new each time, cycling against the pulse instead of reinforcing it. A grid-aligned delay stacks echoes on top of the beat and disappears; this one strews them between the beats, and that strewing is the texture.

  5. Is this beatless music?
    Answer

    No - the pulse persists inside the wash; this is dance music heard through fog, not ambient with percussion. The groove is kept by feel: a few anchor clicks survive untouched while everything around them drifts.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same lost-and-found bar line — a wash that ignores the grid, a pulse you find by feel, at matched loudness.
  • The seed stays sparse: one chord stab and a few clicks in two bars — the delay supplies the density.
  • The delay time must not divide the bar evenly — the echoes have to land somewhere new each repeat.
  • At least one anchor sound stays dry and on the grid throughout; the feel needs something to hold.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a two-bar seed pattern, a long off-grid regenerating delay that smears it, an internal resample that freezes bars of the wash, and fragments of that freeze placed back against the original grid.

Digitakt II · 2-bar seed · stab + clicks → delay · off-grid time · high feedback → internal resample · the wash frozen → fragments re-placed on the grid · 2–3 min

What the off-grid delay does to the bar, drawn as three snapshots. The seed sits on the grid; the echoes land somewhere new each repeat until the bar line dissolves; then frozen fragments of that wash are placed back against the grid — and “one” returns, findable only by feel.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the grid you will smear

Phase focus: the reference and the grid you will smear. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live In Live: ⌘ T, rename REF (⌘ R), drag in a track from Multila, loop three or four characteristic minutes, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Live + Logbook Tap along to REF and write in the logbook where you believe “one” is, and how confident you are, in one line.
  3. Digitakt II Start a new Digitakt II project, press [TEMPO] and set 120 BPM, then set the pattern length to 2 bars: 32 steps at 16th resolution.
  4. Digitakt II + MicroFreak Sample one chord from the MicroFreak through the Digitakt II’s inputs L/R: a dark minor chord, Paraphonic on, filter half closed, struck short — one second is plenty.
  5. Digitakt II + MicroFreak Trim it to a stab.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS with your guessed “one” on paper, and the Digitakt II holds a two-bar grid and one dark stab — the straight world, ready to be bent.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The sampled stab is quiet or one-sidedInput level low, or only one input cable seatedSet the Digitakt II’s input level on a test strike; check both L and R land
The stab rings too long to be a stabChord held rather than struck, or release too longShorten the MicroFreak’s release and strike staccato; re-sample
Phase 2 · ≈ 20 min

The seed — two bars of almost nothing

Phase focus: the seed — two bars of almost nothing. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. T1 — the stab: one trig on the one of bar 1, one syncopated trig late in bar 2 (step 27 or 28 of 32 — let your ear pick between them).
  2. T2 — clicks: a rim, click or short tick from the factory pool, three or four trigs scattered across the two bars, low level, no two on adjacent steps.
  3. T3 — the anchor: one click on every quarter note, dry forever.
  4. This track never touches the delay; it is the pulse the feel will hold onto.
  5. Digitakt II Balance the three tracks on the Digitakt II until the anchor is quietest, then the clicks, then the stab.
  6. Logbook Logbook: total trig count — it should be under a dozen.
Expected result

Two dry bars of almost nothing — a stab twice, a few clicks, a quiet quarter-note anchor — boring on purpose, the way a seed is.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The pattern already feels busy before any delayToo many trigs — the multiplication will bury everythingDelete until under a dozen trigs total; the delay is the orchestra
The anchor dominates the patternIts level set like a metronome you want to hearPull it down until you barely notice it; it works underground
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

The smear — a delay that refuses the bar

Phase focus: the smear — a delay that refuses the bar. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Digitakt II Open the Digitakt II’s delay.
  2. Digitakt II Set TIME to 40 — five sixteenths of the bar, ≈ 625 ms at 120 BPM;
  3. Digitakt II each echo lands a sixteenth later in the count than the last, cycling through the whole bar before repeating.
  4. Digitakt II (Alternative flavor: TIME 25, ≈ 390 ms — just past the dotted eighth, never on the grid at all.)
  5. Digitakt II + Live Feedback high — start around halfway and push until a stab’s tail audibly outlives the two-bar pattern, then stop.
  6. Digitakt II + Live One hand stays near the delay volume; this setting can swell.
  7. Digitakt II + Live If the delay offers tone or width controls, darken the repeats slightly — each echo a little duller than the last, the dub habit.
  8. Live Send T1 hard into the delay and one of T2’s clicks about half as hard.
  9. Live T3 sends nothing — the anchor stays dry.
  10. Live Let it run for two minutes and listen for the moment you lose the bar.
  11. Digitakt II + Logbook Logbook, one line: how many pattern cycles that took.
Expected result

A running wash in which the stab’s echoes strew themselves across the count, the loop’s seam disappears under its own tails, and only the quiet anchor still knows where “one” is.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The echoes reinforce the beat instead of dissolving itDelay time divides the bar evenly — a grid multipleMove TIME off the musical divisions — 40 or 25, not 32, 48 or 64
The wash swells toward self-oscillationFeedback past the regeneration tipping pointBack the feedback off a notch and pull the delay volume down while it settles
Phase 4 · ≈ 25 min

Freeze the wash, place the fragments

Phase focus: freeze the wash, place the fragments. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Digitakt II With the pattern running and the wash at its best, internally resample four bars of the Digitakt II’s output into a new sample slot — two full pattern cycles of pure smear.
  2. Digitakt II Load the resample onto T4.
  3. Digitakt II Audition through it and set start points at two or three beautiful moments — a thick overlap, a darkening tail, an in-between accent the delay invented.
  4. Digitakt II Place fragments against the original grid: one trig per chosen moment, some on the quarter notes, at least one deliberately off.
  5. Digitakt II The smear is now playing in time — a frozen accident, quoted on purpose.
  6. Live Pull T1’s send and level back until the live stab is a ghost;
  7. Live let the resampled fragments carry the body.
  8. Live Keep the dry anchor exactly as it was.
  9. Digitakt II + Live Arrange 2–3 minutes with mutes and a second pattern variation, and record the Digitakt II’s main out through M4 in 3/4 into Live at ≈ −12 dBFS.
  10. Live A/B against REF at matched loudness.
Expected result

A 2–3 minute print in which the wash both drifts and recurs — live smear and frozen smear interleaved over a pulse you find by feel, the bar line lost and quietly returned.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The resampled fragments double the live delay into mudBoth running at full strength — the wash is being played twicePull the live send back to a ghost; the fragments are the body now
The piece is a wash with no groove at allThe anchor got muted or buried in the rebalanceRestore the dry quarter-note click — barely audible is enough; the feel holds onto it
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your print at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Where is “one” — can you still find the bar line?
    Answer

    Barely, and by feel — like the record. Tap along to your print the way you tapped along to REF in phase 1: you should be able to hold a count whose evidence keeps dissolving. If “one” is instantly obvious, the delay time crept onto a grid division or the anchor is too loud; if “one” is gone entirely, you smeared the anchor too and lost the dub — this is dance music in fog, not fog. Relisten to the record and notice it walks the same edge for an hour.

  • What did resampling the wash let you do that the live delay couldn’t?
    Answer

    Freeze a moment of smear and reuse it. The live delay is irreversible weather — its best overlap happens once and decays; the resample turns that moment into material with an address: you placed it on the grid, repeated it, made it answer itself two bars later. That is the difference between an effect and a source, and it is Lab 007’s whole argument in one move — the system’s output became its input, and composition happened in between. Relisten to your print and point to one fragment that recurs; the live wash never could have promised that.
