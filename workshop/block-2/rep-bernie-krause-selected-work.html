Replicate: the niche hypothesis as arrangement

Steal one idea from Bernie Krause's The Great Animal Orchestra: in a healthy natural soundscape, each creature's voice sits in its own frequency band or its own time slot, so the mix is clear without anyone blocking anyone else. Give each of your parts a non-overlapping spectral (frequency) or temporal (time) niche, build a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

You are not copying Krause's frogs, insects, or rainforest. You are copying the mechanism: the acoustic niche hypothesis — the idea that in a healthy habitat, each voice claims its own gap in frequency or time, so the whole mix stays clear without anyone fighting over the same space. You plan those gaps before you play, like drawing a map in advance, instead of reaching for EQ to fix a muddy mix afterward. The niche plan is the form.

Before you start
  • Live's Spectrum device ready to drop on the master — this is your spectrogram, the tool that lets you literally see the niches
  • MicroFreak on Live's input for the pitched / mid and high voices
  • Digitakt II ready for the low and percussive voices — sub, kick, and a high transient layer on separate tracks
  • A blank sheet ruled into frequency bands (sub / low / low-mid / mid / high-mid / air) down one side and bars across the top — the niche map is drawn before any sound is made
  • What you will make: a 60–90 second loop of four or five voices that all play together yet stay individually audible, because each was assigned a niche before it was recorded. Focus on this effect: density without mud, and no EQ used to rescue anything after the fact.
  • What you will turn off: corrective EQ, the urge to add a sixth part, and any voice that wants the same band as one already placed.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: clarity is an emergent property of niche assignment, not of mixing afterward.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about two minutes of a dense Krause biophony — a dawn chorus or rainforest bed. Put Live's Spectrum on the playback so you can watch the bands fill. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Pick one busy moment. How many distinct voices can you count, and do any two of them sit in the same frequency band?
    Answer

    You can usually count far more than you expect — and almost no two share a band. On the Spectrum you see it: a low rumble fills the sub, insects pack a narrow buzz in the high-mids, a bird call sits in the mids, frogs occupy the low-mids. Each band is claimed by one voice, which is exactly why you can hear each one. That spacing is the niche hypothesis made visible.

  2. When two creatures DO share a frequency band, what keeps them from masking each other?
    Answer

    Time. They take turns. When frequency can't separate them, the clock does — one calls in the gap left by the other, so the two voices in the same band are never sounding at the same moment. That time-sharing — call-and-response — is the second tool: when you can't carve out frequency space, you carve out time instead.

  3. Is there any audible EQ "fixing" — anything notched or scooped to make room?
    Answer

    None. Nobody mixed the rainforest. The clarity came from each voice claiming its own band or time slot — it is built into the arrangement, not applied after the fact. That is the lesson: if you assign niches up front, you never need corrective EQ because there is nothing to correct.

  4. If you erased the highest band entirely, how much of the sense of "a whole orchestra" would survive?
    Answer

    Surprisingly little — and that tells you each band is doing real work. The fullness isn't overlap; every niche does something unique, so removing one leaves an audible gap rather than just a quieter mix. That is proof the parts are spaced, not piled on top of each other. In a stacked mix you can lose a layer and barely notice. In a niched mix, every voice has its own job.

Now build the partition you just described. Do not build from memory of the animals — build from the structure: who owns which band, and who shares a band by taking turns.

  • Sound target: four or five voices that play simultaneously and stay legible, with the Spectrum showing each one occupying a band the others leave clear.
  • Frequency voices: a sub voice (Digitakt II), a low-mid body, a mid call (MicroFreak), and an air/high-mid texture — each in its own band.
  • Time-shared pair: two voices that want the same band, made to interleave so they never sound at once.
  • The form: the niche map itself — the partition of the spectrum and the clock — and nothing rescued by EQ afterward.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Draw the spectrogram before you play

Phase focus: assign every voice a niche on paper first, so the mix is designed, not repaired. Compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track and gain-match it with a Utility so A/Bs are honest. Drop Spectrum on the master and watch how the REF partitions the bands.
  2. Rule the niche map: six rows — sub, low, low-mid, mid, high-mid, air — and assign exactly one voice per band. Write the instrument that will own each band before you make a single sound.
  3. Mark one band as shared: pick a band where two voices both want to live, and write "interleave" next to it. Those two will take turns in time instead of stacking.
  4. The map is your score. Every later step is just realising a row of it and checking on the Spectrum that the band you claimed is the band you filled.

Phase 3 — ~25 minutes

Carve the shared band in time

Phase focus: take the one band two voices both want and split it in time, so the two never sound at once — temporal interleaving, then record the whole loop. This is the piece.

Do this
  1. Score the call-and-response: for the shared band, write which voice sounds on which beats — e.g. voice A on 1 & 3, voice B in the gaps on 2 & 4. The two split the bar so their moments never collide.
  2. Sequence the interleave: place each voice's trigs only in its allotted slots. Press [PLAY] and listen: where one stops, the other answers, and the shared band reads as two distinct voices because they never overlap in time.
  3. Confirm with the spectrogram in motion: watch the shared band on the Spectrum — it should pulse between the two voices, lit by one then the other, never both at once.
  4. Record the full loop in one pass — all frequency-niched voices plus the time-shared pair. No EQ on the master, no corrective moves. The clarity must come entirely from the niche map.

Verify against the record

A/B your loop against a dense stretch of Krause's biophony — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness, Spectrum running on both. Then answer two questions in writing.

Can you pick out every voice in yours the way you can in theirs — with no corrective EQ in the chain?

Answer

If you can, the niche map did the work: clarity came from how the voices were spaced, not from anything added afterward. If two voices blur together, the fault is a band that was assigned to two stacked sounds that should have been moved to separate bands or made to take turns in time. Do not reach for EQ to fix it — that replaces the mechanism with the very thing it makes unnecessary. Go back to the map and re-partition.

Does the density survive without anyone masking anyone — does the mix stay legible at full volume?

Answer

It should. That is the niche hypothesis's whole claim: when every voice owns its own frequency band or time slot, you can layer them up and nothing covers anything else, even at full volume. If yours collapses into mud as voices accumulate, two of them are competing for the same space — the partition has a gap. In Krause's recordings the density never costs legibility, because the spacing was decided before any sound was made. That is exactly what you were testing.

  • Name the one band assignment that did the most work — the niche that made the whole mix click into legibility.
