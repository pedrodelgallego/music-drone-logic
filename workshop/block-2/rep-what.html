Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: spectrum as melody

Copy one clear section of Folke Rabe — What??. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a static, harmonically rich fundamental whose individual overtones are selectively reinforced over time, so the spectrum - not the pitch - carries the tune. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged; the Digitakt II rests this lesson
  • “What??” was assembled in a tape studio you cannot visit — you are replicating the system: a rich static tone plus a narrow moving resonance. EQ Eight with one high-Q band is your rig’s honest analogue of that studio’s filters; the MicroFreak’s resonant filter is the hardware alternative
  • New set saved as REP-WHAT — no tempo; the only timing is how long each overtone stays lit
  • A +12 dB resonant peak can bite without warning as it crosses sensitive frequencies — phones at the lab-001 mark or lower, sweeps moved slowly, a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes — half of it goes into one printed tone and one slowly moving band
  • What you will make: a short replica of Folke Rabe — What??. Focus on this effect: a melody happening inside a tone that never changes pitch - high faint tones surfacing one after another, none of them new arrivals.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a static, harmonically rich fundamental whose individual overtones are selectively reinforced over time, so the spectrum - not the pitch - carries the tune.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Folke Rabe — What??. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. When do you first notice the high, faint tones above the fundamental?
    Answer

    Minutes in, usually - and the timestamp says more about your attention than about the record. The tones do not fade in at that moment; your ear arrives at them.

  2. Are those high tones new arrivals, or were they always there?
    Answer

    Always there. Every harmonic you will ever hear in this piece is present from the first second, quietly, inside the fundamental’s spectrum - the holding and the reinforcement make them audible, one at a time.

  3. Does the fundamental ever move?
    Answer

    No. The pitch you would sing along to is the same at minute one and minute twenty - everything that feels like motion happens above it.

  4. If the pitch never changes, what carries the melody?
    Answer

    The spectrum. Which overtone is forward at any moment is the melodic information - a tune played not by changing the note but by changing which part of the note you are given.

  5. How does your attention move as the minutes pass?
    Answer

    Upward. The ear, given nothing new at the bottom, climbs - from the fundamental to the low harmonics to the glassy ones near the top - and the piece is built to escort exactly that climb.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a piece in which a listener hears a melody of surfacing overtones while a tuner and a spectrum analyzer prove the fundamental never moved — both things true at once, as on the record.
  • One printed tone and one narrow EQ band — no second voice, no new layer, nothing entering that was not in the tone from second one.
  • The band dwells at each harmonic — exact multiples of the fundamental, computed on paper, not found by ear alone.
  • The order in which overtones are lit is the melody: compose the order before you automate it.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one harmonically rich low tone printed for six minutes, a single high-Q EQ band boosted hard and walked slowly along the harmonic series — dwelling at each overtone in a composed order — while a tuner and a spectrum view stand witness that the fundamental never moves.

MicroFreak · SawX · one low note · filter open → printed tone · 6 min · TONE → EQ Eight · one bell · high Q · walked along f, 2f, 3f… → Spectrum + Tuner · the fundamental never moves

The same spectrum at three moments. Every bar is present in every panel — the tall fundamental and its family of overtones — and the only thing that changes is which harmonic the narrow band is lighting. Played in time, the sequence of lit bars is the melody.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the surfacing order

Phase focus: the reference and the surfacing order. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “What??” into it, and loop the first five minutes.
  2. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Logbook Listen once through with a pen: every time a new overtone surfaces, write a timestamp and a rough placement — “low, near the fundamental,” “an octave-and-a-fifth up,” “glassy, very high.” This list is the record’s melody, transcribed.
  4. Logbook Hum the fundamental at the start and end of the loop; confirm on paper that it never moved.
  5. Live ⌘ T; rename TONE, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, a timestamped surfacing order is in the logbook, the fundamental’s constancy is confirmed, and one armed track waits.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The tone — rich on purpose, printed long

Phase focus: the tone — rich on purpose, printed long. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak MicroFreak: Type → SawX (or V.Analog with the wave toward sawtooth), one low note — A1 is ideal, its fundamental near 55 Hz.
  2. MicroFreak Filter open, resonance low, zero LFO: the harmonics must survive, unflattered and unmoving.
  3. MicroFreak Attack slow, sustain full — the tone fades in once and then simply exists.
  4. MicroFreak Set the M4 input-1 gain on the held tone, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS;
  5. MicroFreak check after a full minute of holding.
  6. MicroFreak Engage Hold and print 6 minutes into TONE.
  7. MicroFreak One take, one note, no edits.
  8. MicroFreak Drop Audio Effects → Spectrum on the track and look once: the fundamental tall on the left, the series marching up behind it.
  9. MicroFreak Count the visible harmonics — they are your keyboard now.
Expected result

Six printed minutes of one rich, static low tone, peaking ≈ −12 dBFS, with the harmonic series plainly visible in Spectrum — a melody’s worth of material inside a single note.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Spectrum shows little above the fundamentalFilter closed too far, or a wave too pureOpen the cutoff fully and move the wave toward sawtooth; this lesson needs the harmonics the Eleh lesson threw away
The printed tone shimmers or drifts on its ownAn LFO or cycling envelope survived in the patchZero all patch movement and re-print — the only legal motion arrives in phase 3, from the EQ
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Light the harmonics — one band, walked by hand

Phase focus: light the harmonics — one band, walked by hand. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Logbook Write the series on paper from your printed pitch.
  2. Logbook For A1 ≈ 55 Hz: 110 · 165 · 220 · 275 · 330 · 385 · 440 Hz — the second through eighth harmonics.
  3. Logbook These are the only addresses the band may visit.
  4. Logbook Compose the order — the melody.
  5. Logbook Steal the shape of the record’s surfacing list from phase 1 or write your own, e.g.
  6. Logbook 2f → 3f → 5f → 4f → 6f → 3f, 30–60 seconds at each stop.
  7. Live Audio Effects → EQ Eight on TONE: one band, Bell, Q ≈ 8–10, gain +9 to +12 dB, frequency at your first stop.
  8. Live In Arrangement, automate the band’s frequency along your composed order: long dwells at each address, slow glides between — the glide itself should take ten seconds or more, a tone climbing through the family, not a jump cut.
  9. MicroFreak Hardware alternative: re-print instead with the MicroFreak’s filter — resonance high, cutoff swept by hand from harmonic to harmonic.
  10. Live Less exact, more alive; the system is the same.
Expected result

Five to six minutes in which faint high tones surface one after another over an unmoving bass — a melody made entirely of addresses inside one printed note.

If it fails

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The witness instruments — prove nothing moved

Phase focus: the witness instruments — prove nothing moved. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Drop Audio Effects → Tuner on TONE and play the piece end to end.
  2. The Tuner must read the same note from first minute to last — log the verdict.
  3. Watch Spectrum during two surfacings: confirm the partial was visible before the band reached it — present, then lit, never created.
  4. Logbook Write your piece’s surfacing order next to the record’s phase-1 list and compare shapes: where did the record climb, where did it circle back, and what did your order do?
  5. Live A/B one minute against REF at matched loudness, judging only the surfacing: do your overtones arrive with the same patience?
  6. Live The fresh-ears test, on yourself after the break or on a listener: “did new sounds keep entering the piece?” The honest answer — yes to the ear, no to the spectrum — is the record’s effect, replicated.
  7. Live Log it.
Expected result

A tuner that never wavered, two surfacings witnessed in Spectrum as lit-not-created, two melody lists side by side, and a logged contradiction between ear and analyzer — the lesson, in evidence.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Did the fundamental ever move?
    Answer

    No — and you can prove it two ways the ear cannot argue with: the Tuner read one note for six minutes, and Spectrum’s leftmost peak never left its address. Everything that felt like motion happened above an unmoving floor, exactly as on the record. If either witness disagrees, the print drifted and phase 2 owes you a re-take.

  • In what order did the overtones surface — on the record, and in yours?
    Answer

    Two lists, and you should be able to read both aloud: the record’s from your phase-1 transcription, yours from the automation you drew. Comparing them is the real verification — not whether your order matches, but whether you can now hear spectral order at all, the way you hear a tune. If you cannot name your own piece’s order blind, relisten with the phase-3 plan in hand; the gap between plan and perception is next session’s work.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
