Replicate: spectrum as melody

Copy one clear section of Folke Rabe — What??. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a static, harmonically rich tone (one that contains many overtones) whose individual overtones are selectively brought forward over time, so the changing spectrum — not a changing pitch — carries the melody. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring. The Digitakt II is not needed this lesson.
  • ”What??” was assembled in a tape studio you cannot visit. You are replicating the system: a rich static tone plus a narrow moving resonance. EQ Eight with one high-Q band is your version of that studio’s filters. The MicroFreak’s resonant filter is the hardware alternative.
  • New set saved as REP-WHAT — no tempo. The only timing is how long each overtone is held in the foreground.
  • A +12 dB resonant peak can jump to a painful level as it crosses sensitive frequencies. Phones at the lab-001 mark or lower, sweeps moved slowly, real break every 15 minutes.
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes — half of it goes into one printed tone and one slowly moving EQ band.
  • What you will make: a short replica of Folke Rabe — What??. The goal is this effect: a melody happening inside a tone whose pitch never changes — faint high tones surfacing one after another, none of them new arrivals.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and anything that hides whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism — a static, harmonically rich tone whose individual overtones are selectively brought forward over time, so the changing spectrum, not a changing pitch, carries the melody.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences come from your source choices or gear limits, not from missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Folke Rabe — What??. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. When do you first notice the high, faint tones above the main pitch?
    Answer

    Minutes in, usually. The timestamp says more about your attention than about the record. Those tones do not fade in at that moment — your ear arrives at them.

  2. Are those high tones new arrivals, or were they always there?
    Answer

    Always there. Every overtone you will ever hear in this piece is present from the very first second, quietly, inside the tone’s spectrum. The boosting and holding just make them audible, one at a time.

  3. Does the main pitch ever move?
    Answer

    No. The pitch you would sing along to is the same at minute one and minute twenty. Everything that feels like motion happens above it.

  4. If the pitch never changes, what carries the melody?
    Answer

    The spectrum (which overtones are prominent at any moment). The melody is played not by changing the note but by changing which part of the note you are hearing.

  5. How does your attention move as the minutes pass?
    Answer

    Upward. With nothing new happening at the bottom, your ear climbs — from the main pitch to the low overtones to the glassy high ones. The piece is built to guide exactly that climb.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a piece in which a listener hears a melody of surfacing overtones while a tuner and a spectrum analyzer prove the pitch never moved — both things true at once, as on the record.
  • One printed tone and one narrow EQ band. No second voice, no new layer, nothing that was not already inside the tone from second one.
  • The band dwells at each overtone — exact whole-number multiples of the main pitch, calculated on paper, not just found by ear.
  • The order in which overtones are brought forward is the melody. Compose the order before you set up any automation.

The piece in one row: one harmonically rich low tone printed for six minutes, a single narrow EQ band boosted hard and walked slowly along the harmonic series — resting at each overtone in a composed order — while a tuner and a spectrum display prove the main pitch never moves.

MicroFreak · SawX · one low note · filter open → printed tone · 6 min · TONE → EQ Eight · one bell · high Q · walked along f, 2f, 3f… → Spectrum + Tuner · the fundamental never moves

The same spectrum at three moments. Every bar (every overtone) is present in every panel — nothing enters or leaves. The only thing that changes is which overtone the narrow EQ band is highlighting. Played in time, that sequence of highlighted overtones is the melody.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the surfacing order

Phase focus: the reference and the surfacing order. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “What??” into it, and loop the first five minutes.
  2. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Logbook Listen once through with a pen. Every time a new overtone becomes noticeable, write a timestamp and a rough description — “low, near the main pitch,” “an octave-and-a-fifth up,” “glassy, very high.” This list is the record’s melody, written down.
  4. Logbook Hum the main pitch at the start and end of the loop. Confirm on paper that it never moved.
  5. ⌘ T; rename TONE, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Folke Rabe — What?? (1967) and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the surfacing order — as the smallest Live set that proves a static, harmonically rich tone whose individual overtones (higher harmonics inside the note) are selectively brought forward over time — so the changing spectrum, not a changing pitch, carries the melody.

  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects a melody happening inside a tone whose pitch never changes — faint high tones surfacing one after another, none of them new arrivals: they were always there.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The tone — rich on purpose, printed long

Phase focus: the tone — rich on purpose, printed long. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: Type → SawX (or V.Analog with the wave toward sawtooth), one low note — A1 is ideal, its main pitch near 55 Hz.
  2. Filter open, resonance low, LFO zero. The overtones must be present and unchanging.
  3. Attack slow, sustain full — the tone fades in once and then holds steady.
  4. Set the M4 input-1 gain on the held tone, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  5. Check the level again after a full minute of holding.
  6. Engage Hold and record 6 minutes into TONE.
  7. One take, one note, no edits.
  8. Drop Audio Effects → Spectrum on the track and look once: the main pitch tall on the left, the overtones lined up behind it.
  9. Count the visible overtones — they are your notes now.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Light the harmonics — one band, walked by hand

Phase focus: light the harmonics — one band, walked by hand. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Write the overtone series on paper from your recorded pitch.
  2. For A1 ≈ 55 Hz: 110 · 165 · 220 · 275 · 330 · 385 · 440 Hz — the second through eighth overtones.
  3. These are the only frequencies the EQ band may visit.
  4. Compose the order — this is the melody.
  5. Either copy the surfacing order you wrote in phase 1, or write your own, e.g. 2f → 3f → 5f → 4f → 6f → 3f, 30–60 seconds at each stop.
  6. Audio Effects → EQ Eight on TONE: one band, Bell, Q ≈ 8–10, gain +9 to +12 dB, frequency set to your first overtone.
  7. In Arrangement, automate the band’s frequency along your composed order. Dwell long at each overtone, and move slowly between them — each transition should take ten seconds or more, not a jump cut.
  8. Hardware alternative: re-record using the MicroFreak’s filter — resonance high, cutoff swept by hand from overtone to overtone.
  9. Less precise, more alive. The system is the same.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Did the fundamental ever move?
    Answer

    No — and you can prove it two ways the ear cannot argue with: the Tuner read one note for six minutes, and Spectrum’s leftmost peak never left its address. Everything that felt like motion happened above an unmoving floor, exactly as on the record. If either witness disagrees, the print drifted and phase 2 owes you a re-take.

  • In what order did the overtones surface — on the record, and in yours?
    Answer

    Two lists, and you should be able to read both aloud: the record’s from your phase-1 transcription, yours from the automation you drew. Comparing them is the real verification — not whether your order matches, but whether you can now hear spectral order at all, the way you hear a tune. If you cannot name your own piece’s order blind, relisten with the phase-3 plan in hand; the gap between plan and perception is next session’s work.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
