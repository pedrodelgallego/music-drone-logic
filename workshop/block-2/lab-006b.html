Drum Synthesis from Scratch

A drum sound is not a recording — it is a volume shape wrapped around a frequency that moves. This lab builds a complete three-voice kit (kick, hat, clap) entirely from oscillators, noise, and envelopes, with no samples anywhere in the chain. An oscillator is a tone generator, an envelope controls how a sound's volume or pitch changes over time, and noise is a signal containing all frequencies at once. By the end you will be able to draw each voice as a signal flow and explain every millisecond of every envelope.

  • Tools: Ableton Operator (kick, hat), one Operator or Simpler-free noise source for the clap, plus the MicroFreak as the analog alternative path noted in each voice.
  • Expected time: two sessions of 90 minutes — one to build the three voices, one to fault, A/B, and print.
The point

A sample (a recorded drum hit) is fixed — its sharp front edge, main body, and fade are already baked in; you can only trim them. A synthesized drum is adjustable to the millisecond. You decide where the click ends and the body begins, how fast the pitch drops, and how long the noise rings. That precision is the whole reason to synthesize, and this lab puts you in charge of all of it.

1 · Listen first — three records made of synthesized drums

Before building anything, listen to three tracks whose drums are unmistakably synthesized — no acoustic recordings underneath. Each one focuses on a different voice you are about to build.

01 Various — Roland TR-808 demonstrations / any 808-driven record the kick is a falling sine

The 808 kick is the classic synthesized drum: a sine wave (a pure, smooth tone) whose pitch drops fast at the very start and then holds steady, with a short click layered on top. Listen to a version with the decay turned up so you can hear the full body of the sound.

Listen for
  • The two distinct events in one hit — a short bright tick at the front, then a round low tone that rings on.
  • How the pitch of the tone is highest at the instant it starts and settles within a fraction of a second.
  • That the "punch" lives in the first 10 ms, not in the long tail.
After — verify

Try to hum the kick. You can hum the body tone, but not the click at the front — that split between an unpitched sharp hit (called the transient) and a pitched body is why your kick will use two separate generators.

Listen: YouTube

02 Any classic 808/909 hi-hat pattern the hat is gated noise

A drum-machine hi-hat is noise run through a filter, then cut short by an envelope. The closed hat and the open hat use the exact same noise — only the decay length (how quickly the sound fades) differs.

Listen for
  • That the hat has no pitch you can sing — it is broadband noise, shaped only by a filter.
  • The difference between closed and open is purely how long the gate stays open: same source, two decay times.
  • How a too-long hat smears into the next one and turns the groove to mush.
After — verify

How much of the hat is attack (the ramp up to full volume) versus decay (the fade)? Almost all of it is decay. Noise has no rise time — it is loud immediately. The decay length is effectively the whole instrument.

Listen: YouTube

03 Any 909 clap / handclap-machine record the clap is several bursts plus a tail

A synthesized clap is not a single noise burst. It is three or four short bursts fired a few milliseconds apart — imitating a group of hands that are not perfectly in sync. These are run through a band-pass filter (which keeps only a band of mid frequencies) and finished with a short reverb tail that glues them into one recognizable clap.

Listen for
  • The "spread" at the front — the bursts arriving in quick succession, not a single hit.
  • The short ringing tail that follows and fuses the bursts into one clap.
  • The mid-band focus — no deep lows, no extreme highs; the band-pass owns roughly 1–2 kHz.
After — verify

Imagine the track in slow motion: can you hear the front edge flutter — a rapid flurry of tiny hits? That is the staggered bursts. If a clap sounds like a single dry snap, it has lost its multi-burst structure. That is exactly the fault you will create on purpose later in this lab.

Listen: YouTube

Write a one-sentence hypothesis. Name what each voice is made of in terms of mechanism, not how it sounds. Aim for something like: a drum is an envelope shaping a frequency — the kick is a falling sine plus a click, the hat is gated filtered noise, the clap is staggered noise bursts glued by a tail. This is the idea the build will test.

2 · The two specs

3 · Concepts — the four moving parts of any drum

ConceptIn plain words
Amplitude envelopeHow a sound's volume changes during its short life. For drums this mainly means attack (how fast it reaches full volume — for drums, almost instant) and decay (how fast it fades away). Sustain and release barely matter for one-shot hits.
Pitch envelopeA second envelope that moves the pitch instead of the volume. The kick's punch comes from a pitch envelope that drops the tone from a high starting note to a low resting note in a few milliseconds. Fast drop = clicky and punchy. Slow drop = a soft "boop" with no impact.
TransientThe first few milliseconds of a hit — the click, the spit, the sharp edge. It is what makes a drum cut through a mix. Synthesized drums build the transient separately from the main body so you can adjust each one independently.
Filtered noiseWhite noise contains every frequency at once. Run it through a high-pass filter (which removes low frequencies) and you get a hi-hat. Run it through a band-pass filter (which keeps only a middle band) and you get a clap or snare body. The filter chooses the tone colour; the envelope chooses the length.

4 · Build the three voices

Voice 1 — Kick: a falling sine plus a click

sine osc → pitch env
150→50 Hz, ~5 ms → amp env
0 ms atk · ~250 ms dec + click
short noise/high osc → sum → kick
Step 01

Build the kick body in Operator

Do this
  1. Add Operator to a MIDI track named KICK. Set only oscillator A active, waveform Sine, coarse 1, level full. Mute B, C, D.
  2. On oscillator A's amplitude envelope: Attack 0 ms, Decay ~250 ms, Sustain 0, Release short. This is the body length — tune it later between 150–400 ms.
  3. Open the Pitch envelope (Pitch tab, enable Envelope). Set Initial high and Decay very fast so the oscillator starts near 150 Hz and falls to its resting note (around 50 Hz — play a low C/E). Pitch envelope time ≈ 3–8 ms. The amount of drop is the "thump"; the speed is the "click → body" snap.
  4. Set the played note so the resting frequency lands near 45–55 Hz (around F1).
Kick — Operator parameters (sane starting values)
OscillatorA only, Sine, coarse 1
Amp envelopeA 0 ms · D 250 ms · S 0 · R 10 ms
Pitch env amount≈ +1 to +2 octaves above the resting note (start ~150 Hz)
Pitch env time3–8 ms decay (this is the punch — keep it short)
Resting note≈ 50 Hz (F1)
Click (osc B)optional: B as noise or high sine, amp env D ≈ 2–5 ms, level low
Add the click

To add a transient, enable oscillator B as either Operator's Noise waveform or a high-frequency sine (coarse set high), with its own amp envelope decay of 2–5 ms and a low level. Route B in parallel (not as a modulator) so it mixes with the body. This ultra-short second generator is the click — it gives the kick its front edge, independent of how long the body lasts.

MicroFreak alternative

On the MicroFreak: use the Basic/Sawtooth or analog-style oscillator set to a low note, with the cycling or standard envelope assigned to Pitch using a very short attack/decay for the sweep, and a short amp envelope. The MicroFreak has only one envelope, which makes the separate click harder to add — either layer a second short MicroFreak take, or accept a softer transient.

Voice 2 — Hat: gated filtered noise

white noise → high-pass
~7–8 kHz → amp env
0 ms atk · 40 ms dec (closed) → hat
Step 02

Build the hat from noise

Do this
  1. Add Operator to a track named HAT. Set oscillator A waveform to Noise (Operator has a noise waveform); or add Operator with a high-ratio oscillator pair for the metallic route below.
  2. Add an Auto Filter (or use Operator's filter) set to High-pass, cutoff ≈ 7–8 kHz. This removes the low rumble of raw noise and leaves the bright "tss."
  3. Amp envelope: Attack 0 ms, Decay ≈ 40 ms, Sustain 0. That short decay is the closed hat.
  4. Duplicate the track as HAT OPEN and lengthen Decay to ≈ 250–350 ms — same source, longer gate, now an open hat. Program the closed hat to choke the open one (open hat on the off-beat, closed hat cutting it).
Hat — parameters
SourceWhite noise (Operator Noise waveform)
FilterHigh-pass, cutoff ≈ 7–8 kHz, mild resonance
Amp env (closed)A 0 ms · D 40 ms · S 0
Amp env (open)A 0 ms · D 300 ms · S 0
Metallic optiontwo square/saw oscillators at inharmonic ratios (e.g. 1 : 1.41) instead of noise → FM-style metal — see Lab 013b
  • The metallic route: real 808 hats use six square-wave oscillators tuned to inharmonic (non-musical) ratios — that is why they ring metallic rather than airy. You can approximate this in Operator with two oscillators at a non-integer ratio (a preview of Lab 013b). Noise is simpler and sounds more like a modern hat; pick one approach and note it in the logbook.

Voice 3 — Clap: staggered bursts plus a tail

white noise → band-pass
~1–2 kHz → 3–4 bursts
staggered ~12 ms apart → short reverb tail
glue → clap
Step 03

Build the clap from staggered noise bursts

Do this
  1. Add Operator to a track named CLAP, oscillator A waveform Noise. Amp envelope: Attack 0 ms, Decay ≈ 15 ms, Sustain 0 — one short burst.
  2. Add an Auto Filter set to Band-pass, centre ≈ 1–1.5 kHz, moderate resonance. This is the clap's "hand" colour.
  3. Create the stagger: in the clip, place 3–4 MIDI notes roughly 10–20 ms apart (about a 64th-note or finer; nudge them off-grid). Make the last burst slightly louder/longer. These quick repeats are the many-hands flam.
  4. Add a short Reverb on the track (or a send): decay ≈ 150–300 ms, small room, fully wet on a short tail — this glues the bursts into one clap. Without it the bursts read as separate ticks.
Clap — parameters
SourceWhite noise
FilterBand-pass, centre ≈ 1–1.5 kHz, resonance moderate
Burst envelopeA 0 ms · D 15 ms · S 0 (per burst)
Stagger3–4 bursts, ~10–20 ms apart, last one loudest
TailReverb decay ~150–300 ms, small/short, to fuse the bursts
  • Two ways to stagger: place several closely spaced MIDI notes in the clip (simplest), or build the repeats inside a single envelope using an LFO or retrigger. The MIDI-note method is easier to see and tune by nudging notes — use that one for this lab.

5 · The prescribed faults — induce and diagnose

Required

You must create both faults on purpose, hear them clearly, and write the diagnosis. Being able to recognize these problems by ear later — when they happen by accident — is the skill this lab certifies.

Fault A

The transient-less kick

  1. On the kick, slow the pitch envelope time from ~5 ms to 80–150 ms, and mute the click oscillator.
  2. Play it. The kick now sounds like "boooo" — a slow downward glide with no click and no punch. It will not cut through the hat and clap.
  3. Restore the fast pitch envelope and the click. Compare the two versions at the same volume and write the rule.
Fault B

The ringing hat (no decay control)

  1. On the closed hat, set Sustain above 0 or lengthen Decay to 500 ms+ so the gate never really closes.
  2. Play the 8th-note pattern. The hats overlap and blur into a continuous noise wash — the groove disappears.
  3. Restore Sustain 0 and a 40 ms decay. Compare the two versions and write the rule: a hat's character is defined by its decay length.
Diagnosis — symptom → cause → fix
SymptomCauseFix
Kick has no click, sounds like a "boop"Pitch envelope too slow and/or no separate click generatorPitch env time to 3–8 ms; add a 2–5 ms noise/high-osc click in parallel

Kick "flubs" / loses pitchPitch env amount too large or starts too highReduce the drop to ~1–2 octaves above the resting note

Hats sound dull, no "tss"High-pass cutoff too lowRaise HP cutoff to 7–8 kHz

6 · The Transient Test — certified here

Certification

Each voice has a defendable front edge

  1. Solo each voice and play one hit. For the kick, say where the click ends and the body begins (in ms). For the hat, state the decay time. For the clap, count the bursts and name the tail length.
  2. Write one sentence per voice naming the mechanism that makes it sound like a drum. Use numbers: "the kick's punch is the pitch dropping 150→50 Hz in 5 ms" — not "it sounds punchy."
  3. Draw each voice's signal flow by hand — source → shaper(s) → output — matching the diagrams above.
Pass condition

You can point to a specific number for every part of every voice and explain what changing it would do. If any answer is just an adjective ("punchy," "bright") with no mechanism behind it, keep working on that voice until you can name the numbers.

7 · Pass gate — the A/B against samples

Gate

Loudness-matched A/B, gains and losses named

  1. Play your one-bar pattern on the SYNTH KIT group. Then play the same pattern on the parked REF KIT sampled kit, adjusted to the same perceived loudness — a louder kit always sounds better to our ears, so match the levels before judging.
  2. Write three things you gained by synthesizing (for example: "I can tune the kick to the bass," "the hat decay locks to tempo," "every hit is identical or varied on purpose").
  3. Write two things you lost (for example: "the sampled kit has recorded-room character my synth kit lacks," "the sampled snare has a complexity my clap doesn't").
Pass condition

A three-voice kit built entirely from synthesis, each voice drawn as a signal flow with numbered envelope times, compared against a sampled kit at matched loudness — and an honest gains/losses list showing you understand when and why you would choose synthesis.

8 · Report bundle

Filed in the logbook
  • Three one-shot WAVs — kick, hat, clap — printed from your synthesis
  • One-bar pattern bounce of the full kit
  • Three hand-drawn signal-flow diagrams, one per voice, with every envelope time labelled
  • Parameter tables, filled with the values you actually used (not the defaults above)
  • Fault entries A and B — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule, each from your own ears
  • The diagnosis table, completed
  • A/B gains-and-losses list — three gained, two lost
  • Transient Test sentences — one per voice, mechanism not adjective
  • System diff sentence — "I thought a drum sound was X; after synthesizing one, I found it was Y"
Pass standard
Minimum passThe bundle above exists, all three voices are synthesized with no samples, and the Transient Test is passed.
Solid passBoth faults were created and diagnosed by ear. The kit holds up against the sampled reference at matched loudness. The clap still sounds good in mono.
Advanced transferYou can rebuild the same three voices on the MicroFreak (or any other synthesizer) by translating the envelopes and filters — showing that you learned a method, not just a preset.
  1. Which voice was hardest to make convincing? Was it the one with the most moving parts?
  2. At what pitch-envelope time did the kick change from "boop" to "punch"? Name the number.
  3. What did the reverb tail add to the clap that a single longer noise burst could not?
  4. After the A/B, when would you choose a sampled kit over this one — and what is the mechanical reason?
  5. Which skill from Lab 006 (capturing the world) turned out to be irrelevant here, and which one carried over?
Worked system diff — the expected granularity

"I thought a kick was one sound. It is two — a click I built in 5 ms and a body I built in 250 ms. The punch lives entirely in the gap between them."

Track-lab principle

A drum is a volume shape wrapped around a moving frequency. Synthesize it and every millisecond is yours to set.
