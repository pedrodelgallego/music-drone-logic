Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: change below perception

Copy one clear section of Éliane Radigue — Kyema. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: two or three layered sustained tones whose crossfades and balance moves stretch over multiple minutes, each beginning below the threshold of noticing. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged; the Digitakt II rests this lesson
  • “Kyema” was composed on an ARP 2500 — unobtainable, and beside the point. You are replicating the system: layered sustained tones moved at weather speed. Printed MicroFreak takes stand in for the ARP’s oscillator banks; the slowness is the real instrument, and you own it already
  • New set saved as REP-KYEMA — no tempo worth setting; the clock that matters here is the wall clock
  • Sustained tones at steady level load the ear without announcing it — phones at the lab-001 mark, a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — most of it goes into moves that take minutes to happen and listens that take minutes to judge
  • What you will make: a short replica of Éliane Radigue — Kyema. Focus on this effect: a drone that is a different piece every five minutes with no moment anyone can point at - noticing the change is the piece.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: two or three layered sustained tones whose crossfades and balance moves stretch over multiple minutes, each beginning below the threshold of noticing.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Éliane Radigue — Kyema. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Can you catch a single change in the act?
    Answer

    Almost certainly not. You notice - afterwards - that things are different, and you cannot say when they became so. That delay is the whole technique: the modulation runs below the speed of attention.

  2. How different are minute one and minute ten?
    Answer

    Very - often different enough to be different pieces: different register, different weight, different center. The distance is enormous; only the road there is invisible.

  3. Does anything ever arrive — an entrance, an event, a downbeat?
    Answer

    No. What feels like an arrival is an old layer coming forward, the way a hill “appears” out of fog. Relisten to a sound you would swear is new, then scrub back two minutes - it is already there, underneath, waiting.

  4. How many layers are sounding at once?
    Answer

    Fewer than it feels, and the count will not hold still - sustained tones fuse when their balance is even and split apart as it shifts.

  5. What tells you time is passing at all?
    Answer

    Memory, and nothing else. The present moment always sounds still; the only evidence of motion is that your memory of five minutes ago disagrees with what you hear now.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: bounce minute 1 and minute 5 of your piece and play them back to back — two different scenes; then a straight-through listen catches no move in the act. Closeness to the record’s patience is the measure of your hearing.
  • Two or three printed tones and nothing else — nothing new ever enters; every “arrival” is an old layer coming forward.
  • Every move is drawn automation stretched over multiple minutes — drawn first, then slowed further.
  • The test is negative: if any single move can be pointed at, that passage failed.

The piece’s machinery in one row: two or three printed MicroFreak tones stacked in Arrangement, volume crossfades drawn as automation and then stretched past the threshold of noticing, and a verdict delivered by memory — minute 1 against minute 5 — instead of by witness.

MicroFreak · three printed tones · three colors → Arrangement · TONE A + B + C stacked → drawn automation · stretched to minutes → memory test · minute 1 vs minute 5

The layer balance at three moments. No bar ever starts or stops — each is always sounding — and the middle panel is the trap: mid-transit, everything is moving and nothing is catchable. The first and third panels are different pieces made of the same three takes.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the tracks

Phase focus: the reference and the tracks. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Kyema” into it.
  2. Live Find a five-minute span whose ends sound like different scenes, and loop it.
  3. Live Anywhere works — that is the point of this record.
  4. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the span peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  5. Live REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  6. Live ⌘ T once; rename: DRONE, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
  7. Logbook Write in the logbook one line describing the loop’s first thirty seconds and one line describing its last thirty.
  8. Logbook Two scenes, in writing — the gap your piece must cross.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one named, routed, armed track waits for the first tone, and two scene descriptions are on paper.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
REF later feels deeper in a way yours can never reachUtility never trimmed — weight and level conflatedTrim until the looped span peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, same as your tones
No meter movement when testing the inputExt. In channel does not match the physical jackMicroFreak on 1 — the lab-001 wiring table
Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

The tones — three colors, printed long

Phase focus: the tones — three colors, printed long. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, one low note, filter closed until the tone is nearly pure — this is TONE A, the floor.
  2. MicroFreak Attack slow, sustain full, zero LFO.
  3. MicroFreak Engage Hold and print 6 minutes into DRONE; rename the track TONE A.
  4. Live ⌘ T, rename TONE B, Ext. In 1: same patch, the note a fifth or an octave up, filter half-open so a few harmonics survive — a different color, same family.
  5. Live Print 6 minutes.
  6. MicroFreak Optional TONE C: Type → Harmo, a higher register, quiet and partial-heavy.
  7. MicroFreak Print 6 minutes.
  8. MicroFreak Three layers is the record’s budget; do not exceed it.
  9. MicroFreak Set the M4 input-1 gain on each held tone, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — check after a full minute of holding;
  10. MicroFreak a sustained tone has no strike to fool you, only drift.
  11. MicroFreak Drag all takes to bar 1 so they run in parallel, then pull every fader down except TONE A — the opening scene is mostly floor.
Expected result

Two or three 6-minute takes of different colors at matched gain, stacked from bar 1, with A in front. Nothing is a piece yet — the road between balances is about to become the piece.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Two takes beat audibly against each otherPitches too close — adjacent low tones interfereWiden to a fifth or an octave; beating is the “No Sun to Burn” lesson, and this one stays still
A take wobbles or breathes on its ownAn LFO or envelope survived in the patchZero all patch movement and re-print — in this lesson every motion must belong to the automation
Phase 3 · ≈ 35 min

Draw the weather, then slow it down

Phase focus: draw the weather, then slow it down. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Plan three scenes on paper, in plain words: “A in front, C felt not heard” → “all even, fused” → “C in front, A underneath.” That sentence chain is the score.
  2. Live In Arrangement, draw volume-automation crossfades on each track to realize the plan across 5–6 minutes.
  3. Live Let the first draft move in ≈ 1-minute ramps — deliberately too fast.
  4. Live Play it.
  5. Live Every move you can hear happening, grab its breakpoints and drag the ends apart — one minute becomes two, two becomes three.
  6. Live Redraw and replay until no move is catchable in the act.
  7. Live Keep the destinations far apart even as the roads get longer — slow does not mean small.
  8. Live The scenes must clearly differ; only the transit must vanish.
  9. Live The rule, enforced without mercy: a change you witnessed is a change that failed.
  10. Live The record’s changes are only ever discovered afterwards.
Expected result

A 5–6 minute arrangement whose automation lanes look almost flat at full zoom-out, yet whose ends are different scenes — the paper plan performed below the speed of attention.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The piece feels static rather than slowDestinations too close — the scenes never clearly differWiden the plan: the front layer should change identity between scene one and scene three
A move pops out as an audible eventThat ramp still completes inside a minuteDrag its breakpoints further apart and replay — nothing in this lesson is too slow
Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

The memory test

Phase focus: the memory test. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the piece straight through, no touching, and tally every move you catch in the act.
  2. The target is zero.
  3. Live Copy 0:30–1:30 and 4:30–5:30 of all tracks to a new audio track and consolidate each region (⌘ J) — two one-minute clips, side by side.
  4. Live Play them back to back and judge one thing: same piece, or two different scenes?
  5. Live Two scenes is the pass.
  6. Live Run the same two-slice exercise on REF at matched loudness and compare gaps: is your minute-1-to-minute-5 distance as large as the record’s?
  7. Live Log the catch-tally, the verdict, and the comparison.
  8. Live Then take the scheduled break — the next listen will disagree with this one, and that is information.
Expected result

A straight-through listen with zero catches, two slices that read as different scenes when set side by side, and a logbook line measuring your distance against the record’s.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The two slices sound like the same pieceAutomation traveled nowhere — moves too small even if slowWiden the scene plan in phase 3: more dB between front and floor, layers actually trading places
You caught moves during the straight listenSome ramp still runs at human speedSend that passage back to phase 3 and stretch it again — drawn first, then slowed further
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Are your minute 1 and minute 5 different scenes?
    Answer

    Side by side they should sound like two pieces that happen to share materials — different front layer, different weight, different center. If they sound like the same piece at two volumes, the moves were timid: slowness is not smallness, and the record travels enormous distances on invisible roads. Run the same test on REF and measure your gap against its gap.

  • Could anyone witness a transition?
    Answer

    No one should be able to — including you, who drew it. The most a listener should report is “it was different later,” the same sentence the reference pass forced out of you. If any move has an address, that passage goes back to phase 3. Relisten to the record asking the same question; its changes have no addresses either, and never did.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
