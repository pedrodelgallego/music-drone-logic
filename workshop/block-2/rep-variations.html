Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: one object, a whole catalogue

Copy one clear section of Pierre Henry — Variations pour une porte et un soupir. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: dozens of distinct gestures coaxed from a single object - slow creaks, slams, taps, scrapes, rattles - recorded separately, sorted, and composed as a catalogue. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone close to the object — or a mic into M4 input 1, gain by the lab-001 ledger; DT 990 Pro for the sort and the edit; the lab-006 capture log along for the session
  • New set saved as REP-VARIATIONS — no tempo; the gestures bring their own time
  • Slams are the loudest thing in this lesson — set gain on a real slam, not a polite test creak, and keep the phones at the lab-001 mark; also mind the neighbors and the hinge itself
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — one long gesture session with the object, one sort, one composition pass, then file it and stop
  • What you will make: a short replica of Pierre Henry — Variations pour une porte et un soupir. Focus on this effect: a full work’s worth of voices - percussion, melody, texture, drama - drawn almost entirely from one door’s hinge and one sigh.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: dozens of distinct gestures coaxed from a single object - slow creaks, slams, taps, scrapes, rattles - recorded separately, sorted, and composed as a catalogue.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Pierre Henry — Variations pour une porte et un soupir. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many different sounds does the door make?
    Answer

    Far more than you counted - a slow creak, a fast squeal, a slam, a latch click, a judder, a groan, a dry tap on the panel. Each is the same object under a different hand, and Henry treats each as a separate instrument.

  2. Is everything here the door?
    Answer

    Almost. A human sigh threads between the door’s entries, and at moments a musical saw passes through - but the door is the engine, and the breath is the space between its phrases.

  3. What makes a creak read as melody?
    Answer

    Pitch movement. A slow creak is friction at varying speed, and varying speed is a glissando - the hinge literally sings a contour, rising as the hand accelerates and sagging as it slows. Henry plays the door the way a string player bows.

  4. How do the gestures group?
    Answer

    Into the classes the lab’s worksheet language gives you: attack - single impacts, slams, taps, clicks; sustain - held frictions, long creaks, groans; iteration - repeated small events, rattles, judders, flutters.

  5. Why one object instead of many?
    Answer

    Because the constraint forces depth.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: two minutes composed entirely from one object’s gesture catalogue — at least three distinct musical roles filled (percussion, line, texture) by the same hinge.
  • One hinged or friction object only — a door is perfect; a cupboard, gate, piano lid, or squeaky chair also serves.
  • 25+ distinct gestures, recorded separately and named; sorted into attack / sustain / iteration before composing.
  • No pitch processing — the object’s own registers are the lesson; editing, placement, and level only.

The work’s machinery in one row: one object played like an instrument, a long session of separated gestures, a catalogue sorted by gesture class, and a composition that never reaches outside it.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the object

Phase focus: the reference and the object. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag two or three movements of the record in, and loop them.
  2. Live Audio Effects → Utility, trim to ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Live Walk the home or studio and play three candidate objects by hand — doors, cupboards, a gate, a piano lid, a squeaky chair.
  4. MicroFreak Listen for two things: does the friction sing (pitch movement), and does the body ring (resonance)?
  5. MicroFreak Choose one and commit.
  6. MicroFreak The whole lesson uses this object and nothing else.
  7. Logbook One logbook line: the object, and the gesture you suspect will be its best voice.
  8. Logbook Phase 4 will check the suspicion.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one object is chosen for its singing friction and ringing body, and a suspicion is on paper.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Every candidate just clicks or thudsNo friction surface — impacts only, no sustain class possibleFind a hinge, a drawer slide, or anything that resists motion; the squeak you usually oil is the instrument
The hinge sings but barely registers on the micQuiet mechanism, too much distanceMic at the hinge itself, a hand-span or closer — the lab-006 perspective rule, applied to hardware
Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

The gesture session — play it like an instrument

Phase focus: the gesture session — play it like an instrument. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Mic close to the hinge, gain by the lab-001 ledger set on a real slam, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — the loudest gesture sets the level, and everything quieter inherits headroom.
  2. Record 25+ gestures, one per take, naming each aloud first: slow creak, fast squeal, half-creak stopped short, slam, soft close, panel tap, knuckle knock, latch click, scrape, rattle, judder, flutter…
  3. Vary systematically: every gesture at two speeds and two forces before inventing the next one.
  4. Log each take with its verb — the verb, not the object, is the catalogue entry.
  5. Listen back on the DT 990 before leaving the object; re-take anything where the room or your grip out-shouts the gesture.
Expected result

25+ named, separated takes from one object — slams to flutters — clean and close, with the loudest gesture unclipped and the quietest above the room.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Takes 15 onward all sound the sameVarying nothing but repetition — the hand fell into a habitChange one input deliberately per take: a new contact point, a new tool, a new speed
The slam clippedGain set on a polite test, not the real gestureReset by the ledger on a full-force slam and re-take — field clipping is forever
Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The catalogue — sort before you compose

Phase focus: the catalogue — sort before you compose. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import every take into REP-VARIATIONS; trim each to its gesture, air on both sides.
  2. Live Rename every clip verb·class — slam·A, creak·S, rattle·I.
  3. Live Where a gesture straddles classes (a creak ending in a click), split it or name the dominant half.
  4. Live Lay the clips on three tracks — ATTACK, SUSTAIN, ITERATION — your bins, visible at a glance.
  5. Live Cull duplicates until every surviving clip earns its place by being unlike its neighbors.
  6. Live Twenty-five gestures should leave you roughly fifteen to eighteen keepers.
  7. Logbook Count each bin and write the three counts in the logbook — the thinnest bin is your composition’s scarcity, known in advance.
Expected result

Three labeled bins of named gestures — a playable catalogue, with duplicates gone and the scarce class identified before composing starts.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Everything lands in one binThe session explored one gesture family — usually all attacksTen minutes back at the object, recording only the missing classes: hold the friction, repeat the small event
Clips resist classificationTakes contain several gestures eachSplit until one clip is one gesture; the catalogue’s unit is the gesture, not the take
Phase 4 · ≈ 30 min

Compose from the catalogue alone

Phase focus: compose from the catalogue alone. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Compose two minutes in Arrangement from the three bins only.
  2. Live Editing, placement, and level — no pitch processing, no effects.
  3. Live Open with one long sustain — a slow creak as the first phrase.
  4. Live Let it finish before anything answers.
  5. Live Use attacks as punctuation — a slam to end a section, taps as commas.
  6. Live Use iterations as a motor under at least one passage.
  7. Live Repetition is allowed and encouraged: a gesture returning exactly is a motif, the same way the record varies and returns its material.
  8. Live Silence is in the catalogue too — Henry’s door speaks in phrases, and phrases breathe.
  9. Live Leave gaps that are clearly placed, not leftover.
Expected result

Two minutes where one object audibly fills at least three roles — line, punctuation, motor — and never once needs a second source.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
It sounds like a sound-effects reel, not a pieceGestures presented one after another — exhibition, not compositionPick one sustain as a recurring theme and build sections around its returns
The middle sagsAll roles running at once with nothing scarceThin to one class for eight bars — scarcity is what makes the slam land when it comes back
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • How many distinct musical roles did one object fill?
    Answer

    Count honestly: percussion, melodic line, texture, motor, punctuation — three is the pass mark, four means the catalogue was deep. If you count fewer than three, the gesture session recorded one gesture twenty-five times, and the fix is at phase 2, not in the arrangement. Relisten to the record with the same count running; Henry’s door clears five without effort, which is the standard the lesson points at, not the one it requires.

  • Which gesture class does the record lean on hardest?
    Answer

    Sustain. The long creaks carry the melodic argument — they are the phrases you remember, the contours you could hum — while attacks punctuate them and iterations push them forward. Relisten to a movement and notice where your attention rests: almost always on a friction sound in motion. Then check your own piece — most first attempts over-spend the attack bin because impacts are easy to place, and the miss list should say so if yours did.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: Lab 006 hands you three doors into the interface and a world of subjects — this record argues the opposite discipline: one subject, exhausted. Depth of attention is a capture decision too, and the catalogue habit transfers to every session you record from now on.
