Replicate: one object, a whole catalogue

Copy one clear section of Pierre Henry — Variations pour une porte et un soupir. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: dozens of distinct gestures drawn from a single object — slow creaks, slams, taps, scrapes, rattles — recorded separately, sorted into types, and composed as a catalogue. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone close to the object — or a mic into M4 input 1, gain by the lab-001 ledger. DT 990 Pro for the sort and the edit. Bring the lab-006 capture log.
  • New set saved as REP-VARIATIONS — no tempo; each gesture has its own time.
  • Slams are the loudest thing in this lesson — set gain on a real slam, not a gentle test creak. Phones at the lab-001 mark. Mind the neighbors and the hinge itself.
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — one long gesture session with the object, one sort, one composition pass, then file it and stop.
  • What you will make: a short replica of Pierre Henry — Variations pour une porte et un soupir. The goal is this effect: a full piece’s worth of sound — percussion, melody, texture, drama — drawn almost entirely from one object.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and anything that hides whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism — dozens of distinct gestures drawn from a single object, recorded separately, sorted, and composed as a catalogue.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences come from your source choices or gear limits, not from missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Pierre Henry — Variations pour une porte et un soupir. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many different sounds does the door make?
    Answer

    Far more than you counted. A slow creak, a fast squeal, a slam, a latch click, a judder, a groan, a dry tap on the panel. Each is the same object played differently, and Henry treats each gesture as a separate instrument.

  2. Is everything here the door?
    Answer

    Almost. A human sigh threads between the door’s entries, and a musical saw appears briefly — but the door is the main voice, and the breath is the space between its phrases.

  3. What makes a creak sound like melody?
    Answer

    Pitch movement. A slow creak is friction at changing speed, and changing speed produces a glide in pitch (a glissando). The hinge rises as the hand speeds up and falls as it slows. Henry plays the door the way a string player uses a bow.

  4. How do the gestures group?
    Answer

    Into three classes: attack — single impacts: slams, taps, clicks; sustain — held frictions: long creaks, groans; iteration — repeated small events: rattles, judders, flutters.

  5. Why one object instead of many?
    Answer

    Because the constraint forces depth. One object explored fully produces more variety than many objects touched lightly.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: two minutes composed entirely from one object’s gesture catalogue — at least three distinct musical roles filled (percussion, melodic line, texture) by the same object.
  • One hinged or friction object only. A door is ideal; a cupboard, gate, piano lid, or squeaky chair also works.
  • 25+ distinct gestures, recorded separately and named; sorted into attack / sustain / iteration before composing starts.
  • No pitch processing — the object’s own pitches are the lesson. Editing, placement, and level only.

The piece in one row: one object played like an instrument, a session of separately recorded gestures, a catalogue sorted by gesture type, and a composition that never goes outside it.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the object

Phase focus: the reference and the object. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag two or three movements of the record in, and loop them.
  2. Audio Effects → Utility, trim to ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Walk the home or studio and play three candidate objects by hand — doors, cupboards, a gate, a piano lid, a squeaky chair.
  4. Listen for two things: does the friction change pitch as you move it (does it "sing"), and does the body ring after a hit (resonance)?
  5. Choose one and commit.
  6. The whole lesson uses this object and nothing else.
  7. One logbook line: the object, and the gesture you suspect will be its best voice.
  8. Phase 4 will check the suspicion.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Pierre Henry — Variations pour une porte et un soupir (1963) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the object — as the smallest Live set that proves dozens of distinct gestures drawn from a single object — slow creaks, slams, taps, scrapes, rattles — recorded separately, sorted into types, and composed as a catalogue.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects an entire piece’s worth of sounds — percussion, melody, texture, drama — drawn almost entirely from one door’s hinge and one breath.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the object into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates dozens of distinct gestures drawn from a single object — slow creaks, slams, taps, scrapes, rattles — recorded separately, sorted into types, and composed as a catalogue, not just the surface of Pierre Henry — Variations pour une porte et un soupir (1963).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one object is chosen for its singing friction and ringing body, and a suspicion is on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

The gesture session — play it like an instrument

Phase focus: the gesture session — play it like an instrument. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Mic close to the hinge, gain by the lab-001 ledger set on a real slam, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — the loudest gesture sets the level, and everything quieter inherits headroom.
  2. Record 25+ gestures, one per take. Say the name out loud first: slow creak, fast squeal, half-creak stopped short, slam, soft close, panel tap, knuckle knock, latch click, scrape, rattle, judder, flutter…
  3. Vary systematically: try every gesture at two speeds and two force levels before inventing the next one.
  4. Log each take with its verb — the action word, not just the object. The verb is the catalogue entry.
  5. Listen back on the DT 990 before finishing with the object. Re-take anything where the room noise or your hand grip overpowers the gesture itself.

Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The catalogue — sort before you compose

Phase focus: the catalogue — sort before you compose. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import every take into REP-VARIATIONS; trim each to its gesture, air on both sides.
  2. Rename every clip verb·class — slam·A, creak·S, rattle·I.
  3. Where a gesture straddles classes (a creak ending in a click), split it or name the dominant half.
  4. Lay the clips on three tracks — ATTACK, SUSTAIN, ITERATION — your bins, visible at a glance.
  5. Remove duplicates until every surviving clip earns its place by being different from its neighbors.
  6. Twenty-five gestures should leave you roughly fifteen to eighteen keepers.
  7. Count each bin and write the three counts in the logbook. The bin with the fewest clips is your scarcest resource — good to know before composing.

Phase 4 · ≈ 30 min

Compose from the catalogue alone

Phase focus: compose from the catalogue alone. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Compose two minutes in Arrangement from the three bins only.
  2. Editing, placement, and level — no pitch processing, no effects.
  3. Open with one long sustain — a slow creak as the first phrase.
  4. Let it finish before anything answers.
  5. Use attacks as punctuation — a slam to end a section, taps as commas.
  6. Use iterations as a motor under at least one passage.
  7. Repetition is allowed and encouraged: a gesture returning exactly is a motif, the same way the record varies and returns its material.
  8. Silence is part of the catalogue too. Henry’s door speaks in phrases, and phrases need breathing room.
  9. Leave gaps that are deliberate, not leftover.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • How many distinct musical roles did one object fill?
    Answer

    Count honestly: percussion, melodic line, texture, motor, punctuation. Three is the pass mark; four means the catalogue was deep. If you count fewer than three, the gesture session recorded one gesture type twenty-five times, and the fix is at phase 2, not in the arrangement. Henry’s door fills five without effort — that is what the lesson points toward.

  • Which gesture type does the record lean on hardest?
    Answer

    Sustain. The long creaks carry the melodic argument — the phrases you remember, the contours you could almost hum — while attacks punctuate them and iterations drive them forward. Most first attempts spend too much time in the attack bin because impacts are easy to place. Check if yours did, and note it in the miss list.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: Lab 006 gives you many subjects to choose from. This record argues the opposite: one subject, explored fully. Depth of attention is a capture decision too. The catalogue habit applies to every recording session from here on.
