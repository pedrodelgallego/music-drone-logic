Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: one machine gluing many worlds

Copy one clear section of DJ Shadow — “Building Steam with a Grain of Salt”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: every sample chopped, sequenced and summed inside one sampler - an MPC60’s timing and headroom as the shared parent of every sound. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: Digitakt II is the whole studio this lesson; Live is only the record player and the tape deck at the ends, Digitakt II main out → M4 in 3/4, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack
  • New Live set saved as REP-SHADOW; tempo will be set by the break, not by you — leave it until phase 3
  • Sample levels will be uneven across four worlds — set the Digitakt II’s input level before each capture, phones at the lab-001 mark, no surprises from a hot break
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into chopping and balancing inside one box, not into Live
  • What you will make: a short replica of DJ Shadow — “Building Steam with a Grain of Salt”. Focus on this effect: drums, keys, voices and atmosphere from unrelated records breathing as one band, with no audible seam between worlds.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: every sample chopped, sequenced and summed inside one sampler - an MPC60’s timing and headroom as the shared parent of every sound.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of DJ Shadow — “Building Steam with a Grain of Salt”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many source worlds can you count?
    Answer

    At least four - drums, piano, spoken voices, atmosphere - and they came from different records, different rooms, different decades.

  2. What do the drums and the piano have in common that they shouldn’t?
    Answer

    The same dust. A drum kit and a piano recorded years apart in different rooms should wear different surfaces - here they wear one, because both passed through the same sampler’s converters and headroom on the way to the mix.

  3. Where is the timing coming from?
    Answer

    One sequencer. Nothing here keeps its own tempo - the break, the chords and the voice all move to a single grid with a single swing, which is why a phrase spoken in one decade lands on a drum hit recorded in another.

  4. Does any layer sound cleaner than the rest?
    Answer

    No layer escapes the house grain. A cleaner layer would betray a different path - a sound that skipped the machine - and the ensemble illusion would crack there.

  5. When the drums thin out, do the other layers fall apart into strangers?
    Answer

    They don’t - the piano and voices still sound related with the rhythm reduced, which proves the glue is not the break masking the seams. The relationship survives because every layer shares a parent: one machine’s grid and gain path.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same ensemble illusion — four unrelated worlds reading as one band, wearing one shared surface, at matched loudness.
  • Exactly four sources — a drum break, a chord, a voice, one recording of your own — and all four pass through the Digitakt II.
  • No rescue in Live: every balance, tuning and tame happens inside the machine, so every sound shares one path to the mix.
  • 2–3 minutes; the arrangement is mutes and pattern changes — the way a band breathes, not the way a DAW edits.

The piece’s machinery in one row: four sources that share nothing, chopped onto four Digitakt II tracks, sequenced by one grid and summed through one gain path — the shared machine is the band’s shared room.

Phase 1 · ≈ 20 min

The reference and the four strangers

Phase focus: the reference and the four strangers. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Building Steam with a Grain of Salt” in, loop its most characteristic minute, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Live Gather four sources that share no decade, genre or room: a drum break from one record, a sustained chord (keys, strings, guitar) from a second, a spoken or sung phrase from a third — records or streams, captured per lab 006’s methods.
  3. Live The fourth is yours: a lab 006 capture, or one minute recorded now through the M4 — a room, a kitchen, your voice.
  4. Live Anything, as long as it is from your world and not theirs.
  5. Live Crop each in Live to its best 5–15 seconds and render four files named BREAK, CHORD, VOICE, MINE.
  6. Logbook Logbook, one line: what the four have in common.
  7. Logbook The correct answer right now is “nothing.”
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, and four cropped files from four worlds sit named and rendered — still strangers, exactly as they should be.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The four sources already feel relatedYou shopped one genre or one era — the glue would get false creditRe-pick until decades and rooms collide; the test needs real strangers
Your own recording is long, dull or noisyCaptured without choosing a momentCrop to its best 10 seconds; noise is fine — noise is a world too
Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

Into the machine — chop a break, a chord, a voice

Phase focus: into the machine — chop a break, a chord, a voice. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Digitakt II + Live Move the four files into the Digitakt II — over USB with Elektron’s Transfer app, or by playing each out of Live and sampling it through the Digitakt II’s inputs;
  2. Digitakt II + Live either way they end in four sample slots.
  3. Digitakt II + Live Assign: T1 = BREAK, T2 = CHORD, T3 = VOICE, T4 = MINE.
  4. Digitakt II + Live Chop the break: set the sample start on the first kick, then slice — 8 or 16 slices — and audition until each slice lands on a hit, not between two.
  5. Digitakt II + Live Trim the chord to a clean sustain and tune it to the break’s key by ear with coarse TUNE, in semitones — the chord bends to the band, never the other way.
  6. Digitakt II + Live Trim the voice to one phrase and MINE to one gesture.
  7. Digitakt II Then balance all four track levels inside the Digitakt II until they meet at comparable loudness on its main out.
Expected result

Four tracks answer the pads — break slices, a tuned chord, a phrase, your gesture — already meeting at one level inside one box. The strangers have a shared parent now.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Slices land between hits, doubling attacksSample start not on the first kick before slicingRe-set the start point on the downbeat, then slice again
The chord sours against the breakSources in different keys — they always areCoarse-tune the chord by semitones until the clash relaxes; trust the ear, not a tuner
Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

One grid, one path — sequence the band

Phase focus: one grid, one path — sequence the band. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Digitakt II Press [TEMPO] and turn the tempo encoder until the break sits naturally — usually somewhere near 90 BPM for this kind of material.
  2. Digitakt II the break decides, you obey.
  3. Digitakt II Program a 2–4 bar pattern: break slices first, until the drums groove on their own.
  4. Digitakt II Add swing if the slices ask for it.
  5. Digitakt II Place the chord on the bar — long trigs, let it ring.
  6. Digitakt II Place the voice on the grid, not near it; nudge with micro-timing toward the drums’ swing until it phrases with them.
  7. Digitakt II Let MINE enter as atmosphere or punctuation — sparse trigs, low level.
  8. Digitakt II It must sound like the fourth member, not a guest.
  9. Digitakt II + Live Build 2–3 minutes from pattern variations and track mutes — copies of the pattern with members resting and returning.
  10. Live No Live processing anywhere; every balance decision happens on the machine.
Expected result

A 2–3 minute sequence in which four worlds move to one clock and exit one jack — and, somewhere in the middle of programming, a moment where you stop hearing sources and start hearing a band.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
It sounds like a collage, not a bandLevels never met inside the box — one layer sits on top of the othersRebalance the four track levels on the Digitakt II until no layer wins by default
The voice floats free of the grooveTrig placed by eye, not by swingMove it onto the grid, then micro-time it toward the drums until it phrases with them
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Print once, then run the glue test

Phase focus: print once, then run the glue test. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Digitakt II + Live Record the Digitakt II main out through M4 in 3/4 into one stereo Live track, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, the full 2–3 minutes in one take — no punching in;
  2. Digitakt II + Live a band plays through.
  3. Live A/B the print against REF at matched loudness, judging one thing only: do your four worlds wear one surface the way the record’s do?
  4. Digitakt II The glue test: play the pattern again with T1 muted.
  5. Digitakt II Listen to the chord, the voice and your recording alone together — do they still sound related, or did they fall apart into strangers?
  6. Logbook Logbook, two lines: what the shared path did that four separately perfected tracks would not have done, and which layer most resisted joining the band.
Expected result

One stereo print of the band, an A/B that comes down to surface and swing, and a drumless pass in which the remaining three still sound like one room resting — not three downloads.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix

The print sounds smaller than the machine didA leg of the M4 path off the ledger valuesWalk the lab 002 gain ledger across Digitakt II out, M4 trim and the Live fader; reprint at unity
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your print at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Mute the drums — does your ensemble fall apart into strangers?
    Answer

    It should not. If the chord, the voice and your recording still sound related with the break gone, the glue is real — they share a grid and a gain path, not just a drummer loud enough to hide the seams. If they fall apart, the relationship was masking, not gluing, and the balance work goes back into the machine. Relisten to a sparse passage of the record with the same question; its layers stay family even when the kit thins out.

  • What did the shared path do that per-track perfection wouldn’t?
    Answer

    Per-track perfection preserves each source’s origin — four clean strangers, each carrying its own room, its own era, its own hiss. The shared path overwrites those birthmarks with one common surface: one converter’s grain, one headroom’s squeeze, one clock’s swing, applied to everything equally. That is a mix bus made of a machine’s habits — and it is why the record sounds like a band instead of a playlist. Relisten to your print and name the one habit of the Digitakt II you can now hear on all four layers.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 007 — a shared resample path is a mix bus made of generations. You just used one machine’s path as the band’s shared room; the lab measures exactly what that room adds, pass by pass.
