Replicate: one machine gluing many worlds

Copy one clear section of DJ Shadow — “Building Steam with a Grain of Salt”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: every sample chopped, sequenced and summed inside one sampler - an MPC60’s timing and headroom as the shared parent of every sound. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: Digitakt II is the whole studio this lesson; Live is only the record player and the tape deck at the ends, Digitakt II main out → M4 in 3/4, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack
  • New Live set saved as REP-SHADOW; tempo will be set by the break, not by you — leave it until phase 3
  • Sample levels will be uneven across four worlds — set the Digitakt II’s input level before each capture, phones at the lab-001 mark, no surprises from a hot break
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into chopping and balancing inside one box, not into Live
  • What you will make: a short replica of DJ Shadow — “Building Steam with a Grain of Salt”. Focus on this effect: drums, keys, voices and atmosphere from unrelated records sounding like one band, with no audible join between sources.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: every sample chopped, sequenced and summed inside one sampler - an MPC60’s timing and headroom as the shared parent of every sound.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of DJ Shadow — “Building Steam with a Grain of Salt”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many source worlds can you count?
    Answer

    At least four - drums, piano, spoken voices, atmosphere - and they came from different records, different rooms, different decades.

  2. What do the drums and the piano have in common that they shouldn’t?
    Answer

    The same texture. A drum kit and a piano recorded years apart in different rooms should sound like they come from different places — but here they share one surface, because both passed through the same sampler’s converters and gain path on the way to the mix.

  3. Where is the timing coming from?
    Answer

    One sequencer. Nothing here keeps its own tempo — the break, the chords and the voice all move to a single grid with a single swing. That is why a phrase spoken in one decade can land on a drum hit recorded in another.

  4. Does any layer sound cleaner than the rest?
    Answer

    No layer escapes the shared texture. A cleaner layer would give away a different signal path — a sound that bypassed the machine — and the sense of one band would break at that point.

  5. When the drums thin out, do the other layers fall apart into strangers?
    Answer

    They do not — the piano and voices still sound related when the drums thin out. That proves the glue is not the break covering up the joins. The relationship holds because every layer shares a parent: one machine’s grid and gain path.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same result — four unrelated sources sounding like one band, sharing one common texture, at matched loudness.
  • Exactly four sources — a drum break, a chord, a voice, one recording of your own — and all four pass through the Digitakt II.
  • No rescue in Live: every balance, tuning and tame happens inside the machine, so every sound shares one path to the mix.
  • 2–3 minutes; the arrangement is mutes and pattern changes — the way a band breathes, not the way a DAW edits.

The piece in one row: four sources that share nothing, loaded onto four Digitakt II tracks, sequenced by one grid and summed through one gain path — the shared machine gives every source the same character.

four unrelated sources · break · chord · voice · yours → Digitakt II · chopped onto T1–T4 → one grid · one gain path → 2–3 min print · ≈ −12 dBFS

What the machine does to the sources, in three stages. The sources arrive with four different textures; the Digitakt II’s grid and gain path give them one shared timing and character; what comes out sounds like an ensemble. Nothing in panel three was recorded together — it only sounds that way.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 20 min

The reference and the four strangers

Phase focus: the reference and the four strangers. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Building Steam with a Grain of Salt” in, loop its most characteristic minute, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Gather four sources that share no decade, genre or room: a drum break from one record, a sustained chord (keys, strings, guitar) from a second, a spoken or sung phrase from a third — records or streams, captured per lab 006’s methods.
  3. The fourth is yours: a lab 006 capture, or one minute recorded now through the M4 — a room, a kitchen, your voice.
  4. Anything, as long as it is from your world and not theirs.
  5. Crop each in Live to its best 5–15 seconds and render four files named BREAK, CHORD, VOICE, MINE.
  6. Logbook, one line: what the four have in common.
  7. The correct answer right now is “nothing.”
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from DJ Shadow — “Building Steam with a Grain of Salt” (Endtroducing….., 1996) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the four strangers — as the smallest Live set that proves every sample chopped, sequenced and summed inside one sampler — an MPC60’s timing and headroom as the shared parent of every sound.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects drums, keys, voices and atmosphere from unrelated records sounding like one band, with no audible join between sources.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the four strangers into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates every sample chopped, sequenced and summed inside one sampler — an MPC60’s timing and headroom as the shared parent of every sound, not just the surface of DJ Shadow — “Building Steam with a Grain of Salt” (Endtroducing….., 1996).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, and four cropped files from four worlds sit named and rendered — still strangers, exactly as they should be.

Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

Into the machine — chop a break, a chord, a voice

Phase focus: into the machine — chop a break, a chord, a voice. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Move the four files into the Digitakt II — over USB with Elektron’s Transfer app, or by playing each out of Live and sampling it through the Digitakt II’s inputs;
  2. either way they end in four sample slots.
  3. Assign: T1 = BREAK, T2 = CHORD, T3 = VOICE, T4 = MINE.
  4. Chop the break: set the sample start on the first kick, then slice — 8 or 16 slices — and audition until each slice lands on a hit, not between two.
  5. Trim the chord to a clean sustain and tune it to the break’s key by ear with coarse TUNE, in semitones — the chord bends to the band, never the other way.
  6. Trim the voice to one phrase and MINE to one gesture.
  7. Then balance all four track levels inside the Digitakt II until they meet at comparable loudness on its main out.

Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

One grid, one path — sequence the band

Phase focus: one grid, one path — sequence the band. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Press [TEMPO] and turn the tempo encoder until the break sits naturally — usually somewhere near 90 BPM for this kind of material.
  2. the break decides, you obey.
  3. Program a 2–4 bar pattern: break slices first, until the drums groove on their own.
  4. Add swing if the slices ask for it.
  5. Place the chord on the bar — long trigs, let it ring.
  6. Place the voice on the grid, not near it; nudge with micro-timing toward the drums’ swing until it phrases with them.
  7. Let MINE enter as atmosphere or punctuation — sparse trigs, low level.
  8. It must sound like the fourth member, not a guest.
  9. Build 2–3 minutes from pattern variations and track mutes — copies of the pattern with members resting and returning.
  10. No Live processing anywhere; every balance decision happens on the machine.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your print at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Mute the drums — does your ensemble fall apart into strangers?
    Answer

    It should not. If the chord, the voice and your recording still sound related with the break gone, the glue is real — they share a grid and a gain path, not just a loud drummer covering up the joins. If they fall apart, the break was only masking the problem. Redo the balance inside the Digitakt II. Relisten to a sparse passage of the record — its layers stay related even when the kit thins out.

  • What did the shared path do that per-track perfection wouldn’t?
    Answer

    Processing each source separately keeps each one sounding like itself — four clean strangers, each carrying its own room, its own era, its own noise floor. The shared path replaces all of that with one common texture: one converter’s character, one gain path’s compression, one clock’s swing, applied equally to everything. That is what turns four separate sources into something that sounds like a band. Relisten to your print and name the one character of the Digitakt II you can now hear across all four layers.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 007 — a shared signal path is a mix bus made of repeated passes. You just used one machine’s path as the band’s shared character; the lab measures exactly what that character adds, one pass at a time.
