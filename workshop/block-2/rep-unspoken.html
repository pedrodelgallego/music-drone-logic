Replicate: acoustic grains looped into a band

Copy one clear section of Four Tet — “Unspoken”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: short acoustic recordings looped exactly — every repeat identical, the timing inside each loop human and slightly off the grid — stacked one entry at a time into a long build. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: any mic or piezo into M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack, and physical things to play — a tabletop, a jar of rice, keys, a guitar or piano if one is near. The Digitakt II and the MicroFreak are not needed this lesson.
  • New set saved as REP-FOURTET, tempo around 93 BPM to start — you will adjust it to match your loops, not the other way around.
  • Taps and shakers are spiky — their peaks (transients) hit much harder than your ear suggests. Set the M4 gain on your hardest hit. Phones at the lab-001 mark.
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop. The time goes into recording gestures and hearing their timing, not into setting up devices.
  • What you will make: a short replica of Four Tet — “Unspoken”. The goal is this effect: acoustic slivers looping like a machine while keeping the feel of the hands that played them.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and anything that hides whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism — short acoustic recordings looped exactly, every repeat identical, the timing inside each loop human and uneven, stacked one entry at a time into a long build.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences come from your source choices or gear limits, not from missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Four Tet — “Unspoken”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How do you know the drums are sampled rather than played live?
    Answer

    The repeats. A drummer never plays two hits identically; a sampler never plays them differently. Exact repetition is the fingerprint of a captured loop.

  2. What keeps the track warm despite the machine-like repetition?
    Answer

    The acoustic quality of the recordings: room sound, the resonance of wood, the feel of fingers. The player’s touch survives inside each sliver even though their tempo did not. Machine timing carrying human material.

  3. Is the timing inside one loop straight?
    Answer

    No — it pushes and drags the way a hand does. The loop repeats that unevenness exactly every time. The groove is a human wobble copied, not a grid made to feel human.

  4. In what order do the elements enter?
    Answer

    One at a time. Each new entry changes how everything already playing sounds. The build is the composition. Write the order you heard; the second listen usually corrects the first.

  5. How many seconds of actual material do you think the track contains?
    Answer

    Far fewer than its running time. Tiny slivers, looped for minutes. A few gestures, looped exactly, are enough to sound like a band — if each gesture was worth keeping.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same effect — machine repetition carrying human timing, the acoustic quality of the recordings audible inside exact loops, at matched loudness.
  • Every sound is a gesture you physically performed and recorded through the M4. Nothing synthesized, nothing downloaded.
  • No quantizing, no warping to the grid. The timing wobble inside each loop is the material. The loop boundary is the only edit that touches time.
  • Three minutes, loops entering one at a time. The build is the form.

The piece in one row: a handful of physical gestures recorded once, cropped into loops whose inner timing is left alone, and stacked into a three-minute build where each entry changes how the others sound.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the entry map

Phase focus: the reference and the entry map. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Unspoken” in, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Play it once through with a pen: every time a new element enters, write a timestamp and a two-word name — “drums in,” “harp in,” “voice sliver.” This list is the entry map.
  3. Find one passage where a loop’s inner timing noticeably leans — an early hi-hat, a dragged snare — and loop those eight bars for later comparison.
  4. One logbook line: how many separate recordings you think the whole track uses.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Four Tet — “Unspoken” (Rounds, 2003) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the entry map — as the smallest Live set that proves short acoustic recordings looped exactly — every repeat identical, the timing inside each loop human and slightly uneven — stacked one entry at a time into a long build.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects harp, kit and piano slivers looping like a machine while keeping the wood, breath and feel of the hands that played them.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the entry map into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates short acoustic recordings looped exactly — every repeat identical, the timing inside each loop human and slightly uneven — stacked one entry at a time into a long build, not just the surface of Four Tet — “Unspoken” (Rounds, 2003).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record sits at ≈ −12 dBFS with an entry map on paper, a leaning passage looped, and a capture count written down — the form copied before any sound exists.

Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

Record the gestures — wood, shaker, three notes

Phase focus: record the gestures — wood, shaker, three notes. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename GESTURES, Ext. In 1, armed.
  2. Set the M4 input-1 gain on your hardest hit, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — transients spike far above what the ear reports.
  3. Enable Live’s metronome at your project tempo and record, in separate takes of 10–20 seconds each: a tapped rhythm on a tabletop or guitar body;
  4. a shaker pattern (a jar of rice works); three slow notes on piano or guitar — or three struck objects at different pitches if neither is near.
  5. Add one wildcard gesture of your own — a zip, a page turn, a breath.
  6. Five or six takes total.
  7. Play with the click, not mechanically on it — let your timing lean the way a hand naturally leans.
  8. If a take has a push or drag, that is the material, not a mistake.
  9. Listen back once and mark the takes that have feel.
  10. Delete nothing yet.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Loop without killing the push

Phase focus: loop without killing the push. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. For each starred take, find the best one or two bars and crop a clip to them.
  2. Set the clip to loop.
  3. Warp minimally: one warp marker at the loop start, one at the end, stretched to exactly 1 or 2 bars — and no markers in between.
  4. The inside timing must pass through untouched.
  5. Forbidden: Quantize, per-transient warp markers, and any groove applied to these clips.
  6. Make 4–6 loops this way: at least one rhythmic (the taps), one textural (the shaker), one tonal (the three notes), plus the wildcard.
  7. Play any two together and listen for the two timings rubbing against each other — two human leans coexisting over one grid.
  8. That friction is what you are keeping.

Phase 4 · ≈ 25 min

The build — and the quantize experiment

Phase focus: the build — and the quantize experiment. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. In Arrangement, build 3 minutes: first loop alone for a while, then each new loop entering on its own — never two at once.
  2. Steal the record’s pacing, not its sounds.
  3. Let at least one loop leave and return — an exit is an event too, as the entry map showed.
  4. The experiment: duplicate one rhythmic loop, and on the copy add a warp marker to every hit and drag each onto the grid.
  5. A/B the copy against the original in the full stack.
  6. Two logbook lines: what drained out of the quantized copy, and which loop’s timing unevenness the piece cannot lose.
  7. Keep or delete the copy — almost always delete.
  8. Play your three minutes end to end at the mark, then A/B a minute against REF at matched loudness, judging warmth and lean only.

A three-minute build of staggered entries, one murdered duplicate proving by absence what the wobble carries, and a logbook line naming the load-bearing loop.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The build feels flat — new entries don’t registerLoops too similar in pitch range and level, so each new arrival is buried before it landsSpace entries further apart and lower the stack’s level just before each new entry.
The quantized copy sounds betterThe original take’s lean was unintentional sloppiness, not feelRe-record that gesture with intention. A timing push is a direction, not an accident.
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Which loop’s timing unevenness is load-bearing?
    Answer

    The quantize experiment answers this: the loop whose straightened copy drained the most life from the stack is the one that matters most. It is usually the main rhythmic gesture, because its lean is what every other loop leans against. If straightening it changed nothing, your loops were not leaning to begin with — phase 2 needs another take.

  • How does the record sequence its entries?
    Answer

    One at a time, with patience. Each arrival changes how everything already playing sounds, rather than just adding to it. Relisten with your entry map from phase 1 and correct it. The second listen usually finds an entry the first one missed.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 007 — exact repetition is the fingerprint of a captured loop, and you just made it musical. The lab studies what each capture path adds on top of that fingerprint, generation by generation.
