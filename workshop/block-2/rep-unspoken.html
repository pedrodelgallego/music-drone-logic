Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: acoustic grains looped into a band

Copy one clear section of Four Tet — “Unspoken”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: short acoustic captures looped exactly - every repeat identical, the timing inside each loop human - stacked one entry at a time into a long build. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordFour Tet — “Unspoken” (Rounds, 2003)

Before you start
  • Gear: any mic or piezo into M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack, and physical things to play — a tabletop, a jar of rice, keys, a guitar or piano if one is near; the Digitakt II and the MicroFreak rest this lesson
  • New set saved as REP-FOURTET, tempo around 93 BPM to start — you will bend it to your loops, not the loops to it
  • Taps and shakers are spiky — transients peak far above what your ear reports; set the M4 gain on your hardest hit, phones at the lab-001 mark
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into recording gestures and judging their wobble, not into devices
  • What you will make: a short replica of Four Tet — “Unspoken”. Focus on this effect: harp, kit and piano slivers looping like a machine while keeping the wood, breath and push of the hands that played them.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: short acoustic captures looped exactly - every repeat identical, the timing inside each loop human - stacked one entry at a time into a long build.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Four Tet — “Unspoken”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How do you know the drums are sampled rather than played?
    Answer

    The repeats: a drummer never plays two hits identically, a sampler never plays them differently - exact repetition is the fingerprint of capture.

  2. What keeps the track warm despite the grid?
    Answer

    The acoustic generations underneath: room, wood, fingers. The player’s touch survives inside each sliver even though the player’s tempo did not - machine timing carrying human material.

  3. Is the timing inside one loop straight?
    Answer

    No - it pushes and drags the way a hand does, and the loop repeats that push exactly, every time. The groove is a human wobble photocopied, not a grid humanized.

  4. In what order do the elements enter?
    Answer

    One at a time - each entry re-frames everything already playing, and the build is the composition. Write the order you heard; you will check it again at the end of this lesson, and the second listen usually corrects the first.

  5. How many seconds of actual material do you think the track contains?
    Answer

    Far fewer than its running time - slivers of seconds, dwelt on for minutes. The economy is the point: a few gestures, looped exactly, are enough to be a band if each gesture was worth keeping.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same paradox — machine repetition carrying human timing, wood and breath audible inside exact loops, at matched loudness.
  • Every sound is a gesture you physically performed and recorded through the M4 — nothing synthesized, nothing downloaded.
  • No quantizing, no warping to the grid: the wobble inside each loop is the material. The loop boundary is the only edit that touches time.
  • Three minutes, loops entering one at a time — the build is the form.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a handful of physical gestures recorded once, cropped into loops whose inner timing is left alone, and stacked into a three-minute build where each entry re-frames the last.

What “human push kept” means, drawn as three snapshots. The gesture’s hits lean off the grid; the loop repeats that lean identically; the stack lets several leans coexist over one pulse. Quantizing panel one would make panel three a drum machine.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the entry map

Phase focus: the reference and the entry map. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Unspoken” in, and trim with Audio Effects → Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Live + Logbook Play it once through with a pen: every time a new element enters, write a timestamp and a two-word name — “drums in,” “harp in,” “voice sliver.” This list is the entry map.
  3. Live + Logbook Mark one passage where a loop’s inner timing audibly leans — early hat, dragged snare — and loop those eight bars for later comparison.
  4. Logbook Logbook, one line: how many distinct captures you believe the whole track uses.
Expected result

The record sits at ≈ −12 dBFS with an entry map on paper, a leaning passage looped, and a capture count written down — the form copied before any sound exists.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The entry map has only two or three linesListening to the texture, not the arrivalsRelisten asking one question only: “what just changed?” — log every answer
You cannot hear any lean in the loopsJudging timing by feel at full mixTap your foot on the grid and listen for hits that land just off your tap
Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

Record the gestures — wood, shaker, three notes

Phase focus: record the gestures — wood, shaker, three notes. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename GESTURES, Ext. In 1, armed.
  2. Live Set the M4 input-1 gain on your hardest hit, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — transients spike far above what the ear reports.
  3. Live Enable Live’s metronome at your project tempo and record, in separate takes of 10–20 seconds each: a tapped rhythm on a tabletop or guitar body;
  4. Digitakt II a shaker pattern (a jar of rice works); three slow notes on piano or guitar — or three struck objects at different pitches if neither is near.
  5. Digitakt II Add one wildcard gesture of your own — a zip, a page turn, a breath.
  6. Digitakt II Five or six takes total.
  7. Digitakt II Play with the click, not on it — let your timing lean the way a hand leans.
  8. Digitakt II Do not punish a push; that push is the material.
  9. Digitakt II Listen back once and star the takes with feel.
  10. Digitakt II Delete nothing yet.
Expected result

Five or six short takes of physically performed material at ≈ −12 dBFS, sharing one tempo loosely, each carrying room, wood and hand — the acoustic generations the record is warm with.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Clipped spikes on the tapsGain set on average level, not on the hardest transientRe-set the M4 gain while hitting as hard as the take will, then re-record
Takes sound stiff and lifelessPlaying on the click — performing like a quantizerRecord again while listening to the click quietly and your hands loudly
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Loop without killing the push

Phase focus: loop without killing the push. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live For each starred take, find the best one or two bars and crop a clip to them.
  2. Live Set the clip to loop.
  3. Live Warp minimally: one warp marker at the loop start, one at the end, stretched to exactly 1 or 2 bars — and no markers in between.
  4. Live The inside timing must pass through untouched.
  5. Live Forbidden, by name: Quantize, per-transient warp markers, and any groove applied to these clips.
  6. Live Make 4–6 loops this way: at least one rhythmic (the taps), one textural (the shaker), one tonal (the three notes), plus the wildcard.
  7. Live Play any two together and listen for the wobble-against-wobble — two human leans coexisting over one grid.
  8. Live That friction is the sound you are keeping.
Expected result

Four to six clean-cycling loops whose repeats are exact and whose insides lean — machine boundaries around human timing, the record’s mechanism in your hands.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The loop hiccups at the cycle pointEdge cut mid-sound — the boundary lands inside a hit or a ringMove the crop to a gap; let a decay finish before the edge
Two loops together feel like a mess, not a grooveBoth leaning the same direction, or one bar length fighting the otherSwap one loop for a different take, or halve one loop’s length
Phase 4 · ≈ 25 min

The build — and the quantize experiment

Phase focus: the build — and the quantize experiment. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live In Arrangement, build 3 minutes: first loop alone for a while, then each new loop entering on its own — never two at once.
  2. Live Steal the record’s pacing, not its sounds.
  3. Live Let at least one loop leave and return — an exit is an event too, as the entry map showed.
  4. Live The experiment: duplicate one rhythmic loop, and on the copy add a warp marker to every hit and drag each onto the grid.
  5. Live A/B the copy against the original in the full stack.
  6. Live + Logbook Logbook, two lines: what drained out of the quantized copy, and which loop’s wobble the piece cannot live without.
  7. Live + Logbook Keep or kill the copy accordingly — almost always kill.
  8. Live Play your three minutes end to end at the mark, then A/B a minute against REF at matched loudness, judging warmth and lean only.
Expected result

A three-minute build of staggered entries, one murdered duplicate proving by absence what the wobble carries, and a logbook line naming the load-bearing loop.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The build feels flat — entries don’t registerLoops too similar in register and level, every arrival pre-maskedSpace entries further apart and drop the stack’s level just before each one
The quantized copy sounds better to youThe original take’s lean was sloppiness, not feelRe-record that gesture with conviction; a push is a direction, not an accident
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Which loop’s human wobble is load-bearing?
    Answer

    The quantize experiment answers this empirically: the loop whose straightened copy drained the most life from the stack is the load-bearing one — usually the main rhythmic gesture, because its lean is what every other loop leans against. If killing the wobble changed nothing, your loops were never really leaning, and phase 2 wants another take. Relisten to the record’s kit asking the same question; its drag is structural, not decorative.

  • How does the record sequence its entries?
    Answer

    One at a time, with patience — and almost always so that each arrival recasts what was already playing rather than just adding to it. Relisten with your entry map from phase 1 in hand and correct it; the second listen usually finds an entry the first one missed, and the gap between your two maps is a measure of how much the build was doing while you thought you were hearing texture.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 007 — exact repetition is the fingerprint of capture, and you just made it musical. The lab studies what each capture path adds on top of that fingerprint, generation by generation.
