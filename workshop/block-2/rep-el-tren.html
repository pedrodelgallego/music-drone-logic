Replicate: the microphone as camera

Copy one clear section of Chris Watson — El Tren Fantasma. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: one deliberate microphone position per take — inside the carriage, beside the track, under it, across the valley — cut together so a shared sound bridges the listener across every edit. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone at one fixed gain for all five takes; mic into M4 input 1 only if the subject reaches the desk — a kettle does, a street does not. DT 990 Pro for the edit
  • New set saved as REP-EL-TREN — no tempo; the subject’s own cycle is the clock
  • Field line: streets and machines — watch traffic with headphones on, keep hands and cables clear of moving parts, ask before recording people; monitor at a moderate fixed level
  • What you will make: a short replica of Chris Watson — El Tren Fantasma. Focus on this effect: one continuous, cinematic train journey across Mexico - assembled from many captures, days, and positions, with joins a listener cannot find.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a deliberate perspective per take - inside the carriage, beside the track, under it, across the valley.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Chris Watson — El Tren Fantasma. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many perspectives can you count in the track?
    Answer

    Several — inside the carriage, beside the rails, underneath structures, across open distance. Each position is clear within seconds, the way a film cut from cab to crane is.

  2. Can you find a single join?
    Answer

    Barely. Where you do spot one, notice what is happening: a sound common to both sides of the cut — the engine, the rail rhythm, the horn — is already playing, and it carries you over the join. The cut hides inside the subject’s own continuity.

  3. Did this journey ever happen?
    Answer

    Not as one ride. It is many captures from many days and positions, assembled into a continuous journey. Each individual recording is real; the seamless trip is composed.

  4. How clean is each capture?
    Answer

    Very clean — no handling noise, no wind thumps, no accidents, even trackside under a moving train. Every take was prepared: position chosen, mic protected, body still before recording.

  5. What makes it one train rather than five recordings?
    Answer

    The train’s constant voice — its horn, rail rhythm, engine pitch — is present in every take. The microphone position changes; the train does not. So the ear treats each new shot as the same subject from a different place.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a 3-minute journey cut from five takes of one subject, where each edit reads as the same subject from a new place. A solo-flip against REF lands on the same shot-to-shot clarity.
  • Write down your five positions before you record — a shot list: wide, mid, close, under or behind, inside or coupled.
  • One fixed gain for all five takes. Distance comes from where you stand, not from turning the knob. If you adjust gain between positions, the perspective difference becomes dishonest.
  • Place each cut where the subject’s constant voice — its hum, rhythm, or horn-equivalent — is already sounding on both sides.

The piece in one row: one repeatable subject, five microphone positions written down before recording, five takes at one fixed gain, and an arrangement where every hard cut lands on a sound both takes share.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the shot list

Phase focus: the reference and the shot list. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag one El Tren Fantasma track in, and loop it.
  2. Audio Effects → Utility, trim to ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Choose a subject that repeats reliably: a street’s traffic cycle, a washing machine’s program, a kettle you can reboil.
  4. If it cannot perform twice, it cannot be shot five times.
  5. Write the shot list — five positions with a predicted sound for each: “under the table: more drum, less hiss”;
  6. “across the room: mostly reflection.” Wide, mid, close, under or behind, inside or coupled.
  7. Walk the route once without the recorder, standing in each position for thirty seconds, ears only.
  8. Adjust the list to what the ears report.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Chris Watson — El Tren Fantasma (2011) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the shot list — as the smallest Live set that proves one deliberate microphone position per take — inside the carriage, beside the track, under it, across the valley — cut together so a shared sound bridges the listener across every edit.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects one continuous train journey across Mexico — assembled from many captures, many days, and many positions, with joins a listener cannot find.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the shot list into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates one deliberate microphone position per take — inside the carriage, beside the track, under it, across the valley — cut together so a shared sound bridges the listener across every edit, not just the surface of Chris Watson — El Tren Fantasma (2011).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.

The five takes — one gain, five places

Do this

  2. by the lab-001 ledger if you go through the M4. It does not move for any take.
  3. Record 60–90 seconds per position, the subject running its cycle in each.

Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The cut as camera move

Phase focus: the cut as camera move. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the five takes onto one track named JOURNEY, fader at 0 dB — no clip-gain balancing. The volume differences between positions should stay: they represent distance.
  2. Plan the order on paper first. Wide → mid → close → inside → under → wide is an approach and departure. Choose your own arc and write it down.
  3. Cut a 3-minute sequence, 20–50 seconds per shot.
  4. Place each cut where the constant voice is already sounding on both sides — on its beat if it has one.
  5. Use hard cuts (butt cuts), not crossfades. A crossfade suggests the cut did not work on its own. A shared sound is what carries the listener across — not a fade.
  6. Render and listen end to end at the mark, no touching.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The same-subject test and the A/B

Phase focus: the same-subject test and the A/B. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the 3 minutes end to end at the mark, no touching.
  2. At each cut, name silently what changed: place, or subject?
  3. Only place is allowed.
  4. If a second listener is available, ask afterward: “how many things were you hearing?” The right answer is one.
  5. Record their answer.
  6. A/B one of your cuts against one of REF’s joins at matched loudness, judging two things only: does the shared sound bridge yours as confidently, and is your take as clean?
  7. Logbook: one line per position — what it actually changed (subject-to-room ratio, register, attack detail).
  8. Keep the table; it is reusable on every recording you ever make.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your journey at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does each cut read as the same subject from a new place?
    Answer

    That is the pass condition. A listener should report hearing one thing from several places — the way a film viewer reports one train. If a cut reads as a new subject, the shared sound was probably missing at the join, or the subject itself changed state between takes. Relisten to the record’s joins: the bridge sound is always already playing before the cut arrives.

  • What did each position actually change?
    Answer

    Three things, in different amounts: the balance of subject to room; the frequencies that stand out (close positions favor attack and fine detail, far positions favor wash and reflection); and how much other sound leaks in around the subject. Your five-line table captures this in your own words — and it applies to every future recording you make. Relisten to one wide and one close passage of the record and note which of those three things changed.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2, and the position table seeds the one-minute miniature in Lab 006.
  • Carries into the lab: “you compose the recording with your feet” — this record runs that idea as a full system: five positions, five takes, one subject.
