Block 2 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the microphone as camera

Copy one clear section of Chris Watson — El Tren Fantasma. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a deliberate perspective per take - inside the carriage, beside the track, under it, across the valley - cut together so a shared sound carries the listener over every edit. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: recorder or phone at one fixed gain for all five takes; mic into M4 input 1 only if the subject reaches the desk — a kettle does, a street does not. DT 990 Pro for the edit
  • New set saved as REP-EL-TREN — no tempo; the subject’s own cycle is the clock
  • Field line: streets and machines — watch traffic with headphones on, keep hands and cables clear of moving parts, ask before recording people; monitor at a moderate fixed level
  • What you will make: a short replica of Chris Watson — El Tren Fantasma. Focus on this effect: one continuous, cinematic train journey across Mexico - assembled from many captures, days, and positions, with joins a listener cannot find.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a deliberate perspective per take - inside the carriage, beside the track, under it, across the valley.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Chris Watson — El Tren Fantasma. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many perspectives can you count in the track?
    Answer

    Several per track - inside the carriage, beside the rails, underneath structures, across open distance - each one unmistakable within seconds, the way a film cut from cab to crane is.

  2. Can you find a single join?
    Answer

    Barely - and where you do, notice what is happening: a sound common to both shots (the engine, the rail rhythm, the horn) is sounding across the cut, carrying you over it. The join hides inside the subject’s own continuity.

  3. Did this journey ever happen?
    Answer

    Not as one ride - it is many captures from many days and positions, assembled into a continuous fiction. The honesty is in the perspectives, not the chronology; each shot is real, and the journey is composed.

  4. How clean is each capture?
    Answer

    Immaculate - no handling, no wind thumps, no accidents, even trackside under a moving train. Every take was prepared: position chosen, mic protected, body still.

  5. What makes it one train rather than five recordings?
    Answer

    The subject’s constant voice - horn, rail rhythm, engine pitch - survives every change of position, so the ear files each new shot under the same protagonist. Everything local changes; the protagonist does not.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a 3-minute “journey” cut from five takes of one subject, where each edit reads as the same subject from a new place — a solo-flip against REF lands on the same shot-to-shot confidence.
  • Positions chosen on paper before any recording — a shot list: wide, mid, close, under or behind, inside or coupled.
  • One fixed gain for all five takes — distance must be performed by your feet, not the knob, or the perspectives lie.
  • Joins carried by a shared sound: cut only where the subject’s constant voice is sounding on both sides.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one repeatable subject, five microphone positions written down before recording, five takes at one fixed gain, and an Arrangement where every hard cut lands on a sound both takes share.

The shot list as a map. One subject, five stations for the microphone — distance and angle are the only variables. On the record this map is spread across Mexico; on your street it is spread across twenty meters.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the shot list

Phase focus: the reference and the shot list. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag one El Tren Fantasma track in, and loop it.
  2. Live Audio Effects → Utility, trim to ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Live Choose a subject that repeats reliably: a street’s traffic cycle, a washing machine’s program, a kettle you can reboil.
  4. Live If it cannot perform twice, it cannot be shot five times.
  5. Logbook Write the shot list — five positions with a predicted sound for each: “under the table: more drum, less hiss”;
  6. Live “across the room: mostly reflection.” Wide, mid, close, under or behind, inside or coupled.
  7. Recorder Walk the route once without the recorder, standing in each position for thirty seconds, ears only.
  8. Recorder Adjust the list to what the ears report.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one repeatable subject is chosen, and a five-position shot list with predictions exists on paper, corrected by one ears-only walk.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The five positions all sound alike on the walkPositions spread in angle but not in distance or couplingPush the extremes: one shot touching or under the subject, one across the full space
No “inside / coupled” position seems possibleThinking in air onlyCouple through a surface — recorder on the machine’s lid, against the door frame, on the pipe
Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

The five takes — one gain, five places

Phase focus: the five takes — one gain, five places. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Set the gain once on the loudest position — usually the close or coupled shot — so its peaks sit safe;
  2. by the lab-001 ledger if you go through the M4. It does not move for any take.
  3. Record 60–90 seconds per position, the subject running its cycle in each.
  4. Recorder Body still, recorder set down or braced — no handling.
  5. Recorder Make sure the subject’s constant voice — its hum, rhythm, or horn-equivalent — is present in every take;
  6. Recorder a shot without the protagonist is scenery.
  7. Recorder Log each take, lab-006 style: position · distance · level.
  8. Recorder If the subject drifts between takes (traffic thins, the machine changes phase), re-run the cycle rather than accepting a take where the protagonist changed character.
Expected result

Five clean takes at one gain, each a distinct perspective on the same running subject, each with a log line — the raw footage of a journey that has not been cut yet.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The close take clipsGain set on the wide shotSet gain on the loudest position first and keep it for all five — quiet wide takes are correct, clipped close ones are forever
The takes sound like five different subjects alreadyThe subject changed state between positionsShoot all five within one cycle, or restart the cycle per take so the protagonist matches
Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The cut as camera move

Phase focus: the cut as camera move. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Import the five takes onto one track named JOURNEY, fader at 0 dB — no clip-gain balancing;
  2. Live the levels are the distances.
  3. Live Order the shots as a story on paper first: wide → mid → close → inside → under → wide is an approach and departure;
  4. Logbook choose yours and write it down.
  5. Logbook Cut a 3-minute sequence, 20–50 seconds per shot.
  6. Logbook Place each cut where the constant voice is sounding on both sides — on its beat if it has one.
  7. Logbook Butt cuts, not crossfades — the record’s grammar is the hard cut carried by shared sound;
  8. Logbook a crossfade here is an apology.
  9. Logbook Render and listen end to end at the mark, no touching.
Expected result

Three minutes that read as one subject visited from five places in a deliberate order — each cut a camera move, none of them an accident.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A cut reads as a new subject, not a new placeNo shared sound across the joinMove the cut to a moment where the constant voice sounds on both sides; let it bridge
The journey feels like a list of filesShot order has no arcRe-order as approach or departure — distance should trend, not shuffle
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The same-subject test and the A/B

Phase focus: the same-subject test and the A/B. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play the 3 minutes end to end at the mark, no touching.
  2. At each cut, name silently what changed: place, or subject?
  3. Only place is allowed.
  4. If a second listener is available, ask afterward: “how many things were you hearing?” The right answer is one.
  5. Record their answer.
  6. Live A/B one of your cuts against one of REF’s joins at matched loudness, judging two things only: does the shared sound bridge yours as confidently, and is your take as clean?
  7. Logbook Logbook: one line per position — what it actually changed (subject-to-room ratio, register, attack detail).
  8. Logbook Keep the table; it is reusable on every recording you ever make.
Expected result

A listener count of one subject, an A/B that comes down to bridge sounds and capture cleanliness, and a five-line position table in the logbook.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The listener counts two or more subjectsOne take’s protagonist drifted, or a join lacks its bridgeFind the cut where the count jumps; re-place it on shared sound, or re-shoot that position
Your joins need crossfades to surviveCuts placed in silence or in unmatched washHard cuts only — move each join to a beat of the constant voice and try again
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your journey at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Does each cut read as the same subject from a new place?
    Answer

    That is the pass condition, and the one-question test measures it: a listener should report hearing one thing from several places, the way a film viewer reports one train. If a cut reads as a new subject, the positions were not telling one story — usually the shared sound was missing at the join, sometimes the subject itself changed state between takes. Relisten to the record’s joins and notice the bridge sound is always already playing before the cut arrives.

  • What did each position actually change?
    Answer

    Three things, in different ratios: the balance of subject to room, the register that dominates (close favors attack and detail, far favors wash and reflection), and how much of the world leaks in around the protagonist. Your five-line table is the answer in your own words — and it transfers: every future capture is a position on this same map. Relisten to one wide and one coupled passage of the record back to back and file them in the table’s terms.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2, and the position table seeds the one-minute miniature in Lab 006.
  • Carries into the lab: “you compose the recording with your feet” is the étude’s perspective concept — this record is that concept run as a system: five takes, five compositions of one truth.
