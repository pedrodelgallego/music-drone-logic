Replicate: the erased attack

Copy one clear section of Stars of the Lid — Requiem for Dying Mothers. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: sustained instrument notes faded in past their entire attack, layered as slow-moving dyads under long reverb, with the rise and fall of level as the sole structure. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — and optionally a mic or a guitar into the M4 instead; any source that can hold a note for twenty seconds qualifies
  • The record’s strings and horns are unavailable to you — you are replicating the system: sustained notes with their attacks (the beginning of the sound) removed. The fade does the same job the bow does. Once the beginning is gone, a MicroFreak note and a cello note are closer than you think
  • New set saved as REP-REQUIEM — no tempo worth setting; sections are marked by swells, not bars
  • Long reverb at sustained level creeps upward without announcing it — phones at the lab-001 mark, a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes — the printing is quick; the time goes into erasing and listening for survivors
  • What you will make: a short replica of Stars of the Lid — Requiem for Dying Mothers. Focus on this effect: instrumental tones that are already sounding whenever you notice them - no note ever begins, no source can be named, and the only event is a swell.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: sustained instrument notes faded in past their entire attack, layered as slow-moving dyads under long reverb, with the rise and fall of level as the sole structure.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Stars of the Lid — Requiem for Dying Mothers. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Can you say when any note begins?
    Answer

    No — every tone is already sounding by the time you notice it. There is no moment of onset anywhere on the track. The beginnings have been faded out of existence.

  2. What instrument is playing?
    Answer

    You cannot say for certain — strings, horns, organ, all plausible, none provable. The attack (the very start of a note) is where an instrument’s identity lives: the bow’s scrape, the hammer, the breath. Erase it and all sources blur into one anonymous sustain.

  3. What is the shape of one swell, start to finish?
    Answer

    A rise over tens of seconds, and a fall that is usually even longer. You measure these shapes against the pace of slow breathing, not against a beat.

  4. How many layers are sounding at any moment?
    Answer

    Fewer than it feels — usually two or three, fused together by the reverb and by the missing attacks. What sounds like a melody is mostly one layer getting louder while another retreats.

  5. What marks a section when there are no drums and no bar lines?
    Answer

    The swell. A rise is a phrase, a peak is a downbeat, a long fall is a cadence. The shape of the volume level does every structural job that rhythm normally does.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a listener can answer neither “what instrument was that?” nor “when did any note begin?” Both questions must fail, the way they fail against the record.
  • Every clip’s attack is faded past the point where it can be heard. The rule, applied without exceptions: if you can hear a note start, make the fade longer.
  • Pairs of notes (dyads) layered so one swells while another retreats. The swell is the only allowed event.
  • Long reverb fuses the layers — but the erasure must already work without reverb first. Reverb does not hide a bad fade; it only completes a good one.

How the piece works: sustained notes recorded as pairs, clip fades extended past every attack until no note is heard starting, overlapping swells drawn as volume automation, and one long reverb that fuses everything into a single unidentifiable sustain.

MicroFreak · voice · guitar — sustained notes → printed dyads · clip fades erase every attack → overlapped swells · volume automation → long reverb · the source dissolves

One note in three states. The recorded note starts with a clear attack — and that attack reveals what instrument it is. The fade replaces the attack with a slope longer than the original attack. In the finished swell, there is no audible edge left. By the time you hear it, it sounds like it has always been there.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the swell clock

Phase focus: the reference and the swell clock. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Requiem for Dying Mothers” into it, and loop four characteristic minutes.
  2. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Time one full swell — first audibility to gone — and write the rise and fall in seconds.
  4. These numbers calibrate phase 3.
  5. Write your best guess at the instrumentation in one line.
  6. Keep it; the decision check returns to it.
  7. ⌘ T; rename NOTES, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Stars of the Lid — Requiem for Dying Mothers (2001, The Tired Sounds of Stars of the Lid) and use Live audio/MIDI tracks, Utility, automation, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the swell clock — as the smallest Live set that proves sustained instrument notes faded in past their entire attack, layered as slow-moving dyads under long reverb, with the rise and fall of level as the sole structure.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects instrument tones that are already sounding by the time you notice them — no note ever starts, no source can be identified, and the only event is a slow swell in volume.
  1. Use Analog Rytm MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the swell clock into drum voices or samples, trig conditions, parameter locks, scenes, performance macros, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates sustained instrument notes faded in past their entire attack, layered as slow-moving dyads under long reverb, with the rise and fall of level as the sole structure, not just the surface of Stars of the Lid — Requiem for Dying Mothers (2001, The Tired Sounds of Stars of the Lid).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one swell is timed in seconds, an instrumentation guess is on paper, and one armed track waits for notes.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Erase the attacks, build the swells

Phase focus: erase the attacks, build the swells. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. On every clip, drag the fade-in handle out to at least 5–8 seconds and the fade-out longer — toward the rise and fall you timed on the record in phase 1.
  2. Play each clip solo.
  3. The rule, enforced without mercy: if you can hear the note start, erase further — longer fade, gentler curve.
  4. The note must be discovered mid-air, never witnessed taking off.
  5. Arrange the dyads across 4–5 minutes so they overlap: dyad two already rising while dyad one falls.
  6. No silence between events, no gap for a “start” to live in.
  7. Draw track volume automation over the top for the larger swells — one rise and fall spanning each dyad, peaks landing where the phase-1 stopwatch says the record’s peaks land.
  8. Listen once dry, straight through.
  9. The erasure must already work without reverb: no nameable instrument, no findable onsets.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

The dissolve — reverb, and the two-question test

Phase focus: the dissolve — reverb, and the two-question test. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Create a return track with Audio Effects → Reverb: decay 8 s or more, high diffusion, dry/wet 100% on the return.
  2. Send both voice tracks generously.
  3. Re-balance so the reverb fuses the layers without drowning the swell shapes — the rises and falls must still read.
  4. A/B one minute against REF at matched loudness, judging only the dissolve: are your tones as anonymous, your beginnings as absent?
  5. The two-question test, on yourself honestly or on a fresh listener: what instrument was that? and when did any note begin? Both answers must fail.
  6. Log the answers verbatim.
  7. Take the scheduled break — long reverb flatters everything, and the post-break listen is the honest one.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Can a listener say what instrument it was?
    Answer

    They should not be able to — guesses only, none confident. An instrument's identity lives in its attack, and you erased it. What remains is sustain, which all instruments share. Compare this with your own phase-1 guess about the record: you never settled on an answer either, and that uncertainty is the effect working. If your listener answers instantly and correctly, a beginning survived somewhere. Find it.

  • Can anyone say when a note began?
    Answer

    No — the honest answer is “it was there when I noticed it.” Notes must be discovered, never witnessed arriving. If anyone can put a timestamp on any onset, that clip goes back to phase 3 for a longer fade. Relisten to the record and try to find one timestamp. The record refuses, and yours must refuse the same way.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R2.
  • Carries into the lab: back to Lab 005 — the System Spec calls for a tone with no edges, long attack and long release. You have now built that edgelessness by hand, one fade at a time, and you know what a surviving edge costs: the whole dissolve collapses.
