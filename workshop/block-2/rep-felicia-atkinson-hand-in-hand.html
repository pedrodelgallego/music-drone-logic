Replicate: voice, field and synth as one palette

Copy one clear section of Félicia Atkinson — “Hand in Hand”. Build a short study where spoken voice, a field recording and a synth sit as equals, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill is about one decision: treat spoken voice, field recordings and synth as one equal palette of sources. No element is foreground “content” with the others as backing — the voice is a sound among sounds, the field recording carries as much weight as a synth pad, and the listener cannot tell which is the “real” subject. Do only the steps that make that equality audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: a mic into M4 input 1 for voice and a close field capture, one synth voice, DT 990 Pro for judging, and the lab-006 capture log with a column for which of the three families each take belongs to
  • New set saved as REP-HAND-IN-HAND — no tempo grid; this is timed in seconds and breathes at speaking pace
  • Three quiet sources need careful gain — set on the loudest of the three (usually the synth), peaks ≈ −12 dBFS; phones at the lab-001 mark, because whispering invites the knob up
  • Timebox: 90–120 minutes — one round of capturing all three families, one balance pass, one short study, then file it and stop
  • What you will make: a short replica of Félicia Atkinson — “Hand in Hand”. Focus on this effect: voice, field and synth held on one plane as equals.
  • What you will turn off: the instinct to mix the voice loud and centred with everything else as backing.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: spoken voice, field recordings and synth treated as one equal palette of sources.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not one element quietly taking over.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Félicia Atkinson — “Hand in Hand”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Which of the three is the “main” element?
    Answer

    None of them, and that is the point. The voice does not sit on top as a song would; the field recording is not just ambience; the synth is not just a pad. At any moment your attention can settle on any one of them and find it complete.

  2. How is the voice treated?
    Answer

    As a sound, not a message. It is close, often murmured, sometimes barely louder than the room around it. You attend to its grain and rhythm as much as its words — it sits at the same level and the same intimacy as the other two.

  3. What does the field recording contribute?
    Answer

    A real place that the voice and synth inhabit. It is not background colour you could remove — its handling and detail are composed with the same care as the tones, and it carries the same weight in the balance.

  4. What does the synth contribute?
    Answer

    A tone that meets the voice and field on equal terms — never a bed under them, never a lead over them. It is one more colour in a palette of three families that the piece draws from freely.

  5. Where is the composition, if all three are equal?
    Answer

    In the balance and the joins. Choosing which family speaks when, holding all three near the same level, and letting attention pass between them — those decisions are the form. The equality is engineered, not accidental.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: one 60–90 second study where voice, field and synth share one plane and no family dominates.
  • Three families, captured close and quiet: spoken voice / one field recording / one synth tone.
  • The interest comes from balance and hand-offs between equals, not from a lead over a backing.
  • Level, panning and timing only — heavy processing that turns one family into mere texture breaks the equality.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the equality rule

Phase focus: the reference and the equality rule. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag the reference section in, and loop it at ≈ −12 dBFS with Audio Effects → Utility.
  2. Make three tracks, one per family: VOICE, FIELD, SYNTH.
  3. Write the rule in full: all three families sit at the same intimacy and the same level — none is the subject, none is decoration.
  4. Note the intimacy target: everything captured close and quiet, as if held near the ear.
  5. Decide the membership test: if I muted any one family, the study should feel diminished, not merely less busy.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Félicia Atkinson — “Hand in Hand” and use audio tracks for voice and field plus one instrument for synth.
  2. Build the phase goal — the reference and the equality rule — as the smallest Live set that proves spoken voice, field recordings and synth treated as one equal palette of sources.
  3. Keep each family on a named track, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects whether the three stay equal.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: load voice, field and a resampled synth tone into Flex machines, then drive scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the control path so it demonstrates spoken voice, field recordings and synth treated as one equal palette of sources, not just the surface of Félicia Atkinson — “Hand in Hand”.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the balance move in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the three families stay equal.
Expected result

The reference loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, three named family tracks exist, and the equality rule plus the intimacy target are on paper before any capture.

Phase 2 · ≈ 35 min

Capture all three at the same intimacy

Phase focus: capture the three families. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Record 30–60 seconds of close, low voice — speak, do not perform; let pauses and breath stay in. Peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Record 30–60 seconds of one field source close — a kitchen, a window, a small machine — captured at the same near distance as the voice, not as wide room tone.
  3. Play 30–60 seconds of one synth tone at the same quiet level, gentle attack, nothing that demands the foreground.
  4. Log each take with its family letter — the lab-006 log gains a family column here instead of place/distance.
  5. Listen to each family alone: is it interesting enough to hold attention on its own? If not, re-capture before moving on.
  6. Trim each to its best continuous stretch; keep the intimacy consistent across all three.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Félicia Atkinson — “Hand in Hand” and use audio clips for voice and field plus one instrument for synth.
  2. Build the phase goal — capture the three families — as the smallest Live set that proves spoken voice, field recordings and synth treated as one equal palette of sources.
  3. Keep each family on a named track, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample each family to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects whether the three stay equal.

Expected result

Three captures — voice, field, synth — each captured close and quiet, each able to hold attention alone, all at a matched intimacy and logged by family.

Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

The study — balance to one plane

Phase focus: the study. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Lay all three families in Arrangement and set them to roughly the same level — the equality is decided here, in the balance.
  2. Stagger the entries so attention can pass between them: voice alone, then field joins, then synth, then voice recedes while field carries.
  3. Keep gaps — let one family be alone sometimes, so each is shown to be load-bearing.
  4. Pan gently to give each a place, but never push one to the centre and the others to the edges.
  5. Resist compressing the voice loud or ducking the others under it; if you reach for that, the equality is gone.
  6. Print the study to one audio track named PRINT.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Félicia Atkinson — “Hand in Hand” and use the three named tracks plus level and pan automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — the study — as the smallest Live set that proves spoken voice, field recordings and synth treated as one equal palette of sources.
  3. Keep each family on a named track, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects whether the three stay equal.

Expected result

One 60–90 second study where voice, field and synth share one plane, each gets a moment alone, and attention can rest on any of them at will.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your study at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Could a listener say which family is the subject?
    Answer

    In a working study, no — and they should hesitate when asked. If they answer instantly “the voice,” one family has taken over and the palette is no longer equal. Re-balance until attention can be steered to any of the three by will, not pulled to one by the mix. The record passes this test continuously — that even plane is its whole character.

  • Does each family feel load-bearing?
    Answer

    Mute each one in turn. If muting any of them makes the study feel like something essential left, the equality is real. If muting the field or synth changes nothing — it was decoration, not a source. Re-capture the weak family or give it a moment alone in the arrangement so it carries its share, the way all three carry the reference.
