Room & Monitoring

A short primer on the listening environment. This workshop is mainly headphone-based — you already own the Beyerdynamic DT 990 Pro. This page explains what those headphones do well, what they don't, and what to do about it. It also covers what to fix if you add nearfield monitors in Block 1.

The core problem

Every room changes the sound you hear. Parallel walls cause bass to pile up in corners. Nearby surfaces bounce sound back to your ears a few milliseconds after the direct signal arrives — this is a first reflection. If you mix in a room with these problems and don't know they are there, your decisions about bass and reverb will be wrong. Open-back headphones like the DT 990 Pro sidestep the room entirely — but they create a different problem: they don't tell you how the mix will sound on speakers.

Speaker placement

If you add nearfield monitors (Block 1), where you put them matters as much as which ones you buy. Four rules cover most of it.

Equilateral trianglePosition the two speakers and your head at the corners of an equilateral triangle (all sides equal). A common starting point is about 1 metre between speakers and 1 metre from each speaker to your ears. This is the "sweet spot" — the place where stereo imaging is most accurate.
Tweeters at ear heightThe tweeter (small driver, handles high frequencies) should point directly at your ears when you are seated. Angling monitors inward slightly — "toe-in" — helps aim the tweeters correctly.
Pull off the front wallPlacing speakers flush against the wall behind them causes bass build-up (the wall reflects low frequencies back, adding them to the direct signal). Pull speakers at least 30–60 cm away from the front wall to reduce this.
Symmetrical side wallsIf one speaker is 50 cm from a side wall and the other is 120 cm, you will hear different amounts of reflection from each side. This makes the stereo image lean. Keep both speakers the same distance from their respective side walls.

First-reflection points

A first reflection is sound that bounces off a surface and arrives at your ears just after the direct sound. Your brain hears two copies of the same signal a few milliseconds apart — this smears the stereo image and makes it hard to judge reverb tails accurately.

The mirror trick — finding first-reflection points

Sit in your listening position. Have someone move a small mirror along the side wall at speaker height. Wherever you can see a speaker reflected in the mirror, that is a first-reflection point. The same applies to the ceiling (above and slightly forward of your position). Mark those spots — that is where treatment will help most.

Bass build-up in corners

Low frequencies have long wavelengths (a 100 Hz wave is about 3.4 metres long). In a small room, these waves bounce between parallel walls and stack up — some frequencies get louder, others get quieter. This is called a room mode. The effect is worst in corners, where three surfaces meet.

What it sounds like

You mix a kick drum or bass tone and it sounds balanced. You move two metres to the side and it sounds twice as loud, or nearly gone. The bass has not changed — your position relative to the room mode has. This is why "walking the room" while listening is a useful habit: if bass changes dramatically as you move, the room is colouring your judgment.

Basic treatment

Acoustic treatment reduces the room's effect on what you hear. You do not need a purpose-built studio. A few targeted materials make a real difference.

Absorption panels — for reflections

  • Thick, dense, porous material (acoustic foam or rigid fibreglass/rockwool) placed at the first-reflection points you found with the mirror trick
  • Aim for panels at least 5 cm thick — thin foam does almost nothing for low-mid frequencies
  • Positioned on the side walls, ceiling reflection point, and the wall directly behind you (rear wall)
  • Result: less smear in the stereo image; reverb tails are easier to judge

Bass traps — for corners

  • Floor-to-ceiling panels of thick, dense material placed in room corners — especially the front corners (behind the speakers)
  • Bass traps need to be thick (10–30 cm) to affect low frequencies; thin foam does not work for bass
  • Even one or two well-placed corner panels flatten the worst room-mode peaks
  • Result: bass decisions in mixes translate better to other systems

Open-back headphones — what they do and don't do

The DT 990 Pro are open-back headphones (the ear cups have an open grille — you can hear some room sound, and people nearby can hear your headphones). This design has specific strengths and limits for mixing.

What they do wellThey are accurate and detailed in the mid and high frequencies. They sidestep room modes and first reflections entirely — you hear the signal, not the room. They are comfortable for long sessions. They are consistent: they sound the same every time you put them on.
What they don't doHeadphone stereo is perceived "inside the head" rather than projected in front of you — this makes it hard to judge wide panning and reverb tails the way they will sound on speakers. Bass on open-back headphones can be harder to judge for translation: the low end that sounds right on headphones may be too heavy or too thin on speakers. They do not tell you how the mix will sound in a room.
TranslationA mix that "translates" sounds correct on multiple systems — speakers, car stereo, phone, earbuds. Headphone-only mixes often do not translate without a check on at least one speaker system. The DT 990 Pro are a strong primary monitor for this workshop; adding a speaker check (Block 5) makes them even more useful.
  • Check on multiple systems. After finishing a mix on headphones, play it back on one or two other systems — a Bluetooth speaker, a phone speaker, earbuds. Listen for: does the bass disappear or boom? Does the stereo collapse in mono? These checks catch what headphones hide.
  • Use reference tracks. Find two or three released pieces in a style close to what you are making and play them at the same level as your mix. If your mix sits far from all the references in a specific way (far too bright, far too little bass), that is a finding — not a taste preference. The monitoring lab in Block 1 Lab 002 and the translation lab in Block 5 both build this habit.

The signal flow: from mix to ear

Mix in DAW→ Audio interface (MOTU M4)→ Headphones or monitors→ Room (monitors only)→ Ears

With headphones, the room step is removed — which is the advantage. With monitors, the room is in the chain, which is why placement and treatment matter.

What to do now vs. what can wait

Do now — no costSit at a consistent position when you monitor. Walk the room while a bass-heavy track plays and notice where bass peaks. Use reference tracks to calibrate your ears to the headphones.
Block 1 — if adding monitorsApply the equilateral triangle and tweeter-at-ear-height rules before listening critically. Use the mirror trick to find first-reflection points. Add a thick panel (rockwool or acoustic foam, at least 5 cm) at each side-wall reflection point.
Later — if neededCorner bass traps; ceiling cloud; rear-wall absorption. Add these if bass decisions consistently do not translate to other systems after Block 5 translation checks.
