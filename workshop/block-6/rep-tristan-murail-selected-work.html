Replicate: chords built from FM spectra

Copy one mechanism from Tristan Murail — Gondwana (1980): build a chord from a frequency-modulation calculation, voice it as one fused timbre, then slowly morph it into a second FM chord. Build a short version on the rig and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a chord is not chosen by ear — its pitches are computed from carrier and modulator frequencies (FM sidebands) or from sums and differences (ring modulation), and the form is a slow interpolation from one such spectrum into another. Each chord is treated as a single instrument that transforms as one mass. You are stealing the structure (calculate spectrum → voice as one timbre → morph into the next), never Murail's pitches.

The system you are replicating
Source workTristan Murail — Gondwana (1980)
The effectMassive, slowly transforming chords that read as single fused timbres — bell-like, brass-like masses that mutate rather than progress
The mechanismChords derived from FM and ring-modulation spectra (carrier + modulator sidebands, sum/difference tones), each orchestrated as one timbre; the form is a long interpolation morphing one computed spectrum into another

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Live open and a calculator (or VCV Rack with FM operators) for working out sidebands; a way to enter exact pitches/frequencies for each computed partial
  • MicroFreak ready for FM-derived tones (its oscillator can produce FM/metallic spectra); Digitakt II ready to hold sustained sampled tones
  • Pen and paper for two chord charts — every pitch is computed from a carrier and modulator, not invented
  • What you will make: a short FM-spectrum morph. Focus on this effect: a single fused timbre that slowly transforms into a second fused timbre.
  • What you will turn off: melody, ear-chosen harmony, chord progressions, rhythm, and any pitch you picked because you liked it.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: chords are calculated from FM/ring-mod spectra and one mass interpolates into another.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are carrier/modulator choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 12 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to the first several minutes of Gondwana. Write one short answer per question before you touch the gear.

  1. Do the big chords read as harmony — separate notes you could name — or as single fused timbres?
    Answer

    As single fused timbres. Each mass is voiced so densely and so tightly that the ear hears one huge bell-like or brass-like sound rather than a stack of pitches. That fusion is the point of deriving the chord from one FM calculation: the partials belong to a single spectrum.

  2. What is being composed — a melody, or the slow morph between fused spectra?
    Answer

    The morph. There is essentially no tune to hum and no chord progression to follow. The parameter being composed is the gradual interpolation from one computed spectrum into the next — the chords mutate as masses, they do not move as harmony.

  3. When one mass becomes another, is the change sudden or a long interpolation?
    Answer

    A long interpolation. One spectrum slowly bends toward the next over many seconds — pitches glide, partials fade in and out, the index seems to swell. You rarely hear a cut; you hear one timbre becoming another.

  4. Do the chords sound metallic or bell-like rather than like ordinary tonal harmony?
    Answer

    Yes — distinctly metallic and bell-like. That inharmonic ring is the signature of FM and ring-modulation spectra: the sidebands and sum/difference tones land off the harmonic series, so the chords shimmer like struck metal rather than sounding like triads.

Now build the mechanism you described. Do not build from memory of the pitches.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Compute a chord from an FM calculation

Phase focus: get the chord from arithmetic, not from taste.

carrier C + modulator M → sidebands C ± n·M (and sum/difference for ring mod) → chord chart
Do this
  1. Pick a carrier frequency C and a modulator frequency M in a non-integer ratio (for example C = 220 Hz, M = 173 Hz). The off-ratio is what makes the result inharmonic and bell-like.
  2. Compute the FM sidebands: C, C±M, C±2M, C±3M — keep the five to eight that land in a usable range and drop any that go negative or too high. For a ring-mod flavour, also note the sum (C+M) and difference (C−M) tones.
  3. Convert each frequency to the nearest pitch you can play (note name + cents off) and write the chart. This chart IS your chord — you did not choose a single note of it by ear.
  4. Mark which sidebands are loudest (those nearest the carrier, at a moderate FM index) — those carry the timbre.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from the reference and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — Compute a chord from an FM calculation — as the smallest Live set that proves Chords derived from FM and ring-modulation spectra (carrier + modulator sidebands, sum/difference tones), each orchestrated as one timbre; the form is a long interpolation morphing one computed spectrum into another.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects Massive, slowly transforming chords that read as single fused timbres — bell-like, brass-like masses that mutate rather than progress.
  1. Use Analog Rytm MKII for this phase: translate Compute a chord from an FM calculation into drum voices or samples, trig conditions, parameter locks, scenes, performance macros, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates Chords derived from FM and ring-modulation spectra (carrier + modulator sidebands, sum/difference tones), each orchestrated as one timbre; the form is a long interpolation morphing one computed spectrum into another, not just the surface of the reference.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A written chord of five to eight frequencies, every one of them computed from your carrier and modulator — a spectrum, not a chosen harmony.

Phase 2 — ~30 minutes

Orchestrate the spectrum as one fused timbre

Phase focus: make the separate sidebands fuse into a single instrument.

computed sidebands → one tuned voice per partial (MicroFreak + Digitakt II + Live) → fused FM chord at master
Do this
  1. Tune one sustained voice to each computed partial — the MicroFreak's FM oscillator on the carrier and the metallic upper sidebands, Digitakt II holding sampled sustains, Live instruments for the rest. Use the exact cents offsets from your chart.
  2. Balance the levels to match the loudness order — carrier and nearest sidebands loudest, far sidebands quieter — so the spectrum reads as one weighted timbre.
  3. A/B: it should read as ONE bell-like or brass-like sound, not a chord. If you can pick out individual notes, soften and align the attacks, kill vibrato, and tighten tuning to the cents values until they fuse.

Phase 3 — ~30 minutes

Compute a second chord and interpolate one mass into the other

Phase focus: a slow, one-way morph from one FM spectrum to the next — never a cut.

Do this
  1. Compute a SECOND FM chord — change the carrier, the modulator ratio, or the FM index, and work out its sidebands the same way. Write the second chart next to the first.
  2. Write the morph order on paper first: which partial glides to its new pitch first, which fades, which appears, over how many bars. The interpolation order is the composition.
  3. Over a long stretch, automate each voice from its first-chord value to its second-chord value — glide pitches, swell or shrink the FM index/ratio, crossfade voices that exist only in one chord. Move one partial at a time.
  4. Record one continuous take of the full interpolation, first mass to second mass, with no edit point you could name — the timbre transforms as a single object.

One take that begins as the first fused FM chord and ends as the second, with the crossing so gradual nobody can point to where one mass became the other.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The second chord arrives as a sudden swapYou changed all voices at once instead of overlapping individual glidesSpread the interpolation across more voices and more bars; only one partial should be visibly moving at any moment
It reads as two chords with a melody in betweenThe gliding pitches became hummable lines instead of a fused morphSlow the glides, keep voices fused with identical envelopes, and crossfade so no single line stands out as a tune
Verify against the work

A/B your morph against a passage of Gondwana — matched loudness, 15 seconds of yours, 15 of theirs. Then answer two questions in writing.

Do your chords fuse into single metallic timbres the way theirs do?

Answer

If you can still pick out individual pitches in yours but theirs reads as one bell-like mass, your partials have not fused — the give-away is usually attack and level spread, not the FM math. Fusion is the proof you orchestrated a computed spectrum rather than voiced a chord by ear.

Is your change between masses as gradual and one-way as theirs?

Answer

Theirs has no nameable moment where one chord becomes the next; if yours does, you moved too much at once. The structure is a slow interpolation between two computed spectra, and an audible seam means you cut instead of morphing.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: "Matched: …" · "Missed: … (best guess why)" · "Change next: …"
  • Note which carrier:modulator ratio gave the most convincing bell-like fusion — that is your starting spectrum for the next attempt.
  • Note whether the morph or the fusion was the weaker half, and put the next session's time there.
