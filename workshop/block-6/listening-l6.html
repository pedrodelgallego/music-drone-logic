Systems & Chance — four ways to delegate a decision

Three listening sessions about works where the composer stopped choosing the notes. The skill here is hearing the rules behind the sound: what was decided in advance, what was delegated to chance or a machine, and what the human kept for themselves. Everything you build in this block is a delegation you design.

Listening programme · 3 sessions · one full cycle · feeds labs 023–026 and capstone C5 · complete

The point

A generative work is not a sequence of choices — it is a machine for making choices, and the recording you hear is one run of that machine. These sessions teach you to listen past the run to the machine itself, because in Block 6 you become the person who builds the machine and stands behind whatever it decides.

Listening is the loop

These sessions are not homework beside the labs — they are the first step of the operating loop. Every rule you write in this block is stolen from here, and the questions below are the same five System Spec questions the labs use, plus a sixth.

  • Programme: complete — the briefs, references, and method below are final in shape; the worksheets and replication lessons are included below.

New concepts — in plain words

Five terms carry this page. Here is what each one means, before any of them is used.

ConceptIn plain words
Generative system A set of rules that produces musical events on its own once started — like a garden rather than a painting. The composer designs the conditions; the music is what grows. The recording you buy is one season of that garden, not the garden itself.
Chance vs randomness Randomness is what a dice roll gives you; a chance operation is what you build around the dice — which questions the roll answers, which it may never touch. Cage rolled for thousands of decisions, but he chose which decisions were rollable. The design of the question is the composition.
State What the system remembers from one moment to the next. A coin has no state — every flip forgets the last. A tape loop is almost all state — everything it plays is what it remembered. Most musical systems sit between, and where they sit changes everything about how they behave.
Seed The starting condition that makes a “random” process repeatable. Same rules + same seed = the identical run; same rules + new seed = a sibling, recognizably from the same family but never the same piece. Asking “could this run ever happen twice?” is the fastest way into any generative work.
Boundary The fence around what the system may decide. Eno’s loops choose when notes coincide but never which notes exist; Cage’s charts choose everything except the charts. Listen for the fence: the things that never happen in a generative work were forbidden, not unlucky.

How to listen — the six-question frame

Every work in this programme gets the same six questions, answered in writing in your logbook. A few lines per question is enough; vagueness is the only wrong answer.

  1. Inputs — what raw material enters the system, and what decides when it enters?
  2. Transforms — what changes it, in what order?
  3. Feedback / variation — what keeps it alive over time?
  4. Constraints — what prevents clutter — and which constraints are rules rather than taste?
  5. Human role — what is performed, decided, curated — and what was delegated away?
  6. Likely audio path — what had to be routed, recorded, mixed, or performed for this to exist — and, separately, what did the control path look like: where did the decisions travel before any sound did?

The method is three passes per work. First pass: just listen. No notes, no pausing. Second pass: answer questions 1–5. Third pass: question 6 only — and in this block the sixth question is two diagrams, not one. You are guessing at rule sets, dice tables, and patch cords you cannot see. Guessing is the exercise.

A fourth pass — contemplative

For at least one work per session, add a pass with no analysis at all: ten unbroken minutes, fixed moderate level, eyes closed, doing nothing else. Generative and systems music is built to be lived in, not parsed — and some of what these works do only arrives after the analytical mind has given up looking for events. Do not take notes during it. Afterwards, write one sentence about what changed in your attention between minute two and minute eight. The analytical passes tell you how the machine works; this pass tells you what it is for.

Session 1 — Records: four delegation philosophies

Four answers to the same question — what does it mean to let something else choose? Listen to each with the six-question frame, then compare what each composer kept and what each gave away. The differences are the lesson; any one record alone would only teach its own surface.

01 John Cage — “Music of Changes” chance as discipline

1951: a piano piece composed by tossing coins against the I Ching, thousands of times, with every toss answering a question Cage had designed in advance — which sound, which duration, which dynamic, which tempo. It is the founding document of chance operations, and the great misunderstanding-killer: nothing about it is casual. Chance here is more laborious than choosing, and the labor lives entirely in the charts — the boundary — not in the rolls.

Listen for
  • The absence of intention at the phrase level — events follow each other without “leading” anywhere, and your ear’s constant attempt to invent a narrative anyway
  • What never happens: the ranges the charts forbade — listen for the fence, not the field
  • The pianist’s precision — every chance-chosen event is executed exactly; the performance is not improvised, only the composition was delegated
After — verify

Could you tell which decisions were rolled and which were designed? The sounds, durations, and dynamics were rolled; the vocabulary of possible sounds, the chart structure, and the fact of a virtuoso pianist were designed. If the piece felt like “anything could happen”, relisten to any two minutes and list what never happens — no repetition of a theme, but also no glissandi outside the charts, no events the notation could not capture.

02 Brian Eno — “2/1” (Music for Airports) co-prime loops as a chance machine

1978: a handful of sung notes, each on its own tape loop, each loop a different length chosen so the lengths share no common factor. Because the loops never line up the same way twice, the piece composes its own counterpoint forever — a chance system with no dice in it. This is the gentlest possible introduction to generative music, and the most rebuildable on your rig: a few loops, a few lengths, and the lengths do all the composing.

Listen for
  • How few elements there are — count the distinct notes; the answer is smaller than the music sounds
  • Coincidences: the moments two or three notes happen to sound together, and how your ear treats them as composed harmony
  • The absence of state — no loop knows what any other loop is doing, ever; whatever form you hear is in your head, not in the system
After — verify

What is the seed here? The loop lengths and the start positions — set those identically and the whole infinite piece replays identically; nudge one loop by a second and you get a sibling, never the same again. If you heard “a slow composition” rather than colliding cycles, relisten and track one single note: it returns at a perfectly fixed interval, indifferent to everything around it.

03 Laurie Spiegel — The Expanding Universe rules as an instrument, played

1980, made on GROOVE at Bell Labs — a computer system where the program holds the logic and a human rides its parameters in real time. Spiegel’s position is the block’s third pole: not chance, not autonomy, but rules as a playable instrument — the algorithm generates the counterpoint while her hands steer its density, register, and rate. Before this session, revisit Block 1’s “Patchwork” replication lesson — it is the prerequisite here, and this listening builds on what that lesson put in your hands.

Listen for
  • The two time scales: note-to-note decisions too fast and too consistent to be played, and slow arcs of density and brightness that move like a hand, because they are one
  • Where the counterpoint feels lawful — voices that never collide, spacings that never clutter — the boundary, audible as good manners
  • The warmth: how unlike “computer music” clichés it sounds, and which decisions make it so
After — verify

Which layer was the machine and which was Spiegel? The machine chose the notes inside her rules; she performed the rules’ parameters — the swells, thinnings, and openings-out are gestures, captured live. If you couldn’t separate the layers, relisten to the title track and follow only the overall density for five minutes: that contour is the human, and everything inside it is the program.

04 Roland Kayn — Tektra the system as composer

Cybernetic music at installation scale: Kayn built electronic systems whose feedback structures regulate themselves, then stood back and let the system compose for hours. Tektra is the block’s far pole — the most complete delegation in this programme. Where Cage delegated choices and Eno delegated alignment, Kayn delegated authorship: the sound masses that swell, collapse, and reorganize are the system’s own behavior, and the composer’s work is entirely in the wiring. This is what C5 asks you to build a small version of.

Listen for
  • Behavior, not phrases: the music moves like weather — pressures building and releasing — rather than like statements and answers
  • Self-regulation: moments where a swelling texture seems to throttle itself back, as if something inside the system said “enough”
  • Duration as a feature: how your listening changes when you stop waiting for events and start monitoring a process — installation listening, not concert listening
After — verify

What does the system remember? A great deal — this is the highest-state work in the session: feedback paths carry the recent past back into the present, which is why the textures evolve instead of resetting. Could a run be reproduced? Almost certainly not exactly — and naming what is irreproducible (continuous analog state, no discrete seed) is the verify. If it read as “long abstract noise”, relisten to any ten minutes and track one register only, the way you tracked Radigue in L2: the slow life is there.

Spectralism — the system is the spectrum

A fifth delegation, optional but adjacent: hand the decisions not to chance or to a machine but to the physics of sound itself. The spectralists analyzed real spectra and let the overtone structure dictate the harmony — the rule set is an acoustic fact. Each carries a replication lesson; treat it as a sibling strand to Session 1, and ask the same six questions of it.

S1 Gérard Grisey — Partiels a chord derived from analysis, dragged to noise

The founding spectral work: a chord built from a real spectrum analysis of a trombone note, then moved gradually from harmonic clarity into noise. The decision-maker is the spectrum — the composer reads it and obeys.

S2 Tristan Murail — Gondwana chords computed from FM spectra

Harmonies built from frequency-modulation calculations and voiced as single fused timbres, then morphed from one FM chord into the next. The rule is arithmetic — the spectrum is computed before it is heard.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Replication lesson — lesson

S3 Kaija Saariaho — Verblendungen a single sound-to-noise axis as the form

One controllable axis dragging the whole texture from clean pitched tone toward noise and back — the position of that axis over time is the form. The delegation is to a continuum: choose where on it you sit, and the harmony follows.

Session 2 — Samples: extract the rules

Session 2 · Deconstruction

Write the rule set that could have made this

Pick one generative work — one of the Session 1 records, or another work you believe runs on rules. Loop a long passage and reverse-engineer the system: write every rule you can defend from what you hear, one row per rule. For each rule, name what it decides, what evidence you hear for it, and whether it is a rule of generation (makes events) or a rule of prohibition (forbids them). Then fill the three block declarations for the whole system: state, seed, boundary. Finish with the sixth question, drawn as two diagrams — where the audio travels, and where the decisions travel.

Note

You will be wrong, and being wrong in writing is the point. The hardest call in this block is the prohibition rules — the things that never happen sound like nothing, and hearing nothing as evidence is a skill. A guess on paper can be corrected; an impression cannot.

This worksheet is a blueprint

In replication lab R6 you will implement exactly these rules on your rig, declare a seed, and leave the system to run — and find out, empirically, which rules you heard right and which you invented. Write each row as if someone will have to build a machine from it. Someone will: you.

After — verify

Every rule needs evidence, and the rule set as a whole must answer one test: could it produce a passage that is not the one you analyzed, yet recognizably from the same work? If your rules could only reproduce the recording itself, you transcribed the run, not the machine — go back and generalize. The worksheet template is the table above; the rows are enough to run the session now.

Session 3 — Video: who is deciding, on camera

Session 3 · Video

Cage lecturing, Eno explaining, the Dream House running

Three kinds of footage, watched twice each: once as an audience member, then again with one question only — what is the system deciding, and what did the human decide in advance? The suggestions below are searches, not curated picks — choose footage where the working method, not just the result, is visible.

Search: John Cage chance operations lecturewatch for the labor — charts, coins, questions designed before any answer is rolled
Search: Brian Eno generative music talkwatch for the gardener’s stance — seeds and conditions, then deliberate non-intervention
Search: Dream House installation footagewatch for what runs unattended — the system holds the room for years; the humans decided everything once
  1. Decided in advance — list every decision the human froze before the system ran: vocabularies, charts, loop lengths, tunings, levels.
  2. Decided by the system — list what is genuinely open when it runs: which decisions could come out differently next time?
  3. Intervention — does the human ever step back in? When, and what does that admit about the boundary?
  4. The unattended hours — for the Dream House: what keeps working with nobody present, and what had to be engineered for that to be safe?
Watch for the advance decisions

The romance of generative music is the system running alone; the craft is everything decided before it starts. Count the advance decisions in each piece of footage — that count is the real size of the composition, and it is why C5’s spec documentation exists.

After — verify

For each piece of footage, can you state one decision the system makes and one the human made in advance, without rewatching? If either list is empty, rewatch ten minutes with only that list open — the system’s decisions are the ones that would differ on a second run.

Beyond the run — context & lineage

Social & historical context — one prompt per session

For one work in each session, answer in two or three sentences: why was this made then, there, by that person? Cage’s coins are inseparable from postwar Zen and a deliberate refusal of European intention; Eno’s loops grew from cybernetics, art-school systems thinking, and a studio he could not play; Spiegel and Mileece met the machines available to women and outsiders at very different moments; Xenakis brought an engineer’s and a survivor’s mathematics. A system is never neutral — it encodes who had access to which machine, which philosophy made delegation thinkable, and what its maker was reacting against. Naming that context is not trivia; it is the difference between stealing a technique and understanding a stance.

  • Optional lineage track — follow one tradition across 30 years. Pick a single thread and trace it through three to five works spanning roughly three decades: chance operations from Cage (1951) to today’s livecoding; generative tape/loop music from Eno through Mileece and Marsen Jules; algorithmic composition from Hiller’s Illiac Suite to Spiegel to TidalCycles; cybernetic/self-regulating systems from Kayn forward. Listen in order and write one line per work on what the tradition learned between it and the previous entry — what got cheaper, what got delegated further, what stayed a human job. This is an optional seed, not a pass requirement; carry it forward across the workshop if it grips you.

File all worksheets in the logbook. Then write down one stolen principle — a structural idea, never material — before you build anything with it. It will be applied and judged at capstone C5. Naming it in advance makes it a commitment instead of a story you tell afterwards.
