Replicate: pattern as code

Study a TidalCycles / Algorave performance. Rebuild the mechanism — a base pattern reshaped live by named transformation functions — on whatever decides things in your studio. Decision-system-as-performance: the rules are edited in real time.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This is not about the language. The mechanism is that music is held as a short cyclic pattern, and form is made by applying named transformations to that pattern live — every, sometimesBy, chop, jux, off. One unit (the cycle), a vocabulary of operations, composed and re-composed on the fly. You can rebuild that logic with Digitakt conditions and Live's MIDI tools, or actually run TidalCycles (free).

The system you are replicating
SourceAn Algorave set built in TidalCycles (e.g. an Alex McLean / live-coding showcase set)
The effectDense, constantly mutating rhythm that never stops evolving, with audible "edits" landing on cycle boundaries
The mechanismA small cyclic pattern + a vocabulary of transformation functions applied and stacked live; the cycle is the unit, the transforms are the composition

Listen: YouTube · tidalcycles.org

Before you start
  • Pick ONE tool path: (a) TidalCycles + SuperDirt (free, the real thing), or (b) the workshop rig — Digitakt II trig conditions + Live MIDI tools / Max for Live as a hand-driven stand-in
  • Pen and paper: write your transformation vocabulary down before you perform it
  • Clock running; one drum source loaded so the pattern has something to speak through
  • What you will make: a short performance where ONE base pattern is reshaped live, and every change is a named operation you can repeat.
  • What you will turn off: pre-arranged clips, drawn automation, anything that hides whether the transformation made the change.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF share the mechanism — form generated by applying operations to a cycle, not by adding new material.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are tool limits or taste, not a missing mechanism.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Watch and listen to about a minute of a TidalCycles set. Answer each question before touching the tool.

  1. When the music changes, does new material arrive, or is the same material rearranged?
    Answer

    Almost always rearranged. A short pattern is reversed, chopped, density-shifted, or panned in pairs — the source set is small; the operations are what move.

  2. Where do the edits land in time?
    Answer

    On cycle boundaries. The cycle is the unit of change: you hear an edit "take" at the top of the next cycle, which is why the music feels quantised to its own breathing.

  3. Is the change deterministic or chancy?
    Answer

    Both, by choice. every 4 is deterministic (every 4th cycle); sometimesBy 0.3 is probabilistic. The performer decides how much of the form is rule and how much is dice.

  4. Do transformations stack?
    Answer

    Yes — that is the density. A pattern can be chopped, then have an offset copy, then be panned in stereo, all at once. Order matters: the same operations in a different order give a different result.

Build · step 1

State the base pattern and the vocabulary

Write one short pattern (e.g. a 16-step kick/hat/clap line on the Digitakt, or d1 $ s "bd*4 hh*8 ~ cp" in Tidal). Then list four operations you will allow yourself and nothing more: a reorder (reverse/rotate), a density change (chop/retrig), a stereo move (pan-in-pairs), and an offset echo (a delayed copy at a fixed interval). On the rig, map each to a concrete move: trig-condition probability, retrig, per-track pan, and a synced delay throw.

Build · step 2

Perform the operations on cycle boundaries

Record a take. Start with the bare pattern for two cycles, then introduce one operation per cycle, then start stacking. Make every change land at the top of a cycle (in Tidal it does automatically; on the rig, time your edits to the bar). The discipline is the lesson: you are composing form out of operations, not out of new parts.

base pattern→ apply operation→ stack on next cycle→ cycle boundary commits it
Decision check · A/B at matched loudness

Compare with the reference

  1. Loudness-match your take to REF and play them back to back. Does your form move by rearrangement, like the reference, or did you cheat and add parts?
    Answer

    If you added parts, the mechanism failed — you wrote an arrangement, not a transformation system. Strip back to one pattern and let the operations do the work.

  2. Can you name every change you made as a repeatable operation?
    Answer

    If any change can't be named and repeated, it was a fluke, not a system. The whole point of pattern-as-code is that the form is reproducible from the operations.

  • Miss-list: note what your version misses versus the record — and say whether each miss is a tool limit (no live re-evaluation on the rig), a taste choice, or a mechanism you didn't actually steal. File the take in the logbook; carry the corrected analysis to lab-r6.
