The Score as System

A score is not a recording of music — it is a rule set that produces music. In this lab you write one score, realize it twice with different material and different choices, then lay the two results side by side. The gap between them is the whole lesson: it shows you exactly what your rules fixed and what they left free.

Start here
  • Run the three listening anchors below before writing anything — they are this lab's hypothesis source.
  • Save a new set as LAB-025B-score. You will print into it twice, so keep two clearly named arrangements or two sets, R1 and R2.
  • Have paper or a drawing app open. The score is written away from the rig first; the rig only realizes it.
  • Expected time: one full cycle — three sessions: write, realize ×2, compare.
The point

Up to now you have made decisions while the sound played. Here you make them before, in writing, and then hand the writing to two different realizations. If both come out identical, your rules over-specified — you wrote a transcription, not a system. If they share nothing, your rules under-specified — you wrote noise, not a system. A real score lives in the narrow band between, and finding that band is the work.

1 · Listening — three scores, not three recordings

Each of these pieces exists primarily as a set of instructions. The famous recordings are realizations — one possible output among many. Listen for what every realization must share (the rule) versus what changes performance to performance (the freedom). Write your notes in those two columns.

01 Christian Wolff — For 1, 2 or 3 People rules that react to other players

Wolff's score fixes relationships, not notes: "play this only after you hear that," "respond within a held silence," "choose your own sound but enter on a cue you cannot predict." The system is the web of contingencies between players; the actual sounds are left almost entirely open.

Listen for
  • The fixed part — the timing logic, the call-and-response shape — versus the free part — every actual pitch and timbre.
  • How a rule about listening ("respond to the last sound you heard") produces structure without naming a single note.
After — verify

State one rule you are sure is in the score, and one thing you are sure it left to the performer. If you can't separate them, relisten and watch for moments where a sound clearly answers another — that answer is the rule audible.

Replicate it — step-by-step lesson →

02 Earle Brown — December 1952 graphic score, maximal freedom

A single page of floating horizontal and vertical lines — no notes, no clef, no time. The performer reads the rectangles as events in two dimensions (often register × duration) and decides everything else. Two realizations of December 1952 can sound like different pieces entirely, yet both are correct.

Listen for
  • What the graphic does constrain — relative thickness, density, the broad up/down of the page over time.
  • How much is left open: instrumentation, pitch, dynamics, even whether a line is one event or many.
After — verify

Find two recorded realizations. Name one feature they share (that is the score working) and one where they diverge wildly (that is the indeterminacy working). This is exactly the delta you will manufacture in your own piece.

Replicate it — step-by-step lesson →

03 John Cage — Music of Changes the rule is fixed; chance ran once

The opposite pole. Cage used chance operations (the I Ching) to compose the score — but once written, it is fully fixed, notated to the millimetre. The indeterminacy lived in the generating system, not the realization. Useful here as a warning: a rule set can be so determined that two realizations are identical. That is a valid composition — but it is not what this lab is testing.

Listen for
  • How precisely fixed the result is — every duration and dynamic specified, despite being chance-derived.
  • The difference between chance in the writing (Cage here) and freedom in the reading (Wolff, Brown).
After — verify

Decide where you want your own indeterminacy to live: in how you generate the score, or in how it is read. This lab requires the second — at least one zone the realizer must decide — so that R1 and R2 can differ at all.

Replicate it — step-by-step lesson →

Before writing, put one sentence in the logbook naming the claim you are testing. The target is something like: a score is a system whose every rule either fixes an outcome or hands it to the performer — and I can predict which, before I hear a note. Your sentence, your words; the two realizations will confirm or break it.

2 · New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
Score idiom The form your rules take. Three are offered here: text/verbal (prose or a numbered rule list), graphic (shapes read as events), and event-list (an ordered table of moves, like a recipe). Pick one — the idiom shapes what is easy to fix and easy to leave open.
Determinacy vs indeterminacy A rule is determinate if it forces the same outcome every time ("the piece begins on a single low sustained tone"). It is indeterminate if it leaves a real choice ("choose any sound that has not yet appeared"). A score is a mix; this lab requires at least one deliberate indeterminate zone.
Realization One performance of the score on the rig — one printed take. R1 and R2 are two realizations of the same score. They must differ only because the score allowed it, never because you rewrote the rules between them.
The delta The audible difference between R1 and R2. Every difference is evidence of a freedom your score granted; every sameness is evidence of a constraint it imposed. Reading the delta backwards is reading your own rule set.

3 · The two specs

The same shape you fill on every track lab, adapted: here the "system" is the written score itself, and the "engineering" is how you will realize and print it identically enough that the only variable is what the score left free.

4 · Procedure

Step 01

Write the score

Goal: one page of rules, in one idiom, with at least one deliberate indeterminate zone — written before you touch the rig.

Do this
  1. Choose an idiom and commit: text (a numbered rule list), graphic (a drawn page with a key explaining how to read it), or event-list (an ordered table: order · what · how long · how loud · which free).
  2. Write 4–8 rules. For each rule, decide on purpose whether it is determinate (forces an outcome) or indeterminate (hands a choice to the realizer). Mark each rule D or I in the margin.
  3. Guarantee at least one genuine I zone — a choice with real range. "Choose any sound source not yet heard" is a real I; "choose either C or C#" is barely one.
  4. Guarantee at least one firm D rule — something every realization must obey. "Begins and ends in silence"; "exactly three events"; "the lowest sound enters last."
  5. Predict, in writing, what R1 and R2 will share (your D rules) and where they will diverge (your I zones). This prediction is the hypothesis you are about to test.
Expected result

A one-page score a stranger could read and realize without asking you a question — and a written prediction of which rules will show up as sameness and which as difference.

Logbook

File the score itself (photo or scan if graphic), with every rule tagged D/I, and the share/diverge prediction beneath it.

Step 02

Realize R1 and print

Goal: perform the score once, obeying every rule honestly, and print the result without editing it into something the score did not produce.

Do this
  1. Pick the sound sources the score's free zone allows. Note each choice against the rule that permitted it.
  2. Realize the score in one take onto a stereo print track. Resist fixing "mistakes" that are actually legal outcomes of the rules.
  3. Set the gain to the spec target so R2 can match it. Render to 24-bit WAV named R1.
  4. Write a one-line choice log: for each I zone, what you chose this time.
Logbook

The R1 take + its choice log. The choice log is what makes R2 honest: you must choose differently in those same zones, not re-run the same performance.

Step 03

Realize R2 with different material and choices — and print

Goal: the same score, read again, with deliberately different decisions in every free zone and, where allowed, different sound sources entirely.

Do this
  1. Re-read the score from the top as if for the first time. Do not consult the R1 take while realizing — only the R1 choice log, to ensure you choose otherwise.
  2. In each I zone, make a genuinely different choice: a different source, register, order, or duration — whatever the rule left open.
  3. Obey every D rule exactly as in R1. These are the controls you hold constant.
  4. Match the gain target and print format to R1. Render to 24-bit WAV named R2, with its own choice log.
Expected result

Two takes from one page: recognizably the same piece in some respects, audibly different in others. If you cannot hear both at once, the score is mis-tuned — section 6 tells you which way.

Step 04

Lay R1 and R2 side by side — read the delta

Goal: map every audible sameness back to the rule that fixed it, and every difference back to the rule that freed it. This table is the lab's product.

Do this
  1. Put R1 and R2 on two tracks, time-aligned at their starts. A/B them at the marked phones level.
  2. List everything they share. Next to each, name the D rule responsible.
  3. List everything they differ on. Next to each, name the I zone responsible.
  4. Flag any sameness with no rule behind it (an accident you should have varied) and any difference with no rule behind it (a rule you thought was firm but wasn't).

Decision checkpoint

Can you point to at least one clear sameness with a D rule behind it, and at least one clear difference with an I zone behind it? If either column is empty, your score failed in the direction named in section 6 — fix the rule and re-realize before claiming the pass.

5 · Diagnosis & fault

Fault ritual

Mis-tune the score on purpose

The required fault here is ambiguity / under-specification — but you must also taste its opposite, so you learn the band between them by feel.

loosen a rule → re-realize ×2 → hear the collapse → tighten too far → hear the clone → write the rule
  • Under-specify: take your firmest D rule and delete it, or replace it with "do whatever you like." Realize twice quickly. Listen: the two takes now share nothing — the score has stopped being a system and become a permission slip.
  • Over-specify: now take your richest I zone and pin it down completely ("the sound is exactly this preset at this pitch for exactly 8 seconds"). Realize twice. Listen: the two takes are identical — you have written a transcription, and indeterminacy has died.
  • Restore: return to your tuned score. Articulate, in one sentence, the band you found: how much freedom keeps the piece recognizable while still letting it vary.

6 · Pass gate & report bundle

The report bundle — filed in the logbook
  • The score — one page, one idiom, every rule tagged D/I, with one deliberate indeterminate zone
  • R1 — 24-bit WAV + its choice log
  • R2 — 24-bit WAV + its choice log, with genuinely different free-zone choices
  • Delta map — every sameness and difference traced to the rule behind it
  • Fault entry — the under-/over-specified re-realizations and the band statement they produced
  • The written statement (below) — the pass gate proper
  • System diff sentence — "I thought the score would fix X and free Y; the realizations showed it fixed Y and freed X."
Pass gate

Two prints from one score, plus a written statement naming exactly which rule produced the difference between R1 and R2 and which rule produced the sameness. "They sound different" fails. "The 'choose any unheard source' rule made R1 metallic and R2 vocal — that is the difference; the 'begins and ends in silence, three events only' rule made both feel like the same gesture — that is the sameness" passes.

Pass standard
Minimum passThe bundle above exists; one sameness and one difference are each traced to a named rule.
Solid passEvery line of the delta map maps to a rule; the band statement from the fault is specific and defensible.
Advanced transferYou can hand the score to another person, predict the delta of their realization in advance, and be right about which features hold.
  1. Which of your rules turned out to do the most work — and was it a D rule or an I zone?
  2. Was any sameness an accident rather than a rule? What would have varied it?
  3. Where would you place the next piece on the Cage ↔ Brown axis — more fixed, or more free — and why?
  4. If you handed this score to a stranger, which rule would they misread first?
Track-lab principle

Do less, but make every rule earn its place — a score is the smallest set of constraints that still leaves the music somewhere to go.
