Workshop/Block 6 · Decision Systems
Block 6 — Decision Systems

What generates the choices

Generative rules, self-playing patches, chance operations, and tuning systems — designing behavior, then curating its output. Not “random things happened.” In every lab here you stop playing the notes and start authoring the thing that decides them — and you stay responsible for everything it decides.

Engineering focus

Audio path and control path, drawn as two separate diagrams — one shows where sound travels, the other shows where decisions travel, and confusing them is this block’s signature fault. Every lab declares three things before sound is made: state (what does the system remember from one moment to the next?), seed (can the result be reproduced — and if not, what exactly is lost?), and boundary (what may the system decide, and what is forbidden to it?). Every self-running lab passes a 10-minute unattended test: hands off, doors closed, and the system must still be making defensible music when you return.

No hardware? Two escape hatches

Nothing in this block requires a studio rig — the decisions are what matter, and decisions are cheap to host. The software-modular path is VCV Rack (free): a complete modular synthesizer on screen, where sequencers, random sources, clock dividers, and cross-patched modulators carry every rule the labs ask for — lab 024’s organism and lab 024b’s west-coast voice live here natively. The code path is for authoring rules as language: Pure Data (free, visual patching), or SuperCollider / TidalCycles for those who want to write the generator — the natural home for algorithmic work and the engine behind the livecoding scene. Pick a path per lab; the state / seed / boundary discipline is identical across all of them.

Which domain are decisions traveling in?

The modular work in this block lives in the CV domain — control voltage and gate, not audio and not MIDI notes. The four signal domains (CV, gate, MIDI, audio) and the audio-path-versus-control-path discipline this whole block depends on were laid down in Block 1 · Lab 001 — Signal Path & Gain Staging; revisit its two-map drawing before you patch an organism here. A modulator aimed at another modulator is a CV decision; the same idea drawn as MIDI is a different domain with different failure modes. Keep them straight on the control diagram.

Working order

Do these in this order
  1. Programme L6 — run the three listening sessions first and name one stolen principle
  2. Lab 023 — write sequencing rules that generate material; curate what they produce
  3. Lab 024 — patch a modulation organism that plays itself; record hours, keep minutes
  4. Lab 024b — étude: build the west-coast (Buchla) voice — wavefolding and the low-pass gate, in VCV Rack
  5. Lab 025 — let chance decide structure, inside a boundary you wrote first
  6. Lab 025b — write one indeterminate score, realize it twice, and read the gap between the runs
  7. Lab 026 — design a tuning system and let its interference do the moving
  8. R6 — reimplement one L6 generative system and verify the analysis by behavior
  9. Capstone C5 — the Year 2 capstone: the autonomous installation, the reprise, the year synthesis
  • The year closes here. C5 is the Year 2 capstone — it requires Blocks 4–6 and programmes L4–L6 complete, counts as two cycles, and integrates transformation, space, and decision systems into one unattended piece.
  • Year 2 buffer rule. After lab 024 (or after C4, whichever lands when fatigue shows), insert one non-portfolio cycle: repair, remix, documentation cleanup, or a Studio Signal Map revision. The buffer is part of the plan, not a failure of it.

Listen for

Who decided what, and when? For every work: which decisions were made in advance and frozen into rules, which were delegated to chance or the machine, and which were kept back for a human hand at the end?

Session 2 · Samples

Deconstruction — extract the rules

Pick one generative work and write down the rules that could have produced it: what each rule decides, what the system remembers, whether two runs could ever match. The worksheet becomes the blueprint for replication lab R6 — you will build these rules and run them.

Voice — what are you refusing?

A decision system is defined as much by what it forbids as by what it permits — and so is an artistic voice. As you move through this block, keep one running answer in the logbook: what are you refusing? Not which sounds you like, but which you have decided your systems will never make — the grid you will not snap to, the resolution you will not grant, the prettiness you will not allow, the chance you will not take. The forbidden list is the composition, the labs keep saying; at the scale of a year, the forbidden list is the beginning of a voice. Name one refusal now, and watch whether the block changes it.
