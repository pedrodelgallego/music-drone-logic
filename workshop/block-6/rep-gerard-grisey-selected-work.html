Replicate: a chord that becomes noise

Copy one mechanism from Gérard Grisey — Partiels: derive a chord from a real spectrum analysis, then move it gradually from harmonic to noise. Build a short version on the rig and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a chord is not chosen by taste — it is the measured overtones of one real sound, and the form is a slow, ordered migration from those harmonic partials toward inharmonic noise. You are stealing the structure (spectrum → chord → drift to noise), never Grisey's pitches.

The system you are replicating
Source workGérard Grisey — Partiels (1975), from Les Espaces Acoustiques
The effectAn orchestra sounds like one living sound breathing — a single timbre that slowly cracks open into noise and beating
The mechanismThe opening chord is the analyzed harmonic spectrum of a trombone E2, scored across instruments; the form is a gradual morph from that harmonic spectrum toward inharmonicity and noise

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Live open with a spectrum analyzer ready (Spectrum on a track, or free VCV Rack scope/analyzer); a way to play a single sustained note to analyze
  • MicroFreak on its input for one or two of the spectral partials; Digitakt II ready to hold sustained sampled tones
  • Pen and paper for the partial chart — the chord is read off a real analysis, not invented
  • What you will make: a short spectral morph. Focus on this effect: a single fused timbre that slowly opens from harmonic into noise.
  • What you will turn off: melody, chord changes, rhythm, and any pitch you chose because you liked it.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a measured spectrum becomes the chord, and the chord migrates toward noise on a planned curve.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source-sound choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 12 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to the first three to four minutes of Partiels. Write one short answer per question before you touch the gear.

  1. At the very opening, does the sound read as a chord or as one note?
    Answer

    As one note. The instruments are tuned to the partials of a single low trombone tone, so the ear fuses them into one fat, slightly buzzing timbre rather than hearing separate pitches. That fusion is the whole point of starting from a spectrum.

  2. Over the next minutes, what direction is the change — toward order or toward noise?
    Answer

    Toward noise. The clean harmonic stack gradually gains beating, detuning, air, scratch and breath sounds. The structure is a one-way migration: harmonic at the start, inharmonic and noisy by the end.

  3. Is there a tune or a rhythm you could hum or tap?
    Answer

    No. There is essentially no melody and no steady pulse to hold onto. The parameter being composed is timbre and its position on the harmonic-to-noise axis, not melody or rhythm.

  4. When the noise arrives, is it sudden or has it been creeping in for a long time?
    Answer

    Creeping. Each instrument adds a little instability at a time, so by the time you notice it is noisy you cannot say when it stopped being a chord. The migration is slow and overlapped, never a cut.

Now build the mechanism you described. Do not build from memory of the pitches.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Measure a real spectrum and read off the chord

Phase focus: get the chord from a measurement, not from taste.

Do this
  1. Pick one rich, sustained source sound — a low brass sample, a bowed string, a struck metal object, or a held MicroFreak note. Record three to five seconds of it sustaining.
  2. Put a Spectrum analyzer on it (freeze a frame), and read off the five to eight loudest partials by frequency.
  3. Write the partials as a chart: fundamental plus the strongest overtones, in order of loudness. This chart IS your chord — you did not choose a single note of it.
  4. Mark which partials are near-harmonic (integer multiples) and which are slightly off — those off ones are where the drift will start.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from the reference and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — Measure a real spectrum and read off the chord — as the smallest Live set that proves The opening chord is the analyzed harmonic spectrum of a trombone E2, scored across instruments; the form is a gradual morph from that harmonic spectrum toward inharmonicity and noise.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects An orchestra sounds like one living sound breathing — a single timbre that slowly cracks open into noise and beating.
  1. Use Analog Rytm MKII for this phase: translate Measure a real spectrum and read off the chord into drum voices or samples, trig conditions, parameter locks, scenes, performance macros, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates The opening chord is the analyzed harmonic spectrum of a trombone E2, scored across instruments; the form is a gradual morph from that harmonic spectrum toward inharmonicity and noise, not just the surface of the reference.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A written chord of five to eight frequencies, every one of them read off a real analysis of your own source sound.

Phase 2 — ~30 minutes

Orchestrate the spectrum as one fused timbre

Phase focus: make the separate partials fuse into a single sound.

measured partials → one tuned voice per partial (MicroFreak + Digitakt II + Live) → fused chord at master
Do this
  1. Tune one sustained voice to each partial — MicroFreak for the lowest and the buzzier upper ones, Digitakt II holding sampled sustains, Live instruments for the rest. Sine-ish, simple tones.
  2. Balance their levels to match the loudness order from your chart — the fundamental loudest, upper partials quieter.
  3. A/B: it should read as ONE timbre, not a chord. If you can pick out individual notes, soften attacks, remove vibrato, and tighten tuning until they fuse.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
You hear a chord, not one soundAttacks/vibrato/level spread give the partials away as separate notesMake attacks slow and identical, kill vibrato, follow the loudness order so upper partials sit under the fundamental
It sounds thin, not like a real instrument's bodyToo few partials, or only the harmonic ones keptAdd the quieter and slightly-detuned partials from the chart — the body lives in the upper, imperfect ones
Phase 3 — ~30 minutes

Migrate the chord toward noise on a planned curve

Phase focus: a slow, one-way drift from harmonic to noise — never a cut.

Do this
  1. Write the drift order on paper first: which partial detunes first, which adds noise/breath next, in what bars. The order is the composition.
  2. Over a long stretch, slowly detune the upper partials away from their integer ratios — automate small pitch drift, one voice at a time.
  3. Bring in noise gradually: open MicroFreak's noise/cutoff, fade in air or scratch samples, raise resonance into instability — following your written order.
  4. Record one continuous take of the full migration, harmonic start to noisy end, with no edit point you could name.

Expected result

One take that begins as a fused harmonic tone and ends in noise, with the crossing so gradual nobody can point to where the chord stopped being a chord.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Noise arrives as a sudden eventYou changed too much at once instead of overlapping small movesSpread the drift across more voices and more bars; only one partial should be visibly changing at any moment
It drifts but never reaches real noiseDetuning without adding actual noise contentAdd broadband noise, breath, or self-oscillating resonance at the end — detuning alone stays musical
Verify against the work

A/B your migration against the first minutes of Partiels — matched loudness, 15 seconds of yours, 15 of theirs. Then answer two questions in writing.

Does your opening fuse into one timbre the way theirs does?

Answer

If you can still pick out individual pitches in yours but theirs reads as one sound, your partials have not fused — the give-away is usually attack and level spread, not tuning. Fusion is the proof you orchestrated a spectrum rather than voiced a chord.

Is your drift to noise as gradual and one-way as theirs?

Answer

Theirs has no nameable moment where harmony becomes noise; if yours does, you moved too much at once. The structure is a measured migration, and an audible seam means you cut instead of crossing.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: "Matched: …" · "Missed: … (best guess why)" · "Change next: …"
  • Note which single partial did the most work carrying the timbre — that is your fundamental for the next attempt.
