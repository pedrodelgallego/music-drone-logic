Block 6 · Replication R6 · Study

Rebuild the Rules

Take one generative system you analyzed in L6 and reimplement it from your rule-extraction worksheet — then leave it to run. A chance system never reproduces a recording, so the verdict compares behavior, not output: the distribution of events, the density over time, the range of states. Where the behavior matches, your rules were right; where it doesn’t, you have found the rule you couldn’t hear.

Replication lab · S3 · E2 M1 F1 · half-cycle — requires the L6 Samples session · filed as study, never portfolio · scaffold

listen → hypothesis → draw the path → calibrate → build → fault → diagnose → print → delta
Start here
  • Put the L6 rule-extraction worksheet beside the computer; no worksheet, no R6.
  • Save a new set as R6-study.
  • Implement the worksheet’s rules, declare state, seed, and boundary in writing, then run the system unattended — twice.
  • Stop at the timebox. The corrected worksheet is the deliverable; the runs are evidence.
The point

You cannot rebuild the recording — the reference itself is only one run of a machine, and your machine will never roll the same numbers. That is exactly why this lab works: with the output removed from the comparison, everything left to match is the system. If your rules are right, your system behaves like theirs — same kinds of events, at the same kind of rate, inside the same fences — while playing entirely different notes.

What “close” means here

In R1 you matched sounds, notes, and levels; in R2–R5, gestures, processes, and routings. Here the comparison moves up another level, to behavior: the distribution of events (what kinds happen, how often, in what proportions), the density over time (how thick the music runs, how it swells and thins, how long its quiets last), and the range of states (how far the system travels — and what it provably never does). A stranger hearing your run and the reference should say: different music, same animal.

How this lab works

Implement the rules

  • One mechanism per worksheet row — every rule becomes something you can point at on the rig
  • Prohibition rules too: build the fence, not just the generator
  • A rule too vague to implement is itself a finding

Declare, then let go

  • State, seed, and boundary written down before the first run
  • Two unattended 10-minute runs — the block’s test, applied here
  • No touching: an intervention invalidates the run, not the lab

Compare the behavior

  • A/B the reference against your runs — distributions, density, range
  • Compare your two runs against each other: same animal twice?
  • Correct the worksheet — that is what leaves the room

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
Behavior vs outputOutput is what a system played this time; behavior is what it tends to do every time. Two runs of the same system share no output and all behavior. You are rebuilding the tendency, not the take — judging a chance system by one run is like judging a die by one roll.
Event distributionThe census of what happens: which kinds of events occur, how often, in what proportions. If the reference is mostly long quiet tones with rare loud interruptions, and your run is an even stream of medium events, the notes don’t matter — the census already failed.
Density over timeHow thick the music runs, charted across minutes: where it swells, where it thins, how long its silences last. Sketch it as a curve for the reference and for each of your runs — three curves on one page is the fastest behavior comparison there is.
Seed declarationWriting down, before a run, everything needed to restart the system identically: initial settings, start positions, random seeds where they exist — and an honest list of what cannot be restored. The declaration is what makes “can the result be reproduced?” an answerable question instead of a shrug.

1 · Target & analysis

The target is the generative system you took apart in L6’s Samples session. Your rule-extraction worksheet is the blueprint: one row per rule, each marked generation or prohibition, plus the three declarations — state, seed, boundary — and both diagrams. If a rule is too vague to implement, sharpen it before you start. Finding out a rule was unbuildable already counts — that is the lab working.

  • The firewall. Open a new project and name it R6-study. The replica is study evidence, never portfolio output: it is not published, never “finished,” and nothing from this session — no run, no clip, no resample — moves into portfolio work. The corrected worksheet and the rule set you proved are what leave this room; the audio does not. (The rules themselves are a system, and systems may cross the boundary — that is the whole point of the workshop’s originality rule.)
  • Worksheet only. No tutorials, no patch breakdowns, no interviews about how the work was made. Someone else’s analysis would test someone else’s hearing. If you get stuck, go back to the record with the six questions — not to a search bar.
  • Your rig, their logic. Implement on whatever decides things in your studio — Live’s MIDI tools and Max for Live, the Digitakt’s trig conditions and probability, the MicroFreak’s randomizer and mod matrix. The reference’s technology is irrelevant; its logic is the target.
Before you start
  • The rule-extraction worksheet from L6 on the desk — rules, declarations, and both diagrams filled
  • The reference available as a local audio file, with a 10-minute passage chosen as the behavior sample
  • The control path of your implementation drawn before patching — separately from the audio path
  • A new Live set saved as R6-study — the firewall starts at the project name
  • Phones at the lab-001 mark; record-arm checked, because unattended runs cannot be re-pressed
  • Timebox: two sessions, three at most — the two unattended runs count inside it; write the end date down
Before building

Pre-commit the predictions

In the logbook, before you implement any rule: which behaviors do you expect to nail — the event distribution, the density curve, or the range — which do you expect to miss, and why? And one harder prediction: what will your system do in minute eight that the reference would never do? Writing the predictions down first is the only honest way to find out what you actually believed. The misses you failed to predict are worth double.

2 · Rebuild & verdict

Setup

The behavior rig

The reference passage on a track named REF, gain-matched with a Utility; your system’s recorded runs on tracks named RUN-A and RUN-B, grouped as SYSTEM. One solo press flips between them — serial, never layered. Before any run, fill the declaration table below and file it; after both runs, the A/B is three-way: REF against RUN-A, REF against RUN-B, and RUN-A against RUN-B. The full step-by-step setup recipe for this block — including the density-curve sketching method — is forthcoming; the rules below are enough to run the lab now.

Seed declaration — filed before each run
State — what the system remembers, and its contents at start—
Seed — every setting needed to restart this run identically—
Irreproducible — what cannot be restored, honestly listed—
Boundary — what the system may decide; what is forbidden—
Expected result

Two 10-minute unattended recordings, each with a filed declaration, that you can A/B against the reference passage at matched loudness, hands-free.

REF (Utility, gain-matched) → solo → master → phones SYSTEM group (RUN-A · RUN-B) → solo → master → phones

Only one is ever audible at a time — the comparison is serial, never layered, because layering hides density and distribution misses.

  1. Timebox first. Two sessions; three at the absolute most, and each unattended run counts inside them. The replica is finished when the timebox ends, not when it is perfect. The end date is already in the logbook: when it arrives, render and stop, mid-miss if necessary.
  2. Implement the rules. One mechanism per worksheet row, prohibitions included — a forbidden register needs an actual fence (a scale, a clamp, a range limit), not a hope. Draw the control path as built and diff it against the one you drew in advance; the diff is the first finding.
  3. Declare, then run unattended — twice. File the seed declaration, press record, leave the room for ten minutes. Then change only the seed — re-roll, re-position, re-start — declare again, and run again. No touching during runs: if you must intervene, the run is void and the reason for intervening goes in the logbook as a boundary failure.
  4. A/B the behavior at matched loudness. Thirty seconds REF, thirty seconds RUN-A, back and forth — never both at once. Listen for one dimension per pass: event distribution only, then density over time, then range of states, then the fences — what each system never does. Sketch the three density curves on one page.
  5. Compare your two runs. RUN-A against RUN-B is the reproducibility verdict: same behavior, different output means the system works; identical output means you built a sequence, not a system; unrecognizably different behavior means the rules are looser than your worksheet claimed.
  6. Write the miss list. Every behavioral difference from the reference, each assigned to the layer where it lives — use the table below. The layer matters more than the miss: it tells you which worksheet question you answered wrong.
  7. Correct the worksheet, close, and quarantine. Strike the rules the runs disproved and write the corrected ones beside them — including every rule the reference turned out to need that your worksheet never had. Render both runs into the logbook as study evidence, close the project, don’t reopen it for portfolio work. Carry the miss list forward: it is C5’s sharpened hypothesis about what rule systems actually sound like.

The detailed per-step recipes — rule-to-mechanism mapping per device, the density-curve method, the distribution census — are forthcoming with Block 6 authoring. The rules above are final; only their expansion is pending.

Decision check — rule miss or rig miss?

For each item on the miss list: did the behavior fail because your rig cannot implement that rule — no weighted randomness, no slow-enough clock — or because the rule was never on your worksheet until the run exposed its absence? A rig miss is a note for the gear roadmap (the Torso T-1 and Strega arrive this block for a reason). A hearing miss is the gold — it is the precise edge of your current ear for systems, located.

Pass gate — R6 complete when
  • Two unattended 10-minute runs, rendered and filed in the logbook as study
  • Two seed declarations — state, seed, irreproducibles, boundary — filed before each run, not after
  • The rule-extraction worksheet it was rebuilt from, with corrections marked — disproved rules struck, missing rules added
  • Both diagrams — audio path and control path — as drawn in advance and as corrected after building
  • Behavior A/B notes at matched loudness, including the three density curves and the RUN-A vs RUN-B verdict
  • The miss list — each miss assigned to its layer, rule or rig named
  • One sentence: what can you hear now that you couldn’t before?

No fault ritual, no full bundle — this is a half-cycle. If your runs share not a single note with the record, the lab can still pass: the behavior, not the output, is what is being judged.

Replication principle

The recording was never the piece; the machine was. Rebuild the machine, and let it disagree with the record in exactly the ways the record disagrees with itself.
