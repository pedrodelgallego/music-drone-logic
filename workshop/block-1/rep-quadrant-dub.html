Before you start
  • Live set open, MicroFreak on M4 in 1, Digitakt II on M4 in 3–4 — the lab 001/002 wiring

  • What you will make: a short replica of Basic Channel — “Quadrant Dub I”. Focus on this effect: A static loop that feels alive for ten minutes - full of events nobody composed.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: The material is frozen; a filter, a delay send, and a level are performed by hand around it.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Basic Channel — “Quadrant Dub I”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. In one minute, how many events did you count — and what CAUSED each one (a filter? a send? a level)?
    Answer

    A fair count is three to six, and every one of them is a desk move: the filter opening or closing on the chord, a hit pushed into the echo so it blooms, a level leaning in or out.

  2. Did the underlying loop itself ever change?
    Answer

    No. Every event is the desk acting on a static loop, and if you heard the loop change you were hearing the filter open - which is the lesson.

  3. How long is the longest stretch where “nothing” happens?
    Answer

    Genuinely long - stretches of twenty to forty seconds where the desk simply holds and the loop runs bare.

  4. When a delay throw appears, how long does its tail live?
    Answer

    Several bars - long enough to have a clear birth and a slow, audible death, short enough that it is gone before the next throw arrives.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target: every event in your three minutes is a desk move — filter, send, or level — made where the record makes its own.
  • Restraint: your stretches of “nothing” are as long as theirs.
  • Throws: a thrown delay tail reads as an event, not a constant wash — and lives about as long as the record’s.
  • The loop: untouched from first bar to last, with the same chord role, drum spacing, and delay behavior as their loop.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes · session one

Match the material before you touch the desk

Phase focus: match the material before you touch the desk. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Import the record on a REF track. Drop “Quadrant Dub I” onto a dedicated REF track in Live and loop its most characteristic stretch — a 2–3 minute stretch is fine here, the moves are slow.
  2. Live Tap the record’s tempo and set Live’s BPM to it before placing anything.
  3. Live Gain-match the REF with a Utility so comparisons are honest, and keep it one solo away for the whole session.
  4. MicroFreak + Live Voice the chord stab by role, register, and decay. Solo the REF, take one of their chord hits apart by ear
  5. MicroFreak + Live , thenchase it in this order: engine choice first
  6. MicroFreak , thenfilter cutoff and resonance
  7. MicroFreak , thendecay length.
  8. Live A/B single hits — yours, theirs, yours — until the gap is the rig’s, not your ears’.
  9. Live Match the grid. Place your stab, kick, and hat where theirs land in the looped section, feel included.
  10. Live Loop your bare material next to the REF and listen for placement, not just sound.
Expected result

A short loop that, soloed against the REF, reads as a plain copy of their material — same stab character, same grid feel — with no movement of its own yet.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The stab is close but always thinner or duller than theirsChasing decay and filter before the engine — the wrong engine cannot be EQ’d into the right oneGo back to engine choice, commit, then redo filter and decay in that order
Your loop feels busier than the record’sYou added parts the section does not haveStrip back to what you can actually point to in the REF — stab, kick, hat — and nothing else
Placement looks right on the grid but feels wrong against the REFLive’s BPM was never matched to the record, so the two loops breathe at different ratesTap the REF tempo, set Live’s BPM, and re-judge placement only after the tempos agree
Phase 2 — ~20 minutes · session one

Freeze the music, rig the desk

Phase focus: freeze the music, rig the desk. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Freeze it. Resample or consolidate the loop to ONE audio clip in Live, then disable or delete the source tracks.
  2. Live From here on, nothing musical can change.
  3. Live Create the return. Create → Insert Return Track, then drop Audio Effects → Echo onto it.
  4. Live Starting values: sync on, time 3/16 or 1/4, Feedback ≈ 55%, dry/wet 100% wet.
  5. Live Returns run 100% wet because the dry signal already reaches the master on the loop track — any dry copy on the return would only double it.
  6. Live Throw one test hit and A/B its tail against a throw in the REF section;
  7. Live adjust sync and feedback until your tail lives about as long as theirs.
  8. Live Leave the loop track’s A send fully down for now.
  9. MicroFreak Put the filter on the loop. Drop an Audio Effects → Auto Filter directly on the loop’s track — low-pass, resonance low so the filter reads as opening rather than whistling — and close the cutoff most of the way.
  10. Live This filter and that send are your only instruments.
  11. Live Map the filter. Press ⌘ M — Live tints every mappable control.
  12. Live Click the Auto Filter’s Freq dial, then move a Launchpad fader or knob: the assignment appears as a row in the mapping browser on the left.
  13. MicroFreak + Live Set the range in that browser row. Type a Min of 200 Hz for the cutoff so the floor never fully closes — a performance decision made in advance: the darkest pullback still leaves the loop alive.
  14. MicroFreak + Live Leave Max at the top.
  15. Live Map the send. Still in map mode, click the A send dial on the loop track, then move a second Launchpad control.
  16. Live Two rows in the browser, two hands accounted for.
  17. Live Exit map mode. Press ⌘ M again BEFORE performing — leaving map mode open is the classic failure.
  18. Live Then test both controls: moving each one must audibly move the sound.

The whole rig, drawn — the top row is the only thing the audience hears directly; the bottom row is where throws go to die.

loop clip → Auto Filter (mapped) → loop track → master loop track send (the chord) → Echo return · feedback ≈55% → master

The same rig as a topology. The send is a door, not a path — the dry loop never stops reaching the master; the send decides how much of a copy escapes into the echo. Dashed lines are control, not audio.

Expected result

One audio clip you cannot edit, and two mapped controls that each audibly move the sound — the filter never closing below 200 Hz, the send silent at rest. Nothing else in the set can change the music.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
You hear nothing from the return on a test throwThe A send on the loop track is still at −infRaise the loop track’s A send; the return’s own fader stays at 0 dB
Moving the fader does nothingMIDI-map mode is still open — ⌘ M was never pressed a second timeExit with ⌘ M, then test again
The test throw’s tail keeps growing instead of dyingFeedback set past the edge of self-oscillationPull the SEND first, then lower feedback below the runaway point — the safety note’s rule
The darkest filter position kills the loop entirelyNo Min set in the mapping browser row — the cutoff closes to silenceOpen the mapping browser, set the cutoff row’s Min to ≈ 200 Hz
Phase 3 — ~15 minutes · session one

Learn the three gestures before the take

Phase focus: learn the three gestures before the take. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Gesture vocabulary — three moves, two hands
GestureHandsWhat the room hears
Slow openFilter rising across 30+ seconds — move less than feels rightThe loop growing harmonics, as if a part were added
ThrowFlick the send up and immediately back down on ONE hitOne hit blooms into echo and dies over bars
PullbackEverything down to almost nothing, hold, rebuildThe floor drops away — and the return feels earned
Do this
  1. The slow open, three times. From the 200 Hz floor, raise the filter over at least 30 seconds — count bars, not seconds, and move less than feels right.
  2. A slow open that takes a breath and a half is a fill, not an open.
  3. Live Return to the floor between repetitions.
  4. Live The throw, three times. Pick ONE chord hit in the loop and flick the send up and immediately back down on exactly that hit.
  5. Live The tail lives on the return — your hand’s job ends the moment the hit has escaped through the door.
  6. Live Let each tail die completely before the next throw.
  7. Live The pullback, three times. Filter down to the floor, send at zero, hold it there longer than feels comfortable — then rebuild with one slow open.
  8. Live The hold IS the gesture; the rebuild is just its release.
Expected result

Each gesture repeatable without looking at your hands — same speed, same landing point, three times in a row. When a gesture is repeatable, it is vocabulary; until then it is luck.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The slow open is over in five secondsMoving at knob speed instead of music speedCount eight bars out loud while opening; the hand follows the count, not the urge
The throw smears across several hitsSend raised too early or lowered too late — the door stood openAim at one hit: up ON the hit, down before the next one arrives
The pullback feels like the track endedYou cut the hold short and rebuilt in a rushHold past the discomfort — the record sits in the dark for twenty seconds and trusts the loop; so do you
Phase 4 — ~25 minutes · session two

Perform the section’s desk moves where THEY make them

Phase focus: perform the section’s desk moves where THEY make them. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

The record’s shape; adjust to the section you looped.

0:00
Loop bare, filter closed — let it establish; do nothing.
0:40
First slow open — the filter rises over many bars, never in one move.
1:20
First delay throw — send up on one hit, straight back down; let the tail die on its own.
2:00
Pull back to almost nothing — filter nearly closed, and hold it there longer than feels comfortable.
2:30
Rebuild — open again, perhaps one more throw on the way up.
3:00
Out — close down and let the last tail finish the piece.
Do this
  1. Live Set up the recording. Tab into Arrangement view and place the loop clip on the timeline, long enough for three minutes plus tails.
  2. Live Enable the global Automation Arm button in the transport bar — with it on, every mapped knob move writes into an automation lane while recording.
  3. Live Solo the REF section once more and follow their moves with your hands resting on the mapped controls — not moving them, just marking where you WOULD move.
  4. Live Rehearse one full pass without recording. Open the filter when theirs opens, at their speed;
  5. Live throw a hit into the delay where theirs throw; pull back to almost nothing where they sit in the dark.
  6. Live Record the performance. Arrangement record, one full pass with the timeline above as the score, mistakes included.
  7. Live Then record it once more — and keep the better of the two takes, not a composite.
Expected result

Three recorded minutes where every event is a desk move landing roughly where the record’s land — including the stretches where you did nothing at all — and two automation lanes (cutoff and send) written into the Arrangement underneath them.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The audio recorded but no automation existsAutomation Arm was off, or you performed in Session view without writingEnable the global Automation Arm and record the take in Arrangement view
Your moves crowd together — something happens every few barsPerforming from boredom instead of from the recordRehearse once more with the map above in view; the holds are part of the score
Throws read as a constant washThe send goes up but never comes back downTreat the send as a button, not a fader: up on one hit, immediately down
A tail runs away and keeps growing mid-takeFeedback nudged past the edge during the performancePull the SEND first, then feedback — and keep playing; the recovery is part of the take
  • Safety: never push the delay’s feedback past self-oscillation with phones loud. If the tail starts growing on its own, pull the send first.
Phase 5 — ~15 minutes · session two

Listen back, then look at the take

Phase focus: listen back, then look at the take. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Listen back against the record. Solo back and forth — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — through the whole section.
  2. Count the events in each and note where yours arrive early, late, or not at all.
  3. Live Observe — see it, not just hear it. Unfold the loop track’s automation lanes in Arrangement view — pick Freq and the A send in the lane chooser — and LOOK at your three minutes as drawn gestures.
  4. Live Count the moves; compare what you see against what you heard in their section — their lane would be mostly flat, with deliberate strokes exactly where you heard them.
  5. Live The lane is your performance, printed; the diff against theirs is your hearing, printed.
  6. Live Read the gestures by shape. A slow open is a long ramp; a throw is a single spike that returns to the floor;
  7. Live a pullback is a step down followed by a plateau.
  8. Live If a stroke in the lane does not match one of the three shapes, it was a fidget, not a gesture — note where it happened.
Expected result

An automation lane that is mostly flat, with a countable number of deliberate strokes — long flat stretches between them — and a written note of where your events missed theirs.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The lane wiggles everywhereYou rode the controls continuously instead of making discrete movesRe-take with hands OFF the controls between moves — resting position is part of the technique
Every event lands a few seconds lateReacting to the REF in your head instead of anticipating itRehearse once more with the timeline above as the score, not the memory of the record
The send lane never fully returns to zero after throwsThe flick down stops short — the door stays ajar and a faint wash leaks under everythingDrill the throw again in isolation: the gesture ends at zero, not near it
Verify against the record

A/B your take against the looped section of “Quadrant Dub I” — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate? Then answer four questions in writing.

Do your desk moves land where theirs land — filter opening when theirs opens, throws where theirs throw?

Answer

In the record the moves arrive at long intervals and unfold slowly — a filter opening can take half a minute. A good replica lands within a few seconds of each of their moves and matches their speed; if yours arrive early and often, you were performing your own impatience, not their section. The holds count as moves too: landing the silence is landing the move.

Does a thrown delay tail feel like an event the way the record’s do — something that happened, not something placed?

Answer

A throw on the record has a clear birth — one hit suddenly blooms into echo — and a long audible death. The event quality comes entirely from the send going up and back down around a single hit; if yours reads as a wash, the send stayed up, and if it reads as an edit, the tail was cut instead of left to die.

How often does the record actually throw the delay?

Answer

Far less than memory claims — count them in your looped section and the honest number is two or three in a minute, sometimes fewer. Memory inflates the count because each throw is so vivid. Restraint between gestures is the instrument, and your automation lane should show it: long flat stretches between strokes, not a stroke every few bars.

In your darkest pullback, did the loop ever fully disappear?

Answer

It should not have — the 200 Hz Min you set in the mapping browser is exactly this insurance, decided before the performance instead of during it. On the record the dark stretches are dark, never absent: the low fundamentals keep pulsing under a closed filter, which is why the rebuild feels like a return rather than a restart. If your floor went silent, the range was never set — fix the row, not the take.
