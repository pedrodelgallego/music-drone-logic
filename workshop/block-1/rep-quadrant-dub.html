Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the desk as performer

Freeze the music completely, then make everything happen from the mixer.

The point

The loop never changes — every event comes from the mixer: a filter, a send, a level, opened and closed by hand. When the material is locked, the desk becomes the instrument, and you find out that ten minutes of music can come from zero edits.

The system you are replicating
Source recordBasic Channel — “Quadrant Dub I”
The effectA static loop that feels alive for ten minutes — full of events nobody composed
The mechanismThe material is frozen; a filter, a delay send, and a level are performed by hand around it
FidelityAs close to the record’s section as your rig allows — sounds, chords, patterns, and moves included. Closeness is the measure of your hearing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Live set open, MicroFreak on M4 in 1, Digitakt on M4 in 3–4 — the lab 001/002 wiring
  • Launchpad Pro MK3 connected, one fader or knob free to map
  • Phones at the mark · 60–120 minutes clear — the performance has to be rehearsed and then done in one take, and the rehearsal is most of the time
Reference pass — 10 minutes

Describe their section before you match it

Listen to about one minute of “Quadrant Dub I.” Answer in the logbook, one line each, BEFORE touching gear. Check each answer against the model only after yours is written.

  1. In one minute, how many events did you count — and what CAUSED each one (a filter? a send? a level)?
    Answer

    A fair count is three to six, and every one of them is a desk move: the filter opening or closing on the chord, a hit pushed into the echo so it blooms, a level leaning in or out. Nothing melodic or rhythmic ever causes an event — the loop supplies no events of its own, which is why the count is so low.

  2. Did the underlying loop itself ever change?
    Answer

    No. Every event is the desk acting on a static loop, and if you heard the loop change you were hearing the filter open — which is the lesson. The brighter, “busier” chord at minute two is the same chord as minute one with more of its harmonics let through.

  3. How long is the longest stretch where “nothing” happens?
    Answer

    Genuinely long — stretches of twenty to forty seconds where the desk simply holds and the loop runs bare. The record trusts the loop to carry those stretches, and your replica has to hold them just as long; cutting them short is the most common miss.

  4. When a delay throw appears, how long does its tail live?
    Answer

    Several bars — long enough to have a clear birth and a slow, audible death, short enough that it is gone before the next throw arrives. If the echo feels constant rather than thrown, you are hearing a send left up, and the record never does that.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

  • Sound target: every event in your three minutes is a desk move — filter, send, or level — made where the record makes its own.
  • Restraint: your stretches of “nothing” are as long as theirs.
  • Throws: a thrown delay tail reads as an event, not a constant wash — and lives about as long as the record’s.
  • The loop: untouched from first bar to last, and as close to their loop as the rig allows.

Steps

Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Match the material before you touch the desk

The desk only reads as a performance if the material under it is theirs. This phase puts the record on a reference track and chases their chord stab and grid by ear, hit by hit — slow, honest A/B work. Nothing about performing happens yet; you are building the static loop the whole lesson stands on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track. Drop “Quadrant Dub I” onto a dedicated REF track in Live and loop its most characteristic stretch — a 2–3 minute stretch is fine here, the moves are slow. Gain-match it with a Utility so comparisons are honest, and keep it one solo away for the whole session.
  2. Voice the chord stab as close to theirs as the MicroFreak gets. Solo the REF, take one of their chord hits apart by ear, then chase it in this order: engine choice first, then filter cutoff and resonance, then decay length. A/B single hits — yours, theirs, yours — until the gap is the rig’s, not your ears’.
  3. Match the grid. Place your stab, kick, and hat where theirs land in the looped section, feel included. Loop your bare material next to the REF and listen for placement, not just sound.
Expected result

A short loop that, soloed against the REF, reads as a plain copy of their material — same stab character, same grid feel — with no movement of its own yet.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The stab is close but always thinner or duller than theirsChasing decay and filter before the engine — the wrong engine cannot be EQ’d into the right oneGo back to engine choice, commit, then redo filter and decay in that order
Your loop feels busier than the record’sYou added parts the section does not haveStrip back to what you can actually point to in the REF — stab, kick, hat — and nothing else
Phase 2 — ~20 minutes

Freeze the music, rig the desk

Resampling collapses everything you built into one audio clip — after this, the music physically cannot be edited, and that is half the lesson. The other half is building the only two instruments left: a low-pass filter sitting on the loop, and a delay send into an echo return. Two controls, two hands.

Do this
  1. Freeze it. Resample or consolidate the loop to ONE audio clip in Live, then disable or delete the source tracks. From here on, nothing musical can change.
  2. Build the echo hallway to match theirs. Create a return track with a dub delay — Live’s Echo, feedback around 55%, sync 3/16 or 1/4 — then throw one test hit and A/B its tail against a throw in the REF section. Adjust sync and feedback until your tail lives about as long as theirs. Turn the loop track’s send fully down for now.
  3. Put the filter on the loop. Drop an Auto Filter (low-pass) directly on the loop’s track and close the cutoff most of the way. This filter and that send are your only instruments.
  4. Map your two hands. ⌘ M for MIDI-map mode: map the Auto Filter cutoff to one Launchpad fader or knob, and the delay send to another. Exit map mode and test both — moving the fader must audibly move the sound.

The whole rig, drawn — the top row is the only thing the audience hears directly; the bottom row is where throws go to die.

loop clip → Auto Filter (mapped) → loop track → master loop track send (the chord) → Echo return · feedback ≈55% → master
Expected result

One audio clip you cannot edit, and two mapped controls that each audibly move the sound. Nothing else in the set can change the music.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Moving the fader does nothingMIDI-map mode is still openExit with ⌘ M, then test again
The test throw’s tail keeps growing instead of dyingFeedback set past self-oscillationPull the send first, then lower feedback below the runaway point
Phase 3 — ~30 minutes

Perform the section’s desk moves where THEY make them

You are transcribing hands, not inventing gestures. The record’s performance has a shape — long holds, slow openings, a few throws — and your job is to land your moves where theirs land, at their speed. Rehearse it at least once before recording; the take itself is one pass, mistakes included, because a corrected performance is no longer a performance.

The record’s shape; adjust to the section you looped.

0:00
Loop bare, filter closed — let it establish; do nothing.
0:40
First slow open — the filter rises over many bars, never in one move.
1:20
First delay throw — send up on one hit, straight back down; let the tail die on its own.
2:00
Pull back to almost nothing — filter nearly closed, and hold it there longer than feels comfortable.
2:30
Rebuild — open again, perhaps one more throw on the way up.
3:00
Out — close down and let the last tail finish the piece.
Do this
  1. Solo the REF section once more and follow their moves with your hands resting on the mapped controls — not moving them, just marking where you WOULD move.
  2. Rehearse one full pass without recording. Open the filter when theirs opens, at their speed; throw a hit into the delay where theirs throw; pull back to almost nothing where they sit in the dark.
  3. Record the performance. Do it again with recording armed — audio and automation both. One take, mistakes included.
Expected result

Three recorded minutes where every event is a desk move landing roughly where the record’s land — including the stretches where you did nothing at all.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Your moves crowd together — something happens every few barsPerforming from boredom instead of from the recordRehearse once more with the map above in view; the holds are part of the score
Throws read as a constant washThe send goes up but never comes back downTreat the send as a button, not a fader: up on one hit, immediately down
  • Safety: never push the delay’s feedback past self-oscillation with phones loud. If the tail starts growing on its own, pull the send first.
Phase 4 — ~15 minutes

Listen back, then look at the take

The take exists twice: as audio you can A/B against the record, and as automation lanes you can read like a score. Both passes matter — the ears catch events that arrive early or late; the eyes catch the difference between deliberate strokes and nervous wiggling.

Do this
  1. Listen back against the record. Solo back and forth — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — through the whole section. Count the events in each and note where yours arrive early, late, or not at all.
  2. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Open the recorded automation lanes in Arrangement view and LOOK at your three minutes as drawn gestures. Count the moves; compare what you see against what you heard in their section — their lane would be mostly flat, with deliberate strokes exactly where you heard them. The lane is your performance, printed; the diff against theirs is your hearing, printed.
Expected result

An automation lane that is mostly flat, with a countable number of deliberate strokes — and a written note of where your events missed theirs.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The lane wiggles everywhereYou rode the controls continuously instead of making discrete movesRe-take with hands OFF the controls between moves — resting position is part of the technique
Every event lands a few seconds lateReacting to the REF in your head instead of anticipating itRehearse once more with the timeline above as the score, not the memory of the record
Verify against the record

A/B your take against the looped section of “Quadrant Dub I” — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate? Then answer two questions in writing.

Do your desk moves land where theirs land — filter opening when theirs opens, throws where theirs throw?

Answer

In the record the moves arrive at long intervals and unfold slowly — a filter opening can take half a minute. A good replica lands within a few seconds of each of their moves and matches their speed; if yours arrive early and often, you were performing your own impatience, not their section. The holds count as moves too: landing the silence is landing the move.

Does a thrown delay tail feel like an event the way the record’s do — something that happened, not something placed?

Answer

A throw on the record has a clear birth — one hit suddenly blooms into echo — and a long audible death. The event quality comes entirely from the send going up and back down around a single hit; if yours reads as a wash, the send stayed up, and if it reads as an edit, the tail was cut instead of left to die.
