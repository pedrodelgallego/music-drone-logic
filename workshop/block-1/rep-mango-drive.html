Replicate: depth from levels alone

Copy one clear section of Rhythm & Sound — “Mango Drive”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: large, deliberate gaps between element levels. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, Digitakt II → M4 inputs 3/4, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged
  • New set saved as REP-MANGO, tempo 122
  • M4 phones knob at the lab-001 mark — it stays there for the whole lesson
  • What you will make: a short replica of Rhythm & Sound — “Mango Drive”. Focus on this effect: heavy and deep, though nothing is loud.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: large, deliberate gaps between element levels.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Rhythm & Sound — “Mango Drive”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Which element is loudest — and how sure are you?
    Answer

    The chord stabs are the loudest event - but barely; if you said “nothing is loudest”, that is the better answer. The record is built so that no element ever claims the front of the mix.

  2. How far below it does the hi-hat sit — a little, or a lot?
    Answer

    A lot - on the order of ten decibels, far enough that you stop noticing the hat until you hunt for it. It is not a backbeat; it is a texture that happens to keep time.

  3. Is anything as quiet as “barely there”?
    Answer

    Yes - under everything sits a wash of noise and room that you hear as atmosphere rather than as a part. It lives at the very edge of audibility and never moves.

  4. Does anything ever jump forward in level?
    Answer

    No. Nothing changes level by more than a hair for the whole section - the stillness is the design, and it is why the depth reads as a place rather than a mix move. If something seemed to jump, it was your attention moving, not a fader.

  5. Is the depth coming from echo or reverb — or from level distance alone?
    Answer

    There is dub delay and reverb in the production, but the depth survives without them: the hat would still sound far away bone-dry, because it is quiet. Level distance does the placing; the effects only decorate it.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your loop lands on the same four sounds at the same distances.
  • No element asks to be turned up. If one does, that is the louder-is-better trap talking, not the record.
  • The quietest element is still findable when you look for it — buried exactly as deep as the record buries it.

The whole chain for this lesson — two sources in, four tracks, and the faders as the only spatial tool.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the empty tracks

Phase focus: the reference and the empty tracks. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Mango Drive” into it.
  2. Find the most characteristic 8–16 bars for depth-from-levels — chord, hat, and texture all present — and loop them.
  3. Spend a few minutes choosing; the wrong section makes every later pass harder.
  4. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim its gain so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, matched to the build you are about to make.
  5. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  6. ⌘ T four times; rename with ⌘ R: KICK, HAT, CHORD, TEXTURE.
  7. ⌘ ⌥ I to show In/Out.
  8. KICK and HAT: Ext. In 3/4.
  9. CHORD and TEXTURE: Ext. In 1.
  10. All faders at 0 dB — unity, the lab-001 rule.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Rhythm & Sound — “Mango Drive” and use Live audio/MIDI tracks, Utility, automation, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the empty tracks — as the smallest Live set that proves large, deliberate gaps between element levels.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects heavy and deep, though nothing is loud.
  1. Use Analog Rytm MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the empty tracks into drum voices or samples, trig conditions, parameter locks, scenes, performance macros, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates large, deliberate gaps between element levels, not just the surface of Rhythm & Sound — “Mango Drive”.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at a polite ≈ −12 dBFS, and four named, correctly-routed, unity-gain tracks wait for audio. Soloing REF plays the section; everything else is silent.

The drums, transcribed and captured

Phase focus: the drums, transcribed and captured. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this

  2. the page cannot give you the steps — hearing them is the exercise.
  3. Take at least two passes per element: one for the kick alone, one for the hats alone.
  4. Press [RECORD] to enter grid recording.
  5. The [RECORD] key should light red.

  11. Solo each take against REF and listen for a full loop each: same drum role, same envelope length, same weight.
  12. If a sound is clearly wrong, re-audition and re-record now — it is cheap here and expensive in phase 4. Stop the Digitakt II.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

The chord and the wash

Phase focus: the chord and the wash. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: Paraphonic ON, a soft engine (Type → V.Analog), attack slow, sustain high, cutoff ≈ 40 % as a starting point.
  2. Transcribe the chord’s notes from REF by ear — the page cannot give the notes;
  3. hearing them is the exercise.
  4. Sing along with the chord first if it helps; the voice finds intervals the fingers miss.
  5. Make at least three solo-flip passes against REF, changing one parameter per pass — cutoff, then envelope, then voicing — until the flip has the same role and register.
  6. One change per pass, or you will not know which move helped.
  7. Set the M4 input-1 gain so peaks sit ≈ −12 dBFS; arm CHORD and record 8 bars.
  8. Turn Type to the Noise engine, close the cutoff toward ≈ 15 % so it becomes a dull wash, and hold a low note.
  9. Solo-flip against REF until the wash sits in the same register as the record’s — darker is almost always closer.
  10. Record 8 bars into TEXTURE — same gain, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Rhythm & Sound — “Mango Drive” and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.

Phase 4 · ≈ 30 min

Flat, then deep — faders only

Phase focus: flat, then deep — faders only. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

The spread — provided targets to verify by ear
Chordpeaks ≈ −12 dBFS — the loudest element, fader at 0
Kickpeaks ≈ −14 dBFS — just under the chord
Hatpeaks ≈ −22 dBFS — a big gap, on purpose
Texturepeaks ≈ −30 dBFS — the edge of attention
Do this
  1. Loop all four clips with every fader at 0 dB — all four elements at equal level, all at nominal.
  2. Solo-flip against REF for a full minute.
  3. Same sounds, no depth — it is flat and crowded, and it is not the record.
  4. Write one line saying so.
  5. CHORD stays at 0 dB — the loudest element, peaking ≈ −12 dBFS.
  6. Solo-flip against REF after every move from here on.
  7. Pull HAT down until it sits as far below the chord as the record’s hat does — ≈ −10 dB is a starting point;
  8. the record is the answer.
  9. Pull TEXTURE to the edge of attention exactly where the record holds it, ≈ −18 to −24 dB on the fader.
  10. Set KICK between chord and hat — a few dB down, toward the spread table’s ≈ −14 dBFS — until the weight matches.
  11. Make at least two more full passes across all three moving faders, finer each time.
  12. The first pass gets the shape; the later passes get the record.
  13. Touch nothing else — no EQ, no reverb, no clip gain.

Measure, verify, file

Do this

  2. Read each on the same bar of the loop so the peaks are comparable.
  3. Compute the gaps in dB.
  4. Those gaps are your transcription of the record’s level relationships — the logbook gets them.
  5. Compare your measured gaps with the spread table above.
  6. Where they differ, the record won — note by how much.
  7. Write both ledgers. Note the flat fader set (all 0) and the matched set in the logbook, so you can flip between them by hand for the A/B below.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your matched version at matched loudness — phones at the mark, and remember nothing in your version got louder than nominal, things only got quieter.

  • Could a friend, hearing 10 seconds of yours then 10 of the record, say which is which? The longer they hesitate, the better your ears did.
    Answer

    If they hesitate, your levels are doing the record’s work. If they answer instantly, the giveaway is almost always a sound mismatch — the wrong kick family, a brighter chord — rather than a level mismatch. Listen for which element betrays you first; that element is your next matching pass, back in phase 2 or 3.

  • Are the gaps the same size by ear — chord to hat, hat to texture — before you ever look at the meters?
    Answer

    Judge both gaps by ear first, then let the meters confirm. If your ear says “same distances” but the meters disagree by more than a couple of dB, check the comparison itself before doubting your ears — REF’s trim and the phones mark drift more often than hearing does. Relisten to the hat-to-texture gap last; it is the harder of the two and the better test.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
