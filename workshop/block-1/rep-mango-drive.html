Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: depth from levels alone

Rebuild the four sounds of a “Mango Drive” section, set them all equally loud, then match the record’s depth — touching nothing but the faders.

The point

Depth in a mix is made by how far apart the levels sit, not by effects.

The system you are replicating
Source recordRhythm & Sound — “Mango Drive”
The effectheavy and deep, though nothing is loud
The mechanismlarge, deliberate gaps between element levels
Fidelityas close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, Digitakt II → M4 inputs 3/4, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged
  • New set saved as REP-MANGO, tempo 122
  • M4 phones knob at the lab-001 mark — it stays there for the whole lesson
  • Timebox: 60–120 minutes, then file it and stop — the extra time goes into listening passes, not extra gear
Reference pass — 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to about one minute of “Mango Drive” — then listen to it again. Two or three full passes before you write anything; the answers change between the first pass and the third. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Open each model answer only after your own line exists.

  1. Which element is loudest — and how sure are you?
    Answer

    The chord stabs are the loudest event — but barely; if you said “nothing is loudest”, that is the better answer. The record is built so that no element ever claims the front of the mix. Relisten and watch how the chord arrives: it is the biggest thing in the room, and it is still quiet.

  2. How far below it does the hi-hat sit — a little, or a lot?
    Answer

    A lot — on the order of ten decibels, far enough that you stop noticing the hat until you hunt for it. It is not a backbeat; it is a texture that happens to keep time. Relisten for the moment you lose track of it — that disappearing point is the depth you are about to copy.

  3. Is anything as quiet as “barely there”?
    Answer

    Yes — under everything sits a wash of noise and room that you hear as atmosphere rather than as a part. It lives at the very edge of audibility and never moves. If you missed it, relisten to the gaps between chord stabs: whatever fills them is the answer.

  4. Does anything ever jump forward in level?
    Answer

    No. Nothing changes level by more than a hair for the whole section — the stillness is the design, and it is why the depth reads as a place rather than a mix move. If something seemed to jump, it was your attention moving, not a fader. Relisten once doing nothing but watching for level changes; you will come back almost empty-handed.

  5. Is the depth coming from echo or reverb — or from level distance alone?
    Answer

    There is dub delay and reverb in the production, but the depth survives without them: the hat would still sound far away bone-dry, because it is quiet. Level distance does the placing; the effects only decorate it. Relisten to the hat — distant, yet with no audible tail doing the work.

Build only after the answers exist — you are about to copy the system you just described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your loop lands on the same four sounds at the same distances — as close to the record’s section as the rig allows. Closeness is the measure of your hearing.
  • No element asks to be turned up. If one does, that is the louder-is-better trap talking, not the record.
  • The quietest element is still findable when you look for it — buried exactly as deep as the record buries it.

The whole chain for this lesson — two sources in, four tracks, and the faders as the only spatial tool.

MicroFreak out → TS → M4 in 1 → CHORD / TEXTURE tracks Digitakt II main L/R → 2 × TRS → M4 in 3/4 → KICK / HAT tracks four faders · the only tool → master → phones at the mark
Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the empty tracks

Every judgment in this lesson is a comparison, so the thing you compare against has to be in the session, looped, and at the same loudness as the thing you are building. Physically, the Utility trim ensures neither version can win an A/B just by being louder — the louder-is-better reflex is real, and it works against your ears. Set this up once, properly, and every later decision gets easier.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Mango Drive” into it.
  2. Find the most characteristic 8–16 bars for depth-from-levels — chord, hat, and texture all present — and loop them. Spend a few minutes choosing; the wrong section makes every later pass harder.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim its gain so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, matched to the build you are about to make. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  4. ⌘ T four times; rename with ⌘ R: KICK, HAT, CHORD, TEXTURE.
  5. ⌘ ⌥ I to show In/Out. KICK and HAT: Ext. In 3/4. CHORD and TEXTURE: Ext. In 1.
  6. All faders at 0 dB — unity, the lab-001 rule.
Expected result

The record loops at a polite ≈ −12 dBFS, and four named, correctly-routed, unity-gain tracks wait for audio. Soloing REF plays the section; everything else is silent.

Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

The drums, transcribed and captured

The kick and hat come from the Digitakt, and their placement comes from your ears — transcription is the exercise, so the page will not hand you the steps. Physically, remember the lab-001 special case: M4 inputs 3/4 are fixed line inputs with no gain knobs, so the Digitakt’s Main Volume is the record level for everything in this phase. Budget time for several listening passes per element; the first pass gets the obvious hits, the third gets the truth.

Do this
  1. Solo REF and transcribe the kick and hat placement by ear; the page cannot give you the steps — hearing them is the exercise. Take at least two passes per element: one for the kick alone, one for the hats alone.
  2. Press REC to enter grid recording. Track 1: audition kick samples — at least three candidates — and keep the one closest to the record’s kick. Track 2: the closed hat closest to the record’s.
  3. Place the trigs where the record places them — quarters on 1, 5, 9, 13 with offbeat hats is the likely shape, but let the loop, not this page, confirm it.
  4. Mute Digitakt track 2, arm KICK, set the Digitakt Main Volume until Live’s meter peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — nominal, straight from the lab-001 ledger — and record 8 bars.
  5. Swap the mutes, arm HAT, record 8 bars at the same peak.
  6. Solo each take against REF and listen for a full loop each: as close to the record’s drum sounds as the Digitakt’s slots get. If a sound is clearly wrong, re-audition and re-record now — it is cheap here and expensive in phase 4. Stop the Digitakt.
Expected result

Two 8-bar clips, KICK and HAT, both peaking ≈ −12 dBFS, both placed where the record places them. Soloed against REF, each sounds like a sibling of the record’s drum, not a stranger.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

The chord and the wash

Both remaining sounds come from the MicroFreak through M4 input 1 — one synth, recorded twice, wearing two costumes. The chord carries the harmony, so its notes have to come off the record by ear; the texture is a filtered noise wash whose only job is to sit at the bottom of the mix. Everything records at the same nominal peak on purpose: the depth gets made later, by faders, and by nothing else.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: Paraphonic ON, a soft engine (Type → V.Analog), attack slow, sustain high, cutoff ≈ 40 % as a starting point.
  2. Transcribe the chord’s notes from REF by ear — the page cannot give the notes; hearing them is the exercise. Sing along with the chord first if it helps; the voice finds intervals the fingers miss.
  3. Make at least three solo-flip passes against REF, changing one parameter per pass — cutoff, then envelope, then voicing — until the flip is as close as the MicroFreak gets. One change per pass, or you will not know which move helped.
  4. Set the M4 input-1 gain so peaks sit ≈ −12 dBFS; arm CHORD and record 8 bars.
  5. Turn Type to the Noise engine, close the cutoff toward ≈ 15 % so it becomes a dull wash, and hold a low note.
  6. Solo-flip against REF until the wash sits in the same register as the record’s — darker is almost always closer. Record 8 bars into TEXTURE — same gain, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
Expected result

Two more 8-bar clips at ≈ −12 dBFS: a chord that flips against the record without embarrassment, and a wash that could be mistaken for room tone. All four sounds now exist, all equally loud.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
Chord reads thin and bright next to the record’sCutoff too open, attack too fastClose the cutoff below the starting point and slow the attack until the stab blooms instead of spiking
Notes drop out while voicing the chordParaphony is four voices — a fifth note steals oneVoice the chord within four notes; the record’s chord fits
Phase 4 · ≈ 30 min

Flat, then deep — faders only

This is the lesson. All four sounds sit at the same level, which is exactly wrong — and hearing how wrong teaches you what the record is doing. Physically, nothing in this phase gets louder: depth is made entirely by pulling three faders down until the gaps between elements match the record’s gaps. The targets below are provided starting points; the record corrects them.

The spread — provided targets to verify by ear
Chordpeaks ≈ −12 dBFS — the loudest element, fader at 0
Kickpeaks ≈ −14 dBFS — just under the chord
Hatpeaks ≈ −22 dBFS — a big gap, on purpose
Texturepeaks ≈ −30 dBFS — the edge of attention
Do this
  1. Loop all four clips with every fader at 0 dB — all four elements at equal level, all at nominal.
  2. Solo-flip against REF for a full minute. Same sounds, no depth — it is flat and crowded, and it is not the record. Write one line saying so.
  3. CHORD stays at 0 dB — the loudest element, peaking ≈ −12 dBFS. Solo-flip against REF after every move from here on.
  4. Pull HAT down until it sits as far below the chord as the record’s hat does — ≈ −10 dB is a starting point; the record is the answer.
  5. Pull TEXTURE to the edge of attention exactly where the record holds it, ≈ −18 to −24 dB on the fader.
  6. Set KICK between chord and hat — a few dB down, toward the spread table’s ≈ −14 dBFS — until the weight matches.
  7. Make at least two more full passes across all three moving faders, finer each time. The first pass gets the shape; the later passes get the record.
  8. Touch nothing else — no EQ, no reverb, no clip gain.
Expected result

The flip between REF and your loop lands on the same four sounds at the same distances. Nothing in your version got louder than nominal — things only got quieter — and the mix is suddenly deep.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
Still flat after all the movesTimid fader moves — 2–3 dB gaps where the record uses ≈ 10Make the move size the record makes; pull until it feels like too much, then check the flip
Texture has vanished completelyPulled past the record’s depthBring it up until it is findable when you look for it — buried exactly as deep as the record buries it, no deeper
Phase 5 · ≈ 20 min

Measure, verify, file

You matched the depth by ear; now convert it to numbers so the lesson outlives the session. The meters are about to transcribe the record’s level relationships for you — the gaps in dB are the whole system, written down. Then the verification below tells you how close your ears actually got.

Do this
  1. Observe — see it, not just hear it. With the matched version playing, read the Live channel meters and write the numbers: CHORD peak, HAT peak, TEXTURE peak. Read each on the same bar of the loop so the peaks are comparable.
  2. Compute the gaps in dB. Those gaps are your transcription of the record’s level relationships — the logbook gets them.
  3. Compare your measured gaps with the spread table above. Where they differ, the record won — note by how much.
  4. Write both ledgers. Note the flat fader set (all 0) and the matched set in the logbook, so you can flip between them by hand for the A/B below.
  5. Run the verification below, then write the three-line miss list.
Expected result

The logbook holds three peak readings, the gaps between them, and both fader sets — a depth recipe measured off your own ears, checked against the record.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix

Gaps differ wildly between readingsPeaks read on different bars of the loopPick one bar and read every meter on that bar only
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your matched version at matched loudness — phones at the mark, and remember nothing in your version got louder than nominal, things only got quieter.

  • Could a friend, hearing 10 seconds of yours then 10 of the record, say which is which? The longer they hesitate, the better your ears did.
    Answer

    If they hesitate, your levels are doing the record’s work. If they answer instantly, the giveaway is almost always a sound mismatch — the wrong kick family, a brighter chord — rather than a level mismatch. Listen for which element betrays you first; that element is your next matching pass, back in phase 2 or 3.

  • Are the gaps the same size by ear — chord to hat, hat to texture — before you ever look at the meters?
    Answer

    Judge both gaps by ear first, then let the meters confirm. If your ear says “same distances” but the meters disagree by more than a couple of dB, check the comparison itself before doubting your ears — REF’s trim and the phones mark drift more often than hearing does. Relisten to the hat-to-texture gap last; it is the harder of the two and the better test.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
