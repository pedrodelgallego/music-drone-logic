Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: loop the grain

Cut a slice shorter than a second, loop it until the ear stops hearing an edit, and stretch it into the whole track.

The point

Repetition below the level of melody — a sub-second slice looped so fast and so smoothly that it stops being a phrase and becomes a texture. The ear gives up looking for the edit, and from that moment a sliver of borrowed sound can carry minutes.

The system you are replicating
Source recordThe Field — “Over the Ice”
The effectA shimmering pad that is not a pad at all — a tiny loop running so smoothly it reads as a surface
The mechanismA slice under one second, loop-crossfaded until the edit vanishes, tuned and layered into texture with a kick as the clock
FidelityAs close to the record’s section as your rig allows — slice character, wash, and grid included. Closeness is the measure of your hearing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Live set open, an empty MIDI track ready for Simpler — this lesson lives inside the box
  • Digitakt on M4 in 3–4 for the kick, clock-synced to Live — the lab 001/002 wiring
  • Phones at the mark · 60–120 minutes clear — finding the right sliver takes many tries, and the crossfade work is patient, small-move listening
Reference pass — 10 minutes

Describe their texture before you match it

Listen to about one minute of “Over the Ice,” the main looped section. Answer in the logbook, one line each, BEFORE touching gear. Check each answer against the model only after yours is written.

  1. Can you find the loop point — the place where the slice starts over?
    Answer

    Almost certainly not, and that failure is the data: the edit has been smoothed until the repetition reads as a continuous surface. What you hear instead is a fast, even churn — the loop is present as rhythmic texture, absent as event. Relisten trying to tap along with the churn itself rather than the kick.

  2. How long is the loop — your best guess in seconds?
    Answer

    Shorter than you think — well under a second, a syllable rather than a phrase. At that length the loop repeats several times per bar, which is why it stops registering as melody and starts registering as grain. Relisten and try to count repeats inside a single bar; losing count immediately is the right answer.

  3. What might the slice have been before it was cut — voice, strings, guitar?
    Answer

    It hardly matters, and that is the lesson: at sub-second length the source’s identity collapses into pure timbre — a breathy, harmonic shimmer that could be voice or strings or both. The technique deliberately destroys the source’s name while keeping its colour. Relisten and describe the colour in adjectives, not instruments.

  4. What does the kick do to the texture when it enters?
    Answer

    It turns weather into a track. The shimmer alone floats free of time; the kick gives it a clock, and suddenly the churn reads as rhythm instead of haze. Relisten across the kick’s entry and notice how the same unchanged loop seems to start “playing” the moment the pulse arrives.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the texture you described, not the one you remember.

  • Sound target: a sub-second slice looping with no audible edit, matching the section’s shimmer in colour and rate.
  • No phrase: if your loop reads as a melody or a phrase, it is too long — the target is grain, not motif.
  • The clock: a kick under the texture, placed where theirs sits, turning the churn into a track.
  • The number: the slice length in milliseconds, written down — the whole lesson fits in that number.

Steps

Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Capture the slice

The whole replica stands on one sliver of audio, so most of this phase is auditioning candidates. This is study replication: slice from the looped section of the record itself so you can hear exactly how little material the original needs. The slice you keep should be boring on its own — a syllable of shimmer, not an interesting phrase.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track. Drop “Over the Ice” onto a dedicated REF track in Live, loop the main section, and gain-match it with a Utility. Keep it one solo away for the whole session.
  2. Hunt for the sliver. Zoom far into the looped section’s waveform and audition short stretches — places where the shimmer is exposed and the kick is absent or quiet are the best quarries.
  3. Cut under one second. Crop a candidate to well under a second — start around 300–700 ms — and consolidate it to its own clip (⌘ J). Cut on or near zero crossings to keep the raw edit quiet.
  4. Audition three candidates by looping each clip raw. Keep the one whose colour matches the record’s churn; write the other two off without regret.
Expected result

One consolidated clip under a second long whose looped colour already resembles the record’s shimmer — clicks and bumps at the edit allowed, for now.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Every candidate loops as an obvious phraseThe slice is too long — it still contains musical eventsHalve it. Keep halving until the loop reads as texture, not melody
The loop thumps on every repeatA kick transient is inside the sliceRe-slice from a stretch where the low end is quiet, or cut just after a kick hit
Phase 2 — ~25 minutes

Erase the edit in Simpler

Simpler in loop mode is the machine that turns a slice into a surface. The crossfade is the entire trick: it overlaps the loop’s tail with its head so the ear never finds the seam. Tuning comes second — the slice must sit in a useful register before it can pretend to be a pad.

Do this
  1. Load the slice into Simpler on a MIDI track and set the playback mode to Classic with Loop on — the note now sustains by cycling the slice for as long as a key is held.
  2. Crossfade the loop point. Raise Simpler’s loop Fade until the click disappears, and no further — too much fade smears the churn that gives the texture its rate. Hold a note for thirty seconds and listen only for the seam.
  3. Tune it. Use Simpler’s transpose and detune against the REF until your loop’s pitch centre sits where theirs sits. Write the slice length in ms and the transpose value in the logbook.
  4. Stretch it into a pad. Open the amplitude envelope — attack around 200–500 ms, long release — and hold soft chords or single notes. The micro-loop is now an instrument: grain on the inside, pad on the outside.

The whole machine, drawn — one sliver in, a surface out.

sub-second slice → Simpler · loop mode + crossfade → envelope (slow attack) → pad texture → mix, kick under
Expected result

A held note that sustains indefinitely with no audible loop point — a texture you could not identify as an edit if you had not built it.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The seam still clicks at any fade amountThe loop start sits on a sharp transientNudge Simpler’s start point past the transient, then re-set the fade from zero
The texture turns into a featureless smearCrossfade pushed far past the click pointBack the fade down to just past where the click died — the churn is the character, keep it
Phase 3 — ~25 minutes

Give it a clock, match the wash, then look at the sliver

Alone, the texture is weather; the kick makes it a track. This phase places a four-on-the-floor under the micro-loop, balances the two against the REF, and lets the section run. Then comes the observing: open Simpler and look at how little audio is doing all this work.

Do this
  1. Kick under. Program a plain four-on-the-floor on the Digitakt at the record’s tempo, tuned low and soft like theirs — the kick is a clock, not an event.
  2. Let the micro-loop run. Hold or sequence your pad notes over the kick for two to three minutes, matching the record’s register and density — most of the time this is one chord, sustained.
  3. Match the wash. A/B against the REF and balance level and brightness — a touch of EQ or a send is allowed — until 10 seconds of yours could pass for 10 of theirs in a blind switch.
  4. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Open Simpler and LOOK at the slice between the loop markers while the section plays. Write its length in milliseconds in the logbook; that sliver is the whole track, and the number is the proof.

The scale of the trick — what the listener hears versus what actually exists.

~500 ms
What exists: one sliver of audio between two loop markers in Simpler.
1 bar
What the ear gets: several repeats already — too fast to count, churn instead of phrase.
1 minute
A shimmering surface with a pulse — the edit forgotten, the source unnameable.
3 minutes
A track — the same sliver, still looping, now carrying everything.
Expected result

Two to three recorded minutes of kick plus micro-loop that sit convincingly next to the record’s section — and a logbook line with the slice length in ms.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Yours feels static next to theirsThe record layers slow swells over the grain; yours is one bare noteAdd one slow layer only — a second, quieter Simpler note swelling in and out — never a new sound source
The kick fights the textureKick too loud or too sharp — it is an event, not a clockPull it down and soften the transient until it disappears into the churn the way theirs does
Verify against the record

A/B your section against “Over the Ice” — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate? Then answer two questions in writing.

Can a listener find your loop point?

Answer

They should fail exactly the way you failed on the record: the crossfade has dissolved the edit, and the repetition reads as a continuous churn rather than a cycling slice. If the seam is findable, the fade is too short or the loop start sits on a transient. Relisten to a thirty-second held note and hunt for your own seam — honest failure to find it is the pass.

Does your loop read as texture rather than phrase — and how long is it, exactly?

Answer

The test is the count: if you can count the repeats inside a bar, it is a phrase; if you lose count immediately, it is grain. The number in your logbook should be well under 1000 ms — the record’s trick lives at that scale, where source identity collapses into colour. Relisten to yours and theirs back to back and confirm both churn at about the same rate.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: “Matched: …” · “Missed: … (best guess why)” · “Change next: …”
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • And one more line — the slice length in ms, and what shrinking a loop below the phrase did that no longer loop could. The micro-loop is an engine of repetition you can name in your lab 003 hypothesis.
