Replicate: loop the grain

Copy one clear section of The Field — “Over the Ice”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: A slice under one second, loop-crossfaded until the edit vanishes, tuned and layered into texture with a kick as the clock. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Live set open, an empty MIDI track ready for Simpler — this lesson lives inside the box
  • Digitakt II on M4 in 3–4 for the kick, clock-synced to Live — the lab 001/002 wiring
  • What you will make: a short replica of The Field — “Over the Ice”. Focus on this effect: A shimmering pad that is not a pad at all - a tiny loop running so smoothly it reads as a surface.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: A slice under one second, loop-crossfaded until the edit vanishes, tuned and layered into texture with a kick as the clock.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of The Field — “Over the Ice”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Can you find the loop point — the place where the slice starts over?
    Answer

    Almost certainly not, and that failure is the data: the edit has been smoothed until the repetition reads as a continuous surface. What you hear instead is a fast, even churn - the loop is present as rhythmic texture, absent as event.

  2. How long is the loop — your best guess in seconds?
    Answer

    Shorter than you think - well under a second, a syllable rather than a phrase. At that length the loop repeats several times per bar, which is why it stops registering as melody and starts registering as grain.

  3. What might the slice have been before it was cut — voice, strings, guitar?
    Answer

    It hardly matters, and that is the lesson: at sub-second length the source’s identity collapses into pure timbre - a breathy, harmonic shimmer that could be voice or strings or both.

  4. What does the kick do to the texture when it enters?
    Answer

    It turns weather into a track. The shimmer alone floats free of time; the kick gives it a clock, and suddenly the churn reads as rhythm instead of haze.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target: a sub-second slice looping with no audible edit, matching the section’s shimmer in colour and rate.
  • No phrase: if your loop reads as a melody or a phrase, it is too long — the target is grain, not motif.
  • The clock: a kick under the texture, placed where theirs sits, turning the churn into a track.
  • The number: the slice length in milliseconds, written down — the whole lesson fits in that number.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Capture the slice

Phase focus: capture the slice. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track. Drop “Over the Ice” onto a dedicated REF track in Live, loop the main section, and gain-match it with a Utility.
  2. Keep it one solo away for the whole session.
  3. Hunt for the sliver. Zoom far into the looped section’s waveform and audition short stretches — places where the shimmer is exposed and the kick is absent or quiet are the best quarries.
  4. Cut under one second. Crop a candidate to well under a second — start around 300–700 ms — and consolidate it to its own clip (⌘ J).
  5. Cut on or near zero crossings to keep the raw edit quiet.
  6. Audition three candidates by looping each clip raw.
  7. Keep the one whose colour matches the record’s churn; write the other two off without regret.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from The Field — “Over the Ice” and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — Capture the slice — as the smallest Live set that proves A slice under one second, loop-crossfaded until the edit vanishes, tuned and layered into texture with a kick as the clock.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects A shimmering pad that is not a pad at all — a tiny loop running so smoothly it reads as a surface.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate Capture the slice into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates A slice under one second, loop-crossfaded until the edit vanishes, tuned and layered into texture with a kick as the clock, not just the surface of The Field — “Over the Ice”.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

One consolidated clip under a second long whose looped colour already resembles the record’s shimmer — clicks and bumps at the edit allowed, for now.

Phase 2 — ~25 minutes

Erase the edit in Simpler

Phase focus: erase the edit in Simpler. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Load the slice into Simpler on a MIDI track and set the playback mode to Classic with Loop on — the note now sustains by cycling the slice for as long as a key is held.
  2. Crossfade the loop point. Raise Simpler’s loop Fade until the click disappears, and no further — too much fade smears the churn that gives the texture its rate.
  3. Hold a note for thirty seconds and listen only for the seam.
  4. Tune it. Use Simpler’s transpose and detune against the REF until your loop’s pitch centre sits where theirs sits.
  5. Write the slice length in ms and the transpose value in the logbook.
  6. Stretch it into a pad. Open the amplitude envelope — attack around 200–500 ms, long release — and hold soft chords or single notes.
  7. The micro-loop is now an instrument: grain on the inside, pad on the outside.

Phase 3 — ~25 minutes

Give it a clock, match the wash, then look at the sliver

Phase focus: give it a clock, match the wash, then look at the sliver. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Kick under. Program a plain four-on-the-floor on the Digitakt II at the record’s tempo, tuned low and soft like theirs — the kick is a clock, not an event.
  2. Let the micro-loop run. Hold or sequence your pad notes over the kick for two to three minutes, matching the record’s register and density — most of the time this is one chord, sustained.
  3. Match the wash. A/B against the REF and balance level and brightness — a touch of EQ or a send is allowed — until 10 seconds of yours could pass for 10 of theirs in a blind switch.
  4. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Open Simpler and LOOK at the slice between the loop markers while the section plays.
  5. Write its length in milliseconds in the logbook; that sliver is the whole track, and the number is the proof.

The scale of the trick — what the listener hears versus what actually exists.

~500 ms
What exists: one sliver of audio between two loop markers in Simpler.
1 bar
What the ear gets: several repeats already — too fast to count, churn instead of phrase.
1 minute
A shimmering surface with a pulse — the edit forgotten, the source unnameable.
3 minutes
A track — the same sliver, still looping, now carrying everything.
Expected result

Two to three recorded minutes of kick plus micro-loop that sit convincingly next to the record’s section — and a logbook line with the slice length in ms.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Yours feels static next to theirsThe record layers slow swells over the grain; yours is one bare noteAdd one slow layer only — a second, quieter Simpler note swelling in and out — never a new sound source
The kick fights the textureKick too loud or too sharp — it is an event, not a clockPull it down and soften the transient until it disappears into the churn the way theirs does
Verify against the record

A/B your section against “Over the Ice” — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate? Then answer two questions in writing.

Can a listener find your loop point?

Answer

They should fail exactly the way you failed on the record: the crossfade has dissolved the edit, and the repetition reads as a continuous churn rather than a cycling slice. If the seam is findable, the fade is too short or the loop start sits on a transient. Relisten to a thirty-second held note and hunt for your own seam — honest failure to find it is the pass.

Does your loop read as texture rather than phrase — and how long is it, exactly?

Answer

The test is the count: if you can count the repeats inside a bar, it is a phrase; if you lose count immediately, it is grain. The number in your logbook should be well under 1000 ms — the record’s trick lives at that scale, where source identity collapses into colour. Relisten to yours and theirs back to back and confirm both churn at about the same rate.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: “Matched: …” · “Missed: … (best guess why)” · “Change next: …”
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • And one more line — the slice length in ms, and what shrinking a loop below the phrase did that no longer loop could. The micro-loop is an engine of repetition you can name in your lab 003 hypothesis.
