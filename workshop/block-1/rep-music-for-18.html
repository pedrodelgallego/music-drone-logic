Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: pulse and slow change

Copy one clear section of Steve Reich — Music for 18 Musicians. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: Unbroken eighth-note pulses; one chord held for a long span, then exchanged for the next; dynamics swelling like lungs. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Live set open, MicroFreak on M4 in 1 with MIDI from Live — the lab 001/002 wiring
  • MicroFreak in Paraphonic mode, a soft, organ-like patch sketched — four voices available
  • What you will make: a short replica of Steve Reich — Music for 18 Musicians. Focus on this effect: An hour of even pulses that never bores - harmony arrives and leaves without ever announcing itself.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: Unbroken eighth-note pulses; one chord held for a long span, then exchanged for the next; dynamics swelling like lungs.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Steve Reich — Music for 18 Musicians. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What does the pulse do for the entire stretch — does it ever stop, swing, or accent?
    Answer

    It never stops and never varies: even eighth notes, machine-steady, passed between instruments so no single player ever has to breathe wrong.

  2. When does the harmony actually shift — could you point at the moment?
    Answer

    Barely, and that is the design: new notes fade in underneath the pulse while old ones fade out, so the chord has changed before you can say when. You notice you are somewhere new without having felt the door.

  3. How do the voices and winds behave inside a held chord?
    Answer

    They swell in and out like lungs - long crescendos and decrescendos shaped by the length of a human breath, over a pulse that never bends. The breathing gives the static harmony its inner life: nothing changes, yet nothing is still.

  4. Roughly how long does one chord hold before the next arrives?
    Answer

    Whole minutes - far longer than any pop instinct allows, and longer than you remembered.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target: even eighth-note chord pulses at the record’s tempo, soft-edged like mallets and voices, never accented.
  • Breath: dynamics swell in slow waves over the unbending pulse — lungs over a clock.
  • Patience: one chord change per long span, with spans as long as theirs — measured, not guessed.
  • No pointable moment: if a listener can say “the chord changed THERE,” the change was too fast.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

The pulse — even eighths on a paraphonic chord

Phase focus: the pulse — even eighths on a paraphonic chord. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Import the record on a REF track. Drop your chosen section onto a dedicated REF track in Live, loop three to four minutes, gain-match with a Utility, and keep it one solo away all session.
  2. Live + Logbook Match their tempo. Tap Live’s tempo along with the REF’s pulse until the click sits inside their eighths, and write the BPM down.
  3. MicroFreak Voice one chord on the MicroFreak in Paraphonic mode — four voices, mid register, matched by ear against the chord under your looped stretch.
  4. MicroFreak Soften the attack slightly so each pulse blooms rather than hits.
  5. Digitakt II + Live Sequence even eighths. In a Live MIDI clip, place the full chord on every eighth note, all velocities identical, no swing, and loop it.
  6. Digitakt II + Live The grid is the performer here — that is faithful, not lazy.
Expected result

A pulsing chord that could run under the REF without rhythmic friction — same tempo, same evenness, a register and colour in the record’s family.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Pulses cut each other off into a choppy stutterNote lengths overlap and steal paraphonic voicesShorten the MIDI notes to just under an eighth so each chord retriggers cleanly
The pulse reads as a techno stab, not an ensembleAttack too fast, filter too openSlow the attack slightly and close the filter until each pulse blooms — chase the REF’s softness, not a preset
Phase 2 — ~20 minutes

Breathe the dynamics

Phase focus: breathe the dynamics. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Time their breath. Solo the REF and count seconds across one swell — in, then out.
  2. Live That number, typically a handful of seconds, is your wave length.
  3. Live Draw velocity waves. In the MIDI clip’s velocity lane, draw a slow rise and fall across the bar span you measured, repeating — the pulse stays even in time while leaning in and out in weight.
  4. MicroFreak + Live Add the filter lung. Automate a gentle filter-cutoff wave on the same period (clip envelope to the MicroFreak’s cutoff, or an Auto Filter on its audio track) so brightness breathes with loudness, the way air does.
  5. Live A/B the breathing. Solo against the REF: your swells should be as slow and as deep as theirs — if yours read as tremolo or wobble, the wave is far too fast.

The whole system, drawn — a clock with lungs, exchanged one chord at a time.

MIDI clip · even eighths → MicroFreak · paraphonic chord → M4 in 1 → Live track velocity wave (breath in/out) + filter wave (brightness breathes) + one chord change per span
Expected result

A pulse that is metrically rigid but dynamically alive — even eighths that lean in and out in slow waves matching the record’s breath length.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The swells read as wobble or tremoloWave period much shorter than a breathStretch the wave to the seconds you timed on the REF — when in doubt, slower
The breathing is inaudibleVelocity range too narrow, or the patch ignores velocityWiden the velocity wave’s depth and confirm the patch maps velocity to level; the exhale should nearly disappear
Phase 3 — ~30 minutes

Change the harmony at the speed of breath, then measure your patience

Phase focus: change the harmony at the speed of breath, then measure your patience. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Transcribe their span map. From the reference pass, note where in your looped stretch the harmony shifts and how long each chord holds.
  2. Live Two or three changes is plenty.
  3. Live Voice the next chord nearby. Choose a second voicing (and a third, if their stretch has one) that shares notes with the first — the original moves by exchanging a few notes, never by jumping.
  4. Live Make the change a crossfade. Duplicate the pulsing clip with the new chord, and overlap the change across several bars — the old chord’s velocity wave breathing out for the last time as the new one breathes in.
  5. Live Record the result as one long take.
  6. Live Observe — measure it. In Arrangement, drop a locator at each harmonic change and read the span lengths in bars.
  7. Logbook Write the bar counts in the logbook; that patience is the piece, and the numbers are yours to compare against their minutes.

The span map, drawn — adjust the lengths to what you measured on the record.

bars 1–48
Chord I — pulse even, dynamics breathing. Nothing else is allowed to happen.
≈ bar 48
The hand-over — chord II fades in under chord I across several bars; no pointable moment.
bars 49–96
Chord II — held just as long. The patience is the form.
≈ bar 96
Second hand-over, same slowness — then chord III, or back to I, to the end.
Expected result

A recorded stretch with two or three harmonic changes nobody could timestamp on first listen — and a logbook line of measured span lengths in bars.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The chord change lands as an audible eventThe crossfade is too short, or the new voicing shares no notesStretch the overlap to more bars and revoice the new chord to keep common tones
You cut every span short of the record’sProducer instinct — the span FEELS done at a quarter of their lengthSequence the span lengths from the measured map, not from feel; the discomfort is the lesson
Verify against the record

A/B your stretch against the looped section of Music for 18 Musicians — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate? Then answer two questions in writing.

Could a listener point at the moment your harmony changes?

Answer

Barely — that is the pass condition, exactly as it was on the record. The new chord should be present before anyone could say it arrived, because it entered underneath the pulse over several bars of overlap. If your change is timestampable, the hand-over was too short. Relisten to your own take a day later and try to timestamp it yourself; failing is passing.

Are your spans as long as theirs — measured in bars, not in feel?

Answer

The locator measurements settle this without opinion: their spans run whole minutes, and a faithful replica’s bar counts land in the same range, not at the quarter-length your instinct begged for. The numbers in your logbook are the artifact — patience made measurable. Relisten to your longest span straight through and notice it stopped feeling long once the breathing was right.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: “Matched: …” · “Missed: … (best guess why)” · “Change next: …”
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • And one more line — the measured span lengths, and what changing at the speed of breath did that a faster change never could. Pulse with slow change is an engine of repetition you can name in your lab 003 hypothesis — the acoustic ancestor of all the others.
