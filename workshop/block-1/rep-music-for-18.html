Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: pulse and slow change

Even pulses that never break, harmony that changes at the speed of breath — the acoustic ancestor of every loop on this page.

The point

The original repetition engine: keep the pulse perfectly even, and let the harmony change so slowly it is felt before it is noticed. Change at the speed of breath cannot be pointed at — and that unpointability is the design, not a side effect.

The system you are replicating
Source recordSteve Reich — Music for 18 Musicians
The effectAn hour of even pulses that never bores — harmony arrives and leaves without ever announcing itself
The mechanismUnbroken eighth-note pulses; one chord held for a long span, then exchanged for the next; dynamics swelling like lungs
FidelityAs close to the record’s section as your rig allows — tempo, voicing register, swell shape, and span lengths included. Closeness is the measure of your hearing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Live set open, MicroFreak on M4 in 1 with MIDI from Live — the lab 001/002 wiring
  • MicroFreak in Paraphonic mode, a soft, organ-like patch sketched — four voices available
  • Phones at the mark · 60–120 minutes clear — the spans are long by design, and judging a change that takes a minute requires listening for the whole minute
Reference pass — 10 minutes

Describe their patience before you match it

Listen to three to four minutes of Music for 18 Musicians — the opening “Pulses” is ideal. Answer in the logbook, one line each, BEFORE touching gear. Check each answer against the model only after yours is written.

  1. What does the pulse do for the entire stretch — does it ever stop, swing, or accent?
    Answer

    It never stops and never varies: even eighth notes, machine-steady, passed between instruments so no single player ever has to breathe wrong. The evenness is the frame that makes everything else perceptible — any accent would become an event and steal attention from the harmony. Relisten and try to catch a single uneven pulse; you won’t.

  2. When does the harmony actually shift — could you point at the moment?
    Answer

    Barely, and that is the design: new notes fade in underneath the pulse while old ones fade out, so the chord has changed before you can say when. You notice you are somewhere new without having felt the door. Relisten to one shift watching only the bass register, where the change is most catchable.

  3. How do the voices and winds behave inside a held chord?
    Answer

    They swell in and out like lungs — long crescendos and decrescendos shaped by the length of a human breath, over a pulse that never bends. The breathing gives the static harmony its inner life: nothing changes, yet nothing is still. Relisten to one span and count the swells; their regularity is the second pulse of the piece.

  4. Roughly how long does one chord hold before the next arrives?
    Answer

    Whole minutes — far longer than any pop instinct allows, and longer than you remembered. Each chord in the piece gets an extended span, and the patience is structural: the slower the change, the larger it feels when you finally notice it has happened. Relisten with a clock visible and time one full span honestly.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the patience you measured, not the patience you assumed.

  • Sound target: even eighth-note chord pulses at the record’s tempo, soft-edged like mallets and voices, never accented.
  • Breath: dynamics swell in slow waves over the unbending pulse — lungs over a clock.
  • Patience: one chord change per long span, with spans as long as theirs — measured, not guessed.
  • No pointable moment: if a listener can say “the chord changed THERE,” the change was too fast.

Steps

Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

The pulse — even eighths on a paraphonic chord

Everything in this lesson stands on a pulse that never wavers, so it is sequenced, not played — human timing is exactly what the original’s players spent years training out. The MicroFreak’s four paraphonic voices stand in for the ensemble; the patch wants to be soft-edged and breathy, closer to marimba-plus-voice than to a synth stab.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track. Drop your chosen section onto a dedicated REF track in Live, loop three to four minutes, gain-match with a Utility, and keep it one solo away all session.
  2. Match their tempo. Tap Live’s tempo along with the REF’s pulse until the click sits inside their eighths, and write the BPM down.
  3. Voice one chord on the MicroFreak in Paraphonic mode — four voices, mid register, matched by ear against the chord under your looped stretch. Soften the attack slightly so each pulse blooms rather than hits.
  4. Sequence even eighths. In a Live MIDI clip, place the full chord on every eighth note, all velocities identical, no swing, and loop it. The grid is the performer here — that is faithful, not lazy.
Expected result

A pulsing chord that could run under the REF without rhythmic friction — same tempo, same evenness, a register and colour in the record’s family.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Pulses cut each other off into a choppy stutterNote lengths overlap and steal paraphonic voicesShorten the MIDI notes to just under an eighth so each chord retriggers cleanly
The pulse reads as a techno stab, not an ensembleAttack too fast, filter too openSlow the attack slightly and close the filter until each pulse blooms — chase the REF’s softness, not a preset
Phase 2 — ~20 minutes

Breathe the dynamics

A held chord on a grid is dead until it breathes. The record’s inner life comes from swells the length of a human breath — voices and winds rising and falling over the unbending pulse. You will draw the same lungs as slow waves of velocity and filter, and the waves must stay slower than any musical figure: this is breathing, not rhythm.

Do this
  1. Time their breath. Solo the REF and count seconds across one swell — in, then out. That number, typically a handful of seconds, is your wave length.
  2. Draw velocity waves. In the MIDI clip’s velocity lane, draw a slow rise and fall across the bar span you measured, repeating — the pulse stays even in time while leaning in and out in weight.
  3. Add the filter lung. Automate a gentle filter-cutoff wave on the same period (clip envelope to the MicroFreak’s cutoff, or an Auto Filter on its audio track) so brightness breathes with loudness, the way air does.
  4. A/B the breathing. Solo against the REF: your swells should be as slow and as deep as theirs — if yours read as tremolo or wobble, the wave is far too fast.

The whole system, drawn — a clock with lungs, exchanged one chord at a time.

MIDI clip · even eighths → MicroFreak · paraphonic chord → M4 in 1 → Live track velocity wave (breath in/out) + filter wave (brightness breathes) + one chord change per span
Expected result

A pulse that is metrically rigid but dynamically alive — even eighths that lean in and out in slow waves matching the record’s breath length.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The swells read as wobble or tremoloWave period much shorter than a breathStretch the wave to the seconds you timed on the REF — when in doubt, slower

Phase 3 — ~30 minutes

Change the harmony at the speed of breath, then measure your patience

Now the engine itself: one chord change per long span, placed where the record places them. The change must be a hand-over, not a cut — the new chord fades in under the old one so no moment is pointable. Then the observing pass turns your patience into numbers: locators at every change, spans measured in bars.

Do this
  1. Transcribe their span map. From the reference pass, note where in your looped stretch the harmony shifts and how long each chord holds. Two or three changes is plenty.
  2. Voice the next chord nearby. Choose a second voicing (and a third, if their stretch has one) that shares notes with the first — the original moves by exchanging a few notes, never by jumping.
  3. Make the change a crossfade. Duplicate the pulsing clip with the new chord, and overlap the change across several bars — the old chord’s velocity wave breathing out for the last time as the new one breathes in. Record the result as one long take.
  4. Observe — measure it. In Arrangement, drop a locator at each harmonic change and read the span lengths in bars. Write the bar counts in the logbook; that patience is the piece, and the numbers are yours to compare against their minutes.

The span map, drawn — adjust the lengths to what you measured on the record.

bars 1–48
Chord I — pulse even, dynamics breathing. Nothing else is allowed to happen.
≈ bar 48
The hand-over — chord II fades in under chord I across several bars; no pointable moment.
bars 49–96
Chord II — held just as long. The patience is the form.
≈ bar 96
Second hand-over, same slowness — then chord III, or back to I, to the end.
Expected result

A recorded stretch with two or three harmonic changes nobody could timestamp on first listen — and a logbook line of measured span lengths in bars.

Verify against the record

A/B your stretch against the looped section of Music for 18 Musicians — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate? Then answer two questions in writing.

Could a listener point at the moment your harmony changes?

Answer

Barely — that is the pass condition, exactly as it was on the record. The new chord should be present before anyone could say it arrived, because it entered underneath the pulse over several bars of overlap. If your change is timestampable, the hand-over was too short. Relisten to your own take a day later and try to timestamp it yourself; failing is passing.

Are your spans as long as theirs — measured in bars, not in feel?

Answer

The locator measurements settle this without opinion: their spans run whole minutes, and a faithful replica’s bar counts land in the same range, not at the quarter-length your instinct begged for. The numbers in your logbook are the artifact — patience made measurable. Relisten to your longest span straight through and notice it stopped feeling long once the breathing was right.
