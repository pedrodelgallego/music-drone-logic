Block 1 · Programme L1 · Listening

Repetition & Hypnosis — three engines, one effect

Three listening sessions that teach you to hear records the way a builder reads buildings: not “is it beautiful” but “how does it stand up”. Everything you build in this block starts from what you hear here.

Listening programme · 3 sessions · one full cycle · feeds labs 003–004 and capstone C1

The point

You can’t build what you can’t hear. These sessions turn “I like this track” into “I can name the machine that makes this track work” — and that machine, not the track, is what you take.

Listening is the loop

These sessions are not homework beside the labs — they are the first step of the operating loop. Every hypothesis you build in this block is stolen from here, and the questions below are the same five System Spec questions the labs use, plus a sixth.

Start here
  • Create a logbook page titled L1 — repetition and hypnosis.
  • Run three separate sessions: records, samples, video. Do not merge them into one long sitting.
  • Answer the same six questions for every reference, even when the answer is a guess.
  • Finish by naming one stolen principle before any build lab uses it.

New concepts — in plain words

Four terms carry this page. Here is what each one means, before any of them is used.

ConceptIn plain words
System vs material Material is the actual stuff: this melody, this chord sound, this sample. The system is the machinery: what changes, what stays fixed, who decides. Material is theirs; systems are free to take. “A chord like Basic Channel’s” is material. “One element changes at a time” is a system.
The six questions A fixed checklist you ask of every record: what goes in, what changes it, what keeps it alive, what keeps it tidy, what the human does, and what equipment path had to exist. Asking the same six every time is what turns listening into analysis.
Deconstruction Playing eight bars on repeat and taking them apart on paper: list every sound, guess where each came from and what was done to it. You will guess wrong. Written wrong guesses can be corrected; vague impressions cannot.
The stolen principle One structural idea you take from the whole programme, named in writing before you build with it. Naming it in advance makes it a commitment instead of a story you tell afterwards.

How to listen — the six-question frame

Every work in this programme gets the same six questions, answered in writing in your logbook. A few lines per question is enough; vagueness is the only wrong answer.

  1. Inputs — what raw material enters the system?
  2. Transforms — what changes it, in what order?
  3. Feedback / variation — what keeps it alive over time?
  4. Constraints — what prevents clutter?
  5. Human role — what is performed, decided, curated?
  6. Likely audio path — what had to be routed, recorded, mixed, or performed for this to exist?

The method is three passes per work. First pass: just listen. No notes, no pausing. Second pass: answer questions 1–5. Third pass: question 6 only. The sixth is the engineering pass and the hardest — you can’t answer it without guessing at mixing desks, effects, and hands. Guessing is the exercise.

Session 1 — Records: three engines of repetition

One effect — a loop that never gets boring — made by three different machines. Listen to each record with the six-question frame, then compare the engines. The differences are the lesson; any one record alone would only teach its own surface.

01 Basic Channel — “Quadrant Dub I” dub-filtered loop

The chord never changes. The filter around it does, the space around it does, the level around it does. Count the actual events in ten minutes — write the number down. It is fewer than your memory claims, and the gap between the count and the memory is the whole technique.

Engine: the dub-filtered loop — the mixing desk is the instrument. The material is static; the desk performs.

Listen for
  • How muffled or bright the chord is at any moment — the filter is doing the moving
  • How much space and echo surround it, and when that grows or shrinks
  • The actual events in ten minutes — counted and written down
After — verify

Was your ten-minute event count under ten? Most people remember far more events than they heard — that gap is the technique working on you. Can you say what the filter was doing in the last two minutes versus the first two? If not, relisten to just the opening and the closing.

02 Plastikman — Consumed (title track) subtractive automation

Arrangement by subtraction. The question to keep asking is not “what is playing?” but “what is absent right now, and when did it leave?” Follow the 303 and the room around it — notice how much of the piece is the space where something used to be.

Engine: subtractive automation — the piece is carved, not assembled. Removal is the compositional act.

Listen for
  • What is absent right now, and when it left
  • The 303 and the room around it
  • How much of the piece is the space where something used to be
After — verify

Name one element that left, and roughly when it came back. Could you tell whether a change was something added or something removed? In this record it is almost always removal. If you’re not sure, relisten to any two minutes and write down each exit as it happens.

03 Donato Dozzy & Nuel — The Aquaplano Sessions modulation drift

Nothing ever repeats exactly. Pick one element and follow it for five minutes: every cycle is slightly different, and the differences are too small to be arrangement moves. This is modulation drift as the hand on the system — the loop breathes because something inside it is always moving.

Engine: drift — variation lives inside the voice, not the arrangement. The structure holds still; the sound does not.

Listen for
  • One element, followed for five minutes — every cycle slightly different
  • Differences too small to be arrangement moves
  • The thing inside the sound that is always moving
After — verify

Describe in one sentence what changed about the element you followed. Brighter? Closer? Rougher? If you genuinely couldn’t hear it drift, that is a finding — write it down; R1 will test it. Then relisten, following the simplest element instead.

Session 2 — Samples: one track to its sources

Session 2 · Deconstruction

Trace the densest eight bars backwards

Pick one of the three records — or another hypnotic track you love. Loop the densest eight bars you can find. List every audible element: expect four to seven. That number will be lower than you think, and that is the first finding. For each element, guess the likely source and the likely processing chain, in order. Finish by answering the sixth question for the whole track: what path had to exist for this record to exist?

Note

You will be wrong, and being wrong in writing is the point. A guess on paper can be corrected; an impression cannot. The guesses get sharper every block — but only if this block’s guesses exist.

This worksheet is a blueprint

In replication lab R1 you will rebuild these eight bars from this worksheet — and find out, empirically, which guesses were right. Write each row as if someone will have to build from it. Someone will: you.

After — verify

Your element list should have four to seven rows. If you found ten or more, you probably split single sounds into pieces; if two or three, you probably merged separate sounds into one. Either way, relisten once with fresh ears before locking the worksheet.

Session 3 — Video: watch the hands

Session 3 · Video

A hardware-techno live set, watched twice

Pick one hardware-techno live set with clearly visible hands — any era. The canon-breadth rule applies: at least one reference in this programme should come from outside the obvious canon, so consider a contemporary or regional artist for this slot. Watch it twice: once as an audience member, start to finish. Then again, muted, watching only the hands.

Surgeon — Boiler Room London LIVE Setwatch for one-person control, restraint, and recovery risk
Karenn — Boiler Room London Live Setwatch for shared hardware roles and planned instability
Benjamin Damage — Live All-Hardware Techno Lockdown Setwatch for sequencer/mixer balance and what is committed live
  1. Resting position — where are the hands resting between actions?
  2. Monitoring — what is being monitored, and on what?
  3. Plan vs ride — what was pre-planned, and what is being ridden live?
  4. Failure risk — what is the failure risk at any given minute?
  5. Recording — what is being recorded, if anything?
  6. Loss — what is impossible to recover after the set ends?
Watch for restraint

This session is watched for restraint. Count how rarely the performer acts — the gaps between gestures are longer than they feel, and most of the set is monitoring, not moving.

After — verify

Can you describe the performer’s resting position without rewatching? What was the longest gap between actions you saw? If you can’t answer either, rewatch ten minutes — muted.

Deliverable — the stolen principle

File all worksheets in the logbook. Then write down one stolen principle — a structural idea, never material — before you build anything with it. It will be applied and judged at capstone C1. Naming it in advance makes it a commitment instead of a story you tell afterwards.

Pass standard
Minimum passThree sessions filed, six-question notes exist, Session 2 worksheet exists, and one stolen principle is named.
Solid passThe stolen principle is structural enough that another person could build from it without copying any sound.
Advanced transferYou can hear the same principle operating in a different genre or live setup.
  • Valid: “one element changes at a time” — a constraint on how the system moves
  • Valid: “the loop is mixed, not arranged” — a claim about where variation lives
  • Valid: “space is the variation — the send is the melody” — a routing idea, not a sound
  • Invalid: “a chord stab like Basic Channel’s” — that is material, not structure
Programme L1 — deliverables
  • Three record analyses — six questions each, three passes per record
  • One deconstruction worksheet — element / source / chain / confidence
  • One video worksheet — six answers, plus the restraint count
  • One stolen principle — structural, named in advance, evaluated at C1
