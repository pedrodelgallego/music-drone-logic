Repetition & Hypnosis — four engines, one effect

Three listening sessions that train you to hear music like a builder: not “do I like this?” but “how does this work?”. Everything you build in this block starts from what you hear here.

Listening programme · 3 sessions · one full cycle · feeds labs 003–004 and capstone C1

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

You can’t build what you can’t hear. These sessions turn “I like this track” into “I can name what makes this track work”. That structure — not the sounds — is what you take away.

Listening is the loop

These sessions are not extra reading — they are the first step in your workflow. Every idea you test in this block starts with something you heard here. The six questions below are the same ones the labs use.

Start here
  • Create a logbook page titled L1 — repetition and hypnosis.
  • Run three separate sessions: records, samples, video. Do not merge them into one long sitting.
  • Answer the same six questions for every reference, even when the answer is a guess.
  • Finish by naming one stolen principle before any build lab uses it.

New concepts — in plain words

Four key terms appear throughout this page. Here is what each one means.

ConceptIn plain words
System vs material Material is the specific stuff: this melody, this chord, this sample. The system is the machinery behind it: what changes, what stays fixed, who decides. You can’t copy their material — but you can copy the system. “A chord like Basic Channel’s” is material. “One element changes at a time” is a system.
The six questions A fixed six-question checklist you ask of every track. Asking the same six questions every time turns casual listening into structured analysis. The questions are listed in the next section.
Deconstruction Deconstruction means playing eight bars on repeat and breaking them apart on paper. List every sound, guess where each came from, guess what was done to it. You will guess wrong — and that is fine. A wrong guess in writing can be corrected. A vague feeling cannot.
The stolen principle One structural idea you borrow from the listening programme, written down before you build anything with it. Writing it in advance is a commitment. Writing it afterwards is just a story.

How to listen — the six-question frame

Answer these six questions in your logbook for every track you analyse. A few lines per question is enough. The only wrong answer is a vague one.

  1. Inputs — what raw material enters the system?
  2. Transforms — what changes it, in what order?
  3. Feedback / variation — what keeps it alive over time?
  4. Constraints — what prevents clutter?
  5. Human role — what is performed, decided, curated?
  6. Likely audio path — what had to be routed, recorded, mixed, or performed for this to exist?

Use three passes per track. First pass: just listen, no notes. Second pass: answer questions 1–5. Third pass: question 6 only. Question 6 is the hardest — you have to guess at the mixing desk, effects, and how the performer's hands moved. Guessing is the point.

Session 1 — Records: four engines of repetition

Four records, one shared effect: a loop that never gets boring. Each track achieves it by a different method. Listen to all four with the six-question frame, then compare. The differences between them are the real lesson.

01 Basic Channel — “Quadrant Dub I” dub-filtered loop

The chord never changes. But the filter around it does, the reverb and space around it do, and the level does too. Count the actual events in ten minutes and write the number down. It will be fewer than you remember. That gap between the real count and your memory is the technique.

Engine: the dub-filtered loop — the mixing desk is the instrument. The underlying sound stays still; the desk moves around it.

Listen for
  • How muffled or bright the chord is at any moment — the filter is doing the moving
  • How much space and echo surround it, and when that grows or shrinks
  • The actual events in ten minutes — counted and written down
After — verify

Was your ten-minute event count under ten? Most people remember far more events than they heard — that gap is the technique working on you. Can you say what the filter was doing in the last two minutes versus the first two? If not, relisten to just the opening and the closing.

02 Plastikman — Consumed (title track) subtractive automation

This piece is built by taking things away. The key question is not “what is playing?” but “what is missing right now, and when did it leave?” Follow the Roland TB-303 bass line and the reverb around it. Notice how much of the track is the silence where something used to be.

Engine: subtractive automation — the piece is carved out by removing things, not built up by adding them. Removal is the compositional act.

Listen for
  • What is absent right now, and when it left
  • The 303 and the room around it
  • How much of the piece is the space where something used to be
After — verify

Name one element that left, and roughly when it came back. Could you tell whether a change was something added or something removed? In this record it is almost always removal. If you’re not sure, relisten to any two minutes and write down each exit as it happens.

03 Donato Dozzy & Nuel — The Aquaplano Sessions modulation drift

Nothing here repeats exactly. Pick one sound and follow it for five minutes. Every cycle is slightly different — the changes are too small to be intentional arrangement moves. That slow, continuous wobble is modulation drift: an automatic movement built into the sound itself that keeps the loop alive.

Engine: drift — the variation lives inside the sound, not in the arrangement. The structure holds still; the sound keeps shifting.

Listen for
  • One element, followed for five minutes — every cycle slightly different
  • Differences too small to be arrangement moves
  • The thing inside the sound that is always moving
After — verify

Describe in one sentence what changed about the element you followed. Brighter? Closer? Rougher? If you genuinely couldn’t hear it drift, that is a finding — write it down; R1 will test it. Then relisten, following the simplest element instead.

04 Coil — Time Machines the zero-rate loop

One synthesizer tone, held for ten or more minutes with nothing else happening. There are no repeating loops because the sound never stops — this is the extreme version of repetition. It forces the six questions hardest. With no events at all, question 3 (“what keeps it alive?”) must find its answer inside the tone itself: slow beating between two nearly identical oscillators tuned slightly apart, and nothing else.

Engine: the drone — the length of the sound is the point. Without rhythmic hits arriving, your ear loses track of time. That distortion of time is what the piece is made of.

Listen for
  • The fastest change you can hear — time its cycle; nothing in the piece moves faster
  • Whether the movement is performed or built into the tuning
  • How long the excerpt felt versus how long it actually was — write both numbers down
After — verify

Did your felt duration match the clock? Almost no one’s does. Without rhythmic hits, your ear cannot count time accurately. That gap between the two numbers is the technique working on you. If the piece sounded “completely static,” relisten to one minute and time the slow wavering pulse of the beating tones — it was moving the whole time.

Decision check

Which engine will you use? Not which record you liked most — which mechanism you plan to build. Lab 003 asks you to write a System Spec. That spec must name your chosen engine and say which record you took it from.

Session 2 — Samples: one track to its sources

Session 2 · Deconstruction

Trace the densest eight bars backwards

Pick one of the four records above — or another hypnotic track you love. Loop the busiest eight bars you can find. List every sound you can hear: expect four to seven. That number is almost always lower than you think, and noticing that is your first finding. For each sound, guess where it likely came from (a drum machine, a synth, a sample?) and what was done to it. Then answer question 6 for the whole track: what had to be set up, routed, and recorded for this record to exist?

Note

You will guess wrong — that is expected. A wrong answer in writing can be corrected. A vague impression cannot. Your guesses get more accurate every block, but only if you write them down now.

This worksheet is a blueprint

In replication lab R1 you will actually try to rebuild these eight bars using this worksheet. That is when you find out which guesses were right. Write each row as if someone else will have to build from it — because you will.

After — verify

Your element list should have four to seven rows. If you found ten or more, you probably split single sounds into pieces; if two or three, you probably merged separate sounds into one. Either way, relisten once with fresh ears before locking the worksheet.

Session 3 — Video: watch the hands

Session 3 · Video

A hardware-techno live set, watched twice

Pick one hardware-techno live set where you can clearly see the performer’s hands — any era. Try to include at least one artist from outside the obvious canon: a contemporary or regional artist you might not have heard. Watch it twice. First time: watch as an audience member, start to finish, no notes. Second time: mute the video and watch only the hands.

Surgeon — Boiler Room London LIVE Setwatch for one-person control, restraint, and recovery risk
Karenn — Boiler Room London Live Setwatch for shared hardware roles and planned instability
Benjamin Damage — Live All-Hardware Techno Lockdown Setwatch for sequencer/mixer balance and what is committed live
  1. Resting position — where are the hands resting between actions?
  2. Monitoring — what is being monitored, and on what?
  3. Plan vs ride — what was pre-planned, and what is being ridden live?
  4. Failure risk — what is the failure risk at any given minute?
  5. Recording — what is being recorded, if anything?
  6. Loss — what is impossible to recover after the set ends?
Watch for restraint

The main thing to notice is how little the performer actually does. Count how rarely they touch anything. The gaps between actions are longer than they seem. Most of the set is watching and listening — not moving.

After — verify

Can you describe the performer’s resting position without rewatching? What was the longest gap between actions you saw? If you can’t answer either, rewatch ten minutes — muted.

Deliverable — the stolen principle

File all worksheets in the logbook. Then write down one stolen principle — a structural idea, never a specific sound — before you build anything with it. It will be applied and judged at capstone C1. Writing it before you build makes it a commitment. Writing it after is just a description.

Pass standard
Minimum passThree sessions filed, six-question notes exist, Session 2 worksheet exists, and one stolen principle is named.
Solid passThe stolen principle is structural enough that another person could build from it without copying any sound.
Advanced transferYou can hear the same principle operating in a different genre or live setup.
  • Valid: “one element changes at a time” — a constraint on how the system moves
  • Valid: “the loop is mixed, not arranged” — a claim about where variation lives
  • Valid: “space is the variation — the send is the melody” — a routing idea, not a sound
  • Invalid: “a chord stab like Basic Channel’s” — that is material, not structure
Programme L1 — deliverables
  • Four record analyses — six questions each, three passes per record
  • One deconstruction worksheet — element / source / chain / confidence
  • One video worksheet — six answers, plus the restraint count
  • One stolen principle — structural, named in advance, evaluated at C1
