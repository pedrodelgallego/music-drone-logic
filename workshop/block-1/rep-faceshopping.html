Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: clipping as sound design

Take one metallic hit from “Faceshopping”, build a clean source for it, then clip that source so hard — on purpose, gain-compensated — that the clipped edge becomes the sound itself.

The point

Clipping isn’t only damage or warmth — pushed to the extreme on purpose, at a chosen device, it stops being a fault and becomes a timbre.

The system you are replicating
Source recordSOPHIE — “Faceshopping”
The effectclipping so extreme it becomes the sound — metallic, plastic, percussive
The mechanisma simple bright source, hard-clipped at a chosen device, sculpted into rhythm
Fidelityas close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, Digitakt II → M4 inputs 3/4, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged
  • New set saved as REP-FACESHOPPING, tempo 130
  • M4 phones knob at the lab-001 mark — this lesson’s material is harsh; the mark is a ceiling, not a suggestion
  • Timebox: 60–120 minutes, then file it and stop — the extra time goes into matching the hit, not adding more devices
Reference pass — 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to about one minute of “Faceshopping” — at least twice, once following the harsh sounds and once following everything around them. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Open each model answer only after your own line exists.

  1. Which sounds are the distortion?
    Answer

    The metallic, plastic, percussive hits that carry the rhythm — they have no clean original underneath; remove the clipping and there is no sound left. That is the difference between a distorted sound and a sound made of distortion. Relisten to one hit in isolation and try to imagine its undistorted version — the failure of that imagination is the answer.

  2. Is anything left clean?
    Answer

    The vocal — heavily processed but intelligible — and stretches of near-silence around the hits. The harshness reads as design partly because it is surrounded by space and by a voice you can follow. Relisten for how much emptiness sits between the hits; the clipping never smears into a wall.

  3. Are the clipped sounds sustained — or short?
    Answer

    Short. The most extreme clipping arrives in percussive bursts — transients with hard edges, gone before they can fatigue. Sustained clipping at this severity would be unlistenable; the record uses duration as the safety valve. Relisten and time one hit: it is over in a fraction of a second.

  4. Does a given hit repeat exactly, or wander?
    Answer

    Exactly — the same metallic edge every time it returns, placed on the same beats. Identical repetition is the fingerprint of a designed sound: an accident would vary with the input level. Relisten across two choruses and follow one hit; its sameness is the proof of intent.

  5. Where does each hit’s energy live — in the fundamental, or above it?
    Answer

    Above it — the metallic character is a spray of high harmonics that the clipping generates, far above whatever pitch the source had. That spray is what your Spectrum analyzer will show as a wash across the whole band. Relisten for how little “note” and how much “sheet metal” each hit contains.

Build only after the answers exist — you are about to copy the system you just described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your sketch lands on a hit with the same metallic edge and the same placement — as close to the record’s hit as MicroFreak + Saturator get. Closeness is the measure of your hearing.
  • The clipped edge is the sound — if bypassing the Saturator leaves something usable, you have not pushed far enough.
  • Gain-compensated throughout: the Saturator’s output comes down as the Drive goes up, so the A/B judges timbre, never loudness.
  • Short bursts in rhythm, surrounded by space — the record’s safety valve, matched.

The whole machine in one row: a clean bright source, a chosen clipping device pushed past damage into design, the output pulled back down, and the result placed into rhythm. The drive lives at one address — the Saturator — and nowhere else in the chain.

MicroFreak · bright hit · ledger volume → Saturator · Hard Curve · Drive high → Output pulled down · gain-comp → mix · the edge is the sound

What the clipping does to the wave, drawn. The rounded wave carries one strong harmonic; the flat-topped wave is the same note with its top sheared off — and the shearing sprays new harmonics across the whole band. That spray is the metal.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the transcription

Every timbre judgment in this lesson is a comparison against one chosen hit from the record, so that hit has to live in the session at matched loudness. Physically, the Utility trim keeps the A/B honest, and the transcription here is rhythmic, not melodic: where the hit lands, how long it lasts, what surrounds it.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Faceshopping” into it.
  2. Find the most characteristic 8–16 bars — a stretch where the metallic hits carry the rhythm — and loop them.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim its gain so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  4. Choose one hit from the loop to replicate — the most metallic one — and write three lines about it: which beats it lands on, roughly how long it lasts, and what the texture suggests (sheet metal? plastic? rubber?).
  5. ⌘ T twice; rename: SRC CLEAN (Ext. In 1) and HITS (no input — it will hold edited clips). Faders at 0 dB.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one target hit is named and described in the logbook, and two named tracks wait for audio.

If it fails

Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

Design the source — bright, simple, tight

The record’s metal is not a complicated source — it is a simple bright source destroyed well. Physically, a hard clipper generates harmonics from whatever it is fed, so the source’s job is only to be bright (so there is energy to shear) and tight (so the clipping arrives as a burst, not a drone). What goes to disk is completely clean, at the ledger gain; the destruction happens inside Live where it can be A/B’d fairly.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, Paraphonic OFF, cutoff ≈ 80 %, resonance ≈ 10 % — bright and plain on purpose.
  2. Envelope: attack 0, decay short, sustain 0 — a tight percussive blip, nothing held.
  3. Solo REF and find the pitch region of your target hit — exact pitch matters less than register; test an octave up and down and keep the one that sits where the record’s hit sits.
  4. Set the M4 input-1 gain so peaks sit ≈ −12 dBFS — the ledger value, no red anywhere — and record 8 bars of single staccato notes into SRC CLEAN, several per bar, evenly played.
  5. Listen back once: the take should sound politely useless — a clean, bright blip with none of the record’s violence. That is correct; the violence is the next phase’s decision.
Expected result

An 8-bar clean take of tight bright blips, peaking ≈ −12 dBFS, in the register of the record’s hit. No grit anywhere yet.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
The blip is dull, and clipping it later sounds woollyCutoff too low — nothing bright for the clipper to shearOpen the cutoff toward ≈ 80 %; the source must carry high-frequency energy
Notes smear into each otherDecay too long, or sustain above zeroShorten the decay and confirm sustain is at 0 — the burst must end before the next one
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Clip it — until the edge is the sound

Now the decision: a Saturator on a duplicate track, curve set hard, Drive pushed far past anything lab-001 called safe — and the Output pulled down by the same amount, so the A/B judges the timbre and not the level. Physically, the flat top the clipper cuts into the wave is what generates the harmonic spray; more Drive means a flatter top and a wider spray. The record, not the default, decides how far.

  • Safety: this phase produces harsh clipped transients on purpose. Phones at the mark or lower · audition in short bursts · never turn the phones up to inspect a harsh sound — pull the clip gain up instead, then back.
Do this
  1. Select SRC CLEAN, ⌘ D, rename the copy SRC CLIPPED. Identical audio, two addresses.
  2. On SRC CLIPPED, drop in Audio Effects → Saturator and set the curve to Hard Curve.
  3. Push Drive up — start at +24 dB — and pull Output down by roughly the same amount until both tracks meter the same.
  4. Solo-flip against REF and walk the Drive until the edge matches the record’s hit — more Drive is more metal, less is more plastic. Re-compensate the Output after every change, and make at least three flip-and-correct passes.
  5. Test the bypass: toggle the Saturator off. If what remains sounds usable, you are still in “distorted sound” territory — push further until the clean version is obviously not the instrument anymore.
  6. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Drop Audio Effects → Spectrum after the Saturator. At matched loudness, watch the display while you raise the Drive: the flat-topped wave sprays harmonics across the whole band, and the spray widens as the top flattens. Note in the logbook roughly where the spray reaches at your final setting.
Expected result

Two tracks with identical audio and one decision between them: the clipped copy is a metallic designed hit at the same loudness, and bypassing the device removes the sound’s identity, not just its dirt.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
The clipped version simply sounds “better” in every A/BOutput not pulled down — loudness is votingGain-compensate until both tracks meter the same, then judge again
Harsh but never metallic — just fizzySource too dull, or curve too softRe-record phase 2 with the cutoff open, and confirm the curve is Hard Curve, not the default
Phase 4 · ≈ 25 min

Sculpt it into the rhythm and A/B

A designed hit only proves itself in rhythm — the record’s clipping works because it is placed, with space around it, on a grid the body can follow. Physically, nothing new happens to the signal here: you are editing the clipped take into the section’s pattern and checking that the design survives repetition the way the record’s does.

Do this
  1. Crop the best single hit from SRC CLIPPED into a short clip and drag copies onto HITS.
  2. Place the copies where the record places its hit — the beats from your phase 1 transcription — across 8 bars, and loop.
  3. Add the record’s frame minimally: one Digitakt kick pattern into a new track via inputs 3/4, recorded clean at the ledger level, nothing else.
  4. In Arrangement view, tile the loop into a 1–2 minute sketch and mirror the record’s entries and exits, keeping the space around the hits.
  5. Solo-flip REF against the sketch at matched loudness and correct one thing at a time: placement first, hit length second, Drive last.
Expected result

A short sketch where your clipped hit carries rhythm over a clean kick with real space between events — and the flip against REF lands on the same edge in the same places.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
The sketch fatigues within a minute; the record doesn’tYour hits are longer or denser than the record’sShorten the clips and remove placements — the record’s safety valve is space
The kick buzzes tooThe Digitakt is overdriving the fixed 3/4 line inputsLower Main Volume — on this pair the instrument’s knob is the record level
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your sketch at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Bypass the Saturator on your hit. Is anything usable left? Name what disappeared in one sentence.
    Answer

    Almost nothing should be left — a polite blip with no identity. The sentence should say that the timbre, not just the dirt, lived in the clipping: the device was the instrument and the synth was only fuel. If the bypassed version still sounds like the sound, return to phase 3 and push further. Relisten to the record’s hit afterwards and notice there is no “clean version” to imagine.

  • Could a friend, hearing 10 seconds of your section then 10 of the record’s, say which is which? Where do you lose them — texture, or placement?
    Answer

    Texture misses come from the source and the curve — a dull source clips fizzy, a soft curve clips warm, and neither makes metal. Placement misses come from transcription, and the ear catches a wrong beat faster than a wrong harmonic. Fix placement before touching the Drive again, then relisten to one chorus of the record following only your chosen hit’s grid.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
