Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: drift you can’t point to

Patch two slow, unsynced movers into one sound, then keep your hands off and let it never repeat.

The point

If something inside the sound moves slower than attention, the loop never repeats exactly — and nobody can say why it stays alive. The variation is not performed and not arranged; it is built into the patch and then left alone.

The system you are replicating
Source recordDonato Dozzy & Nuel — The Aquaplano Sessions
The effectA loop that breathes for minutes with no audible hand on it — no two passes identical
The mechanismSlow, unsynced modulators inside the voice: they never line up with the bars, so the timbre never lands in the same place twice
FidelityAs close to the record’s section as your rig allows — sounds, chords, patterns, and moves included. Closeness is the measure of your hearing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • MicroFreak on M4 in 1, recording armed path tested in Live
  • Gain set by the lab 001 ledger — a quiet patch tempts you to over-gain
  • Phones at the mark · 60–120 minutes clear — the movers are slower than attention, so judging them takes whole minutes of listening per adjustment, and the lesson cannot be rushed
Reference pass — 10 minutes

Describe their section before you match it

Listen to about one minute of The Aquaplano Sessions. Answer in the logbook, one line each, BEFORE touching gear. Check each answer against the model only after yours is written.

  1. Pick one sound and follow it for the minute — what about it changed (brightness? distance? roughness?)?
    Answer

    Brightness, mostly — the tone breathes between dull and slightly open — and with it a sense of distance, as if the sound drifts nearer and farther without moving. Naming the dimension matters: it tells you which destination the modulators are wired to, which is exactly what you patch in the build.

  2. Could you point to the MOMENT anything changed?
    Answer

    No — and that is the test of the mechanism. The change is continuous and slower than attention, so by the time you notice a difference it has already been happening for many bars. A nameable moment would be a performed move, and this record has none.

  3. Does the drift ever line up with the bars, or does it float free?
    Answer

    It floats free. Listen across several loop cycles: the brightest point lands somewhere different against the bar every time. A synced modulator would land in the same place each cycle and the loop would read as a repeating pattern — the spell depends on it never lining up.

  4. Is anyone’s hand audible — or does it feel like weather?
    Answer

    Weather. There is no gesture, no intention, no moment of decision anywhere in the minute — the variation was built into the patch and then left alone. That is the whole claim of the record, and the whole discipline of this lesson: zero hands after record is pressed.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

  • Sound target: a tone as close to the section’s as the MicroFreak gets, drifting at the record’s own rate.
  • No edges: no nameable moment of change — in yours or theirs.
  • Free-floating: the drift floats free of the bar grid — unsynced, like theirs.
  • Hands: zero, after pressing record.

Steps

Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Match the tone before anything moves

The drift only reads against a tone that is already theirs — a wrong tone drifting at the right rate is a different record. This phase is static sound-matching: engine, pitch, register, filter, A/B’d against the reference until the remaining gap is the hardware’s. No modulation is allowed yet; you need to know what the still version sounds like to hear the movers later.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track. Drop your chosen track from The Aquaplano Sessions onto a dedicated REF track in Live and loop its most characteristic stretch — a 2–3 minute stretch is fine here, the drift is slow. Gain-match it with a Utility so comparisons are honest, and keep it one solo away for the whole session.
  2. Match the patch’s tone. On the MicroFreak, chase the section’s central sound as close as the rig gets: pick the engine that gets nearest, match the pitch and register by ear, set the filter where theirs sits.
  3. A/B against the REF — yours, theirs, yours — until the gap is the hardware’s, not your ears’.
Expected result

A static tone that sits next to the record’s central sound as a plausible sibling — same register, same weight, same filter colour — and does not move at all yet.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Every engine sounds wrongChasing the record’s space instead of its tone — their sound carries room and tape you do not have yetMatch the dry core only; accept that the air around it is theirs, not the patch’s
The tone matches at one note but not in contextWrong register — the same patch an octave off reads as a different instrumentRe-check pitch and octave against the REF before touching anything else
Phase 2 — ~25 minutes

Wire the two movers

This is the record’s actual machine: two modulators with different, unsynced periods, each nudging the timbre by a barely audible amount. Because their cycles never coincide, the combined movement never repeats — the loop’s notes are identical every pass, but the sound they come out in never is. Both routings happen in the MicroFreak’s mod matrix, and both amounts are smaller than your instinct says.

Two movers slower than attention.

Do this
  1. Route the first mover. In the mod matrix, route LFO → Cutoff at a SMALL amount. Small means you barely hear it on one pass — listen to how faint the record’s movement is and start there.
  2. Slow the LFO to the record’s rate. Set the LFO very slow and UNSYNCED — free-running. Solo the REF and time their drift: how long does one breath of brightness take? Match it. A synced LFO repeats with the loop and the spell collapses — theirs never lines up with the bars, so neither should yours.
  3. Add the second mover at a different speed. Route the cycling envelope → Wave or Timbre, again at a small amount, with rise and fall times that give it a clearly different period from the LFO. Set its depth so the secondary movement is as faint as the record’s.
Expected result

On a single pass of the loop, almost nothing audible. Over a minute, an unmistakable slow breathing that never lands in the same place against the bar twice.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The movement is an obvious wobbleModulation amount too big — it reads as an effect, not weatherHalve the amount, then halve it again; the record’s movement is barely there on any one pass
The drift repeats in step with the loopThe LFO is synced to tempoSet it free-running (unsynced) and re-time it against the REF’s breath
Phase 3 — ~30 minutes

Set it running, then record with your hands off

Now the patch performs and you do not. First calibrate against the record — a full minute of each, because the movers cannot be judged on one pass — then arm the track and record three minutes without touching anything. Writing down what you wanted to fix, instead of fixing it, is the discipline that separates this lesson from a synth tweak session.

MicroFreak (patch running) → TS → M4 in 1 → USB-C → Live track (record armed) → master → phones
Do this
  1. Set it running and A/B the drift. Sequence a pattern matching the section’s figure — or hold the tone the way they do — and let it run next to the REF. A/B a minute of each: does yours breathe at their rate, and as deep? Adjust speed and amount until it does, then hands off.
  2. Record 3 minutes into Live without touching anything. Hands off is the discipline. If you hear something drifting away from the record, write it down instead of reaching for it.
Expected result

Three recorded minutes with zero performed events — and a tone that, A/B’d in any 10-second window, drifts at the record’s rate and depth.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Yours breathes noticeably faster than theirsThe LFO rate was set by impatience, not by the REF — their breath can take half a minuteRe-time one full breath of the record with a watch, then slow the LFO until yours matches
You touched it mid-takeA drift away from the record felt like an error to fixDelete the take and go again — the note you should have written is the data, not the tweak
Phase 4 — ~15 minutes

Listen back, then look at the waveform

The proof of the mechanism is in the comparison: the same notes, minutes apart, coming out as visibly different sound. One listening pass for the experience, one for the timbre against the record — then the eyes confirm what the patch did while your hands were in your lap.

Do this
  1. Listen back twice. Once as an audience member, start to finish. Then once more A/Bing against the REF, following only the timbre — where is yours bright while theirs is dull, and does either ever announce the moment it changed?
  2. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Zoom the waveform at minute 0 and minute 2½ and LOOK at two cycles of the loop side by side — visibly different shapes from the same notes. The patch did that, not you — the same engine the record left running.
Expected result

Two waveform snapshots of the same bar that do not match — and no moment in either listening pass where you could say “there, it changed.”

Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the looped section — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate? Then answer two questions in writing.

Over a full minute of each, does yours breathe at their rate and depth, or does one of you drift faster?

Answer

Time it rather than feel it: one breath of brightness on the record can take twenty or thirty seconds, far slower than a first patch usually dares. If yours is faster, the LFO rate is the miss; if yours is deeper — a wobble where theirs is a haze — the amount is. Almost every first attempt errs fast and deep, because faint-and-slow feels like nothing is happening while you build it.

Can you point to the exact moment anything changed, in either take? (You must not be able to.)

Answer

In the record, no — and that is the pass condition for yours too. If you can name a moment in your take, something moved faster than attention: a mover too quick, an amount too large, or a hand that touched the patch mid-take. The drift should be a fact you can only verify by comparing distant minutes, never an event you can catch happening.
