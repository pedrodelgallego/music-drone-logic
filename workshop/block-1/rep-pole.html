Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the noise floor as lead

Harvest the hiss and crackle your gain ledger treats as a hazard, then place it exactly where a “Tanzen 2” section places it — the lead voice.

The point

What your gain ledger calls noise becomes music the moment it is placed on purpose and everything else makes room.

The system you are replicating
Source recordPole — “Tanzen 2” (from 1)
The effectbackground noise keeping time as the lead instrument
The mechanismnoise placed rhythmically, everything else quiet and sparse beneath it
Fidelityas close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, Digitakt II → M4 inputs 3/4, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged
  • New set saved as REP-POLE, tempo 120
  • M4 phones knob at the lab-001 mark — it stays there for the whole lesson
  • Timebox: 60–120 minutes, then file it and stop — the extra time goes into harvesting and placement passes, not extra material
Reference pass — 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to about one minute of “Tanzen 2” — twice at least; this record rewards the second pass more than most. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Open each model answer only after your own line exists.

  1. Is the crackle rhythmic or random?
    Answer

    Rhythmic — the same crackle figure returns bar after bar, locked to the pulse. Random noise never repeats; this loops. Relisten and try to predict the next crackle before it lands: the fact that you can is the proof it is a part, not a fault.

  2. What is the second loudest thing — and how far down is it?
    Answer

    The chord — a deep, blurred dub chord sitting well below the crackle, on the order of ten decibels down. The kick is further down still, more felt than heard. Relisten for how far you have to reach to grab the chord; that reach is the distance you are about to copy with a fader.

  3. Could the crackle be mistaken for a broken system — and what convinces you it isn’t?
    Answer

    For the first bar, yes — it is literally the sound of a broken filter. What convinces you it isn’t is repetition and placement: faults do not keep time, do not return with the loop, and do not leave room for music underneath. Relisten to the silence around each crackle — a genuinely broken system would never be that tidy.

  4. How much space — actual silence — surrounds each event?
    Answer

    A lot — real silence, not filler. Each event sits alone with audible nothing around it, and that emptiness is what lets sounds this quiet read as a lead. If you answered “not much”, relisten with the volume untouched and count how often nothing at all is playing.

Build only after the answers exist — you are about to copy the system you just described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your loop lands on the same crackle keeping the same time at the same depth — as close to the record’s section as the rig allows. Closeness is the measure of your hearing.
  • Everything else sits as far below the crackle as the record holds it; nothing competes, and nothing gets turned up to win — the loudest event stays at nominal.
  • The track survives the pause test: stop playback and music stops, not just hiss.

The harvest path — the same chain lab 001 taught you to keep quiet, run loud on purpose, then turned into an instrument and placed at the top of a quiet mix.

M4 in 1 · open, gain high → record hiss / crackle → slice → Digitakt pads / Simpler slices → quiet mix · crackle leads
Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the tracks

The whole lesson is a comparison against one looped minute of the record, so that loop has to live in the session at the same loudness as your build. Physically, the Utility trim keeps the A/B fair — both versions of this music are quiet, and neither is allowed to win by being less quiet. Three destination tracks cover the system: the noise, the chord, the kick.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Tanzen 2” into it.
  2. Find the most characteristic 8–16 bars for noise-as-lead — the stretch where the crackle most clearly keeps time over everything else — and loop them.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim its gain so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, matched to the build you are about to make. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  4. ⌘ T three times; rename with ⌘ R: NOISE, CHORD, KICK.
  5. ⌘ ⌥ I to show In/Out. NOISE and CHORD: Ext. In 1. KICK: Ext. In 3/4. Faders at 0 dB.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS and three named, routed, unity-gain tracks wait for material. Nothing has been harvested yet; the gain ledger still reads nominal everywhere.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
REF later drowns your quiet buildUtility never trimmed — the file is mastered louder than it feelsTrim until the looped section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, same as your loudest event
No meter movement when testing an inputExt. In channel does not match the physical jackMicroFreak on 1, Digitakt on 3/4 — the lab-001 wiring table
Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

Harvest the floor

Everything lab 001 taught you to avoid, you now do on purpose: open the preamp and record what the electronics themselves sound like. Physically, the hiss is the preamp’s own self-noise amplified to audibility, and the crackle is a contact fault or filtered noise burst — the exact materials Pole built a career on. The harvest is short; the listening that chooses between sources is where the time goes.

  • Safety: these harvests run the preamp gain high — you are amplifying the floor. Phones at the mark or lower · record only the seconds each harvest asks for, no more · reset the gain immediately afterwards · never turn the phones up to inspect a harsh sound.
Do this
  1. Harvest hiss. Leave M4 input 1 with nothing playing into it — cable unplugged, or the MicroFreak connected but silent. Turn the input-1 gain knob most of the way up: the meter now shows the preamp’s own noise floor. Arm NOISE and record 30 seconds of pure hiss. Rename the clip HISS.
  2. Harvest crackle — two sources. Solo REF first and decide which grain is closer to the record’s crackle: gently jiggle the ¼″ TS jack in input 1 while recording 30 seconds (gently — half-seat it, do not force it), or play the MicroFreak’s Noise engine (Type → Noise) through a nearly-closed filter, cutoff ≈ 10 %, in short staccato stabs.
  3. If time allows, record both candidates and pick by ear afterwards — a solo-flip against REF chooses faster than theory does. Rename the keeper CRACKLE.
  4. Observe — see it, not just hear it. While harvesting, watch the Live input meter and write the noise-floor level in dBFS with the gain high, then again back at nominal. The difference between those two numbers is lab 001’s quiet-take lesson, read off a meter — the logbook gets both readings.
  5. Reset the input-1 gain to the ledger value. Confirm it physically, not from memory.
Expected result

Two raw clips, HISS and CRACKLE, plus two meter readings in the logbook. The gain knob is back at nominal and the bench is safe again — the hazard existed for under two minutes, on purpose.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
Meter barely moves with the gain highA quiet preamp — the M4’s floor is lowGood news, use the other source: the jack-jiggle or the Noise engine gives you louder material
The harvest is hum, not hiss or crackleThe open input is picking up a power supply or USB hub nearbyReroute the cable away from power — lab 001’s first-aid table — and record again
Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

Turn crackle into an instrument

A 30-second noise recording is material; an instrument is that material cut into playable pieces. Physically, slicing maps short bursts of the recording onto pads, so a crackle can be triggered on a grid like any drum hit — which is the entire trick of the record: the fault becomes a part the moment it can be placed.

Do this
  1. Either path works. Digitakt: transfer the recording into a sample slot, assign it to track 1, trim to a short crackle hit.
  2. Live: drag the CRACKLE clip onto a new MIDI track — it lands in Simpler — set it to Slice mode so each pad plays one short crackle burst.
  3. Audition the slices and keep the three or four most distinct bursts — too-similar slices make the pattern read as static noise instead of rhythm.
  4. Play the pads freely for a minute against the soloed REF, just to learn which slice resembles which of the record’s crackle events.
Expected result

Pads that play short, distinct crackle bursts on demand. What entered the session as a hazard now answers to a clock.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
Pads trigger long washes instead of hitsSlices or trim set far too longShorten each slice to a single burst — tens of milliseconds, not seconds
Pads are silentSample not assigned to the track, or the MIDI track’s output is not the SimplerRe-check the slot assignment / drag the clip onto the MIDI track again and confirm Slice mode
Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

Place the crackle — the lead voice

Placement is what separates rhythm from fault, so this phase is pure transcription: the record’s crackle pattern, off the record, by ear, onto your grid. Physically, nothing changes about the sound itself — only when it happens — and that alone moves it from “broken system” to “lead instrument”. Expect several passes; irregular-but-repeating patterns hide their logic on the first listen.

Do this
  1. Solo REF and transcribe the crackle pattern by ear; the page cannot give you the steps — hearing them is the exercise. Two passes minimum before you program anything.
  2. Program a one-bar, 16-step pattern of short crackle hits on the grid you hear — irregular but repeating; 1, 4, 7, 11, 14 is only a starting shape.
  3. Loop and solo-flip until your pattern and the record’s keep the same time — rhythm, not fault.
  4. Make a final flip pass listening only to what happens between the hits — the record’s silences are part of the pattern, and a stray extra hit fills a silence the record protects.
Expected result

A looping one-bar crackle pattern that keeps the record’s time. Heard alone, it already sounds like music — before any other element exists.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
The pattern reads as random noise, not rhythmToo many hits — the silences got filledDelete hits until the pattern breathes; the record uses fewer events than you think
It still sounds like a faultNo exact repetition — the loop drifts or was played liveQuantise to the one-bar grid and let it repeat identically; repetition is what reads as intent
Phase 5 · ≈ 30 min

Everything underneath — and the pause test

Now the rest of the record’s system: a deep chord and a buried kick, both placed under the crackle. Physically this is the “Mango Drive” lesson inverted — the lead is the quietest kind of sound there is, so everything else has to be quieter still, and the only legal direction for any fader is down. The targets below are provided starting points; the record corrects them.

Everything sits below the crackle — starting points, corrected by the record
Crackle (lead)peaks ≈ −14 dBFS — the loudest event in the mix
Kickpeaks ≈ −20 dBFS — felt more than heard
Chordpeaks ≈ −26 dBFS — deep in the background
Do this
  1. MicroFreak: Paraphonic ON, Type → V.Analog, slow attack, cutoff ≈ 30 %. Transcribe the record’s deep, sparse chord from REF by ear — the page cannot give the notes — and change it only where the record changes it.
  2. Record 8 bars into CHORD at nominal, then pull its fader until it sits as far below the crackle as the record’s chord does, ≈ −12 dB down as a starting point.
  3. Digitakt: REC, kick where the record puts it, Main Volume low. Record 8 bars into KICK, then sit its fader exactly as deep as the record buries its low end — felt more than heard.
  4. Keep everything quiet. The crackle must be the loudest event in the mix — peaks ≈ −12 to −14 dBFS and nothing above it. If the kick or chord competes, pull them down; never push the crackle up — the record’s distances, not louder ones.
  5. Loop the whole thing for two minutes at the mark, solo-flipping against REF. Then make one more pass over both lower faders, finer.
  6. Run the pause test. Stop playback mid-loop and listen to the silence. What disappeared was your music; whatever remains is your playback system’s own floor. Write down which was which.
Expected result

A quiet, sparse loop where the crackle leads, the chord glows underneath, and the kick is a pressure rather than a sound — and stopping playback stops music, not just hiss.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
The chord swallows the crackleChord fader too high — the louder-is-better trap, sidewaysPull the chord down further; never push the crackle up
The pause test leaves loud hiss behindThe harvest gain was never reset — the floor is still amplifiedReturn input-1 gain to the ledger value and re-check the whole ledger
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your loop at matched loudness — phones at the mark, both quiet, neither turned up to impress.

  • Could a friend, hearing 10 seconds of yours then 10 of the record, say which is which? The longer they hesitate, the better your ears did.
    Answer

    If they hesitate, your crackle’s grain, pattern, and depth are all close. The usual giveaway is grain, not levels — your crackle sounds like your preamp, the record’s sounds like a broken filter — and that is a sound-source miss for the miss list, not a fader problem. Relisten to one crackle event from each, back to back, and name the textural difference.

  • The pause test: when you stop playback, did music stop — or just hiss?
    Answer

    The crackle pattern, the chord, and the kick should all vanish at once — that was the music. Whatever continues is your playback system’s own floor at nominal gain: a different, quieter, unplaced noise. If you cannot tell the two apart, your quietest layer is sitting on top of the floor instead of above it — re-check that the harvest gain was reset, then pull the texture of your mix up just enough to clear it.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
