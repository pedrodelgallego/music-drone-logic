Replicate: the noise floor as lead

Copy one clear section of Pole — “Tanzen 2”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: noise placed rhythmically, everything else quiet and sparse beneath it. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, Digitakt II → M4 inputs 3/4, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged

  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: noise placed rhythmically, everything else quiet and sparse beneath it.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Pole — “Tanzen 2”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Is the crackle rhythmic or random?
    Answer

    Rhythmic - the same crackle figure returns bar after bar, locked to the pulse. Random noise never repeats; this loops.

  2. What is the second loudest thing — and how far down is it?
    Answer

    The chord - a deep, blurred dub chord sitting well below the crackle, on the order of ten decibels down. The kick is further down still, more felt than heard.

  3. Could the crackle be mistaken for a broken system — and what convinces you it isn’t?
    Answer

    For the first bar, yes - it is literally the sound of a broken filter. What convinces you it isn’t is repetition and placement: faults do not keep time, do not return with the loop, and do not leave room for music underneath.

  4. How much space — actual silence — surrounds each event?
    Answer

    A lot - real silence, not filler. Each event sits alone with audible nothing around it, and that emptiness is what lets sounds this quiet read as a lead.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your loop lands on the same crackle, the same time, and the same depth.
  • Everything else sits as far below the crackle as the record holds it; nothing competes, and nothing gets turned up to win — the loudest event stays at nominal.
  • The track survives the pause test: stop playback and music stops, not just hiss.

The harvest path — the same chain lab 001 taught you to keep quiet, run loud on purpose, then turned into an instrument and placed at the top of a quiet mix.

M4 in 1 · open, gain high → record hiss / crackle → slice → Digitakt II pads / Simpler slices → quiet mix · crackle leads

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the tracks

Phase focus: the reference and the tracks. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Tanzen 2” into it.
  2. Find the most characteristic 8–16 bars for noise-as-lead — the stretch where the crackle most clearly keeps time over everything else — and loop them.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim its gain so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, matched to the build you are about to make.
  4. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. ⌘ T three times; rename with ⌘ R: NOISE, CHORD, KICK.
  6. ⌘ ⌥ I to show In/Out.
  7. NOISE and CHORD: Ext. In 1.
  8. KICK: Ext. In 3/4.
  9. Faders at 0 dB.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Pole — “Tanzen 2” (from 1) and use Drum Rack, MIDI clips, Groove Pool, and clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the tracks — as the smallest Live set that proves noise placed rhythmically, everything else quiet and sparse beneath it.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.

Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

Harvest the floor

Phase focus: harvest the floor. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

  • Safety: these harvests run the preamp gain high — you are amplifying the floor. Phones at the mark or lower · record only the seconds each harvest asks for, no more · reset the gain immediately afterwards · never turn the phones up to inspect a harsh sound.
Do this
  1. Harvest hiss. Leave M4 input 1 with nothing playing into it — cable unplugged, or the MicroFreak connected but silent.
  2. Turn the input-1 gain knob most of the way up: the meter now shows the preamp’s own noise floor.
  3. Arm NOISE and record 30 seconds of pure hiss.
  4. Rename the clip HISS.
  5. Harvest crackle — two sources. Solo REF first and decide which grain is closer to the record’s crackle: gently jiggle the ¼″ TS jack in input 1 while recording 30 seconds (gently — half-seat it, do not force it), or play the MicroFreak’s Noise engine (Type → Noise) through a nearly-closed filter, cutoff ≈ 10 %, in short staccato stabs.
  6. If time allows, record both candidates and pick by ear afterwards — a solo-flip against REF chooses faster than theory does.
  7. Rename the keeper CRACKLE.
  8. Observe — see it, not just hear it. While harvesting, watch the Live input meter and write the noise-floor level in dBFS with the gain high, then again back at nominal.
  9. The difference between those two numbers is lab 001’s quiet-take lesson, read off a meter — the logbook gets both readings.
  10. Reset the input-1 gain to the ledger value.
  11. Confirm it physically, not from memory.

Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

Turn crackle into an instrument

Phase focus: turn crackle into an instrument. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Either path works.
  2. Digitakt II: transfer the recording into a sample slot, assign it to track 1, trim to a short crackle hit.
  3. Live: drag the CRACKLE clip onto a new MIDI track — it lands in Simpler — set it to Slice mode so each pad plays one short crackle burst.
  4. Audition the slices and keep the three or four most distinct bursts — too-similar slices make the pattern read as static noise instead of rhythm.
  5. Play the pads freely for a minute against the soloed REF, just to learn which slice resembles which of the record’s crackle events.

Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

Place the crackle — the lead voice

Phase focus: place the crackle — the lead voice. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Solo REF and transcribe the crackle pattern by ear; the page cannot give you the steps — hearing them is the exercise.
  2. Two passes minimum before you program anything.
  3. Program a one-bar, 16-step pattern of short crackle hits on the grid you hear — irregular but repeating;
  4. 1, 4, 7, 11, 14 is only a starting shape.
  5. Loop and solo-flip until your pattern and the record’s keep the same time — rhythm, not fault.
  6. Make a final flip pass listening only to what happens between the hits — the record’s silences are part of the pattern, and a stray extra hit fills a silence the record protects.

Phase 5 · ≈ 30 min

Everything underneath — and the pause test

Phase focus: everything underneath — and the pause test. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Everything sits below the crackle — starting points, corrected by the record
Crackle (lead)peaks ≈ −14 dBFS — the loudest event in the mix
Kickpeaks ≈ −20 dBFS — felt more than heard
Chordpeaks ≈ −26 dBFS — deep in the background
Do this
  1. MicroFreak: Paraphonic ON, Type → V.Analog, slow attack, cutoff ≈ 30 %.
  2. Transcribe the record’s deep, sparse chord from REF by ear — the page cannot give the notes — and change it only where the record changes it.
  3. Record 8 bars into CHORD at nominal, then pull its fader until it sits as far below the crackle as the record’s chord does, ≈ −12 dB down as a starting point.
  4. Digitakt II: REC, kick where the record puts it, Main Volume low.
  5. Record 8 bars into KICK, then sit its fader exactly as deep as the record buries its low end — felt more than heard.
  6. Keep everything quiet. The crackle must be the loudest event in the mix — peaks ≈ −12 to −14 dBFS and nothing above it.
  7. If the kick or chord competes, pull them down; never push the crackle up — the record’s distances, not louder ones.
  8. Loop the whole thing for two minutes at the mark, solo-flipping against REF.
  9. Then make one more pass over both lower faders, finer.
  10. Run the pause test. Stop playback mid-loop and listen to the silence.
  11. What disappeared was your music; whatever remains is your playback system’s own floor.
  12. Write down which was which.

Expected result

A quiet, sparse loop where the crackle leads, the chord glows underneath, and the kick is a pressure rather than a sound — and stopping playback stops music, not just hiss.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The chord swallows the crackleChord fader too high — the louder-is-better trap, sidewaysPull the chord down further; never push the crackle up
The pause test leaves loud hiss behindThe harvest gain was never reset — the floor is still amplifiedReturn input-1 gain to the ledger value and re-check the whole ledger
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your loop at matched loudness — phones at the mark, both quiet, neither turned up to impress.

  • Could a friend, hearing 10 seconds of yours then 10 of the record, say which is which? The longer they hesitate, the better your ears did.
    Answer

    If they hesitate, your crackle’s grain, pattern, and depth are all close. The usual giveaway is grain, not levels — your crackle sounds like your preamp, the record’s sounds like a broken filter — and that is a sound-source miss for the miss list, not a fader problem. Relisten to one crackle event from each, back to back, and name the textural difference.

  • The pause test: when you stop playback, did music stop — or just hiss?
    Answer

    The crackle pattern, the chord, and the kick should all vanish at once — that was the music. Whatever continues is your playback system’s own floor at nominal gain: a different, quieter, unplaced noise. If you cannot tell the two apart, your quietest layer is sitting on top of the floor instead of above it — re-check that the harvest gain was reset, then pull the texture of your mix up just enough to clear it.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
