Block 1 · Lab 003 · Track lab

Repetition as Engine

A hypnotic track is a short loop that plays for eight minutes without getting boring. The trick is not the loop — it is changing exactly one thing at a time, slowly, so the listener keeps noticing without knowing why. You will build one here, from one chord and a few drum sounds.

The point

When almost nothing changes, the smallest change becomes an event. Keep the frame still, move one thing at a time, and a single chord can carry eight minutes.

How this lab works

Make material that loops

  • Build steps 1–4
  • One chord on the MicroFreak, one drum grid on the Digitakt, tuned to each other
  • Test: can the bare loop hold your attention for four minutes?

Give it an engine

  • Build steps 5–7
  • Add the delay send — the place change will come from
  • Write the evolution map: a small table of what changes, and when

Perform, print, subtract

  • Build steps 8–12
  • Record the ride in one take, mistakes included
  • Commit the loops to audio, then arrange by removing things

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
The engineThe thing that creates change over time. The loop is the car’s body; the engine is what moves it. Three engines exist in this style: filtering the loop from outside, removing and adding parts over time, or letting something inside the sound drift on its own.
Send / returnA separate channel where an effect lives — here, a delay. You send a copy of a sound to it; the original stays clean, and you control how much of the copy goes in. Like a hallway with an echo: you choose how wide to open the door.
One-variable ruleOnly one parameter is allowed to change at a time, at most one move per 8 bars. Not a style choice: if two things move at once, you cannot know which one worked.
Parameter lockOn the Digitakt, a setting applied to a single step only: hold the step, turn a knob. The 5th hat can be longer than all the others without needing a second track.
Conditional trigA step that only plays sometimes — for example, 1 time in 4 loops. Cheap variation: the pattern stays alive without any new sounds.
Print / resampleRecording the result of your effects and riding as a new audio file. It commits you: the printed version is now the instrument, and you cannot endlessly tweak it. That limit is the point.

Listening — the hypothesis source

Seven records, one effect — a loop that never bores — built by seven different engines. Each gets a short brief: what to listen for before you press play, and how to check yourself after. The full six-question worksheets live in programme L1.

01 Basic Channel — “Quadrant Dub I” dub-filtered loop

The chord never changes — every event comes from outside it: a filter, an echo, a level.

Listen for
  • How muffled or bright the chord is, moment to moment
  • How much echo surrounds it, and when that changes
  • The actual events in two minutes — count them
After — verify

Was your count under ten? Most people remember more than they heard — the difference is the trick working. If you counted “lots,” relisten watching only the chord.

1 · Overview

Hypnotic techno sounds like music in which nothing happens. In fact, exactly one thing happens at a time, inside a frame so stable that the ear has nothing else to attend to. The loop carries the weight, but it does not move the piece — the engine does: a filter opening over two minutes, an echo growing slightly on each pass, a hat that breathes from bar to bar. Because the frame never changes, the smallest change becomes an event.

The one-variable rule, defined above, is the working law of the whole build. The instinct to fight is additive — more notes, more tracks, a bigger drop. This lab forbids all of it. If the piece gets boring while you build, that is information, not failure: it tells you which element has stopped changing. The fix is never a new element — it is more movement in one you already have.

Done means four checks: the piece runs 6–8 minutes at 126–132 BPM; it uses six tracks or fewer; it is fully original — the listening analysis steals a structure, never a sound; and the ten-item report bundle is filed. This is an S1 lab — the most scaffolded kind — so both specs below are already filled in for you. Build the piece and mark every place your studio disagreed with the page. Those marks are the lesson, and they are exactly what the report’s system-diff sentence exists to capture.

4 · Build

  1. Design the chord. Initialise one MicroFreak engine, build a single paraphonic voicing, and leave the filter mostly closed. The chord must bore you slightly when static — if it is interesting on its own, it leaves the engine nothing to do. Full sound-design walk: palette.
  2. The grid. Kick and closed hat on the Digitakt: kick on 1/5/9/13, hats on the offbeats with one ghost on 16 leading back to the downbeat. Program it in grid recording and save the pattern before touching anything else. Full version, including swing and micro-timing: the build.
  3. Tune the kick to the chord root. Use the Digitakt’s SRC tune until the kick’s fundamental agrees with the MicroFreak voicing — a detuned kick under a tonal chord is permanent low-end blur. Name the note in the logbook; “somewhere around A” is not a measurement.
  4. The loop test. Loop the bare chord plus grid for four full minutes without touching anything. If your attention dies in the first minute, the material is wrong, not the arrangement — go back to step 1 or 2 and change one thing.
  5. Add the delay send. Put a dub-style delay on a return, send the chord to it, and find the feedback point just below self-oscillation — that edge is where the engine lives. Ride the send once through the loop to learn its range before anything is recorded.
  6. Write the evolution map. A small table of bar spans × the one variable that changes in each span: bars 1–32 nothing moves, 33–64 filter cutoff drifts up, 65–96 delay feedback rises, and so on to the end. If a span lists two variables, the map is wrong. The long-form arc this compresses: arrangement.
  7. Add percussion and texture as variations, not patterns. Both enter as parameter-locked changes to existing trigs — decay, sample start, filter — never as new musical material. Conditional trigs (25–50%, or 1:4) keep the grid alive without new elements. Depth reference: palette.
  8. Record the ride take. Filter cutoff and delay send, one take, the full length of the piece, mistakes included — a corrected performance is no longer a performance. Print it as automation and audio. Riding technique and mapping: the build.
  9. Resample. Print two or three 8-bar loops of the synth–delay interaction back into the Digitakt or Live. These become transition material: the only “new” sounds the arrangement is allowed, and they are made entirely of old ones.
  10. Arrange by subtraction. The piece is the loop with things removed at the right moments — the strip-back makes the earlier fullness retroactively bigger. Mark one removal decision for the report: what was cut, and what cutting it did. Full timeline: arrangement.
  11. Inject the fault. Run the fault ritual below in full — predict, create, observe, diagnose, fix, rule — and log the entry before continuing. The fault is part of the lab, not an interruption of it.
  12. Mono check, then render. Collapse the master to mono for ten seconds and confirm the chord and returns survive. Render 24-bit WAV, master peaking −3 dBFS, 6–8 minutes.

The twelve steps above are the map of the build. Four of them carry most of the failure modes; those four are expanded below as full recipes — the numbers match the list.

Build step 01, expanded

The chord on the MicroFreak

Do this
  • Pick one engine with the Type encoder and stay on it — auditioning engines is a different session.
  • Press Paraphonic so it lights — four voices are now available.
  • Voice one chord shape and hold it. This shape is the whole piece’s harmony; choose it slowly.
  • Close the Filter cutoff to roughly 30 %, resonance low. The closed filter is the headroom the evolution map spends later.
  • Glide at 0 — pitch movement belongs to nothing in this piece.
  • Hold Save and store the preset to a slot. The slot number goes in the logbook.
Expected result

A chord that slightly bores you when static — if it is interesting frozen, it leaves the engine nothing to do.

Build step 08, expanded

The ride take

Do this
  • Arm the chord’s audio track with the monitoring mode you declared in pre-flight — not the one that happens to be set.
  • ⌘ M for MIDI-map mode: map the Return A send of the chord track to a Launchpad fader. Exit map mode.
  • Record the full length in one take: one hand rides the MicroFreak Filter cutoff, the other rides the mapped send.
  • Mistakes stay — a corrected performance is no longer a performance.
Expected result

A take with audible intention and at least one imperfection.

If it fails

Fault ritual · faultType: gain

Intermittent clipping that follows the modulator

predict → create → observe → diagnose → fix → rule

The fault: add +6 dB of clip gain on the texture track, so it clips its saturator only when the filter LFO opens. At idle every meter reads clean — the distortion appears and disappears with the modulation, which is exactly the kind of fault a static meter check misses. Write your predicted symptom first, then create the fault and loop a section where the LFO swings. Locate it with the channel meters watched across a full LFO cycle and with your ears, fix it at the clip gain, and write the rule: intermittent distortion follows the modulator, not the fader — when a fault comes and goes with the music, look for what is moving.

5 · Diagnosis & report

The full report bundle — ten items, filed in the logbook
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, master peaking −3 dBFS
  • Routing diagram — drawn before patching, corrected after; the diff is the lesson
  • Recording plan — what was printed, when, at what stage
  • Patch notes — the settings that mattered
  • Session notes
  • Reflection answers, including the listening comparison — which record contributed what to the hypothesis
  • One removal decision — what was cut from the finished piece and what cutting it did
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule
  • Print map — what now exists as audio, MIDI, automation, patch state, or device memory; what is reproducible and what is gone
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the system worked like X; after building it, I discovered it worked like Y.”
  • Minimum pass gate: audible output · routing diagram · fault entry · print map · system diff. Without these five, the lab is incomplete regardless of audio quality — a musically strong piece cannot bypass the engineering spine.
