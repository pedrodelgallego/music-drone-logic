Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: Patterns from One Voice

One small cell, one clean voice, and a grid of Session clips — replicate a section of “Patchwork” closely enough to feel how permutation, not material, makes a piece.

The point

Before DAWs, composition was permutation — a small cell rearranged by rule. The logic is audible, and the logic is the music.

The system you are replicating
Source recordLaurie Spiegel — “Patchwork”
The effecta few notes heard as a whole composition
The mechanismone cell permuted by rule — reversed, shifted, doubled, gapped — on one voice
Fidelityas close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • MicroFreak ¼″ out → M4 in 1; Digitakt stays off — this lesson is one voice and a grid of clips.
  • New set saved as REP-patchwork; set the tempo by ear against the record — it is not a techno grid.
  • Session view basics: clips launch quantized, and ⌘ D duplicates a clip in place — that is the whole toolkit.
  • Timebox: 60–120 minutes. The cell is a handful of notes, so transcription is quick — hearing the permutation rules and chaining them in the record’s order are the slow parts. Stop when the chain plays the form, not before.
Listen first

Reference pass — 10 minutes

Listen to about two minutes of “Patchwork”. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear — and write your answer before opening each reveal.

  1. Find the cell. How many notes is it?
    Answer

    A handful — a short figure of roughly three to five notes that everything else in the piece is built from. Write down your count and sing it once. Relisten to the opening, where the cell arrives nearly naked, and check your version against hers.

  2. How many rearrangements of the cell did you count in two minutes?
    Answer

    More than you expect — the piece moves through new orderings constantly, and most arrive every few bars. The exact number matters less than noticing that every “new idea” is the old cell re-ordered. Relisten and tally; your count will rise on the second pass as your ear learns the rules.

  3. What rules generate the rearrangements — name two or three.
    Answer

    The classics of pattern music: the cell reversed, the cell shifted in register, the cell doubled against itself, the cell with notes removed. Spiegel was working on Bell Labs computers where these were literal operations on data. Relisten and label each arrival with its rule — once you can name them, you can build them.

  4. Does the timbre ever change, or only the pattern?
    Answer

    The voice stays essentially constant — clean, plain, unornamented — which is exactly what makes the pattern logic audible. With nothing else changing, your ear has nowhere to go but the orderings. Relisten and notice how the plainness of the sound is doing structural work.

  5. Is there a moment the cell appears naked — alone and unpermuted?
    Answer

    Yes — the piece states its material plainly, especially early, the way a fugue states its subject. That naked statement is your transcription source and the listener’s key to everything after. Relisten to the first minute and mark the cleanest statement you can find.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

  • Sound target — what success sounds like:
  • The cell, transcribed by ear, close enough that a friend hearing yours then hers would hesitate.
  • One clean voice, no effects — plain enough that the orderings carry all the interest.
  • Each permutation traceable to a named rule, never to new material.
  • The chain follows the record’s form — her order of operations, not yours.

Steps

The whole rig for this lesson — one voice, one column of Session clips, and a recorded chain.

Session clips (permutations) → MicroFreak → M4 in 1 → Live: MF PRINT (record)
Phase 1

Set the reference

Every judgement in this lesson — the cell’s notes, the plainness of the voice, the order of the permutations — is made against the record, so the record goes on the bench first, looped and gain-matched. Pick a stretch that starts at or near a naked statement of the cell, because that statement is your transcription source.

Do this
  1. Drop “Patchwork” onto an audio track named REF.
  2. Loop a one-to-two-minute stretch that opens with the cleanest statement of the cell you marked in the reference pass.
  3. Gain-match with a Utility on REF.
  4. Keep REF one solo away for the whole session.
Expected result

A clean loop of the section whose permutation order you will be matching, at an honest level.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The loop stumbles at the seamLoop brace fighting a tempo that isn’t griddedTurn warping off for REF and loop generously — this record doesn’t owe your grid anything
REF always sounds “better” in A/BIt’s just louderPull the Utility down until levels genuinely match
Phase 2

Transcribe the cell, keep the voice clean

The cell — three to five notes — is the entire material of the piece, so it gets transcribed by ear from the naked statement. The voice stays deliberately plain: one engine, filter open enough to be clear, no matrix motion, no effects. Plainness is not a compromise here; it is what makes the logic audible, on her record and in your replica.

Do this
  1. Transcribe the cell by ear into a one-bar Session clip on a MIDI track, MIDI To → MicroFreak; name the clip CELL.
  2. Solo REF at the naked statement, hum it, place it, compare; revise until the cells match.
  3. Dress the voice plain: one engine (Waveshaper or V.Analog), LP filter mostly open, low resonance, soft attack, medium decay, Paraphonic OFF.
  4. No mod-matrix routings, no effects — confirm the chain is voice → M4 → Live, dry.
Expected result

One clip and one plain voice — the cell, recognisably hers, stated as simply as the record states it.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Your cell sounds right alone but wrong against REFAn interval is off by a step — small cells expose everythingCheck each interval one note-pair at a time against the naked statement
The voice draws attention to itselfToo much character — resonance, motion, or effects crept inStrip it back: the voice should disappear behind the pattern
Phase 3

Build the permutations as Session clips

This is the record’s engine, rebuilt as a clip column: each permutation is the cell transformed by exactly one named rule, duplicated and edited — never composed fresh. Where Spiegel’s computer applied operations to data, your Session view applies them to clips; the column becomes a literal permutation table. The set below covers the rules you logged in the reference pass; add a rule only if you heard it in the record.

  • Safety — before the octave-up clip: shifting the cell up an octave on a clean open-filter patch lands in the ear’s most sensitive range and reads much louder than the original. Headphones at the mark or lower — launch OCT+ once at the set level before looping it, and never turn up to inspect.
Do this
  1. Duplicate CELL down the track with ⌘ D, once per rule in the table below.
  2. Edit each copy by its rule only — reverse the note order by hand, drag octaves, double, delete. No new pitches anywhere.
  3. Name every clip by its rule; the names are the score.
  4. Launch each clip against REF and keep only the permutations the record actually uses.
Permutation table — clip / rule
CELLthe transcribed cell, untouched — the subject
REVthe cell with its note order reversed
OCT+ / OCT−the cell shifted an octave up / down
DBLthe cell doubled against itself, offset by a few steps
GAPthe cell with one or two notes deleted — the holes are part of the pattern
Expected result

A named column of clips, every one derived from CELL by a single rule — the piece’s entire material in one screen.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A permutation sounds like a new melodyEditing drifted into composing — pitches changedDelete it, re-duplicate CELL, and apply the rule mechanically
DBL is mudThe offset stacks the cell on top of itselfShift the doubled copy until the two voices interlock instead of colliding — listen for how the record spaces them
Phase 4

Chain the permutations, record the form

Now the clips get composed — in her order, not yours. Map the section’s form as a sequence of rules, then perform that sequence by launching clips while Live records the MicroFreak. The arrangement is nothing but the order of permutations; that was true at Bell Labs and it is true in your Session view.

Do this
  1. Listen through the REF loop and write the form as a rule sequence — e.g. CELL → REV → CELL → DBL → GAP… — one entry per change you hear.
  2. Arm MF PRINT — Audio From → Ext. In → 1 — and record while you launch the clips in that order, holding each as long as the record holds it.
  3. Stay quantized — launches snap to the bar, and the rigidity is period-correct.
  4. A/B the recorded chain against REF and re-take until the changes land where hers land.
Expected result

A recorded pass through the section’s form — the same cell, the record’s order of operations, no material that isn’t a permutation.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Your chain wanders where hers buildsPermutations held for arbitrary lengthsTime the record’s holds — write bar counts into the rule sequence and obey them
You keep wanting to add a pad underneathThe itch is the lessonAdd a permutation instead — OCT− under CELL is the record’s way of thickening
Phase 5

Observe the grid, compare, export

Now make the lesson visible. Look at the Session view: one column of named clips, every one a rule applied to the first. That grid is a permutation table — the music sitting on screen as combinatorics, the same way it sat in Spiegel’s computer as operations on a few bytes of pattern. Describe it in the logbook, then verify against the record and print.

Do this
  1. Write one logbook line describing the grid: how many clips, how many rules, and the fact that the whole piece is visible without playing a note.
  2. Mark with locators where your permutation changes land in the recorded chain and where REF’s land; measure the gaps.
  3. Export the chain — File → Export Audio/Video, WAV, named REP-patchwork.
Expected result

One written line about music-as-combinatorics, paired locators a few seconds apart at worst, and a finished WAV.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A clip in the grid has no rule name you can defendIt was composed, not derivedRebuild it from CELL with one rule, or cut it — the table must stay honest
The export contains REFReference track not muted at renderMute REF, re-export, and listen to the file before filing it
Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the looped section at matched volume, then answer:

  • Sing the cell from memory, then pick any moment of your chain — can you name the rule that produced it?
    Answer

    If the cell is singable and every moment has a nameable rule, you replicated the system, not just the surface. A moment with no rule is composed material that leaked in — the record never needs any. Relisten to the densest stretch of “Patchwork” and watch it resolve into named operations now that you have built them.

  • How many rearrangements does your chain contain — and is the count in the record’s neighbourhood?
    Answer

    Count your clip launches and compare with the tally from your reference pass — the record changes orderings more often than first listening suggests, every few bars in places. If your chain is far sparser, your holds are too long. Relisten with your rule sequence in hand and mark every change you missed the first time.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines:
  • Matched: what your replica got closest to — the cell, the plainness, the order of operations; start with the rule that landed nearest the record’s.
  • Missed: what the record does that yours doesn’t (best guess why).
  • Change next: the one thing you would do differently on the next attempt.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1. Carries into étude 004: 004’s A and B patterns — one note apart — are a two-clip permutation table; this lesson is the same idea with more rows.
