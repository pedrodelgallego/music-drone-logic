Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: saturation as mass

Hold one low MicroFreak chord through heavy saturation for minutes at a time, the way “It Took the Night to Believe” holds its wall — and hear mass come from sustained harmonics, not from loudness.

The point

Mass is sustained saturated harmonics, not level — one held note through heavy drive fills the spectrum like an amplifier wall, at any volume.

The system you are replicating
Source recordSunn O))) — “It Took the Night to Believe”
The effecta wall of mass — one chord filling the whole spectrum, for minutes
The mechanisma sustained drone through heavy saturation, changing only by slow filter movement
Fidelityas close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, Digitakt II → M4 inputs 3/4, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged
  • New set saved as REP-SUNN-O, tempo 60 — mostly symbolic; this lesson is measured in breaths, not bars
  • M4 phones knob at the lab-001 mark or lower — sustained drone fatigues faster than rhythm; plan breaks before you need them
  • Timebox: 60–120 minutes, then file it and stop — the extra time goes into holding and listening, not extra devices
Reference pass — 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to about two minutes of “It Took the Night to Believe” — this record needs the longer window, and it needs a moderate level; the mass survives quiet listening. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Open each model answer only after your own line exists.

  1. How many notes are actually being played?
    Answer

    Shockingly few — a chord, sometimes a single note, held and occasionally re-struck. Everything else you hear is saturation multiplying that note across the spectrum. The wall sounds orchestral, but the score would fit on a napkin. Relisten and count only the pitch changes in a minute; the count is the answer.

  2. How long is a note held?
    Answer

    Minutes, or until the next re-strike — there is no envelope in the percussive sense, only sustain and the slow life inside it. Duration is doing the work that rhythm does elsewhere in this strand. Relisten and time one held tone from strike to change; the length will be longer than you guessed.

  3. What changes while the note holds — anything?
    Answer

    The color, slowly: harmonics swell and recede, the top of the wall opens and closes — filter-speed movement, never note-speed movement. Nothing arrives and nothing leaves; the existing sound breathes. Relisten to thirty seconds of one tone and name two moments where the brightness changed.

  4. Where does the sense of “huge” come from — level, or something else?
    Answer

    Density — the chord occupies the whole spectrum at once, bottom to top, the way a full arrangement would, so the ear reads one note as everything. Turn it down and it is still huge, which level alone could never survive. Relisten quietly on purpose and notice the mass is intact.

  5. Is the distortion an event — or a place?
    Answer

    A place — the saturation is constant, the room the music lives in, not a hit or an effect that comes and goes. That constancy is what makes it read as an amplifier wall rather than a fault: nothing this steady is an accident. Relisten for a single clean moment; there isn’t one, by decision.

Build only after the answers exist — you are about to copy the system you just described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your wall lands on the same kind of mass — one chord filling the spectrum, breathing slowly, at matched loudness. Closeness is the measure of your hearing.
  • Full-spectrum at ≈ −12 dBFS peaks — the wall is dense, never loud; the meter stays at nominal while the spectrum fills.
  • One note count, enormous harmonic count — if you add notes to get mass, the lesson is being dodged.
  • Change happens at filter speed only — minutes-long tones whose color moves, not whose pitch does.

The wall’s machinery in one row: a low sustained source, heavy saturation generating the spectrum, slow filter movement as the only motion, and a gain-compensated output so the mass never becomes mere level.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the tracks

Every mass judgment in this lesson is a comparison against the record at matched loudness — and this record is mastered dense, so untrimmed it would win every flip on sheer level. Physically, the Utility puts the wall at nominal, where its mass has to come from density alone, exactly the claim you are testing.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “It Took the Night to Believe” into it.
  2. Find the most characteristic two to three minutes — one chord establishing, holding, breathing — and loop them.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  4. ⌘ T once; rename: DRONE, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB. One source track is the whole session — the Digitakt rests this lesson.
  5. Write the pacing in the logbook: how long the record holds before anything changes. That number, not the tempo, is this lesson’s clock.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one named, routed, unity-gain track waits for the drone, and the record’s pacing is on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

The drone patch — low, slow, endless

The source under the record’s wall is simple: something low that never stops. Physically, the saturation in phase 3 will manufacture the spectrum, so the patch only has to supply a strong low fundamental and infinite sustain — a tone that behaves like a bowed string or a wall of amplifiers idling, not like a key being pressed.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, Paraphonic ON for a low two-note chord (a fifth works; the record’s register, not its exact pitches — your ear finds the depth). One octave below comfortable is the right neighborhood.
  2. Envelope: attack slow — the note should bloom in over a second or more — sustain full, release long. Cutoff ≈ 30 %, resonance low.
  3. Hold the chord and listen for a full minute, at the mark or lower. If the tone is finished before the minute is — if nothing in you wants to keep listening — slow the attack further and darken the cutoff; patience must be in the patch.
  4. Set the M4 input-1 gain so the held chord peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — sustained material reads hotter than hits; set the gain on the held tone, not on the strike.
  5. Record two to three minutes into DRONE: strike, hold, re-strike at the record’s pacing from phase 1. Weight the keys or use the MicroFreak’s Hold so your hands can leave.
Expected result

Minutes of clean low drone at ≈ −12 dBFS — dark, patient, and politely empty. It should sound nothing like the record yet: the wall does not exist until the saturation builds it.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
The drone wobbles or beats unpleasantlyThe two notes are too close — an interval problem, not a gain problemWiden to a fifth or an octave; the record’s walls are built on stable intervals
Peaks fine on the strike, then the meter sagsGain set on the attack instead of the sustainRe-set the input gain while the chord holds — the held tone is the signal here
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Saturate it — heavy, sustained, slow

Now the wall gets built, at a chosen address: a Saturator on the drone, driven heavily, output pulled down so the mass never becomes mere level. Physically, heavy saturation on a sustained low tone generates a harmonic series that climbs the whole spectrum and stays there — that standing series is the mass. The only motion allowed afterward is filter-speed movement, because that is all the record allows.

  • Safety: this phase is a sustained, saturated, low-frequency drone — the most fatiguing material in Block 1. Phones at the mark or lower · take a real break every 15 minutes · never turn up to feel the mass; the mass is in the spectrum, not the level.
Do this
  1. On DRONE, drop in Audio Effects → Saturator. Push Drive heavily — start at +24 dB — and pull Output down by roughly the same amount, until the track meters ≈ −12 dBFS with the device on, same as off.
  2. Solo-flip against REF and walk the Drive until the density matches the record’s wall — at matched loudness, density is the only thing left to compare. Make at least three flip-and-correct passes.
  3. Add Audio Effects → Auto Filter after the Saturator: low-pass, cutoff high. Map nothing; move it by hand, slower than feels musical — minutes-long sweeps. This is the breathing from your reference pass, and it is the only motion this lesson permits.
  4. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Drop Audio Effects → Spectrum at the end of the chain. At matched loudness, raise the Drive from low to your setting and watch the display fill from the bottom up — the snapshots from the diagram, live — while the played note count stays exactly one chord. Write in the logbook how far up the band your wall reaches.
Expected result

The same patient drone transformed into a wall: one chord occupying the whole spectrum at ≈ −12 dBFS, its color breathing under the hand-moved filter, its meter never asking for more level than the ledger allows.

Phase 4 · ≈ 25 min

Hold the wall — minutes, then A/B

The record’s final ingredient is duration: the wall means nothing in eight-bar excerpts, because mass accumulates in the listener, not in the meter. Physically nothing new happens here — you perform the piece at the record’s pacing, hold it for minutes, and then put your wall and the record’s side by side.

Do this
  1. In Arrangement view, lay out 3–4 minutes: the recorded drone (or a fresh performance), re-strikes at the pacing you wrote in phase 1, filter sweeps written as slow automation or ridden by hand.
  2. Play it end to end without touching anything else. Sit through it at the mark or lower. Resist the urge to add an element — the urge is the lesson arriving.
  3. A/B the wall: one minute of REF, one minute of yours, at matched loudness. Judge only mass — does one chord fill the band the way the record’s does, and does it breathe at the same patience?
  4. Write one sentence in the logbook on where the mass came from — with the words “loud” and “louder” banned from the sentence.
Expected result

Minutes of wall that hold attention without rhythm, melody, or level — and an A/B against REF that comes down to density and patience, the record’s actual materials.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
Your wall is boring where the record’s is hypnoticNo life inside the sustain — the filter is parkedRide the filter through the whole take, at minute-speed; the breathing is the performance
Listening fatigue arrives in minutesLevel crept up, or the wall is fizzier than the record’sReturn to the mark, take the scheduled break, and darken the top before judging again
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your wall at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • How many notes is your wall actually playing — and does it still feel like more than that?
    Answer

    One chord — two or three pitches at most — and yes, it should feel like a building’s worth, because the saturation has stacked a full harmonic series on top of it. If it feels like exactly what it is — a couple of notes — the Drive or the source’s sustain is short of the record. Relisten to the record asking the same question; its answer is the same napkin-sized score.

  • Turn your wall down well below the mark. Is the mass still there?
    Answer

    It should be — density survives low volume; level does not. The record stays enormous at a whisper because the whole spectrum is still occupied, just quieter. If your wall shrinks when turned down, its size was borrowed from level, and the fix is more harmonic density at the Saturator, not more gain anywhere. Relisten to the record quiet, one last time, and hear how little the hugeness cares about the volume knob.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
