Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: saturation as mass

Copy one clear section of Sunn O))) — “It Took the Night to Believe”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a sustained drone through heavy saturation, changing only by slow filter movement. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, Digitakt II → M4 inputs 3/4, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged
  • New set saved as REP-SUNN-O, tempo 60 — mostly symbolic; this lesson is measured in breaths, not bars
  • M4 phones knob at the lab-001 mark or lower — sustained drone fatigues faster than rhythm; plan breaks before you need them
  • What you will make: a short replica of Sunn O))) — “It Took the Night to Believe”. Focus on this effect: a wall of mass - one chord filling the whole spectrum, for minutes.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a sustained drone through heavy saturation, changing only by slow filter movement.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Sunn O))) — “It Took the Night to Believe”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many notes are actually being played?
    Answer

    Shockingly few - a chord, sometimes a single note, held and occasionally re-struck. Everything else you hear is saturation multiplying that note across the spectrum. The wall sounds orchestral, but the score would fit on a napkin.

  2. How long is a note held?
    Answer

    Minutes, or until the next re-strike - there is no envelope in the percussive sense, only sustain and the slow life inside it. Duration is doing the work that rhythm does elsewhere in this strand.

  3. What changes while the note holds — anything?
    Answer

    The color, slowly: harmonics swell and recede, the top of the wall opens and closes - filter-speed movement, never note-speed movement. Nothing arrives and nothing leaves; the existing sound breathes.

  4. Where does the sense of “huge” come from — level, or something else?
    Answer

    Density - the chord occupies the whole spectrum at once, bottom to top, the way a full arrangement would, so the ear reads one note as everything. Turn it down and it is still huge, which level alone could never survive.

  5. Is the distortion an event — or a place?
    Answer

    A place - the saturation is constant, the room the music lives in, not a hit or an effect that comes and goes. That constancy is what makes it read as an amplifier wall rather than a fault: nothing this steady is an accident.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your wall lands on the same kind of mass — one chord filling the spectrum, breathing slowly, at matched loudness.
  • Full-spectrum at ≈ −12 dBFS peaks — the wall is dense, never loud; the meter stays at nominal while the spectrum fills.
  • One note count, enormous harmonic count — if you add notes to get mass, the lesson is being dodged.
  • Change happens at filter speed only — minutes-long tones whose color moves, not whose pitch does.

The wall’s machinery in one row: a low sustained source, heavy saturation generating the spectrum, slow filter movement as the only motion, and a gain-compensated output so the mass never becomes mere level.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the tracks

Phase focus: the reference and the tracks. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “It Took the Night to Believe” into it.
  2. Live Find the most characteristic two to three minutes — one chord establishing, holding, breathing — and loop them.
  3. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Live REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. Live ⌘ T once; rename: DRONE, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB.
  6. Digitakt II One source track is the whole session — the Digitakt II rests this lesson.
  7. Logbook Write the pacing in the logbook: how long the record holds before anything changes.
  8. Logbook That number, not the tempo, is this lesson’s clock.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one named, routed, unity-gain track waits for the drone, and the record’s pacing is on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

The drone patch — low, slow, endless

Phase focus: the drone patch — low, slow, endless. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, Paraphonic ON for a low two-note chord (a fifth works; the record’s register, not its exact pitches — your ear finds the depth).
  2. MicroFreak One octave below comfortable is the right neighborhood.
  3. MicroFreak Envelope: attack slow — the note should bloom in over a second or more — sustain full, release long.
  4. MicroFreak Cutoff ≈ 30 %, resonance low.
  5. MicroFreak Hold the chord and listen for a full minute, at the mark or lower.
  6. MicroFreak If the tone is finished before the minute is — if nothing in you wants to keep listening — slow the attack further and darken the cutoff;
  7. MicroFreak patience must be in the patch.
  8. MicroFreak Set the M4 input-1 gain so the held chord peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — sustained material reads hotter than hits;
  9. MicroFreak set the gain on the held tone, not on the strike.
  10. MicroFreak Record two to three minutes into DRONE: strike, hold, re-strike at the record’s pacing from phase 1. Weight the keys or use the MicroFreak’s Hold so your hands can leave.
Expected result

Minutes of clean low drone at ≈ −12 dBFS — dark, patient, and politely empty. It should sound nothing like the record yet: the wall does not exist until the saturation builds it.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The drone wobbles or beats unpleasantlyThe two notes are too close — an interval problem, not a gain problemWiden to a fifth or an octave; the record’s walls are built on stable intervals
Peaks fine on the strike, then the meter sagsGain set on the attack instead of the sustainRe-set the input gain while the chord holds — the held tone is the signal here
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Saturate it — heavy, sustained, slow

Phase focus: saturate it — heavy, sustained, slow. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

  • Safety: this phase is a sustained, saturated, low-frequency drone — the most fatiguing material in Block 1. Phones at the mark or lower · take a real break every 15 minutes · never turn up to feel the mass; the mass is in the spectrum, not the level.
Do this
  1. Live On DRONE, drop in Audio Effects → Saturator.
  2. Live Push Drive heavily — start at +24 dB — and pull Output down by roughly the same amount, until the track meters ≈ −12 dBFS with the device on, same as off.
  3. Live Solo-flip against REF and walk the Drive until the density matches the record’s wall — at matched loudness, density is the only thing left to compare.
  4. Live Make at least three flip-and-correct passes.
  5. MicroFreak + Live Add Audio Effects → Auto Filter after the Saturator: low-pass, cutoff high.
  6. MicroFreak + Live Map nothing; move it by hand, slower than feels musical — minutes-long sweeps.
  7. Live This is the breathing from your reference pass, and it is the only motion this lesson permits.
  8. Live Observe — see it, not just hear it. Drop Audio Effects → Spectrum at the end of the chain.
  9. Live At matched loudness, raise the Drive from low to your setting and watch the display fill from the bottom up — the snapshots from the diagram, live — while the played note count stays exactly one chord.
  10. Logbook Write in the logbook how far up the band your wall reaches.
Expected result

The same patient drone transformed into a wall: one chord occupying the whole spectrum at ≈ −12 dBFS, its color breathing under the hand-moved filter, its meter never asking for more level than the ledger allows.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The wall simply sounds “bigger” in every A/B and you stop judgingOutput not pulled down — level is impersonating massGain-compensate until on/off meter the same, then compare density only
Harsh fizz on top instead of mass belowSource too bright into heavy drive — the energy is clipping in the wrong registerDarken the MicroFreak cutoff and let the Saturator, not the source, supply the highs
Phase 4 · ≈ 25 min

Hold the wall — minutes, then A/B

Phase focus: hold the wall — minutes, then A/B. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live In Arrangement view, lay out 3–4 minutes: the recorded drone (or a fresh performance), re-strikes at the pacing you wrote in phase 1, filter sweeps written as slow automation or ridden by hand.
  2. Live Play it end to end without touching anything else.
  3. Live Sit through it at the mark or lower.
  4. Live Resist the urge to add an element — the urge is the lesson arriving.
  5. Live A/B the wall: one minute of REF, one minute of yours, at matched loudness.
  6. Live Judge only mass — does one chord fill the band the way the record’s does, and does it breathe at the same patience?
  7. Logbook Write one sentence in the logbook on where the mass came from — with the words “loud” and “louder” banned from the sentence.
Expected result

Minutes of wall that hold attention without rhythm, melody, or level — and an A/B against REF that comes down to density and patience, the record’s actual materials.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Your wall is boring where the record’s is hypnoticNo life inside the sustain — the filter is parkedRide the filter through the whole take, at minute-speed; the breathing is the performance
Listening fatigue arrives in minutesLevel crept up, or the wall is fizzier than the record’sReturn to the mark, take the scheduled break, and darken the top before judging again
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your wall at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • How many notes is your wall actually playing — and does it still feel like more than that?
    Answer

    One chord — two or three pitches at most — and yes, it should feel like a building’s worth, because the saturation has stacked a full harmonic series on top of it. If it feels like exactly what it is — a couple of notes — the Drive or the source’s sustain is short of the record. Relisten to the record asking the same question; its answer is the same napkin-sized score.

  • Turn your wall down well below the mark. Is the mass still there?
    Answer

    It should be — density survives low volume; level does not. The record stays enormous at a whisper because the whole spectrum is still occupied, just quieter. If your wall shrinks when turned down, its size was borrowed from level, and the fix is more harmonic density at the Saturator, not more gain anywhere. Relisten to the record quiet, one last time, and hear how little the hugeness cares about the volume knob.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • Carries into the lab: the meter measures level; it does not measure mass. Two signals peaking at −12 dBFS can be a blip and a wall — the ledger needs your ears for the difference.
