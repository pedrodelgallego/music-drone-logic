Replicate: beating as the only rhythm

Copy one clear section of Phill Niblock — Disseminate. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: many sustained tones tuned only a few cents apart, so the interference beating between them is the only rhythm in the piece. No drums, no events — just a dense cluster whose internal pulsing is the entire sense of motion. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged; the Digitakt II rests this lesson
  • New set saved as REP-NIBLOCK — no tempo worth setting; the “rhythm” is the beat rate set by tuning
  • Dense sustained clusters are deceptive — they meter loud and load the ear hard, and several near-pitches stacked can build energy fast. Phones at the lab-001 mark or lower, a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into tuning the tones against each other, not adding more of them
  • What you will make: a short replica of Phill Niblock — Disseminate. Focus on this effect: a dense wall of held tones whose only rhythm is the beating between them.
  • What you will turn off: drums, any event, any LFO — the pulse must come from tuning, not from a modulator.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: many sustained tones tuned microtonally apart so the beating between them is the only rhythm.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Phill Niblock — Disseminate. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Is there any percussion?
    Answer

    None. There are no drums and no struck events. And yet you feel a rhythm — a throb, a shimmer, a pulsing. That pulse is not played; it is a by-product of the tones interfering.

  2. What is creating the sense of pulse?
    Answer

    Beating. When two sustained tones are tuned a few cents apart, their waveforms drift in and out of phase, and the combined loudness rises and falls at the rate of their pitch difference. Stack many such pairs and you get a whole field of throbbing — rhythm from tuning alone.

  3. How many tones are in the cluster?
    Answer

    Many — a thick band of pitches clustered close together, not a clean chord. You can’t count them, and that density is the point: the more near-pitches, the more beat rates, the richer the throb.

  4. Does the pulse rate stay the same?
    Answer

    No — different pairs of tones beat at different rates, so the pulsing is layered and shifting. As tones drift very slightly in tuning over the piece, the beat rates change, and the whole field slowly reorganises.

  5. Where is the composition if there are no notes being played?
    Answer

    In the tuning. Which pitches you stack, and how many cents apart they sit, determines every beat rate in the piece. The score is a tuning chart, and the rhythm falls out of it automatically — no rhythm was ever written.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Live 12 tuning: stack several copies of one held tone on separate tracks and offset each with Utility → Pitch (cents) or the instrument’s fine-tune — small cent offsets create the beat rates.
  • MicroFreak: hold one note per take; use the fine-tune to shift each printed layer a few cents. Keep the engine simple and the LFO off — the pulse must come from tuning between layers, never from a modulator.
  • Digitakt II: idle this lesson — there is no rhythm to sequence; the rhythm is the beating.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same throb — a dense held cluster pulsing on its own, with no drum anywhere.
  • Sustained tones only — no percussion, no events, no LFO faking the pulse.
  • The rhythm is the beating; it is set entirely by how many cents apart the tones sit.
  • Change the tuning, not the parts — every adjustment is a cent value, not a new sound.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a section of Disseminate into it.
  2. Loop a two-to-three-minute stretch where the throb is strong and you can count a pulse.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so it peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. ⌘ T for the first tone, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed; name it TONE 1.
  6. Write in the logbook the rough beat rate of the strongest pulse — count pulses over ten seconds and divide — and whether you hear one rate or several at once.
  7. Keep that reading; phase 2 will set the detune to match it.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Phill Niblock — Disseminate and stack several detuned Operator/Wavetable held tones.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the track — as the smallest Live set that proves beating between microtonally detuned sustained tones is the only rhythm.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the detune that most affects the throb.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: several FM voices on near-identical pitches with small cent offsets give the beating; record main-out.
  2. Program it so it demonstrates beating as the only rhythm, not just the surface of Disseminate.
  3. Keep one clear performance control — the cluster detune spread — and name it before recording.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a target-box shortcut only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A reference loop at ≈ −12 dBFS with an audible throb, one named tone track waiting, and a beat-rate reading on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The tones — one note, printed and detuned

Phase focus: the tones. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: pick a simple engine with some harmonic content and hold one note — full sustain, no LFO, no filter movement.
  2. Print 1–2 minutes of that held tone into TONE 1, peaking ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. ⌘ T three or four more times; print the same note again into each, but nudge the MicroFreak fine-tune a few cents each time — a different small offset per layer.
  4. Name them TONE 2, TONE 3, and so on. The pitches should all be “the same note,” just microtonally spread.
  5. Set the offsets so the closest pair gives a pulse near the rate you logged: a 1-cent gap beats very slowly, several cents beats faster.
  6. Solo any two layers and confirm you hear a pulse with no drum. Lower each layer’s fader so the stack peaks ≈ −12 dBFS together, not individually.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: duplicate one held-tone track several times and offset each with Utility Pitch (cents) or instrument fine-tune.
  2. Build the phase goal — the detuned cluster — as the smallest Live set that proves the pulse comes from tuning, not a modulator.
  3. Keep each layer on a named track, then use Utility to match the stacked level against REF before judging it.
  4. Resample to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B the throb against REF and adjust the cent offsets to match the beat rate.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: several FM voices on one pitch with small per-voice cent offsets give the cluster.
  2. Program it so it demonstrates beating from microtonal tuning, not just the surface of Disseminate.
  3. Keep the cluster spread as the one named control for later.
  4. Record the cluster into Live or a recorder, then compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a shortcut only when the same throbbing wall stays audible.
Expected result

Four or five layers of the same note, each detuned a few cents, stacked into a dense cluster that throbs on its own — the strongest pulse near the record’s rate, the whole stack at ≈ −12 dBFS.

The cluster sounds like an out-of-tune chord, not a throbThe offsets are too large — the tones read as separate pitchesPull the detune in to a few cents so they fuse and beat instead of clashing
The stack clips or overpowersLayers summed without lowering each faderDrop each layer’s level so the combined stack peaks ≈ −12 dBFS
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Compose with tuning only

Phase focus: compose with tuning only. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Write a tuning chart on paper: each layer’s offset in cents from the root. That chart is the score — every beat rate in the piece is decided here.
  2. Choose offsets that give several different beat rates — say +1, +3, −2, +6 cents — so the throb is layered rather than a single even pulse.
  3. To make the field evolve with no events, very slowly automate one or two layers’ tuning by a cent or two across the take — the beat rates drift, and the whole cluster reorganises.
  4. Add nothing percussive and no LFO. If you want more rhythm, you add a tone with a new offset; the rhythm always comes from tuning.
  5. Keep the stack level steady — this is not a level piece; the interest is the throb.
  6. The rule: every change you make is a cent value. If you reach for a drum or a tremolo, delete the reach.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: set each layer’s cents per the chart, optionally automate one or two slowly.
  2. Build the phase goal — compose with tuning only — as the smallest Live set that proves the rhythm is in the tuning chart.
  3. Keep each layer on a named track, then use Utility to match the stacked level against REF before judging it.
  4. Resample to PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B the throb against REF and adjust only the cent offsets.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: set per-voice detune as the chart; automate one or two cents slowly for evolution.
  2. Program it so it demonstrates tuning as the composition, not just the surface of Disseminate.
  3. Keep the cluster spread as the named, repeatable control.
  4. Record a full pass into Live or a recorder, then compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a shortcut only when the same tuning-driven rhythm stays audible.
Expected result

A 2–4 minute dense cluster whose entire rhythm is interference beating — several layered pulse rates from the tuning chart, slowly reorganising as one or two tunings drift, with no percussion anywhere.

The drift is heard as a sweep, not a reorganisationThe tuning automation moves too fast or too farSlow it and shrink it to a cent or two across the whole take
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Hold it, A/B it, and check the beat rate

Phase focus: hold it, A/B it, and check the beat rate. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play your take end to end, no touching, at the mark or lower.
  2. If the urge to add a kick to “anchor” the rhythm arrives, it is the lesson arriving — the throb is the rhythm.
  3. A/B one minute of yours against REF at matched loudness, judging two things: is your throb as rich, and is it clearly coming from the tones rather than a tremolo.
  4. Count your strongest beat rate and compare it to your phase-1 reading of the record — same ballpark?
  5. Write in the logbook how loud the dense cluster felt versus its meter reading.
  6. Take the scheduled break. Dense sustained clusters fatigue the ear quickly.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: judge the print against REF at matched level.
  2. Confirm the smallest Live set still proves beating as the only rhythm.
  3. Use Utility to match the stacked level against REF before judging.
  4. Keep the proof as a printed PRINT track.
  5. Adjust only the cent offsets that shape the throb.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: verify the detuned cluster and any tuning drift hold across the pass.
  2. Confirm the program demonstrates tuning-driven beating as the rhythm.
  3. Keep the cluster spread as the named, repeatable control.
  4. Record the full pass and compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a shortcut only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A piece that feels rhythmic with no percussion, an A/B that comes down to the richness and source of the throb, and a logbook line about how a dense cluster loads the ear versus its meter reading.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Could a listener find a drum or a tremolo?
    Answer

    They should not be able to, in your piece or the record. If they can point at a “rhythm device,” the pulse is coming from the wrong place and that section goes back to phase 2 — the rhythm must be the tones beating, nothing else.

  • Did the tuning chart alone produce the rhythm?
    Answer

    That is the test. No rhythm was written — only cent offsets — and yet the piece pulses. If your throb was rich and layered, the chart did its job. If it was thin, the fix is never a drum; it is more layers and a wider spread of cents. Relisten to the record: every pulse you hear is two held tones rubbing together.
