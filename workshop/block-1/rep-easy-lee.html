Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: micro-swing

Program a few percussion voices dead straight, then give each its own tiny fixed offset — one early, one late, one on the line — until the ensemble rolls like the record.

The point

When each percussion voice has its own small consistent offset, the ensemble rolls — groove as a table of tiny numbers, one per voice.

The system you are replicating
Source recordRicardo Villalobos — “Easy Lee”
The effectthe percussion seems to rotate — many small clocks, one rolling feel
The mechanisma different fixed micro-timing offset per voice, over a steady frame
FidelityAs close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Digitakt II main outs → M4 inputs 3/4, clock-slaved to Live as in the previous lessons
  • New set saved as REP-easy-lee
  • Phones knob at the Lab 001 mark
  • Timebox: one sitting, 60–120 minutes — tuning a combination of offsets takes more passes than tuning one, and the passes are the lesson
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to two or three minutes of “Easy Lee” and choose the 8–16 bars where the percussion’s roll is most exposed — that section is what you will replicate. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Check each answer against the reveal only after you have written your own.

  1. The rolling feeling — where does it live? In one sound, or between sounds?
    Answer

    Between sounds. Solo any single voice in your head and it is just a steady part; the rotation only appears when the voices play together, because it is made of the fixed timing gaps between them. That is the lesson’s whole claim: the groove is a property of the combination, not of any member. Relisten twice — once following one voice, once letting the whole field blur — and notice the roll only exists in the second listening.

  2. Pick one percussion voice and follow only it for a minute. Is it consistent?
    Answer

    Yes — eerily so. Whichever voice you picked repeats its placement exactly, minute after minute; nothing in the percussion is wandering. The ensemble rolls while every part stands still, which is only possible if each part carries its own fixed offset. Relisten with a different voice and confirm the same thing: consistent part, rolling whole.

  3. Does the frame — kick, bass — move at all?
    Answer

    No. The kick holds minimal-techno time for minutes without a flicker, and the bass holds with it — that steadiness is what gives the small offsets something to be measured against. If the frame moved too, the offsets would smear into mush instead of rotating. Tap quarters through the section: your foot locks to the kick and never needs to correct.

  4. Why does it roll instead of sounding like a timing mess?
    Answer

    Because every offset is tiny, fixed, and repeated — the ear learns the pattern of gaps within a few bars and starts riding it, the way it rides swing. A mess is offsets that change; a roll is offsets that hold. The amounts also stay small, milliseconds-scale, so no voice ever reads as a mistake on its own. Relisten and feel how quickly the rotation becomes predictable — by bar four your body already knows the route.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: three or four percussion voices, each with its own fixed micro-offset, rolling together over a dead-steady frame — like the record’s section.
  • Each voice on its own sounds straight and consistent; only the combination rolls.
  • You can write the groove down as a table: voice, direction, amount.
Phase 1 · ~25 min

Transcribe and program the voices — dead straight

First the control condition: the record’s percussion patterns with every trig exactly on the grid. The straight version is the zero column of the offsets table you will build — without it you cannot hear, or measure, what the offsets add.

Do this
  1. Drop “Easy Lee” into Live on a track named REF and loop the 8–16 bars you chose. Gain-match it with a Utility, set Live’s tempo to the record’s by tapping, and keep REF one solo away all sitting.
  2. Transcribe the frame first: their kick pattern on the Digitakt, dead on the grid.
  3. Transcribe 3–4 percussion voices — shaker, rim, conga, or whatever your section carries — onto their own tracks, closest sounds you have, every trig on the grid.
  4. Loop against REF and correct voice by voice until the patterns are theirs. No swing, no micro-timing, anywhere — confirm every track reads 50% swing and every trig sits at zero.

Expected result

The record’s percussion, machine-straight in your sounds. Against REF it is recognizably the same parts — but flat where theirs rotates. The rotation is missing because the offsets are missing; that is the gap the next phase closes.

Phase 2 · ~30 min

Give each voice its own clock

Now the mechanism: one small fixed offset per voice. Start with a deliberate spread — one voice early, one late, one on the line — and keep each voice internally identical: every trig of a voice carries that voice’s number, no exceptions. The kick gets nothing; it is the clock the other clocks are measured against.

Kick · 0 + Shaker · a touch early + Rim · a touch late + Conga · on the line = one rolling feel
Do this
  1. On the Digitakt, hold each shaker trig and tap the left arrow the same number of clicks — two or three to start. Every shaker trig, the same number.
  2. Hold each rim trig and tap the right arrow the same number of clicks — late, by its own amount, every rim trig identical.
  3. Leave the conga (or your third voice) at zero for now — one voice on the line keeps the ensemble anchored.
  4. Loop and listen for a couple of minutes before judging. Micro-offsets need a few bars to register as a feel rather than a flaw.
Expected result

The flatness breaks: the percussion field starts to circulate, even at this first guess. It will not yet roll like the record — the directions are probably right, the amounts probably wrong. That is what Phase 3 tunes.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A voice now sounds like a flam against the kickIts offset is too big — it reads as a wrong note, not a feelHalve that voice’s clicks; micro-swing lives below the threshold where a hit sounds displaced
The roll stutters once per barOne trig in a voice missed its clicks — internal inconsistencyHold every trig of that voice and read its value; set the strays to the voice’s number
Phase 3 · ~25 min

Tune the combination against the record

Now the tuning passes: A/B against the record and adjust one voice at a time, one click at a time, until your ensemble rotates the way theirs does. You are tuning a combination, so change only one number between listens — otherwise you cannot know which change did what.

Do this
  1. Loop yours against REF, a few bars each, back and forth. Ask one question per pass: is the rotation too wide (offsets too big), too subtle (too small), or circling the wrong way (a direction wrong)?
  2. Adjust a single voice by one click and listen again. Resist touching two voices between listens, however sure you feel.
  3. Try moving your third voice off zero and back — hear what anchoring costs and what it buys. Keep whichever the record agrees with.
  4. Stop when the A/B stops settling — when yours and theirs roll at the same width and the same way. Then play yours alone for a minute and confirm the roll survives without the reference.
Expected result

A combination of three or four small numbers at which the loop rolls like the section — and a felt sense of how sensitive the system is: one click on one voice audibly changes the whole field’s rotation.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Every adjustment seems to make it worseChanging several voices between listens — no stable baselineZero everything back to the Phase 2 spread and restart the tuning, one voice, one click, one listen
It rolls, but not like the recordRight mechanism, wrong assignments — maybe their shaker is the late oneSwap the directions between two voices and re-tune; direction is a hypothesis, test it
Phase 4 · ~15 min

Observe — write the groove as a table

Last, the groove becomes a document. You print the loop, verify each voice’s offset with the ms ruler, and write the offsets table. That table is the groove — the rolling feel you tuned by ear, stated as engineering, reproducible on any machine that can read it.

Digitakt II · per-voice offsets → main outs → M4 in 3/4 → Live record · Ext. In 3/4 · Monitor Off
Do this
  1. Restart the transport — the Lab 002 reset rule — then record 2 minutes of the tuned loop into Live (Ext. In 3/4, Monitor Off).
  2. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Set the ruler to minutes:seconds:milliseconds, as in Lab 002, and zoom to one hit of each voice. Measure its distance from the grid line — early as minus, late as plus.
  3. Check the same voice in a distant bar: the number must repeat. Consistent part, rolling whole — now visible.
  4. Fill in the offsets table below and copy it into the logbook, clicks and measured ms both.

Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the REF section, then answer:

  • Pick one voice in your loop and follow only it for a minute, then do the same with theirs. Is each voice consistent while its ensemble rolls?
    Answer

    Both should pass the same test: any single voice, theirs or yours, sounds like a metronome with a personality — fixed placement, no wander — while the combined field rotates. If one of your voices wanders, the table is not yet true on the machine: some trig disagrees with its voice’s number. Fix the stray, not the feel, and relisten following that one voice until it bores you — boring parts, rolling whole is exactly the spec.

  • 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — does the rotation circle the same way, at the same width? Would a friend hesitate?
    Answer

    Same way and same width is the honest target — a roll can match in size but circle differently if your early/late assignments are swapped relative to theirs. If the friend would not hesitate, listen once more for which voice leads the rotation in each version; the leader is usually the earliest voice, and swapping two directions is a one-minute experiment. Log whether your miss was width (amounts) or direction (assignments) — they are different fixes, and naming the right one is the skill.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • What carries into the lab: a groove can be designed per voice and written down — the offsets table is a patch sheet for time.
