Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: micro-swing

Copy one clear section of Ricardo Villalobos — “Easy Lee”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a different fixed micro-timing offset per voice, over a steady frame. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordRicardo Villalobos — “Easy Lee”
The effectthe percussion seems to rotate — many small clocks, one rolling feel

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Digitakt II main outs → M4 inputs 3/4, clock-slaved to Live as in the previous lessons
  • New set saved as REP-easy-lee
  • Phones knob at the Lab 001 mark
  • What you will make: a short replica of Ricardo Villalobos — “Easy Lee”. Focus on this effect: the percussion seems to rotate - many small clocks, one rolling feel.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a different fixed micro-timing offset per voice, over a steady frame.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Ricardo Villalobos — “Easy Lee”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. The rolling feeling — where does it live? In one sound, or between sounds?
    Answer

    Between sounds. Solo any single voice in your head and it is just a steady part; the rotation only appears when the voices play together, because it is made of the fixed timing gaps between them.

  2. Pick one percussion voice and follow only it for a minute. Is it consistent?
    Answer

    Yes - eerily so. Whichever voice you picked repeats its placement exactly, minute after minute; nothing in the percussion is wandering.

  3. Does the frame — kick, bass — move at all?
    Answer

    No. The kick holds minimal-techno time for minutes without a flicker, and the bass holds with it - that steadiness is what gives the small offsets something to be measured against.

  4. Why does it roll instead of sounding like a timing mess?
    Answer

    Because every offset is tiny, fixed, and repeated - the ear learns the pattern of gaps within a few bars and starts riding it, the way it rides swing. A mess is offsets that change; a roll is offsets that hold.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: three or four percussion voices, each with its own fixed micro-offset, rolling together over a dead-steady frame — like the record’s section.
  • Each voice on its own sounds straight and consistent; only the combination rolls.
  • You can write the groove down as a table: voice, direction, amount.
Phase 1 · ~25 min

Transcribe and program the voices — dead straight

Phase focus: transcribe and program the voices — dead straight. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Drop “Easy Lee” into Live on a track named REF and loop the 8–16 bars you chose.
  2. Live Gain-match it with a Utility, set Live’s tempo to the record’s by tapping, and keep REF one solo away all sitting.
  3. Digitakt II Transcribe the frame first: their kick pattern on the Digitakt II, dead on the grid.
  4. Digitakt II Transcribe 3–4 percussion voices — shaker, rim, conga, or whatever your section carries — onto their own tracks, closest sounds you have, every trig on the grid.
  5. Digitakt II + Live Loop against REF and correct voice by voice until the patterns are theirs.
  6. Digitakt II No swing, no micro-timing, anywhere — confirm every track reads 50% swing and every trig sits at zero.

Expected result

The record’s percussion, machine-straight in your sounds. Against REF it is recognizably the same parts — but flat where theirs rotates. The rotation is missing because the offsets are missing; that is the gap the next phase closes.

Phase 2 · ~30 min

Give each voice its own clock

Phase focus: give each voice its own clock. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Kick · 0 + Shaker · a touch early + Rim · a touch late + Conga · on the line = one rolling feel
Do this
  1. Digitakt II In grid recording, hold each shaker [TRIG] key and press [LEFT] the same number of clicks — two or three to start.
  2. Digitakt II Every shaker trig, the same number.
  3. Digitakt II Hold each rim trig and tap the right arrow the same number of clicks — late, by its own amount, every rim trig identical.
  4. Digitakt II Leave the conga (or your third voice) at zero for now — one voice on the line keeps the ensemble anchored.
  5. Digitakt II Loop and listen for a couple of minutes before judging.
  6. Digitakt II Micro-offsets need a few bars to register as a feel rather than a flaw.
Expected result

The flatness breaks: the percussion field starts to circulate, even at this first guess. It will not yet roll like the record — the directions are probably right, the amounts probably wrong. That is what Phase 3 tunes.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A voice now sounds like a flam against the kickIts offset is too big — it reads as a wrong note, not a feelHalve that voice’s clicks; micro-swing lives below the threshold where a hit sounds displaced
The roll stutters once per barOne trig in a voice missed its clicks — internal inconsistencyHold every trig of that voice and read its value; set the strays to the voice’s number
Phase 3 · ~25 min

Tune the combination against the record

Phase focus: tune the combination against the record. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Loop yours against REF, a few bars each, back and forth.
  2. Live Ask one question per pass: is the rotation too wide (offsets too big), too subtle (too small), or circling the wrong way (a direction wrong)?
  3. Live Adjust a single voice by one click and listen again.
  4. Live Resist touching two voices between listens, however sure you feel.
  5. Live Try moving your third voice off zero and back — hear what anchoring costs and what it buys.
  6. Live Keep whichever the record agrees with.
  7. Live Stop when the A/B stops settling — when yours and theirs roll at the same width and the same way.
  8. Live Then play yours alone for a minute and confirm the roll survives without the reference.
Expected result

A combination of three or four small numbers at which the loop rolls like the section — and a felt sense of how sensitive the system is: one click on one voice audibly changes the whole field’s rotation.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Every adjustment seems to make it worseChanging several voices between listens — no stable baselineZero everything back to the Phase 2 spread and restart the tuning, one voice, one click, one listen
It rolls, but not like the recordRight mechanism, wrong assignments — maybe their shaker is the late oneSwap the directions between two voices and re-tune; direction is a hypothesis, test it
Phase 4 · ~15 min

Observe — write the groove as a table

Phase focus: observe — write the groove as a table. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Digitakt II · per-voice offsets → main outs → M4 in 3/4 → Live record · Ext. In 3/4 · Monitor Off
Do this
  1. Live Restart the transport — the Lab 002 reset rule — then record 2 minutes of the tuned loop into Live (Ext. In 3/4, Monitor Off).
  2. Live Observe — see it, not just hear it. Set the ruler to minutes:seconds:milliseconds, as in Lab 002, and zoom to one hit of each voice.
  3. Live Measure its distance from the grid line — early as minus, late as plus.
  4. Live Check the same voice in a distant bar: the number must repeat.
  5. Live Consistent part, rolling whole — now visible.
  6. Logbook Fill in the offsets table below and copy it into the logbook, clicks and measured ms both.

Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the REF section, then answer:

  • Pick one voice in your loop and follow only it for a minute, then do the same with theirs. Is each voice consistent while its ensemble rolls?
    Answer

    Both should pass the same test: any single voice, theirs or yours, sounds like a metronome with a personality — fixed placement, no wander — while the combined field rotates. If one of your voices wanders, the table is not yet true on the machine: some trig disagrees with its voice’s number. Fix the stray, not the feel, and relisten following that one voice until it bores you — boring parts, rolling whole is exactly the spec.

  • 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — does the rotation circle the same way, at the same width? Would a friend hesitate?
    Answer

    Same way and same width is the honest target — a roll can match in size but circle differently if your early/late assignments are swapped relative to theirs. If the friend would not hesitate, listen once more for which voice leads the rotation in each version; the leader is usually the earliest voice, and swapping two directions is a one-minute experiment. Log whether your miss was width (amounts) or direction (assignments) — they are different fixes, and naming the right one is the skill.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • What carries into the lab: a groove can be designed per voice and written down — the offsets table is a patch sheet for time.
