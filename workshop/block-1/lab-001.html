Signal Path & Gain Staging

Between your synth and your headphones, the volume of a sound gets changed in about ten different places. This lab shows you where every one of them is, what to set it to, and how to tell — by ear and by meter — when one of them is wrong.

The point

If you know where every volume control in your studio is and what it is set to, you can record anything cleanly and find any problem fast. If you don’t, every problem looks the same: “it sounds bad somewhere.”

How this lab works

Press one key on the synth and count how many times that note’s volume can be changed before it reaches your ears: the synth’s own volume knob, the gain knob on the audio interface, the recording level in Live, the clip’s own gain, every effect on the track, the track fader, maybe a group fader, the master fader, and finally the headphone knob. That is eight to ten volume controls, for every sound, every time. Each one was either set on purpose or is sitting wherever it was last left — and a forgotten knob does not stay neutral.

This lab moves through that chain in three passes:

Know the path

  • Plug in every cable, into a named jack (step 01)
  • Tell Live which box carries sound in and out (step 02)
  • Get first sound, and draw where it traveled — marking every point where the volume can change (steps 01–03)

Set the levels

  • Agree one target volume and write it down (step 04)
  • Set every control in the chain to its agreed value, one ledger row at a time (step 05)
  • Prove it: record the same phrase too quiet, on target, and too loud, and hear the difference (step 06)

Break it on purpose

  • Overload the sound in four different places, one at a time, and learn each one’s fingerprint (step 07)
  • Correct your drawing against the real cables (step 08)
  • Hide one fault from your future self, then find it cold (fault ritual)

Everything in this lab is provided: the wiring, the target levels, the full procedure. Your job is to check the provided answers against your actual studio. The drawing you make in step 01 will be wrong somewhere — finding where is the lesson.

What this lab certifies

By the end you can: draw your whole studio path, point at every volume control in it, record the same phrase at three volumes and explain which one you would keep, tell four kinds of overload apart by ear and by meter, and say exactly what you are listening through.

You will produce six things. They are named now so nothing gets invented at the end:

  • Studio Signal Map v1 — audio path and control path, drawn by hand, corrected against reality
  • Gain ledger — one row per volume control, with its agreed setting
  • Three-level recording — the same phrase too quiet, on target, and too hot, with one level defended
  • Clipping diagnosis table — four places, filled in from your own ears and meters
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the path worked like X; it worked like Y”

New concepts — in plain words

Five terms come up constantly in this lab and the rest of the workshop. Here is what they mean, in everyday words. Come back to this table whenever a step uses one of them.

ConceptIn plain words
Gain stage Any point where a signal’s volume can be changed: a knob, a fader, a slider. Your bench has about ten of them. “Gain” is just the audio word for “how much this point turns the volume up or down.”
Nominal level The volume you agree to aim for at every stage — decided once and written down. Like a cruising speed on a road trip: the exact number matters less than everyone agreeing on it, because then “too fast” and “too slow” actually mean something.
Unity gain The “change nothing” setting: the signal leaves at the same volume it arrived. A fader at 0 dB is at unity. Start everything at unity, and whenever something is louder or quieter you know a person decided it.
Converter clipping vs float overs The converter is the chip that turns electricity into numbers. Feed it a signal that is too loud and it flattens the top of the wave permanently — like scissors cutting paper. Inside Live, “too loud” is just a big number: pull a fader down and the sound is perfectly restored, like a calculator. Same red light on the meter; completely different damage.
Monitoring discipline Keeping your listening volume fixed at one marked position. Louder always sounds better, so if your listening level drifts up during a session, your judgment drifts with it.
nominal level unity gain gain stage converter clipping vs float overs monitoring discipline

Listening — ten records about volume

Before touching any gear, listen to ten records. Each one makes a different decision about volume — quiet on purpose, overloaded on purpose, noise on purpose, clipped on purpose — and each decision is something you can hear.

01 Rhythm & Sound — “Mango Drive” depth from levels alone

Nothing in it is loud, but it feels heavy and deep. All of that weight comes from how quiet everything is compared to everything else.

Listen for
  • How far below the chord the hi-hat sits — it is barely there, on purpose.
  • How much empty space surrounds the bass.
  • That nothing ever jumps out at you, start to finish.
After — verify

Name the loudest element. You will struggle, because there barely is one — and that is the point. Did you want to turn it up? Good: the depth comes from restraint, and turning it up would erase it. If you couldn’t name anything, listen again and focus only on the gaps between the chord stabs — what fills them, and how quietly.

02 Gesaffelstein — “Pursuit” overdrive on purpose

This track makes the opposite choice from “Mango Drive” — the distortion is the actual sound. Everything is driven hard on purpose.

Listen for
  • Which elements are distorted — the lead, the bass — and which are left clean.
  • Anything undistorted quietly holding the track together underneath the heavy sound.
  • Whether the distortion is constant, or changes as the track moves.
After — verify

Could you tell “driven on purpose” from “accidentally too loud”? Purposeful drive stays consistent; an accident wanders. Then name one clean element you heard. If you can’t, relisten to the first minute and follow a single undistorted sound all the way through.

03 Pole — “Tanzen 2” (from 1) noise floor as lead

Stefan Betke built it around what most people would call a mistake — the crackle of a broken filter. He kept everything else quiet enough that this background noise becomes the lead instrument.

Listen for
  • The crackle as a rhythm, not a flaw — it keeps time.
  • How quiet and sparse everything else is, so the crackle stays on top.
After — verify

Can you tell Pole’s intended crackle from any hiss your own speakers or headphones add? If unsure, pause the track. What disappears was the music; what stays is your system’s own noise. That difference is exactly what step 06’s quiet take teaches.

04 SOPHIE — “Faceshopping” clipping as sound design

The clipping tour in this lab teaches you to avoid the red light. SOPHIE drives straight through it. Here, clipping (when a sound is too loud and the top gets cut off, adding a harsh edge) is pushed so far that it becomes a deliberate texture — metallic, plastic, percussive sounds whose whole character is the distortion.

Listen for
  • Which sounds are the distortion — not distorted versions of something else, but sounds with no clean original underneath.
  • The textures the clipping produces: metal sheet, plastic snap, rubber — sound design, not damage.
  • How precisely each clipped hit starts and stops — nothing here is an accident drifting through the mix.
After — verify

Pick one harsh sound and try to imagine its clean version. If you can’t — if removing the distortion would remove the sound — you have found clipping used as sound design rather than as a fault. Can’t hear it? Relisten to the chorus and follow a single metallic hit each time it returns. Same edge, same placement, every time. That repeatability is the sign of a deliberate choice.

05 Theo Parrish — “Falling Up” the pushed mix

Instead of pushing one element hard, Parrish pushes the whole mix — gently, everywhere at once. Every sound grits together as if they all came from the same room. Nothing is clean, and that is the decision.

Listen for
  • The shared texture: kick, chords, and hats all coated the same way, instead of sitting in separate clean spaces.
  • How the gentle overload makes the rhythm feel heavier without anything getting louder.
  • Any element that sounds clean. You will not find one — the drive is on the full mix, not on one target.
After — verify

Compare this to “Pursuit” in your head. There, one element snarls over a clean floor. Here, everything wears the same grit. Can you name where the drive lives — on a single sound, or on the whole mix? If unsure, listen to the chords alone, then the hats alone. The same warm fuzz coats both, which tells you the distortion is placed after all the sounds were combined.

06 DJ Sprinkles — “Grand Central, Pt. I” quiet as depth

This takes “Mango Drive” even further: the loudest element is soft, almost conversational. The depth of the track comes from leaving space where most records pile in more sound.

Listen for
  • How complete the track feels while peaking low — nothing is missing, yet nothing is pushed.
  • The space between elements: each one surrounded by air, none competing for the top of the meter.
  • How quiet the loudest moment is — soft, not climactic.
After — verify

Play it quietly — actually quiet, phones below the mark. The detail and depth should come forward instead of vanishing, because the balances were built for that kind of headroom. If it thinned out instead, your listening level was doing the mix’s work. Relisten at the lower volume and notice which elements seem to step toward you.

07 Sunn O))) — “It Took the Night to Believe” saturation as mass

This feels massive, but not because it is loud. A held chord is pushed through heavy amplifier saturation (gentle overload that adds warmth and grit, and piles up extra frequencies called harmonics). Those harmonics fill the whole sound range. The size comes from drive and time, not from turning up the volume.

Listen for
  • One chord filling the whole frequency range, top to bottom — the harmonics are doing the work a full arrangement usually does.
  • How slowly anything changes — the sustain itself is the event.
  • Where the sense of “huge” comes from: the density of those harmonics, not the meter level.
After — verify

Count how many notes are actually being played. The honest answer is very few — one chord, maybe one note, held. Everything else you hear is saturation multiplying it across the spectrum. If the wall still sounds like “many instruments,” relisten to one minute and track the moment a note changes. The long gaps between changes show how little is actually being played.

08 Eleh — “Floating Frequencies / Intuitive Synthesis” level as composition

Two or three pure low tones — and the only thing that ever happens is their volume relationship slowly shifting. The volume balance is the whole composition. There are no other events.

Listen for
  • How many tones are sounding — you will overcount. Pure tones blend into one sound when their balance is even, then seem to separate again as it shifts.
  • How long one change takes: a rise begins before you notice it and finishes after you stopped waiting for it.
  • That nothing ever changes pitch or timbre — every “event” is just one tone moving in front of another.
After — verify

Try to name a single event that is not a volume change. You will fail, and that is the answer: which tone is in front is the entire content. Can’t hear it as events at all? Relisten to three minutes and write a tone count at three different moments. The counts will disagree with each other — and that disagreement is the balance moving.

09 Thomas Köner — “Permafrost” music your monitoring decides

Almost everything in this piece sits below ≈ 200 Hz — the lowest part of the hearing range, felt as much as heard. On a playback system that can reproduce it, it feels vast and physical. On a laptop speaker, it is near-silence. The monitoring chain (your interface, headphones, or speakers) decides whether the music exists — not the file.

Listen for
  • The highest-pitched sound you can find — almost nothing above the low mids; the bass register is the whole track.
  • How many distinct events happen in three minutes: almost none — only slow shifts, one layer of rumble leaning into another.
  • Where the music registers first — in your chest and skull as pressure, before the ear files it as a sound.
After — verify

Play one minute on the DT 990s at the mark, then the same minute on your laptop speakers. What survived? Maybe a thin hiss, maybe nothing — yet the meter would still show a healthy signal, because it reads the file, not the air coming out of the speakers. If both listenings sounded similar, your headphone path is wrong or far below the mark. Relisten on the headphones first and check the lab wiring. That gap between the two listenings is the monitoring row of the gain ledger, made audible.

10 Emptyset — “Demiurge” the path is the instrument

There's almost nothing here: a low pulse and a plain sine tone. All the weight and grit come from how hard that signal is pushed through each stage of the chain. Saturation and limiting are doing the work an arrangement usually does.

Listen for
  • How little source material sits under all that weight — imagine stripping out the distortion in your head and almost nothing remains.
  • Sections changing by adding more drive, not new parts: the pattern barely moves, but the pressure on the chain increases.
  • That the biggest-feeling moments barely get louder — the output level is pinned, and what grows is density, not volume.
After — verify

Pick the densest passage and ask what was added since the barest one. The honest answer is nothing — the same skeleton, pushed harder into the same stages. If it still sounds like new instruments arriving, relisten across one section change and track the pulse pattern through it. Same hits, thicker coat. That coat is the signal path being pushed harder.

Procedure

Step 01

Patch the bench

Goal: every cable in its named jack, exactly as the drawing above shows. Everything that happens later depends on you knowing where each cable goes.

Do this
  1. Plug the ¼″ TS cable into the MicroFreak’s rear Audio Out, the other end into the M4’s front Input 1 combo jack.
  2. Plug one ¼″ TRS cable from Digitakt II Main Out L into the M4’s rear Input 3; the second TRS from Main Out R into Input 4.
  3. Plug the DT 990 Pro into the M4’s front Phones jack. Confirm the phones knob is still near minimum.
  4. Connect the M4 to the computer with USB-C — a direct port, not a hub.
  5. Connect MicroFreak, Digitakt II, and Launchpad to the computer by USB.
Expected result

Nothing — no sound, no meters. Nothing is configured yet, so silence is correct. If the bench is already making noise at this point, that is worth investigating.

Logbook

Start Studio Signal Map v1 now, by hand, based on what you just plugged. Draw the audio path and the control path as two separate drawings. Label every box and arrow. Mark every point where volume can change with a small g — those marks become the rows of the gain ledger in step 05. Date the drawings.

Step 02

Configure Live

Goal: tell Live that the M4 handles all sound coming in and going out. One box in charge of audio, with the right inputs and outputs turned on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ , → Audio tab.
  2. Audio Input Device: MOTU M4. Audio Output Device: MOTU M4.
  3. Sample Rate: 48000. Buffer Size: 256.
  4. Click Input Config: enable mono inputs 1 and 2, and the stereo pair 3/4.
  5. Click Output Config: enable stereo 1/2, plus mono outputs 3 and 4 — lab 002 drives output 3 and you do not want to reopen preferences mid-measurement.
Expected result

MOTU M4 listed in both device menus, and the Input Config dialog showing 1, 2, and 3/4 lit. The Latency section now shows a round-trip figure — note it once; lab 002 will check whether it is accurate.

Step 03

Create the test tracks and confirm signal

Goal: first sound. Both instruments should move Live’s meters and be audible in the headphones, through one listening path only.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T twice for two audio tracks; rename with ⌘ R: MF TEST and DT TEST.
  2. ⌘ ⌥ I to show the In/Out section. MF TEST: Ext. In, channel 1. DT TEST: Ext. In, channel 3/4.
  3. Set Monitor to In on both tracks. Faders at 0 dB, master at 0 dB.
  4. Turn the M4’s monitor blend knob fully to computer playback — lab 002 explains why; for now the rule is one listening path only.
  5. Raise the M4 phones knob slightly from minimum.
  6. On the MicroFreak, choose any preset that makes sound from the keyboard. Hold one middle-register key; if the preset is a short pluck, hold repeated notes instead. Rear master Volume ≈ 75 %.
  7. On the Digitakt II, use any project or pattern that already contains a sample. Press play, or manually trigger any track that makes sound. Main Volume ≈ 12 o’clock.
If first sound stalls
  1. MicroFreak: turn its rear volume up, confirm the TS cable is in rear Audio Out, then watch M4 input 1 while holding a key. If the M4 meter moves but Live does not, the problem is in Live’s input routing. If the M4 meter does not move, the problem is before Live.
  2. Digitakt II: do not program a beat for this lab. Any audible sample is enough. If no project makes sound, load any sample onto one track, trigger it manually, then watch M4 inputs 3/4. If only one side moves, fix the L/R cabling before continuing.
  3. Headphones: if Live meters move but you hear nothing, the sound is already in the computer. Check M4 output device, M4 phones knob, and whether you are monitoring through exactly one path.
Expected result

The MF TEST meter moves while the note is held. The DT TEST meter moves on the pads. You hear both in the headphones. The M4’s front display shows the same activity on its input meters — both meters agreeing is your first proof the path works.

Step 04

Define nominal and mark the phones knob

Goal: pick a target volume — the nominal level (the agreed volume you aim for and write down) — so “too quiet” and “too hot” mean something for the rest of the workshop. One unit first: Live’s meters read in dBFS (decibels full scale), where 0 is the absolute maximum the system can record. So −12 dBFS means twelve steps below the top.

Do this
  1. Write the nominal definition into the logbook: tracking peaks ≈ −12 dBFS on Live’s input meter, never above −6 dBFS. The space left above is not wasted — it is headroom for saturation, reverb build-up, and the master bus later.
  2. Set unity gain (the "change nothing" setting, 0 dB) everywhere downstream: all track faders 0 dB, all clip gain 0 dB, master fader 0 dB. If a recording is quiet at these settings, the fix is upstream — at the source or at the preamp (the small amplifier inside the interface, controlled by its input gain knob) — never at the fader.
  3. Play nominal-level material, set the M4 phones knob to a comfortable level, then mark the knob position physically — tape, paint pen, a scratch.
Expected result

A visible mark on the phones knob and a nominal sentence in the logbook. The DT 990 Pro is a 250 Ω open-back headphone that makes it tempting to turn up “just a little” throughout a session — because louder always sounds better. The mark is your defense. Every session starts at the mark. This is monitoring discipline (keeping your listening volume fixed at one position), made physical.

Decision checkpoint

Can you state your nominal level and point at the mark on the phones knob? If either answer is a shrug, this step is not finished.

Logbook

“Nominal: peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, ceiling −6. Phones knob marked, dated.”

Step 06

Record the three levels

Goal: hear what level decisions cost. Record the same 8-bar MicroFreak phrase three times — too quiet, on target, too hot. Only the volume settings change; the music stays the same. Choose something repeatable.

Do this
  1. Press Tab until you are in Arrangement View. You want three clips on a timeline, not three Session slots.
  2. Arm MF TEST. Set Monitor to In if you need to hear through Live, or Off if you are hearing through the M4’s direct monitor path. Use one path only.
  3. Make the phrase repeatable: play one simple 8-bar line by hand, or hold one note and move one MicroFreak control slowly. The phrase can be boring; consistency is the point.
  4. Set the M4’s input-1 gain knob down until Live’s meter peaks ≈ −40 dBFS. Click Arrangement Record, play the 8 bars, stop. Select the recorded clip and rename it (⌘ R) MF -40.
  5. Move the cursor later on the timeline. Raise the M4 input-1 gain until peaks sit ≈ −12 dBFS — exactly as the ledger prescribes. Record the same 8 bars; rename the clip MF -12.
  6. Move the cursor later again. Raise the gain until the M4’s red clip LED fires on peaks. Record anyway; rename the clip MF HOT. Then immediately return the input gain to the ledger value.
  7. Double-click MF -40 to open Clip View. Raise its Gain until it is roughly as loud as MF -12. Do not use the track fader for this comparison; matched clip loudness lets you hear the cost of recording too quiet.
  8. Loop one bar of each clip and A/B all three at matched loudness on the DT 990s — phones at the mark. Fill the three-level recording table before moving on.
Expected result

The quiet take plays back with the phrase — and the background hiss has turned up with it. That hiss was always there. Your gain setting decided whether you recorded the music far above it or right next to it. The hot take has a crackle and harshness on peaks that no fader, plugin, or normalization can remove. That is converter clipping — it is baked into the file permanently.

Logbook

Keep all three takes. Defend exactly one in writing. After the listening section, “too hot, on purpose, for this sound” is a valid answer. “I didn’t check” never is.

One more take — the held tone

Record a fourth take: thirty seconds of one sustained MicroFreak note (slow attack, full sustain, gain set while the note holds), to the same −12 dBFS peak target as MF -12. Play both at the marked phones level. The held tone will feel much louder and will tire your ear much faster — even at the same peak reading. A kick drum is mostly silence between peaks; a drone is almost none. The ratio of peak level to average level is called crest factor. The ledger's peak numbers assume spiky material, like drums. A sustained drone behaves differently. Note the difference in the logbook now; étude 004b turns it into a formal ledger amendment for sustained sources.

Step 07

The clipping tour

Goal: overload the sound on purpose in four different places, one at a time. For each place, learn two things — what it sounds like, and which meter catches it. (“Locus” just means “place in the chain.”) This builds the diagnosis table in the reference section from your own experience, not from reading.

Locus 1 — interface input
  • Do this: drive the M4 input-1 gain into its red clip LED with a loud MicroFreak note. Record a few seconds, then reset the preamp to nominal.
  • Expected: hard, brittle crackle on peaks, permanent in the file. The M4’s input meter goes red while Live’s meter can still read under −6 — the damage happened upstream of the meter you usually watch.
Locus 2 — track, into a device
  • Do this: on the MF -12 clip, push clip gain up +12 dB into a Saturator at default settings.
  • Expected: the device, not the mixer, falls apart — choked, harsh, pumping. The track meter reads “over” yet the mix bus is clean; the device’s own input display tells the truth.
Locus 3 — bus
  • Do this: duplicate the hot track three or four times (⌘ D), group them (⌘ G), and put a device on the group.
  • Expected: intermittent crunch only when peaks coincide. Every member track meters healthy; only the group meter goes over — the sum is the problem, not any part.
Locus 4 — master
  • Do this: remove the group device, then raise the master fader until the master meter goes over while everything plays at once.
  • Expected: grain over the whole mix. The master meter is the one place where “over” is always real once audio leaves Live — it prints into every export.

The key difference: input clipping happens before the converter (the chip that turns the electrical signal into numbers). The wave arrived already flattened — like paper already cut. That damage is permanent. Inside Live, sound is just numbers with a very large range (technically called 32-bit float), so an “over” on a track meter is just big arithmetic — pull the fader down and the sound is perfectly restored. But effects and plugins designed around a target level will still misbehave when you feed them +12 dB, and that misbehavior gets printed into your export. Same word, “clipping” — four different events in four different places.

Logbook

Fill the clipping diagnosis table in the reference section — symptom and meter tell per locus, in your own words.

Step 08

Correct the map

Goal: make the drawing agree with the actual cables. Put the gap between what you expected and what you found on paper.

Do this
  1. Go behind the desk and walk the real cabling against the step 01 drawings, connector by connector.
  2. Mark every discrepancy in a second color — a swapped input pair, a forgotten gain stage, a control path you drew as audio. Do not redraw a clean copy; the corrections must stay visible, because the diff is the lesson.
Expected result

At least one correction. A map that needed none means either an unusually careful step 01, or a walk that was not careful enough. Walk it again.

Decision checkpoint

For any audible distortion, can you say where it lives — before the converter, in a device, at a sum, or at the output? If any of the four places still sounds like the others to you, repeat its leg of the tour until it has a distinct fingerprint.

Logbook

The system diff sentence, written under the map: “I thought the path worked like X; it worked like Y.”

Fault ritual

Clip at the wrong stage, then find it

predict → create → observe → diagnose → fix → write the rule
  • Predict: roll a die to pick one of the four loci (re-roll 5–6). Before creating anything, write what you expect to hear and which meter you expect to catch it.
  • Create: make the clip subtly — for example +6 dB of clip gain on one element, not +12 on everything. Then walk away from the studio.
  • Observe & diagnose: return later, cold. Play the session and locate the fault using meters only — no soloing by ear first, no undo history. Name the locus before you touch a control.
  • Fix: repair it at the stage where it lives, not by turning something else down to hide it.
  • Write the rule: file the fault entry in the fault log — symptom, cause, fix, and one prevention rule you will actually follow (e.g. “glance at the gain ledger before every print”).

This is a ritual, not a punishment. You are practicing the skill cheaply now, so an accidental clip later doesn't cost you an hour of confusion in the middle of a piece.

Background — three things the bench assumes

The procedure above assumes three things: that you can trust what you hear, that the recording format has enough range to be accurate, and that the cables themselves are behaving. Read these once; come back when a symptom points here.

Monitoring & room — can you trust what you hear?

Every volume decision in this lab is only as good as the listening path that judges it. Before you can worry about the room, the level and the playback system have to be right.

Calibrate the listening, not just the knob

  • Reference level: pick one listening loudness and mark it (step 04). On studio monitors, the common practice is to calibrate so a −20 dBFS pink-noise test tone reads around 79–83 dB SPL at the listening position. On headphones, your physical mark is the substitute.
  • One level for judging. Check things loud and quiet on purpose, but make every keep/cut decision at the mark — louder always sounds better, which means louder always lies.
  • Same path, every session. A monitoring setup that keeps changing is a ruler that keeps changing length. You can't measure your mixes against it.

The room is part of the instrument

  • First reflections — sound bouncing off the desk, side walls, and ceiling — arrive milliseconds after the direct sound and color it with a hollow, comb-like tone. You'll meet this effect acoustically in lab 002.
  • Bass modes: small rooms have loud spots and dead spots below ≈ 300 Hz that shift depending on where you sit. Basic treatment: absorption at the first-reflection points, bass trapping in the corners, and keeping the setup symmetrical left-to-right.
  • Headphones bypass the room but not ear fatigue — which is why this block uses the DT 990s and treats room treatment as a later upgrade.
  • Hearing health is a gain stage too. Listening fatigue sets in long before pain. After an hour at the mark, your judgment of brightness and level drifts, and you start compensating for tired ears without noticing. Take a real break — several minutes of silence — every 30–45 minutes. Keep the mark conservative. Your ears are the one piece of gear you can't replace. Sustained material (lab 004b) tires them fastest; take breaks more often there.

Recording fundamentals — why 48 kHz / 24-bit

Step 02 set the bench to 48 kHz, and the ledger aims for −12 dBFS peaks. Here is why those two numbers, and what the converter is actually doing.

The recording format, in plain words
Sample rate — 48 kHzHow many times per second the converter measures the audio wave. To capture a frequency accurately, you must sample at least twice as fast, so 48 kHz cleanly covers the full ≈ 20 kHz range of human hearing with room to spare. 48 kHz (not 44.1) is the standard for anything that may be used with video, and it keeps the whole bench running on one clock.
Bit depth — 24-bitHow finely each measurement is graded — the number of steps between silence and full scale. 24-bit gives ≈ 144 dB of range, which is why you can record at a conservative −12 dBFS and still keep the noise floor far below anything audible. 16-bit (the CD format) is fine for a finished file but leaves less headroom while you are working.
A/D and D/AThe converter is two chips. A/D (analog-to-digital) turns the incoming electrical signal into numbers that Live records. D/A (digital-to-analog) turns those numbers back into an electrical signal for your headphones. Every measurement in lab 002 passes through both. Converter clipping happens at the A/D stage, before any number exists to pull back.
Noise floorThe quiet hiss always present under the signal — from the preamp, the converter, and the cable. Recording too quietly (the MF -40 take) does not lower the noise; it just records your music closer to it. The point of the nominal level is to keep the signal far above the noise floor without hitting the ceiling.

Physical layer — the wires are not neutral

The cable table above lists TS and TRS without explaining why the difference matters. Each choice is a small electrical decision that can add hum, lose level, or quietly degrade a signal.

What the cabling is actually doing
Balanced vs unbalancedA balanced (TRS) cable carries the signal twice — one copy is inverted. The receiving end flips one copy back and adds them together. Any hum picked up along the way is identical on both wires, so it cancels out. Unbalanced (TS) has no such trick, so it works fine over short runs but picks up noise on long ones.
Impedance-balanced outputsThe Digitakt II main outs are impedance-balanced — not a true two-copy balanced signal, but wired so a TRS cable still gets the hum-cancelling benefit. That is why TRS is used on those two runs and plain TS on the mono MicroFreak.
Line vs instrument levelTwo very different signal strengths. Line level is what synths and the Digitakt II put out — strong, ready for the M4’s line inputs. Instrument level (from a guitar pickup, for example) is far weaker and needs a preamp or DI box to bring it up. Feed line level into an instrument input and you overload it. Feed instrument level into a line input and it disappears into the noise floor.
DI boxA direct-injection box converts a weak, high-impedance instrument signal into a stronger balanced line signal. It bridges a guitar to a balanced line input. You don’t need one for this bench’s synths, but it’s why a guitar can’t be plugged straight into input 3.
Cable lengthLonger unbalanced runs lose high-frequency detail and collect hum. Keep TS cables short, and use balanced TRS when a run must be long.
Ground loopsA faint hum that appears when two pieces of gear share more than one electrical ground path. Symptom: a steady 50/60 Hz buzz that changes when you touch a chassis. First fixes: use a single power strip for the whole bench, keep audio cables away from power supplies, and prefer balanced connections — the same hum-cancelling property also helps with ground-loop noise.
