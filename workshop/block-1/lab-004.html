One Oscillator, One Filter

One synthesizer voice: one tone generator, one filter, one volume shape, nothing else. Three minutes of music. It sounds impossible until you find how much variety hides inside a single sound.

Étude · S1 · D2 E1 M1 F1 · deep techno · half-cycle — pair with a listening session or a review week

Start here
  • Physically remove extra sources from the bench. The constraint starts by being visible.
  • Save a new set as LAB-004-etude.
  • Choose one oscillator engine and one filter path, then stay with them for the whole piece.
  • Expected time: one focused half-cycle, or two short sessions.
The point

When you can’t add anything new, you have to find the variation inside the sound itself. That skill is worth more than any new gear.

How this lab works

Design one voice

  • Build a tone that works low as bass and high as lead.
  • Find the drums hiding in the same patch.
  • Steps 1–3.

Make it move by itself

  • Give the patch one slow internal motion — no hands.
  • Sequence patterns A and B, one note apart.
  • Steps 4–5.

Record, subtract, finish

  • Record three passes — low, mid, high.
  • Arrange by removing, down to exactly three minutes.
  • Steps 6–8.
Pass standard
Minimum passThree-minute piece exists, made from one synth voice, with one small fault and one system diff.
Solid passThe same voice clearly plays more than one musical role because filter, register, envelope, or modulation changed.
Advanced transferYou can make a second étude from a different single voice without adding tracks to solve the problem.

New concepts — in plain words

This is the first time you design a sound from scratch. Here are six key terms, in plain words.

ConceptIn plain words
OscillatorThe part that makes the raw sound: a buzzing tone before any shaping. The MicroFreak offers many “engines” — different kinds of raw tone. You pick one and stay with it.
FilterA tone control that removes part of the sound, usually the high-frequency brightness. Closing it muffles the tone. Opening it lets more through. “Cutoff” is the frequency point where it starts cutting.
ResonanceThe filter ringing at its own cutoff point. A little adds character; a lot makes it whistle. At the extreme, paired with a very short volume shape, it becomes a click or a thump. That is where this étude’s drums come from.
EnvelopeThe shape of a note over time: how fast it starts (attack), how fast it falls (decay), where it settles (sustain). A piano and an organ playing the same note differ mostly by envelope.
LFOA slow automatic knob-mover. It cycles too slowly to hear as a tone, so you hear it as movement in the sound.
Mod matrixThe MicroFreak’s routing grid. You connect movers (LFO, envelopes) to the knobs they should turn. “Route the LFO to cutoff, small amount” means the LFO gently works the tone control on its own.

Listening first

Ten records, all built from very little. Listen for one thing: where does variation come from when nothing new ever arrives?

A whole career built from very few sounds. Every element does a job nothing else does.

  • Notice how each one does a job nothing else does.
After — verify

Name each element and its job in one word (pulse, answer, tension…). If two elements share a job, listen again — one of them is doing something you missed.

08 Adonis — “No Way Back” one bassline, whole record

One synth-bass riff over a bare drum machine for six minutes. The riff is the melody, the groove, and the hook all at once, made on very cheap gear. There is nothing else — and nothing else is needed.

Where does the hook live? In the riff’s brightest pass — the same notes with the filter opened further. If you remember a lead entering, relisten: it is the bassline in a different filter position.

09 Sleeparchive — “Elephant Island” pattern logic as variation

Minimal techno from what sounds like one tiny blip voice. The sound itself never changes. All the development comes from which steps fire, retrigger, or rest, and which pattern block is active.

10 Surgeon — “Magneze” economy with teeth

One modulated voice rides the whole track through escalating drive. It feels bigger every bar, but nothing is ever added. All the energy comes from the same sound under more pressure.

Listen for

Brief & system

The brief

A three-minute piece from one MicroFreak voice: one oscillator engine, the analog filter, the volume envelope. Everything in the piece — bass, lead, drums if any — comes from this one voice.

The drums are not an exception — they are the test. High resonance plus a very short envelope makes clicks and thumps from the same patch. Allowed in Live: recording, arrangement, and a utility/limiter on the master. Forbidden: the Digitakt, any Live instruments, any effect devices — no delay, no reverb. One take per moment, plus at most one overdub pass. D2 means sequenced: notes come from Live’s sequencer or the Launchpad, not free playing.

Build & report

  1. Pick one engine and commit. You can audition a few, but after step 2 there is no switching. Turn Type to your engine, write its name in your log, and do not touch that knob again.
  2. Design the core tone with the filter half-open. It must work as bass in a low octave and as lead in a high octave. If it only works in one range, keep shaping. Start from the provided patch and adjust only Wave and Timbre until octave 1 reads as bass and octave 3 reads as lead.
  3. Find the percussion inside the voice. Set high resonance and the fastest envelope setting — you get a click or thump. Dial the second table’s settings, then write both settings groups on paper so you can switch quickly.
  4. Sequence an A pattern (16 steps) and a B pattern that differs by exactly one note or one octave. The difference should be felt before it is consciously noticed. Draw A in a one-bar MIDI clip, then ⌘ D to duplicate it, and change exactly one note.
  5. Use the mod matrix for motion. Add one LFO or cycling-envelope routing, set slow. This is where the variation lives — the patch must move on its own without your hands. Turn the Matrix encoder to LFO → Cutoff, press it, and dial a small amount.
  6. Record three passes at different octave and pattern combinations. Each pass is one recording of the same voice. Together they are the raw material for the arrangement. Follow the recipe below exactly.
  7. Arrange to exactly three minutes by subtraction. Start with everything playing and remove clips until the piece breathes. Lay all three passes across the timeline, then delete until every remaining bar earns its place.
  8. Render. Finishing is part of the étude. File → Export Audio/Video, WAV, 24-bit, named LAB-004-etude.
Recipe

The sequencing pass, exactly

Do this
  • MIDI track: MIDI To → MicroFreak, channel 1.
  • Draw the 16-step A pattern in a one-bar clip; ⌘ D the clip, change exactly one note or octave → B pattern.
  • Audio track MF PRINT: Audio From → Ext. In → 1, armed — it records while the MIDI clip plays.
  • Record three passes at different octave/pattern combinations; rename the clips PASS-LOW, PASS-MID, PASS-HIGH with ⌘ R.
Expected result

Three recordings that are clearly the same voice playing three different roles.

If it fails
MIDI notes do nothingtrack output not set to the MicroFreak port — check local keys vs USB
recorded audio is double-speed flutter or silencesample-rate mismatch — re-check 48 kHz

A suggested three-minute shape to start from, not a rule.

0:00–0:30
Percussion pattern alone — the clicks are the intro.
0:30–1:15
Low pass enters; the voice takes the bass role.
1:15–2:00
Mid/high pass over it; the B pattern appears.
2:00–2:40
Subtraction begins — remove one pass at a time.
2:40–3:00
The first sound alone again, then out.
  • Prescribed fault: record one extra pass with the M4 preamp deliberately too hot, pushing into clipping (where the signal is too loud and the top gets cut off, adding a harsh edge). Compare that distortion against the sound of the MicroFreak’s own filter resonance driven hard. Write one rule that tells them apart — preamp clipping is fixed and sits at the input; filter drive is musical and changes with the patch. No full write-up; the rule is the deliverable.

Do not file the full track-lab bundle. Keep it simple. Timebox the whole étude to a handful of sessions. An étude that balloons defeats its own lesson.

  • Coda — same voice, no clock: this étude assumed a sequencer underneath everything. Étude 004b repeats the exercise on the same patch with the grid removed. Rhythm comes from beating between two detuned tones; structure comes from volume rides alone. Run both back to back and the difference between the two renders shows you exactly what the clock was doing.
Étude principle

A constraint is not a limitation. It is where the variation lives.
