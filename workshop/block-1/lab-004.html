Block 1 · Lab 004 · Étude

One Oscillator, One Filter

One synthesizer voice — one tone generator, one filter, one volume shape — and nothing else. Three minutes of music. It sounds impossible until you discover how much variety hides inside a single sound.

Étude · S1 · D2 E1 M1 F1 · deep techno · half-cycle — pair with a listening session or a review week

listen → hypothesis → draw the path → calibrate → build → fault → diagnose → print → delta
Start here
  • Physically remove extra sources from the bench; this étude starts by making the constraint visible.
  • Save a new set as LAB-004-etude.
  • Choose one oscillator engine and one filter path, then stay with them for the whole piece.
  • Expected time: one focused half-cycle, or two short sessions.
The point

When you can’t add anything new, you are forced to find the variation inside the sound itself — and that skill is worth more than any new piece of gear.

How this lab works

Design one voice

  • Build a tone that works low as bass and high as lead.
  • Find the drums hiding in the same patch.
  • Steps 1–3.

Make it move by itself

  • Give the patch one slow internal motion — no hands.
  • Sequence patterns A and B, one note apart.
  • Steps 4–5.

Record, subtract, finish

  • Record three passes — low, mid, high.
  • Arrange by removing, down to exactly three minutes.
  • Steps 6–8.
Pass standard
Minimum passThree-minute piece exists, made from one synth voice, with one small fault and one system diff.
Solid passThe same voice clearly plays more than one musical role because filter, register, envelope, or modulation changed.
Advanced transferYou can make a second étude from a different single voice without adding tracks to solve the problem.

New concepts — in plain words

This is the first time you design a sound from scratch, so here is the vocabulary. Six words, no mystery.

ConceptIn plain words
OscillatorThe part that actually makes the raw sound: a rich, buzzing tone, like a violin string before the player shapes it. The MicroFreak offers many “engines” — different kinds of raw tone. You pick one and stay.
FilterA tone control that removes part of the sound, usually the brightness. Closing it muffles the tone — like a hand over your mouth. Opening it lets the sound through. “Cutoff” is where it starts cutting.
ResonanceThe filter ringing at its own cutting point. A little adds character; a lot makes it whistle. At the extreme, paired with a very short volume shape, it becomes a click or a thump — that is where this étude’s drums come from.
EnvelopeThe volume (or brightness) shape of one note over time: how fast it starts (attack), how fast it falls (decay), where it settles (sustain). A piano and an organ playing the same note differ mostly by envelope.
LFOA slow, invisible hand turning a knob for you, around and around, in cycles. Too slow to hear as a tone — you hear it as movement.
Mod matrixThe MicroFreak’s patch bay: a grid where you connect the movers (LFO, envelopes) to the knobs they should turn. “Route the LFO to cutoff, small amount” means the invisible hand gently works the tone control.

Brief & system

The brief

A three-minute piece from one MicroFreak voice: one oscillator engine, the analog filter, the volume envelope. Everything in the piece — bass, lead, drums if any — comes from this one voice.

The drums are not an exception — they are the test. Resonance clicks, short-envelope blips, filter-driven thumps: the drum kit is hiding inside the patch. Allowed in Live: recording, arrangement, and a utility/limiter on the master. Forbidden: the Digitakt, any Live instruments, any effect devices — no delay, no reverb. Dryness is the constraint. One take per moment, plus at most one overdub pass. D2 means sequenced: notes come from Live’s sequencer or the Launchpad, not free playing.

Build & report

  1. Pick one engine and commit. Audition if you must, but after step 2 there is no engine-surfing — the constraint starts here. Turn Type to your engine, write its name in your log, and do not touch that knob again.
  2. Design the core tone with the filter half-open. It must work as both bass and lead depending on octave; if it only works in one register, keep shaping. Start from the provided patch and shape only Wave and Timbre until octave 1 reads as bass and octave 3 reads as lead.
  3. Find the percussion inside the voice: high resonance plus the fastest envelope is a click. Dial the second table, then write both settings groups on paper.
  4. Sequence an A pattern (16 steps) and a B pattern that differs by exactly one note or one octave. The difference should be felt before it is noticed. Draw A in a one-bar MIDI clip, then ⌘ D and change exactly one note.
  5. Use the mod matrix for motion: one LFO or cycling-envelope routing, slow. This is where the variation lives — the patch must move on its own. Turn the Matrix encoder to LFO → Cutoff, press it, and dial a small amount.
  6. Record three passes at different octave and pattern combinations. Each pass is one voice; together they are the arrangement’s raw material. Follow the recipe below exactly.
  7. Arrange to exactly three minutes by subtraction — start with everything playing and remove until the piece breathes. Lay all three passes across the timeline below, then delete until every remaining bar earns its place.
  8. Render. Done is part of the étude. File → Export Audio/Video, WAV, 24-bit, named LAB-004-etude.
Recipe

The sequencing pass, exactly

Do this
  • MIDI track: MIDI To → MicroFreak, channel 1.
  • Draw the 16-step A pattern in a one-bar clip; ⌘ D the clip, change exactly one note or octave → B pattern.
  • Audio track MF PRINT: Audio From → Ext. In → 1, armed — it records while the MIDI clip plays.
  • Record three passes at different octave/pattern combinations; rename the clips PASS-LOW, PASS-MID, PASS-HIGH with ⌘ R.
Expected result

Three takes that are obviously the same voice in three roles.

If it fails
MIDI notes do nothingtrack output not set to the MicroFreak port — check local keys vs USB
recorded audio is double-speed flutter or silencesample-rate mismatch — re-check 48 kHz

A default three-minute shape to deviate from, not a rule.

0:00–0:30
Percussion pattern alone — the clicks are the intro.
0:30–1:15
Low pass enters; the voice takes the bass role.
1:15–2:00
Mid/high pass over it; the B pattern appears.
2:00–2:40
Subtraction begins — remove one pass at a time.
2:40–3:00
The first sound alone again, then out.

Étude principle

Economy as system — a constraint is not a limitation, it is the location of the variation.
