Replicate: One Gesture, Geologic Patience

Copy one clear section of Mike Parker — “Spiral Snare”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: one parameter moving slower than perception. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start

  • New set saved as REP-spiral-snare, tempo around 124.
  • Cutoff, resonance, envelope unclear? The concepts table in Lab 004 covers all of them in plain words.

  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: one parameter moving slower than perception.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Mike Parker — “Spiral Snare”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How long before you noticed anything had changed?
    Answer

    For most listeners it takes well over a minute - often two - before the suspicion lands that something is different. The honest logbook line is usually “I can’t say when it started”.

  2. What changed — and could you name the moment it happened? (You shouldn’t be able to.)
    Answer

    The filter is opening - brightness creeping into the central sound - but the moment of change doesn’t exist; the movement is slower than your ten-second auditory memory, so each present moment sounds identical to the one before it.

  3. Does the change have a direction (opening? approaching?)?
    Answer

    Yes - one direction, held without wavering: the sound opens, as if approaching. Direction is what separates this from random drift; your ear extrapolates the line and starts waiting for an arrival that never quite comes.

  4. How does the track hold you with no events at all?
    Answer

    By keeping you checking. The change sits below the threshold of any single moment, so your attention keeps re-sampling the loop to confirm what it half-noticed. No events, no drops - the engagement is the act of listening itself.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target — what success sounds like:
  • Your central sound, solo’d against the record, would make a friend hesitate.
  • In any random 10 seconds, nothing audibly changes — same as the record.
  • A/B’d minute against minute, your rate of change tracks theirs: undeniable across the stretch, invisible inside it.
  • Nothing else moves at all.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.

The whole rig for this lesson — one sound source, one print track, and exactly one automation lane: filter cutoff.

Phase 1

Set the reference

Phase focus: set the reference. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Drop “Spiral Snare” onto an audio track named REF.
  2. Live Loop its most characteristic stretch — the move is slow, so a longer stretch is right: several minutes rather than 8 bars.
  3. Live Gain-match with a Utility on REF.
  4. Live Keep REF one solo away for the whole session.
Expected result

A multi-minute loop you can drop into anywhere, at the level your own print will play at.

If it fails

Phase 2

Match the central sound

Phase focus: match the central sound. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak Pick the MicroFreak engine that gets closest with the Type encoder and commit — no engine-surfing after this step.
  2. MicroFreak Start the filter mostly closed: cutoff around 25 %, resonance modest, around 20 %.
  3. MicroFreak Paraphonic OFF — one voice.
  4. MicroFreak + Live Tweak against REF until the tone has the same bite, body, and decay.
Expected result

A dark, close cousin of the record’s central sound — with obvious room left above it for the filter to open into.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Close but never convincingWrong engine — filter tweaks can’t fix raw characterAllow one engine change, then commit for good
Already bright at the startCutoff opened while matching toneMatch darkness too — return cutoff toward 25 % and accept a duller start
Phase 3

Transcribe the figure, arm the print

Phase focus: transcribe the figure, arm the print. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak + Live Transcribe the looped figure by ear into one 1-bar MIDI clip, MIDI To → MicroFreak — their notes, your best hearing of them.
  2. Live Loop it against REF until the two figures circle each other.
  3. Live Create the print track: audio track MF PRINT, Audio From → Ext. In → 1, armed.
Expected result

Your figure and theirs looping together without friction, and meters moving on MF PRINT.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
MIDI notes do nothingTrack output not reaching the MicroFreak portRe-check MIDI To and the channel — USB, not guesswork
MF PRINT stays silentWrong input or unarmed trackConfirm Ext. In → 1 and the record-arm button
Phase 4

The gesture — four minutes, one lane

Phase focus: the gesture — four minutes, one lane. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak + Live Draw ONE automation envelope in Arrangement view — filter cutoff moving in the record’s direction over four full minutes, aiming for the record’s rate of change: total movement maybe 30 % of the knob’s range.
  2. MicroFreak Alternatively: ride the hardware cutoff knob by hand — same direction, same patience.
  3. Live A/B your minute against their minute as you go.
  4. Live Change nothing else.
  5. Live No mutes, no new notes, no second envelope — the record’s discipline is the lesson.
  6. Live Record the full four minutes in one pass while the loop plays.
Expected result

A four-minute take that sounds static inside any ten seconds, and unmistakably opened by the end.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
You can hear the filter moving inside ten secondsRamp too steep — too much movement, or too soonFlatten the envelope; spread the same end-points over the full four minutes
Minute 4 sounds like minute 0Total movement too small for the patchRaise the end-point — never the speed
Phase 5

Listen back, observe the numbers

Phase focus: listen back, observe the numbers. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Drop the playhead into any random 10 seconds of your recording — nothing should audibly change.
  2. Play minute 0 and minute 4 side by side — and play each next to the matching minute of the record.
  3. MicroFreak + Logbook Open the drawn automation envelope (or the recorded ride) and read the cutoff value at minute 0 and at minute 4 — write both numbers in the logbook and onto the diagram above.
Expected result

Two numbers a knob’s-width apart — and four minutes of music living inside that small difference.

Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the looped stretch at matched volume, then answer:

  • 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate before saying which is which?
    Answer

    This one should genuinely be hard, because in any ten seconds both recordings are static loops. What gives a replica away is almost always the sound — the figure’s tone and the space around it — not the gesture. If yours fails here, go back to the patch, not the envelope.

  • Minute against minute, does your change arrive at the record’s pace — undeniable across four minutes, invisible inside any ten seconds? Both must be true at once; that contradiction is the record’s engine.
    Answer

    Play minute 0 next to minute 4: the difference must be undeniable. Drop into any random ten seconds: nothing should move. If the change is audible inside ten seconds your ramp is too steep — flatten it. If minute 4 sounds like minute 0, the total movement is too small — extend the end-point, never the speed.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines:
  • Matched: what your replica got closest to — the sound, the figure, or the rate of change; start with how far the cutoff actually moved versus how far it felt like it moved.
  • Missed: what the record does that yours doesn’t (best guess why).
  • Change next: the one thing you would do differently on the next attempt.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1. Carries into étude 004: this is the patience setting for 004’s three minutes.
