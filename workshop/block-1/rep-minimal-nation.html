Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: Every Element Load-Bearing

Copy one clear section of Robert Hood — Minimal Nation. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: each element does one job nothing else does. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Digitakt II and MicroFreak on the bench, both into the M4, levels already set.
  • New Live set saved as REP-minimal-nation, tempo 125.
  • If words like filter, resonance or envelope feel fuzzy, reread the concepts table in Lab 004 first.
  • What you will make: a short replica of Robert Hood — Minimal Nation. Focus on this effect: 3-5 elements feel like a full track.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: each element does one job nothing else does.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Robert Hood — Minimal Nation. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many elements are there — count, don’t estimate?
    Answer

    On most Minimal Nation tracks an honest count lands at three to five: kick, hat, a clap or percussion hit, and one synth figure - sometimes a separate bass.

  2. What is each one’s job, in one word (pulse, answer, weight, tension)?
    Answer

    A typical mapping: kick - pulse, hat - time (it carries the offbeat air), clap or perc - answer, synth figure - tension. Your words can differ; what matters is that every word is different.

  3. Do any two share a job?
    Answer

    No - and that is the record’s whole argument. Two elements that seem to share a job turn out to differ in register, placement or length once you solo your attention on each.

  4. If you could delete one, which — and would the track survive?
    Answer

    There is no safe deletion. Remove the hat and the track loses its forward lean; remove the clap and the call has no response; remove the figure and there is no reason to stay.

  5. How does it stay interesting with so few parts?
    Answer

    Placement and micro-variation, not new material. The elements lock into one groove and your ear rides the tiny shifts - a velocity here, a nudge there - plus the tension of waiting for changes that arrive rarely.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target — what success sounds like:
  • The same element count as the record’s section — count theirs, build that many, no more.
  • Each sound and pattern close enough that, solo’d against the record, a friend would hesitate.
  • Muting any element of your replica hurts the way it would hurt the record.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.

Phase 1

Set the reference

Phase focus: set the reference. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Pick one track from Minimal Nation and drop it onto an audio track named REF.
  2. Live Find the most characteristic 8–16 bars and set the loop brace around them.
  3. Live Gain-match with a Utility on REF so every comparison is honest — a louder reference always sounds “better”.
  4. Live Keep REF one solo away for the whole session; you will return to it dozens of times.
Expected result

A clean loop that cycles without a stumble, at roughly the level your own tracks will play at.

Close the gaps and print

Phase focus: close the gaps and print. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this

Verify against the record

A/B your 2 minutes against the looped section at matched volume, then answer cold the next day:

  • 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate before saying which is which?
    Answer

    Hesitation comes from tone before pattern — patterns are easy to copy; the kick’s weight and the hat’s brightness are not. If a friend would call it instantly, A/B each element solo against REF and fix the one that gives you away first. That element is usually the kick.

  • Does every mute on your replica still hurt — and can you name each element’s one job in one word (pulse, answer, tension…)?
    Answer

    Every mute should still hurt, because you copied a system in which every part is load-bearing. If a mute doesn’t hurt in your replica but would in the record, your version of that element is either too quiet or covering a job another element already does — go back to the gap-closing round, not the patterns.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines:
  • Matched: what your replica got closest to — which sound, which pattern, and what the mute test taught you about why it’s there.
  • Missed: what the record does that yours doesn’t (best guess why).
  • Change next: the one thing you would do differently on the next attempt.
