Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: Every Element Load-Bearing

Replicate a section of Minimal Nation as closely as your rig allows, then prove — by muting your replica — that every single element is load-bearing.

The point

In minimal music an element earns its place by being missed — if muting it doesn’t hurt, it was decoration.

The system you are replicating
Source recordRobert Hood — Minimal Nation
The effect3–5 elements feel like a full track
The mechanismeach element does one job nothing else does
Fidelityas close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Digitakt II and MicroFreak on the bench, both into the M4, levels already set.
  • New Live set saved as REP-minimal-nation, tempo 125.
  • If words like filter, resonance or envelope feel fuzzy, reread the concepts table in Lab 004 first.
  • Timebox: 60–120 minutes. Transcription is the slow half — counting, tuning and placing by ear — and rushing it defeats the lesson. Stop when an A/B round stops improving, not when the clock says so.
Listen first

Reference pass — 10 minutes

Listen to about one minute of Minimal Nation. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear — and write your answer before opening each reveal.

  1. How many elements are there — count, don’t estimate?
    Answer

    On most Minimal Nation tracks an honest count lands at three to five: kick, hat, a clap or percussion hit, and one synth figure — sometimes a separate bass. If you counted more, you almost certainly counted one element twice in two different filter or velocity states. Count again on your fingers, one full loop per element.

  2. What is each one’s job, in one word (pulse, answer, weight, tension)?
    Answer

    A typical mapping: kick — pulse, hat — time (it carries the offbeat air), clap or perc — answer, synth figure — tension. Your words can differ; what matters is that every word is different. If you need the same word twice, one of those two elements is doing something you haven’t heard yet.

  3. Do any two share a job?
    Answer

    No — and that is the record’s whole argument. Two elements that seem to share a job turn out to differ in register, placement or length once you solo your attention on each. Listen to the same loop twice, once per candidate, and the overlap dissolves.

  4. If you could delete one, which — and would the track survive?
    Answer

    There is no safe deletion. Remove the hat and the track loses its forward lean; remove the clap and the call has no response; remove the figure and there is no reason to stay. Pick one anyway and imagine eight bars without it — the discomfort you feel is the design.

  5. How does it stay interesting with so few parts?
    Answer

    Placement and micro-variation, not new material. The elements lock into one groove and your ear rides the tiny shifts — a velocity here, a nudge there — plus the tension of waiting for changes that arrive rarely. Hood trusts repetition to do the work most producers ask new sounds to do.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

  • Sound target — what success sounds like:
  • The same element count as the record’s section — count theirs, build that many, no more.
  • Each sound and pattern close enough that, solo’d against the record, a friend would hesitate.
  • Muting any element of your replica hurts the way it would hurt the record.

Steps

The session’s routing, fixed for the whole lesson — REF stays one solo away at every phase.

Digitakt II + MicroFreak → M4 inputs → Live audio tracks → A/B against REF
Phase 1

Set the reference

Every judgement in this lesson is a comparison, so the thing you compare against has to be set up honestly first. A looped, gain-matched section of the record sitting one solo away is what keeps you from drifting toward the version in your memory — which is always simpler and always wrong.

Do this
  1. Pick one track from Minimal Nation and drop it onto an audio track named REF.
  2. Find the most characteristic 8–16 bars and set the loop brace around them.
  3. Gain-match with a Utility on REF so every comparison is honest — a louder reference always sounds “better”.
  4. Keep REF one solo away for the whole session; you will return to it dozens of times.
Expected result

A clean loop that cycles without a stumble, at roughly the level your own tracks will play at.

Phase 2

Count the elements, match each sound

The count from your reference pass becomes a build list, and each entry gets a sound matched against the record. Physically this is two machines doing two jobs: the Digitakt supplies the drums, tuned hit by hit, and the MicroFreak supplies the synth figure, whose notes you transcribe by ear. Fill the table as you go — it is the contract for everything after.

Do this
  1. Count the section’s elements — count, don’t estimate; that count is your build list. Build that many, no more.
  2. Write each element into the table below with its one-word job before you shape any sound.
  3. On the Digitakt, tune kick, hat and clap/perc until each sits as close to the record’s as the machine allows — sample choice first, then tuning and decay.
  4. On the MicroFreak, match the synth figure’s tone, then transcribe its actual notes by ear into a Live MIDI clip — sing the line, place the notes, compare against REF, revise.
  5. Record the MicroFreak to audio via Audio From → Ext. In → 1.

Phase 3

Transcribe the patterns

Now the sounds go where the record puts them — not roughly, exactly. This is slow, honest work: solo REF, follow one element through the loop, mark its hits, program what you marked, compare. The grid below is your scaffold; marking on paper before programming makes your ears commit before your fingers do.

Do this
  1. Solo REF and follow one element at a time — kick first, then hat, then perc.
  2. Mark each hit on the grid below, then program what you marked — Digitakt grid recording for the drums, the MIDI clip for the figure.
  3. Place every hit where the record places it — solo REF, listen, place, compare.
  4. Loop your replica against the section until the two move as one groove.

Phase 4

Run the mute test

Now that the elements are theirs, feel why each one is load-bearing. Muting removes one beam from the structure at a time while the rest keeps playing — what collapses tells you what the beam was holding. Writing it down is not optional: the table is the observe step; doing it without writing it doesn’t count.

Do this
  1. Mute each element alone — Digitakt track mutes for the drums, Live track mute for the MicroFreak — for 8 full bars, in turn, while the others keep playing.
  2. Write one line per element: what broke when it left? Rhythm? Weight? Answer? Tension?
  3. If you can’t name what broke, listen to the record’s section again — Hood put it there for a reason.
  4. Fill the table below as you go, then run the whole test a second time, cold, the next day.

Phase 5

Close the gaps and print

A first replica is always a sketch; the distance to the record closes in rounds, not in one pass. Two or three rounds is normal. Then the study gets printed — done is part of the lesson.

Do this
  1. Solo REF against your replica element by element — level, tuning, placement.
  2. Whatever a friend would catch in ten seconds, fix. Repeat the round until the catches run out.
  3. Record 2 minutes of the final replica to an audio track.
  4. File → Export Audio/Video, WAV, named REP-minimal-nation.
Expected result

A 2-minute WAV that survives the verify questions below.

Verify against the record

A/B your 2 minutes against the looped section at matched volume, then answer cold the next day:

  • 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate before saying which is which?
    Answer

    Hesitation comes from tone before pattern — patterns are easy to copy; the kick’s weight and the hat’s brightness are not. If a friend would call it instantly, A/B each element solo against REF and fix the one that gives you away first. That element is usually the kick.

  • Does every mute on your replica still hurt — and can you name each element’s one job in one word (pulse, answer, tension…)?
    Answer

    Every mute should still hurt, because you copied a system in which every part is load-bearing. If a mute doesn’t hurt in your replica but would in the record, your version of that element is either too quiet or covering a job another element already does — go back to the gap-closing round, not the patterns.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines:
  • Matched: what your replica got closest to — which sound, which pattern, and what the mute test taught you about why it’s there.
  • Missed: what the record does that yours doesn’t (best guess why).
  • Change next: the one thing you would do differently on the next attempt.
