Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the pushed mix

Build a small clean loop, then drive the whole of it — gently, gain-compensated, on a bus — until the elements melt into one room the way a “Falling Up” section does.

The point

Drive on an element picks a target; drive on the whole mix builds a room — everything grits together, and the glue is the sound.

The system you are replicating
Source recordTheo Parrish — “Falling Up”
The effectthe whole mix gently overdriven — nothing clean, everything glued
The mechanismsaturation on the sum, gain-compensated, ridden with the section
Fidelityas close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, Digitakt II → M4 inputs 3/4, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged
  • New set saved as REP-FALLING-UP, tempo 122
  • M4 phones knob at the lab-001 mark — it stays there for the whole lesson
  • Timebox: 60–120 minutes, then file it and stop — the extra time goes into the loop and the riding passes, not extra devices
Reference pass — 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to about one minute of “Falling Up” — at least twice, once hunting for anything clean and once following the texture they all share. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Open each model answer only after your own line exists.

  1. What is pushed?
    Answer

    Everything — gently. The chords, the kick, the hats all carry the same warm grit, which tells you the drive sits after they were summed rather than on any one of them. Relisten to two different elements back to back and notice they wear the same fuzz; that shared coat is the address of the saturation.

  2. Is anything clean?
    Answer

    No — and that is the decision. Nothing escapes the push, so there is no clean reference point inside the track, and the ear accepts the grit as the room instead of as damage. Relisten hunting for one untouched element; the failure of the hunt is the answer.

  3. Does the drive jump out at you — or did you only notice it when asked?
    Answer

    It hides in plain sight: the push is gentle enough that it reads as warmth and weight, not as distortion. That subtlety is the amount you will be matching — far less Drive than the word “pushed” suggests. Relisten once imagining the track perfectly clean; the imagined version sounds thinner, and the difference is the device’s entire contribution.

  4. What does the push do to the groove?
    Answer

    It thickens it — the drums feel heavier and the elements lean on each other, because saturation on a sum responds to the combination, swelling slightly every time kick and chord coincide. The groove sounds played-together rather than assembled. Relisten to the kick’s landings and notice the whole mix breathes faintly with each one.

  5. Is the pushed mix actually louder — or just denser?
    Answer

    Denser. At matched loudness the section does not sit above a clean mix — it is thicker, with the gaps between elements filled by shared harmonics. That is exactly what the gain-compensated bus toggle will demonstrate on your own loop: meters agree, density doesn’t. Relisten for fullness, not level.

Build only after the answers exist — you are about to copy the system you just described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your loop lands on the same kind of room — everything sharing one warm grit, nothing clean, nothing harsh. Closeness is the measure of your hearing.
  • Glued and thicker, not louder — the bus Saturator’s output is gain-compensated so the toggle judges density, never level.
  • The push is gentle: if any single element sounds distorted on its own, the bus has gone past the record.
  • The amount breathes with the section — ridden, not set and forgotten.

The routing that makes a room: every element stays clean on its own track, all of them meet in one group, and the only drive in the session lives on that group — gain-compensated, before the master.

CHORD · clean + KICK · clean + HAT · clean → group MIX BUS → Saturator · gentle · gain-comp → master

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the tracks

Every glue judgment in this lesson is a comparison against the record at matched loudness, so the record lives in the session, trimmed. Physically, the Utility on REF is what keeps the comparison fair — a louder reference always sounds warmer and fuller, which is exactly the quality you are trying to judge.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Falling Up” into it.
  2. Find the most characteristic 8–16 bars — chords, kick, and hats all present and all gritting together — and loop them.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  4. ⌘ T twice; rename: CHORD (Ext. In 1) and DRUMS (Ext. In 3/4). Faders at 0 dB.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS and two named, routed, unity-gain tracks wait for audio.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
REF later wins every A/B effortlesslyUtility never trimmed — the file is mastered loudTrim until the looped section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, same as your loop
No meter movement when testing an inputExt. In channel does not match the physical jackMicroFreak on 1, Digitakt on 3/4 — the lab-001 wiring table
Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

The loop, recorded clean at nominal

The record’s glue only means something because the parts underneath are ordinary — a chord, a kick, a hat, played warm and recorded clean. Physically, everything in this phase lands at the ledger level with no drive anywhere, because the entire lesson is the difference between this clean state and the pushed one, heard fairly.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, Paraphonic ON, cutoff ≈ 45 %, resonance ≈ 10 %, attack short, decay long — a soft, warm chord voice.
  2. Solo REF and transcribe the chord stab’s rhythm — where it lands across 4 bars — and find two or three voicings in the record’s register; the exact notes are your ear’s job, the placement is the groove’s.
  3. Set the M4 input-1 gain so peaks sit ≈ −12 dBFS and record 8 bars of the chord part into CHORD.
  4. Digitakt: a kick on 1, 5, 9, 13 and a loose hat pattern — match the record’s feel, not its exact kit. Main Volume set so peaks sit ≈ −12 dBFS; record 8 bars into DRUMS.
  5. Loop everything together and listen once: it should sound like a polite, slightly thin demo of the record — separate, clean, unglued. That thinness is the before-state the bus will transform.
Expected result

An 8-bar loop — chord, kick, hat — all clean, all peaking ≈ −12 dBFS, recognisably aimed at the record but missing its weight entirely.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
The chord already sounds grittyInput gain hot, or resonance high — grit before the decisionRe-check the ledger value on input 1 and pull resonance back; the clean state must be clean
The drums buzz on peaksThe Digitakt is overdriving the fixed 3/4 line inputsLower Main Volume — on this pair the instrument’s knob is the record level
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Push the bus — until the elements melt together

Now the decision, at the sum’s address: group everything and saturate the group, gently, gain-compensated. Physically, a saturator on a bus responds to the combination — it generates shared harmonics whenever elements coincide, which is why the result reads as one room instead of three sounds. The record decides the amount, and the amount is smaller than you expect.

Do this
  1. Select CHORD and DRUMS, ⌘ G, rename the group MIX BUS.
  2. On the group, drop in Audio Effects → Saturator — default curve, nothing exotic. Push Drive up gently — start around +6 dB.
  3. Pull the Output down by roughly the same amount until the group meters the same with the device on and off.
  4. Walk the Drive in small steps, solo-flipping against REF, until the elements start melting together — the gaps fill, the kick and chord lean on each other — at the record’s gentleness. If any soloed element sounds distorted, you have passed it; back off.
  5. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Toggle the bus Saturator on and off at matched loudness. The group meter barely moves; the density changes completely. Fill the level/density table above from what you hear, then write one sentence in the logbook on what “glue” means now that you have heard it appear.
Expected result

One device on one group, set gently, and a loop that has changed from three clean parts into one room — at the same meter reading. The flip against REF lands on the same kind of warmth.

If it fails

Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

Ride the amount through the section

A pushed mix is not a preset — the record’s warmth breathes with the arrangement, because the bus responds to whatever the section feeds it. Physically, every element you mute changes what the saturator sees, so the same Drive setting sounds different in a sparse bar than a full one. The ride is the musicianship of this lesson.

Do this
  1. In Arrangement view, tile the loop into a 2-minute sketch and mirror the record’s section — elements entering and leaving where the record’s do.
  2. Play it once end to end and listen only to the bus: where the arrangement thins, the glue thins too. Note the bars where the room collapses.
  3. Automate the Saturator’s Drive by a small amount — a couple of dB — to keep the density consistent through the thin bars, re-checking the compensation where you ride.
  4. Play it once more against REF: the room should now hold from the first bar to the last, the way the record’s does.
Expected result

A 2-minute sketch where the glue survives the arrangement’s changes — gentle, consistent, and ridden by hand where the section demanded it.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
The sketch gets harsher as it fills upThe full arrangement drives the bus harder than the loop didRide the Drive down in the full bars, or trim the members a touch into the group
The ride is audible as an effectAutomation moves too largeHalve every automation move — the ride should be felt as consistency, never heard as motion
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your sketch at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Toggle the bus device once more. Can you name, in one sentence, what changed — without using the word “louder”?
    Answer

    The sentence should be about space and weight: the gaps between elements filled, the parts started leaning on each other, three sounds became one room. If “louder” is the only word that comes, the compensation has drifted — re-match the group meters and toggle again. Relisten to the record once afterwards and hear the same filled gaps there.

  • Solo each element of your sketch. Is every one of them still clean on its own?
    Answer

    Yes — that is the proof the drive lives on the sum and nowhere else, which is the record’s system. A distorted solo means the push went past “Falling Up” into per-element territory, a different lesson (“Pursuit” territory). Back the Drive off until the solos clean up while the combination still glues, then relisten to the record’s chords alone: warm, but never broken.
