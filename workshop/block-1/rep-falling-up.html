Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the pushed mix

Copy one clear section of Theo Parrish — “Falling Up”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: saturation on the sum, gain-compensated, ridden with the section. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

  • New set saved as REP-FALLING-UP, tempo 122
  • M4 phones knob at the lab-001 mark — it stays there for the whole lesson

  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: saturation on the sum, gain-compensated, ridden with the section.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Theo Parrish — “Falling Up”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is pushed?
    Answer

    Everything - gently. The chords, the kick, the hats all carry the same warm grit, which tells you the drive sits after they were summed rather than on any one of them.

  2. Is anything clean?
    Answer

    No - and that is the decision. Nothing escapes the push, so there is no clean reference point inside the track, and the ear accepts the grit as the room instead of as damage.

  3. Does the drive jump out at you — or did you only notice it when asked?
    Answer

    It hides in plain sight: the push is gentle enough that it reads as warmth and weight, not as distortion. That subtlety is the amount you will be matching - far less Drive than the word “pushed” suggests.

  4. What does the push do to the groove?
    Answer

    It thickens it - the drums feel heavier and the elements lean on each other, because saturation on a sum responds to the combination, swelling slightly every time kick and chord coincide.

  5. Is the pushed mix actually louder — or just denser?
    Answer

    Denser. At matched loudness the section does not sit above a clean mix - it is thicker, with the gaps between elements filled by shared harmonics.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your loop lands on the same kind of room — everything sharing one warm grit, nothing clean, nothing harsh.
  • Glued and thicker, not louder — the bus Saturator’s output is gain-compensated so the toggle judges density, never level.
  • The push is gentle: if any single element sounds distorted on its own, the bus has gone past the record.
  • The amount breathes with the section — ridden, not set and forgotten.

The routing that makes a room: every element stays clean on its own track, all of them meet in one group, and the only drive in the session lives on that group — gain-compensated, before the master.

CHORD · clean + KICK · clean + HAT · clean → group MIX BUS → Saturator · gentle · gain-comp → master

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the tracks

Phase focus: the reference and the tracks. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Falling Up” into it.
  2. Live Find the most characteristic 8–16 bars — chords, kick, and hats all present and all gritting together — and loop them.
  3. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Live REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. Live ⌘ T twice; rename: CHORD (Ext. In 1) and DRUMS (Ext. In 3/4).
  6. Live Faders at 0 dB.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS and two named, routed, unity-gain tracks wait for audio.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
REF later wins every A/B effortlesslyUtility never trimmed — the file is mastered loudTrim until the looped section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, same as your loop
No meter movement when testing an inputExt. In channel does not match the physical jackMicroFreak on 1, Digitakt II on 3/4 — the lab-001 wiring table
Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

The loop, recorded clean at nominal

Phase focus: the loop, recorded clean at nominal. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, Paraphonic ON, cutoff ≈ 45 %, resonance ≈ 10 %, attack short, decay long — a soft, warm chord voice.
  2. Live Solo REF and transcribe the chord stab’s rhythm — where it lands across 4 bars — and find two or three voicings in the record’s register;
  3. Live the exact notes are your ear’s job, the placement is the groove’s.
  4. Live Set the M4 input-1 gain so peaks sit ≈ −12 dBFS and record 8 bars of the chord part into CHORD.
  5. Digitakt II Digitakt II: a kick on 1, 5, 9, 13 and a loose hat pattern — match the record’s feel, not its exact kit.
  6. Digitakt II Main Volume set so peaks sit ≈ −12 dBFS; record 8 bars into DRUMS.
  7. Digitakt II Loop everything together and listen once: it should sound like a polite, slightly thin demo of the record — separate, clean, unglued.
  8. Digitakt II That thinness is the before-state the bus will transform.
Expected result

An 8-bar loop — chord, kick, hat — all clean, all peaking ≈ −12 dBFS, recognisably aimed at the record but missing its weight entirely.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The chord already sounds grittyInput gain hot, or resonance high — grit before the decisionRe-check the ledger value on input 1 and pull resonance back; the clean state must be clean
The drums buzz on peaksThe Digitakt II is overdriving the fixed 3/4 line inputsLower Main Volume — on this pair the instrument’s knob is the record level
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Push the bus — until the elements melt together

Phase focus: push the bus — until the elements melt together. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Select CHORD and DRUMS, ⌘ G, rename the group MIX BUS.
  2. Live On the group, drop in Audio Effects → Saturator — default curve, nothing exotic.
  3. Live Push Drive up gently — start around +6 dB.
  4. Live Pull the Output down by roughly the same amount until the group meters the same with the device on and off.
  5. Live Walk the Drive in small steps, solo-flipping against REF, until the elements start melting together — the gaps fill, the kick and chord lean on each other — at the record’s gentleness.
  6. Live If any soloed element sounds distorted, you have passed it;
  7. Live back off.
  8. Live Observe — see it, not just hear it. Toggle the bus Saturator on and off at matched loudness.
  9. Live The group meter barely moves; the density changes completely.
  10. Logbook Fill the level/density table above from what you hear, then write one sentence in the logbook on what “glue” means now that you have heard it appear.
Expected result

One device on one group, set gently, and a loop that has changed from three clean parts into one room — at the same meter reading. The flip against REF lands on the same kind of warmth.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The pushed version simply sounds “better” in every toggleOutput not pulled down — loudness is votingGain-compensate until the group meters the same both ways, then judge again
Glue never appears — just fuzz on topDrive too high, or the parts too quiet into the deviceHalve the Drive and confirm the elements reach the group at ≈ −12 dBFS peaks; gentle in, gentle through
Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

Ride the amount through the section

Phase focus: ride the amount through the section. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live In Arrangement view, tile the loop into a 2-minute sketch and mirror the record’s section — elements entering and leaving where the record’s do.
  2. Live Play it once end to end and listen only to the bus: where the arrangement thins, the glue thins too.
  3. Logbook Note the bars where the room collapses.
  4. Live Automate the Saturator’s Drive by a small amount — a couple of dB — to keep the density consistent through the thin bars, re-checking the compensation where you ride.
  5. Live Play it once more against REF: the room should now hold from the first bar to the last, the way the record’s does.
Expected result

A 2-minute sketch where the glue survives the arrangement’s changes — gentle, consistent, and ridden by hand where the section demanded it.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The sketch gets harsher as it fills upThe full arrangement drives the bus harder than the loop didRide the Drive down in the full bars, or trim the members a touch into the group
The ride is audible as an effectAutomation moves too largeHalve every automation move — the ride should be felt as consistency, never heard as motion
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your sketch at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Toggle the bus device once more. Can you name, in one sentence, what changed — without using the word “louder”?
    Answer

    The sentence should be about space and weight: the gaps between elements filled, the parts started leaning on each other, three sounds became one room. If “louder” is the only word that comes, the compensation has drifted — re-match the group meters and toggle again. Relisten to the record once afterwards and hear the same filled gaps there.

  • Solo each element of your sketch. Is every one of them still clean on its own?
    Answer

    Yes — that is the proof the drive lives on the sum and nowhere else, which is the record’s system. A distorted solo means the push went past “Falling Up” into per-element territory, a different lesson (“Pursuit” territory). Back the Drive off until the solos clean up while the combination still glues, then relisten to the record’s chords alone: warm, but never broken.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.

  • Carries into the lab: the bus is a gain stage with an opinion — what you learned as “locus 3, a place clipping hides” is also a place sound is made.
