Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: quiet as depth

Copy one clear section of DJ Sprinkles — “Grand Central, Pt. I”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: sparse elements, enormous level gaps, the whole mix held far below the ceiling. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, Digitakt II → M4 inputs 3/4, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged
  • New set saved as REP-GRAND-CENTRAL, tempo 120
  • M4 phones knob at the lab-001 mark — this lesson will tempt you to turn up constantly; the mark is the whole exercise
  • What you will make: a short replica of DJ Sprinkles — “Grand Central, Pt. I”. Focus on this effect: depth and intimacy from quietness - complete while peaking low.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: sparse elements, enormous level gaps, the whole mix held far below the ceiling.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of DJ Sprinkles — “Grand Central, Pt. I”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is the loudest element — and how loud is it, really?
    Answer

    Probably the kick or the piano chord, and even that is conversational - the level of someone speaking across a table, never raised. Naming it takes effort because nothing campaigns for the title, which is the system working.

  2. How much air sits around each element?
    Answer

    An enormous amount - every element is surrounded by space both in time and in level, with nothing crowding the gaps. The air is not leftover headroom; it is what makes the room feel deep and the elements feel placed.

  3. Does the track feel incomplete — like something is missing?
    Answer

    No - it feels finished and whole, which is the paradox you are here to copy: completeness has nothing to do with how much of the meter is used. Sparseness reads as intent because every element is exactly placed.

  4. What happens when you listen quieter than usual?
    Answer

    It blooms - the depth and detail come forward instead of vanishing, because the balances were built to survive low volume rather than to impress at high volume. Most records thin out when turned down; this one settles in.

  5. Is the quietness a missing decision — or a made one?
    Answer

    Made, everywhere, deliberately - levels this consistent and gaps this exact do not happen by neglect. An unmixed track is uneven; this is uniform restraint, which takes more decisions, not fewer.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your loop lands on the same intimacy — sparse elements, conversational peaks, enormous air.
  • The master peaks ≈ −12 dBFS while the loop feels complete — the gap between that number and 0 is the lesson, not a problem.
  • Nothing gets turned up to win. Depth comes from how far apart the levels sit, not from how high any of them reach.
  • The loop survives the quiet test: played well below the mark, it blooms instead of vanishing.

The whole system is levels: four sparse elements, each recorded clean at the ledger, then spread apart by fader decisions — and a master that never climbs. No device makes this sound; the mixer does.

soft kick · −15 + chord · −20 + hat · −28 + detail · −34 → master · peaks ≈ −12 dBFS → phones at the mark · it blooms

The spread, as numbers. These are starting points in dBFS peaks per element — the record, heard through your solo-flips, corrects them. What must survive every correction is the size of the gaps.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the tracks

Phase focus: the reference and the tracks. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Grand Central, Pt.
  2. Live I” into it.
  3. Live Find the most characteristic 8–16 bars — kick, chord, and the quiet detail all present — and loop them.
  4. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  5. Live REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  6. Live ⌘ T twice; rename: CHORD (Ext. In 1) and DRUMS (Ext. In 3/4).
  7. Live Faders at 0 dB for recording — the spread comes later, by decision.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS and two named, routed, unity-gain tracks wait for audio.

If it fails

Phase 2 · ≈ 30 min

The sparse loop — four elements, no more

Phase focus: the sparse loop — four elements, no more. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, Paraphonic ON, cutoff ≈ 40 %, attack soft, decay long — a warm chord that lands without an edge.
  2. MicroFreak Transcribe the record’s chord rhythm by ear: sparse landings, lots of nothing between them.
  3. MicroFreak Set the M4 input-1 gain so peaks sit ≈ −12 dBFS and record 8 bars into CHORD.
  4. Digitakt II Digitakt II: the softest kick in the project on 1, 5, 9, 13, and a closed hat pattern with gaps — match the record’s sparseness, not a techno grid.
  5. Digitakt II Main Volume so peaks sit ≈ −12 dBFS; record 8 bars into DRUMS.
  6. MicroFreak Record one quiet detail onto a third track — a single MicroFreak note, a hat variation, a short noise — anything that can sit at the very edge of attention.
  7. MicroFreak One pass, 8 bars, ledger level.
  8. MicroFreak Resist additions: if the loop feels empty, that is the point — phase 3 turns the emptiness into depth.
Expected result

Four clean recordings — kick, chord, hat, detail — all peaking ≈ −12 dBFS at unity faders, sounding flat and equal and nothing like the record yet. The mix does not exist; only the material does.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The chord lands with a click or an edgeAttack at zero — the record’s chord breathes inSoften the attack until the landing has no corner
The loop feels crowded before any mixingToo many trigs — sparseness lost at the pattern stageDelete trigs until silence is the most common event, like the record
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Spread the levels — enormous gaps, low ceiling

Phase focus: spread the levels — enormous gaps, low ceiling. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Pull the faders to the spread: kick ≈ −15, chord ≈ −20, hat ≈ −28, detail ≈ −34 dBFS peaks on the master meter, master fader at 0 dB.
  2. Confirm the whole loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS on the master.
  3. If it peaks higher, lower elements — never the listening level.
  4. Live Solo-flip against REF and correct one fader at a time, phones at the mark.
  5. Live Judge distances, not elements: is your hat as far below your chord as the record’s is?
  6. Live Make at least three flip-and-correct passes.
  7. Live The urge to nudge everything up will arrive in each one;
  8. Live the mark on the phones knob is what you push against instead.
  9. Logbook Observe — see it, not just hear it. Play the finished loop and write the master peak number in the logbook while the music feels complete.
  10. Logbook Then write the gap between that number and 0.
  11. Logbook That gap — felt as depth, read as headroom — is the lesson of this lesson.
Expected result

The same four recordings, transformed by faders alone: a deep, intimate room peaking ≈ −12 dBFS, with the detail at the edge of hearing and nothing asking to be louder.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Your loop sounds weak next to REF, not deepThe gaps are too small — everything pulled down together is just quieter, not deeperKeep the kick where it is and pull the others further apart; depth is distance, not level
The detail disappears entirely−34 is below your room’s noise or the loop’s densityRaise it until it just returns at the mark — the edge of attention, not past it
Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

The quiet test — it should bloom

Phase focus: the quiet test — it should bloom. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live In Arrangement view, tile the loop into a 2-minute sketch, mirroring the record’s patience — one element resting and returning, nothing dramatic.
  2. Live Turn the phones knob down — noticeably below the mark.
  3. Live This is the one sanctioned departure from the mark in Block 1, and it goes the safe direction.
  4. Live Play the sketch end to end at the low level.
  5. Live Listen for blooming: detail stepping forward, the room getting deeper, the music feeling complete at a whisper.
  6. Live Do the same with REF, then your sketch again.
  7. Logbook Note in the logbook which elements survived the quiet and which vanished.
  8. Live Return the phones knob to the mark, fix only what vanished — raise it just to the edge of return — and play the sketch once more at the mark to confirm nothing else moved.
Expected result

A sketch that blooms at low volume the way the record does — complete at a whisper, deep at the mark, and never dependent on the listening level for its weight.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The sketch thins out and dies when quietThe balance leans on level — gaps too small, or the kick carrying everythingRe-spread in phase 3: keep the loudest element conversational and push the others further down
You keep reaching for the phones knob to “check”Judging excitement instead of balanceFinish the full pass at one level before allowing any change — the urge itself goes in the logbook
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your sketch at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • What number does your master peak at while the music feels complete — and what does the gap to 0 dBFS mean now?
    Answer

    Around −12 dBFS, and the gap is no longer empty meter — it is the depth you can hear, held in reserve on purpose. The record proves a mix can be finished while using a fraction of the system’s range, because completeness lives in balance, not in level. Relisten to the record once watching your master meter: the most affecting minute of the strand barely moves it.

  • Play your sketch and the record quiet, back to back. Do both bloom — and if only one does, where is the difference?
    Answer

    If the record blooms and yours thins, the miss is almost always gap size: your elements sit too close together in level, so quietness collapses them into one gray layer. Distance between levels is what low volume reveals. Re-spread, then relisten quiet — the moment the loop keeps breathing at a whisper, you have copied the system.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • Carries into the lab: headroom is not unused space — “quiet, on purpose, for this music” is a defensible level decision at every stage of the ledger.
