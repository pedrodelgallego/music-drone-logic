Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: One Voice, Every Role

One bassline, one filter, your two hands — replicate a section of “Acid Tracks” closely enough to feel how one 303 carried a whole record.

The point

A single synthesizer line becomes “many sounds” when the filter and resonance are ridden — the listener counts instruments that don’t exist.

The system you are replicating
Source recordPhuture — “Acid Tracks”
The effectone machine heard as a whole band
The mechanismcutoff + resonance ridden through distinct states
Fidelityas close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • MicroFreak ¼″ out → M4 in 1; Digitakt connected and level-checked.
  • New set saved as REP-acid-tracks, tempo 122.
  • Resonance and cutoff are explained in plain words in the Lab 004 concepts table.
  • Timebox: 60–120 minutes. Transcribing the 303 line by ear is the slow part, and the filter ride usually needs a practice pass before the recorded one. Budget for both; stop when the ride lands, not before.
Listen first

Reference pass — 10 minutes

Listen to about one minute of “Acid Tracks”. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear — and write your answer before opening each reveal.

  1. How many “different sounds” would a casual listener count?
    Answer

    Most casual listeners count five or six “sounds” plus drums — a dull bass, a squelchy lead, a screaming noise, something rubbery. Write your number down before reading the next answer; the gap between the two numbers is the record’s trick.

  2. How many sound sources actually exist?
    Answer

    Two: one TB-303 line and a drum machine. Every “new instrument” you counted is the same 303 with the filter in a different state. Relisten to one minute and watch for the moment your ear re-labels it.

  3. What exactly changes when a “new sound” appears — pitch, or the filter’s state?
    Answer

    The filter’s state. The notes barely change — the pattern loops for minutes at a time — while cutoff and resonance redraw the timbre so completely that the pitch seems to change too. That illusion is the record.

  4. Is the squelch constant or ridden by hand?
    Answer

    Ridden by hand. The moves are irregular — they lean, hesitate, overshoot — in a way no LFO does. That human timing is exactly what your replica must capture, which is why this lesson records the ride live instead of drawing it.

  5. Where does the track breathe — does the filter ever close fully?
    Answer

    The filter pulls back down between peaks — never fully shut, but dull enough to reset your ear. Those choked stretches are the breathing; without them the screaming-open state would be exhausting instead of thrilling.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

  • Sound target — what success sounds like:
  • The 303 line, transcribed by ear, close enough that a friend hearing yours then theirs would hesitate.
  • The squelch states land where the record lands them — traceable to knob positions, not new notes.
  • The ride sounds like their hand, not an LFO.
  • Kick sits where theirs sits, plain, so the voice owns all attention.

Steps

The whole rig for this lesson — one voice does everything; the Digitakt kick stays on its own track and out of the way.

MIDI clip → MicroFreak → M4 in 1 → Live: MF PRINT (record) + Digitakt kick → own track
Phase 1

Set the reference

Everything in this lesson — the notes, the knob states, the timing of the squelch — is judged against the record, so the record goes on the bench first, looped and gain-matched. With a track this hypnotic it is especially easy to replicate the version in your head; the loop is the antidote.

Do this
  1. Drop “Acid Tracks” onto an audio track named REF.
  2. Find the most characteristic 8–16 bars and loop them.
  3. Gain-match with a Utility on REF.
  4. Keep REF one solo away for the whole session.
Expected result

A clean loop of the section whose squelch states you will be matching, at an honest level.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The loop stumbles at the seamLoop brace not on bar boundariesSnap the brace to whole bars; warp the clip to 122 if needed
REF always sounds “better” in A/BIt’s just louderPull the Utility down until levels genuinely match
Phase 2

Transcribe the line, match the sound

The 303 line is the entire melodic content of the record, so it gets transcribed by ear, note by note — slow, honest work that trains exactly the hearing this lesson is about. Then the MicroFreak gets dressed as a 303: low-pass filter, high resonance, a fast envelope. It will never be a clone; as close as the MicroFreak gets is the assignment.

Do this
  1. Transcribe the 303 line by ear — their notes, your best hearing of them — into a 16-step Live MIDI clip, MIDI To → MicroFreak.
  2. Solo REF, hum it, place it, compare; revise until the lines chase each other.
  3. Match the sound: low-pass, cutoff low, resonance around 60–70 %.
  4. Envelope: fast attack, medium decay. Paraphonic OFF.
  5. Tweak against REF until it is as close to the record’s 303 as the MicroFreak gets.
Expected result

Your line and theirs looping together — same notes, same gait, recognisably the same family of squelch.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The line sounds right but limps against REFMissed accents or slides — the 303’s gait lives thereVary velocity on the accented steps; revisit note lengths
Tone never squelches, just whistlesResonance high but cutoff parked too highStart cutoff low — the squelch lives in the bottom third of the knob
Phase 3

Arm the print, ride the filter

This is the performance at the heart of the record: the loop plays unchanged while your two hands move cutoff and resonance through the record’s four states. Physically, the resonant peak of the analog filter is sweeping across the line’s harmonics — each state parks that peak somewhere new, and your ear hears a new instrument. Record from before the first touch; the hesitations are part of the take.

  • Safety — before the “screaming open” state: resonance near self-oscillation gets loud and piercing fast. Headphones at the mark or lower — never turn up to inspect. Sweep slowly, hold the loud states for seconds only, and if it bites, pull resonance down first and reset immediately.
Do this
  1. Arm the print track — MF PRINT, Audio From → Ext. In → 1 — and start recording before you touch anything.
  2. Loop the clip and ride cutoff + resonance by hand, matching the record’s squelch states where they happen in the section: dull rubber → mid squelch → screaming open → choked back down, each arriving where theirs arrives.
  3. Record audio the whole time — no stopping to fix; a flawed full take beats a perfect fragment.
  4. Mind the safety note above before the “screaming open” state.
0:00–1:00
Dull rubber — cutoff low, the line is a muffled bass; let it establish.
1:00–2:00
Mid squelch — cutoff creeping up, the resonant peak starts to vowel.
2:00–3:00
Screaming open — cutoff high, resonance biting; hold the peaks for seconds only.
3:00–4:00
Choked back down — pull toward dull, never fully shut; the breath before the end.

Place the changes where the record places them — the minutes here are a scaffold; the record supplies the timing.

Expected result

One continuous take in which the same sixteen notes pass through four distinct characters, at the record’s moments.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The ride sounds like an LFOMovements too regular and continuousMove in gestures, not cycles — park in a state, lean, then move; listen to REF’s hesitations
States arrive late or pile upFirst recorded pass doubled as the rehearsalDo one practice ride without recording, then record the second
Phase 4

See it, then anchor it with the kick

First make the illusion visible: a spectrum analyzer shows the resonant peak as a bright spike that physically moves as you sweep — the “new sounds” are that spike in different places. Then the only other element enters: a plain kick, exactly where the record puts it, so the voice keeps all the attention.

Do this
  1. Drop a Spectrum on the synth track and watch the resonant peak — a bright spike that moves as you sweep cutoff. See it once and you’ll hear it forever.
  2. Add only a Digitakt kick where theirs sits — plain hits on the 16-step grid, placed where the record places them.
  3. No other elements, no effects — the dryness keeps the illusion honest.
Expected result

You can point at the spike that your ear was calling “a new instrument”, and the kick carries the pulse without stealing the show.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Spectrum shows no moving spikeDevice on the wrong track, or resonance too low to peakPut Spectrum on MF PRINT and raise resonance toward the matched setting
Kick swallows the lineKick level set by habit, not by the recordA/B against REF — their kick is plainer and lower than you think
Phase 5

Mark, compare, export

The verification is spatial: locators turn “it sounds close” into a measurable distance between where your states land and where theirs do. Then the study gets printed and filed.

Do this
  1. Listen back and mark with locators where each “new sound” appears in yours and where it appears in REF.
  2. Name the knob position that made each one — and how far it landed from the record’s moment.
  3. Export the four minutes — File → Export Audio/Video, WAV, named REP-acid-tracks.
Expected result

Paired locators a few seconds apart at worst, each labelled with a knob position — and a finished WAV.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A “new sound” has no knob position to nameIt came from a note change or an accident, not the filterRe-listen to that moment in REF — if their change is filter-only, re-ride yours there
The export contains REFReference track not muted at renderMute REF, re-export, and listen to the file before filing it
Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the looped section at matched volume, then answer:

  • 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate before saying which is which?
    Answer

    The squelch states are the giveaway: anyone can copy the notes, but a 303 peak has a specific vowel-like resonance the MicroFreak only approximates. If the friend wouldn’t hesitate, A/B one squelch peak solo against REF and adjust resonance and envelope decay before touching anything else.

  • Do your squelch states arrive where the record’s do — and can you trace every apparent new sound to a knob position rather than a new source?
    Answer

    Use your locators: each apparent “new sound” in yours should line up with a knob position you can name and a moment within a few seconds of the record’s. If one can’t be traced to a knob, you added a change the record doesn’t make — remove it. The record’s power is that the answer is always the filter.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines:
  • Matched: what your replica got closest to — the line, the squelch, the placement; start with which knob state landed nearest the record’s.
  • Missed: what the record does that yours doesn’t (best guess why).
  • Change next: the one thing you would do differently on the next attempt.
