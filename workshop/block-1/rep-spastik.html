Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: machine-tight, bone dry

Build a pattern with no echo, no swing, and a clean clock path — and prove with a ruler that nothing moved for 16 bars.

The point

Total tightness is not a feel — it is a clean clock path plus the discipline to leave everything dry.

The system you are replicating
Source recordPlastikman — “Spastik”
The effectevery hit lands once, on the grid, nothing to hide in
The mechanismone clock master + zero effects + zero doubles
FidelityAs close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Digitakt II main outs → M4 inputs 3/4, as wired in Lab 001
  • New set saved as REP-spastik, with Lab 002’s driver error compensation already entered
  • Phones knob at the Lab 001 mark
  • Timebox: one sitting, 60–120 minutes — the transcription and the A/B passes take the time; the clock setup is minutes
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to about one minute of “Spastik” and choose the 8–16 bars where the machine-tightness is most exposed — that section is what you will replicate. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Check each answer against the reveal only after you have written your own.

  1. Can you hear any echo tail, anywhere?
    Answer

    No — the track is bone dry, and the dryness is the design. Every hit stops the instant the drum sound itself stops; there is no reverb tail and no delayed copy for anything to hide in. If you think you hear a tail, relisten across a gap or a drop: what feels like “space” on this record is loudness and density, not echo.

  2. Do any two drums ever flam — land almost-together?
    Answer

    No. When two voices share a step they land as one composite hit — never as two attacks a few milliseconds apart. That is the sound of a single clock placing every event, and it is exactly what a sloppy clock chain destroys first. Pick the busiest moment of your section and listen for the attack: one edge, not two.

  3. Does the pattern’s feel change over a minute, or is it frozen?
    Answer

    The timing is frozen — the grid never breathes, drags, or pushes for the whole track. What changes constantly is the sound of the hits: tunings, decays, and timbres sweep while the placement stays locked. That separation — timing frozen, timbre alive — is the whole aesthetic you are replicating.

  4. What carries the excitement if nothing is loose? (Hint: p-lock-style timbre changes, not timing.)
    Answer

    Continuous timbral movement: drum voices detuned, opened, shortened, and rolled while the grid holds still. Because the timing offers zero variation, the ear locks onto the only thing that moves — the tone — and tiny changes feel enormous. Relisten and follow one voice for thirty seconds; you will hear it morph while never moving a millisecond.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: zero tail after the last hit — exactly as dry as the record.
  • No audible flam between any two voices, and no flam against REF when both play.
  • A solo’d bar of yours could pass for a solo’d bar of theirs.
  • Variation, if there is any, lives in timbre — not in timing — just as it does on the record.
Phase 1 · ~30 min

Reference in, pattern transcribed

Tightness can only be judged against the record, so the record comes into the set first. Then you transcribe the section’s pattern by ear, voice by voice, onto the Digitakt — not a generic techno pattern, theirs. This is the longest phase, and the correction passes against REF are the actual ear training.

Do this
  1. Drop “Spastik” into Live on a track named REF and loop the 8–16 bars you chose in the reference pass.
  2. Gain-match it with a Utility so it sits at your working level, and keep it one solo away for the whole sitting.
  3. On the Digitakt, press REC to enter grid recording and program the section’s actual pattern voice by voice — kick first, then snare, then hats.
  4. Loop yours against REF and correct until every hit lands where theirs lands. Do at least one full pass per voice — solo the kick against the record, then the snare, then the hats.
  5. Pick the closest Digitakt sounds to their kit and tune each one against the record — pitch, decay, attack — until a solo’d bar of yours could pass for theirs.

Phase 2 · ~10 min

Strip it bare, like the record

Dryness is a discipline, not an absence — every default send, swing amount, or reverb tail you forgot to remove is a second copy the record does not have. This phase is short but unforgiving: you go through every track and turn off everything that adds time.

Do this
  1. Set pattern swing to zero — no swing anywhere.
  2. Turn every FX send to zero: no delay, no reverb, on every track.
  3. Check each trig sits exactly on its step — no micro-timing offsets left over from jamming.
  4. Play the pattern and listen to the silence after the last hit. If a parameter adds a tail or a second copy the record does not have, it is off.
Expected result

Stop the pattern: the sound ends the instant the last drum’s own decay ends. Nothing rings, nothing smears, nothing repeats.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A tail remains after the last hitA send or master FX still activeWalk every track’s send amounts and the master FX page; zero them all
One voice still feels looseA leftover micro-timing nudge on its trigsHold each trig and confirm its micro-timing reads zero
Phase 3 · ~10 min

One clock master — wire the chain

Machine-tightness over minutes is a clock decision: exactly one device sets the tempo, and everything else follows. Live becomes the master, the Digitakt the follower, and the audio comes back in through the path you calibrated in Lab 002. This chain is what you will then prove with a ruler.

Live 12 · clock master → USB sync → Digitakt II · clock + transport receive → main outs → M4 in 3/4 → Live record · Ext. In 3/4 · Monitor Off
Do this
  1. Slave the Digitakt to Live’s clock, exactly as Lab 002 configured: in Live, ⌘ , → Link, Tempo & MIDI → enable Sync on the Digitakt output port.
  2. On the Digitakt: SETTINGS → MIDI CONFIG → SYNC → clock and transport receive on.
  3. Press play in Live and confirm the Digitakt starts and shows Live’s tempo.
  4. Restart the transport — stop and press play in Live so the Digitakt realigns to bar one. This is the Lab 002 reset rule, now in use before the take that matters.
Expected result

The Digitakt starts and stops with Live’s transport and holds Live’s tempo — one clock in the room, everything else following it.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Pattern runs at its own tempoClock receive off, or Sync enabled on the wrong port rowRe-check both ends: Live’s Sync on the Digitakt output port; Digitakt clock + transport receive on
Starts mid-pattern or offset by a beatTransport never restarted after syncingStop and press play in Live — the follower realigns to bar one
Phase 4 · ~20 min

Record 16 bars and measure the proof

Now the claim “nothing moved” becomes a measurement. You record 16 bars through the calibrated path, then zoom in and measure how far each kick sits from the grid line — at the start and at the end. A constant offset is latency: knowable, correctable. A growing offset is drift, and drift means the clock chain is wrong, not the feel.

Do this
  1. Arm an audio track set to Ext. In 3/4, Monitor Off, with driver error compensation already set from Lab 002.
  2. Restart the transport, then record 16 bars of the pattern into Live.
  3. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Switch the ruler to minutes:seconds:milliseconds, zoom to a kick at bar 1 and a kick at bar 16, and measure each one’s distance from the grid line.
  4. Write both offsets in ms in the logbook: constant = latency, growing = drift — exactly Lab 002’s distinction.
Expected result

Both kicks the same small distance from the grid — ideally on it, since compensation is entered. Sixteen bars apart, the offset has not grown: that is the record’s tightness, proven with a ruler.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Constant offset on every kickDriver error compensation unset or staleRe-run Lab 002’s loopback at the current buffer size and enter the difference

Phase 5 · ~20 min

Render and A/B against the record

The final judgment is comparative: sounds, pattern, and behavior together, your render against their section. Expect several passes — the first listens catch the big misses, the later listens catch the tone of a single hat. Each pass, name one difference out loud before you fix anything.

Do this
  1. Render the recorded loop to a clip on its own track.
  2. A/B it against the REF section: 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, levels matched.
  3. After each pass, write one line: the first thing that gave yours away. Fix only that one thing, then pass again.
  4. Stop after three to five passes, or when a pass finds nothing new — whichever comes first.
Expected result

A render where the remaining differences are ones you can name — and they live in timbre, not in timing. Would a friend hesitate to say which is which?

Verify against the record

A/B your render against the REF section, then answer:

  • At sample zoom, is every kick the same distance from the grid? Constant = latency. Growing = drift — go back to the clock chain.
    Answer

    On a healthy chain the answer is yes: every kick sits the same distance from its line, and with compensation entered that distance is near zero. A constant offset is the recording path’s latency and is fixable with one number; a growing offset cannot be fixed by any number, because it means two clocks disagree about how long a bar is. The measurement, not the feel, is what tells them apart.

  • Clap along for 30 seconds — do you drift, or does it? Dryness check: is there any tail after the last hit? There is none on the record.
    Answer

    If your clap and the loop part ways, you moved — a synced machine playing a frozen pattern does not. That is the same test the record passes: clap along to “Spastik” and the track never bends toward you. And after the last hit there should be nothing at all; any ringing is a send you missed in Phase 2, not the room of the record, because the record has no room.

  • 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate to say which is which? If not, is it the sounds or the timing that gives yours away?
    Answer

    By this point it should be the sounds — with a clean clock chain your timing is as machine-tight as theirs, so the remaining distance is timbre: the exact tuning of the kick, the snap of the snare, the length of the hat. That is the honest result of this lesson: timing is a solved problem once the chain is right, and the rest is sound design. Write whichever it is into the miss list.
