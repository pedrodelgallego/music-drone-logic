Block 1 · Replication lesson · Required drill 1 of 3 for Lab 003

Replicate: machine-tight, bone dry

Build a pattern with no echo, no swing, and a clean clock path — and prove with a ruler that nothing moved for 16 bars.

The point

Total tightness is not a feel — it is a clean clock path plus the discipline to leave everything dry.

Reference pass · 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to about one minute of “Spastik” and choose the 8–16 bars where the machine-tightness is most exposed — that section is what you will replicate. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Check each answer against the reveal only after you have written your own.

  1. Can you hear any echo tail, anywhere?
    Answer

    No — the track is bone dry, and the dryness is the design. Every hit stops the instant the drum sound itself stops; there is no reverb tail and no delayed copy for anything to hide in. If you think you hear a tail, relisten across a gap or a drop: what feels like “space” on this record is loudness and density, not echo.

  2. Do any two drums ever flam — land almost-together?
    Answer

    No. When two voices share a step they land as one composite hit — never as two attacks a few milliseconds apart. That is the sound of a single clock placing every event, and it is exactly what a sloppy clock chain destroys first. Pick the busiest moment of your section and listen for the attack: one edge, not two.

  3. Does the pattern’s feel change over a minute, or is it frozen?
    Answer

    The timing is frozen — the grid never breathes, drags, or pushes for the whole track. What changes constantly is the sound of the hits: tunings, decays, and timbres sweep while the placement stays locked. That separation — timing frozen, timbre alive — is the whole aesthetic you are replicating.

  4. What carries the excitement if nothing is loose? (Hint: p-lock-style timbre changes, not timing.)
    Answer

    Continuous timbral movement: drum voices detuned, opened, shortened, and rolled while the grid holds still. Because the timing offers zero variation, the ear locks onto the only thing that moves — the tone — and tiny changes feel enormous. Relisten and follow one voice for thirty seconds; you will hear it morph while never moving a millisecond.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: zero tail after the last hit — exactly as dry as the record.
  • No audible flam between any two voices, and no flam against REF when both play.
  • A solo’d bar of yours could pass for a solo’d bar of theirs.
  • Variation, if there is any, lives in timbre — not in timing — just as it does on the record.
Phase 1 · ~30 min

Reference in, pattern transcribed

Tightness can only be judged against the record, so the record comes into the set first. Then you transcribe the section’s pattern by ear, voice by voice, onto the Digitakt — not a generic techno pattern, theirs. Three voices are enough for the whole drill: kick, snare, closed hat. This is the longest phase, and the correction passes against REF are the actual ear training — Lab 003’s build step 02 assumes you can do every move in this list without looking it up.

Do this
  1. Drop “Spastik” into Live on a track named REF and loop the 8–16 bars you chose in the reference pass.
  2. Gain-match it with a Utility so it sits at your working level, and keep it one solo away for the whole sitting.
  3. On the Digitakt, select an empty pattern — a slot you have never jammed in, so no leftover trigs or parameter locks haunt the build.
  4. Select track 1 and load a kick from the +Drive sample browser — audition candidates against the record and take the closest, not the best. Track 2: the closest snare. Track 3: the closest closed hat. Three sounds; that is the whole kit.
  5. Press REC to enter grid recording. With track 1 selected, place the kick trigs with the 16 trig keys — a key lights where a trig sits. Then select track 2 and place the snare, then track 3 and the hats.
  6. Where the record accents or ghosts a hit, hold that trig and turn the velocity/level parameter — the change lands on that one step only. Velocity differences are allowed; timing differences are not.
  7. Loop yours against REF and correct until every hit lands where theirs lands. Do at least one full pass per voice — solo the kick against the record, then the snare, then the hats.
  8. Tune each sound against the record — pitch, decay, attack — until a solo’d bar of yours could pass for theirs. Then save the pattern: an unsaved pattern is a pattern you will lose.

Phase 2 · ~10 min

Strip it bare, like the record

Dryness is a discipline, not an absence — every default send, swing amount, or reverb tail you forgot to remove is a second copy the record does not have. The Digitakt makes it easy to forget: sends and swing persist per pattern, set once in some jam months ago and waiting. This phase is short but unforgiving: you go through every track and turn off everything that adds time. Dryness is the constraint of this drill, not a preference.

Do this
  1. Set pattern swing to zero — open the swing setting and confirm it reads 50%, the no-swing value, not a leftover groove from a jam.
  2. Turn every FX send to zero: on each of the three tracks, open its FX page and set the delay send and the reverb send to 0. Walk all three tracks; do not trust memory.
  3. Check each trig sits exactly on its step — hold every trig and confirm its micro-timing reads zero. No offsets left over from jamming.
  4. Velocity locks from Phase 1 stay — the record accents in timbre and level, never in time.
  5. Play the pattern and listen to the silence after the last hit. If a parameter adds a tail or a second copy the record does not have, it is off.
  6. Save the pattern again. The stripped state is the state Phase 4 records — and the state Lab 003 inherits as “a clean grid.”
Expected result

Stop the pattern: the sound ends the instant the last drum’s own decay ends. Nothing rings, nothing smears, nothing repeats. Swing reads 50%, every send reads 0, every trig’s micro-timing reads zero.

Phase 3 · ~10 min

One clock master — wire the chain

Machine-tightness over minutes is a clock decision: exactly one device sets the tempo, and everything else follows. Live becomes the master, the Digitakt the follower, and the audio comes back in through the path you calibrated in Lab 002. The chain has two ends and both must be set — Live must send, the Digitakt must receive — and a miss on either end produces a different, recognizable symptom. This exact configuration is the third line of Lab 003’s pre-flight checklist; here you wire it slowly, once, so there it takes thirty seconds.

Live 12 · clock master → USB sync → Digitakt II · clock + transport receive → main outs → M4 in 3/4 → Live record · Ext. In 3/4 · Monitor Off
Do this
  1. In Live, open ⌘ , → Settings/Preferences → Link, Tempo & MIDI → MIDI Ports and enable Sync on the Digitakt output port row — output, because the clock travels from Live out to the box. Exactly as Lab 002 configured it.
  2. On the Digitakt: Settings → MIDI Config → Sync → clock receive on and transport receive on. Both — clock alone follows the tempo but ignores play and stop.
  3. Press play in Live and confirm the Digitakt starts by itself and its display shows Live’s tempo.
  4. Change Live’s tempo by a few BPM and watch the Digitakt’s display follow. This proves the number is received, not coincidental.
  5. Restart the transport — stop and press play in Live so the Digitakt realigns to bar one. This is the Lab 002 reset rule, and from now on it runs before every take: stop, then play, then record.
Expected result

The Digitakt starts and stops with Live’s transport, holds Live’s tempo, and tracks a tempo change live on its display — one clock in the room, everything else following it.

Phase 4 · ~20 min

Record 16 bars and measure the proof

Now the claim “nothing moved” becomes a measurement. You record 16 bars through the calibrated path, then zoom in and measure how far each kick sits from the grid line — at the start and at the end. A constant offset is latency: knowable, correctable with one number. A growing offset is drift, and drift means the clock chain is wrong, not the feel. One recording detail matters before anything else: M4 inputs 3/4 are fixed line inputs with no gain knobs, so the Digitakt’s Main Volume is the record level — the Lab 001 ledger row that catches people.

Do this
  1. Create an audio track set to Ext. In 3/4, Monitor Off — you monitor the Digitakt through the path that already works, not through Live. Driver error compensation is already set from Lab 002.
  2. Check the level before the take: play the pattern and confirm Live’s input meter peaks ≈ −12 dBFS. If it does not, the only control is the Digitakt’s Main Volume — note its position for the numbers table below.
  3. Arm the track. Stop Live, press play — the transport restart from Phase 3 — then record 16 bars of the pattern and stop.
  4. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Switch the ruler to minutes:seconds:milliseconds, zoom all the way in on the first kick of bar 1, and measure its distance from the grid line. Then jump to bar 16 and measure that kick the same way.
  5. Write both offsets in ms into the numbers table: constant = latency, growing = drift — exactly Lab 002’s distinction, now applied to a musical take.

Phase 5 · ~20 min

Render and A/B against the record

The final judgment is comparative: sounds, pattern, and behavior together, your render against their section. Loudness matching matters here as much as it did in Lab 001 — the louder clip always wins, so an unmatched A/B measures volume, not closeness. Expect several passes — the first listens catch the big misses, the later listens catch the tone of a single hat. Each pass, name one difference out loud before you fix anything.

Do this
  1. Render the recorded 16 bars to a clip on its own track — consolidate the take so it loops cleanly from bar 1.
  2. A/B it against the REF section: 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, levels matched with a Utility until neither is obviously louder. Phones at the mark.
  3. After each pass, write one line: the first thing that gave yours away. Fix only that one thing, then pass again.
  4. Stop after three to five passes, or when a pass finds nothing new — whichever comes first.
Expected result

A render where the remaining differences are ones you can name — and they live in timbre, not in timing. Would a friend hesitate to say which is which?

Verify against the record

A/B your render against the REF section, then answer:

  • At sample zoom, is every kick the same distance from the grid? Constant = latency. Growing = drift — go back to the clock chain.
    Answer

    On a healthy chain the answer is yes: every kick sits the same distance from its line, and with compensation entered that distance is near zero. A constant offset is the recording path’s latency and is fixable with one number; a growing offset cannot be fixed by any number, because it means two clocks disagree about how long a bar is. The measurement, not the feel, is what tells them apart.

  • Clap along for 30 seconds — do you drift, or does it? Dryness check: is there any tail after the last hit? There is none on the record.
    Answer

    If your clap and the loop part ways, you moved — a synced machine playing a frozen pattern does not. That is the same test the record passes: clap along to “Spastik” and the track never bends toward you. And after the last hit there should be nothing at all; any ringing is a send you missed in Phase 2, not the room of the record, because the record has no room.

  • 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate to say which is which? If not, is it the sounds or the timing that gives yours away?
    Answer

    By this point it should be the sounds — with a clean clock chain your timing is as machine-tight as theirs, so the remaining distance is timbre: the exact tuning of the kick, the snap of the snare, the length of the hat. That is the honest result of this lesson: timing is a solved problem once the chain is right, and the rest is sound design. Write whichever it is into the miss list.

  • Your loop is tight but feels stiff next to the record — what would Spastik say is wrong?
    Answer

    Nothing is wrong; stiff is the aesthetic here, and the difference you sense is dryness plus zero variation — your hits never change timbre, while the record’s morph constantly over a frozen grid. Do not fix it by loosening the timing: that would un-learn the drill. The next two required drills add the vocabulary that loosens it on purpose — a deliberate double in one, an echo that breathes in the other.

  • Which two numbers prove the claim “nothing moved,” and what does each verdict mean?
    Answer

    The kick’s offset from the grid at bar 1 and at bar 16, in milliseconds. Equal offsets mean the only error is latency — one knowable number, already compensated or compensatable. A growing offset means drift: two devices disagreeing about time, which no compensation value can fix, only a re-wired clock chain. These two numbers, written in the logbook, are what Lab 003’s pre-flight “clock master declared” line silently assumes you can produce on demand.
