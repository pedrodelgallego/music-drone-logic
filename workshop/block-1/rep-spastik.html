Replicate: machine-tight, bone dry

Copy a short dry drum section, sync the Digitakt II to Live, record 16 bars, and measure that the hits stay in the same place.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches one simple thing: tight timing is something you can set up, record, and measure. The sound stays dry so nothing hides the timing.

Before you start
  • Digitakt II main outs → M4 inputs 3/4, as wired in Lab 001
  • New set saved as REP-spastik, with Lab 002’s driver error compensation already entered
  • Phones knob at the Lab 001 mark
  • If you split the work: session one = listen, program, strip, sync. Session two = record, measure, compare.
  • What you will make: a short Digitakt II drum pattern that copies one 8–16 bar section of “Spastik”.
  • What you will turn off: swing, delay, reverb, and micro-timing.
  • What you will prove: the kick at bar 1 and the kick at bar 16 sit the same distance from the grid.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are sound choices, not timing problems.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of “Spastik”. Choose the clearest 8–16 bars. That is the section you will copy. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Can you hear any echo tail, anywhere?
    Answer

    No. The track is dry. The drum hit ends, then the sound is gone. If you hear “space”, check again during a gap: it is density and level, not reverb or delay.

  2. Do any two drums ever flam — land almost-together?
    Answer

    No. When two sounds hit together, they arrive as one attack. You should not hear one hit slightly after the other. That “almost together” sound is what this drill removes.

  3. Does the pattern’s feel change over a minute, or is it frozen?
    Answer

    The timing is frozen. The hits do not push, drag, or swing. What changes is the tone of the hits: tuning, decay, brightness, and pressure.

  4. What carries the excitement if nothing is loose? (Hint: p-lock-style timbre changes, not timing.)
    Answer

    Tone changes carry the excitement. The grid stays still, but the drum sounds change shape. Follow one sound for 30 seconds and listen for pitch, decay, or brightness changes.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: dry drums. No echo. No reverb tail.
  • Timing target: no flams between your sounds, and no flams against REF.
  • Copy target: a soloed bar of yours should feel close to a soloed bar of the record.
  • Variation rule: change tone if needed. Do not loosen timing.
Your rig
Phase 1 · ~30 min

Put the reference in Live and copy the pattern

First, put the record in the Live set so you can compare against it quickly. Then copy the pattern onto the Digitakt II one sound at a time. Use only three tracks: kick, snare, closed hat. The goal is not to invent a good techno loop. The goal is to copy where the record puts its hits.

Do this
  1. Drop “Spastik” into Live on a track named REF.
  2. Loop the 8–16 bars you chose in the listening pass.
  3. Put a Utility on REF and set the level so it is comfortable and not louder than your work.
  4. Press [PTN], use [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to choose a bank, then press an unused [TRIG] slot.
  5. Press [PLAY]. If you hear anything, press [STOP] and choose another pattern slot.
  6. [TRK] + [TRIG 1] selects track 1. Load the closest kick you can find in the +Drive.
  7. Press [SRC], turn DATA ENTRY knob D to open the sample list, audition with the active track key, and press [YES] to load the sample.
  8. [TRK] + [TRIG 2] selects track 2. Load the closest snare.
  9. Press [SRC], turn DATA ENTRY knob D to open the sample list, audition with the active track key, and press [YES] to load the sample.
  10. [TRK] + [TRIG 3] selects track 3. Load the closest closed hat.
  11. Press [SRC], turn DATA ENTRY knob D to open the sample list, audition with the active track key, and press [YES] to load the sample.
  12. Press [RECORD] to enter grid recording.
  13. The [RECORD] key should light red.
  14. [TRK] + [TRIG 1] selects track 1. Place the kick trigs with the numbered [TRIG] keys.
  15. A lit trig key means there is a hit on that step.
  16. [TRK] + [TRIG 2] selects track 2. Place the snare trigs with the numbered [TRIG] keys.
  17. [TRK] + [TRIG 3] selects track 3. Place the hat trigs with the numbered [TRIG] keys.
  18. If one hit is louder or softer on the record, hold that trig and change velocity or level for that step only.
  19. Compare one voice at a time: kick against REF, then snare, then hat.
  20. Fix placement first.
  21. Then tune pitch, decay, and attack.
  22. Press [FUNC] + [YES] to temporarily save the active pattern.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Plastikman — “Spastik” and use Drum Rack, MIDI clips, Groove Pool, and clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — Put the reference in Live and copy the pattern — as the smallest Live set that proves one clock master, no swing, no delay, no reverb.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects each drum hit is exact, dry, and exposed.

  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates one clock master, no swing, no delay, no reverb, not just the surface of Plastikman — “Spastik”.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.

Phase 2 · ~10 min

Turn off everything that smears timing

Now remove anything that can blur the hits. Swing moves hits away from the grid. Delay creates extra copies. Reverb adds tails. Micro-timing nudges hits early or late. This drill needs none of that.

Do this
  1. Press [TEMPO].
  2. Turn DATA ENTRY knob D until SWING reads 50%.
  3. Confirm SWING still reads 50% before you leave the tempo page. On the Digitakt II, 50% means no swing.
  4. [TRK] + [TRIG 1] selects track 1. Press [FX]. Set delay send to 0 and reverb send to 0.
  5. [TRK] + [TRIG 2] selects track 2. Press [FX]. Set delay send to 0 and reverb send to 0.
  6. [TRK] + [TRIG 3] selects track 3. Press [FX]. Set delay send to 0 and reverb send to 0.
  7. Press [RECORD] for grid recording.
  8. Hold each lit [TRIG] key and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] until micro-timing is centered at zero.
  9. Keep velocity or level locks if you used them.
  10. Those change loudness, not timing.
  11. Play the pattern.
  12. Stop it.
  13. Listen after the last hit.
  14. Nothing should ring or repeat.
  15. Press [FUNC] + [YES] again to update the temporary save.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Plastikman — “Spastik” and use Drum Rack, MIDI clips, Groove Pool, and clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — Turn off everything that smears timing — as the smallest Live set that proves one clock master, no swing, no delay, no reverb.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects each drum hit is exact, dry, and exposed.

Phase 3 · ~10 min

Make Live the clock master

Only one device should decide the tempo. In this drill, Live is the master and the Digitakt II follows. Set both ends: Live must send clock, and the Digitakt II must receive clock and transport.

Live 12 · clock master → USB sync → Digitakt II · clock + transport receive → main outs → M4 in 3/4 → Live record · Ext. In 3/4 · Monitor Off
Do this
  1. Open ⌘ , → Settings/Preferences → Link, Tempo & MIDI → MIDI Ports.
  2. Find the Digitakt II output port row.
  3. Turn Sync on for that output row.
  4. Output matters because the clock is leaving Live and going to the Digitakt II.
  5. Press [SETTINGS].
  6. Use [UP]/[DOWN] or LEVEL/DATA to select MIDI Config, press [YES], select Sync, and press [YES].
  7. Highlight Clock Receive and press [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] until it reads ON.
  8. Highlight Transport Receive and press [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] until it reads ON.
  9. This lets Live’s play and stop buttons control the Digitakt II.
  10. Press play in Live.
  11. The Digitakt II should start by itself.
  12. Change Live’s tempo by a few BPM.
  13. The Digitakt II display should follow.
  14. Stop Live.
  15. Press play again.
  16. This resets the Digitakt II to bar 1 before the take.

Phase 4 · ~20 min

Record 16 bars and measure the kicks

Now prove the timing with a recording. Record 16 bars. Zoom in on the first kick and the bar 16 kick. Measure how far each one is from the grid line. If the distance stays the same, the clock is stable. If the distance grows, something is drifting.

Do this
  1. Press ⌘ T in Live to create a new audio track.
  2. Set its input to Ext. In 3/4.
  3. Set the track Monitor selector to Off.
  4. Play the Digitakt II pattern and check Live’s input meter.
  5. Aim for peaks around −12 dBFS.
  6. If the level is wrong, change the Digitakt II Main Volume.
  7. Write down the Digitakt II Main Volume position.
  8. Click the track Arm button so it turns red.
  9. Stop Live.
  10. Press play again so the Digitakt II restarts at bar 1.
  11. Record 16 bars.
  12. Stop.
  13. Switch Live’s ruler to minutes:seconds:milliseconds.
  14. Zoom in on the first kick of bar 1. Measure the distance from the grid line to the start of the waveform.
  15. Zoom in on the kick at bar 16. Measure the same distance.
  16. Write both numbers in the table below.

Phase 5 · ~20 min

Compare your loop to the record

Last, compare your recording with the reference section. Match the loudness first. A louder clip will usually sound better, even if it is not closer. Fix one problem per pass.

Do this
  1. Select the 16-bar recording and press ⌘ J to consolidate it so it loops cleanly from bar 1.
  2. Put it on its own track.
  3. Use Utility to match the loudness of your loop and REF.
  4. Listen to 10 seconds of yours.
  5. Listen to 10 seconds of the record.
  6. Write one sentence: “The first thing that gives mine away is ___.”
  7. Fix only that one thing.
  8. Repeat the A/B pass three to five times, or stop when you stop hearing new problems.

Final check

Play your loop against the REF section and answer these in the logbook:

  • At sample zoom, is the bar 16 kick the same distance from the grid as the bar 1 kick?
    Answer

    Yes is the target. Same distance means the system is stable. If both kicks are equally late, that is latency. If the bar 16 kick is farther away, that is drift, and you need to fix the clock chain.

  • After the last hit, is there any tail, repeat, or room sound?
    Answer

    No is the target. If something rings, delay or reverb is still on somewhere. Go back to Phase 2 and check each track.

  • When you switch between yours and theirs, what gives yours away first: sound choice, pattern placement, or timing?
    Answer

    By the end, timing should not be the main problem. If the clock is stable, the remaining gap is usually sound: the kick tuning, snare snap, hat length, or level balance.

  • If your loop feels stiff, what should you avoid changing?
    Answer

    Do not loosen the timing. Stiffness is part of this lesson. If you want more life, change tone, decay, level, or accents while keeping the grid locked.

  • Which two numbers prove the timing?
    Answer

    The kick offset at bar 1 and the kick offset at bar 16, both in milliseconds. Equal numbers mean stable timing. Growing numbers mean drift.
