Block 1 · Replication R1 · Study

Rebuild the Loop

Take the eight bars you analyzed on paper in L1 and build them yourself. Where your version sounds close, your analysis was right. Where it doesn’t, you have found — precisely — what you can’t hear yet. That gap is the product of this lab.

Replication lab · S1 · E2 M1 F1 · half-cycle — requires the L1 Samples session · filed as study, never portfolio

listen → hypothesis → draw the path → calibrate → build → fault → diagnose → print → delta
Start here
  • Put the L1 Session 2 worksheet beside the computer; no worksheet, no R1.
  • Save a new set as R1-study.
  • Build only the eight bars you analyzed, from your own guesses.
  • Stop at the timebox. The corrected worksheet is the deliverable; the replica is evidence.
The point

Rebuilding a record from your own analysis is the fastest honest test of your ears — you can’t fake a rebuild.

Why replication

Painters copy masters; players transcribe solos. A worksheet analysis is a guess until something tests it — and the rebuild is the test. You are not copying to sound like the record; you are copying to find out what you failed to hear.

How this lab works

Set up a fair fight

  • The record on one track, your rebuild on others
  • Both equally loud, so neither side cheats
  • One solo press flips between them — the A/B rig

Build each claim

  • Every worksheet row becomes one Live track
  • Building it proves the guess — or breaks it
  • A row too vague to build is itself a finding

Name every miss

  • List every difference you can hear
  • File each one under the layer it lives in
  • Correct the worksheet — that is what leaves the room

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
Replication vs copyingCopying imitates a result, usually from a tutorial. Replication rebuilds a system from your own analysis, to test whether the analysis was right. Same hands, opposite purpose.
A/BSwitching between two versions of the same passage — quickly, over and over — to hear the differences. Serial, one at a time, never both at once: playing them together hides timing and space differences.
Matched loudnessMaking both versions equally loud before comparing them. The louder one always sounds better — automatically, even to professionals. Match first, judge second.
The four layersEvery difference between your rebuild and the record lives somewhere. Material: it’s a different sound-object. Signal: same object, different place or size in the room. Control: same sound, but it moves differently over time. Time: same sound, but it lands differently against the beat. Naming the layer tells you which question you answered wrong.
Gear miss vs hearing missA gear miss is “my equipment can’t make that sound” — a note for the shopping list. A hearing miss is “I didn’t even notice that until I tried to build it” — the real finding: the exact edge of your current ear.

1 · Target & analysis

The target is the track you took apart in L1’s Samples session — one of the three records, or the hypnotic track you chose instead. Pick the densest eight bars. Your Session 2 worksheet is the blueprint: each element, your guess at its source, your guess at its chain, in order. If a row is too vague to build from, sharpen it before you start. Finding out a row was unbuildable already counts — that is the lab working.

  • The firewall. Open a new project and name it R1-study. The replica is study evidence, never portfolio output: it is not published, never “finished,” and nothing from this session — no clip, no patch, no resample — moves into portfolio work. The corrected worksheet is what leaves this room; the audio does not.
  • Worksheet only. No tutorials, no preset packs, no breakdown videos. Someone else’s analysis would test someone else’s hearing. If you get stuck, go back to the record with the six questions — not to a search bar.
Before you start
  • The Session 2 worksheet from L1 on the desk
  • The reference track available as a local audio file — a stream cannot be dragged onto a track
  • A new Live set saved as R1-study — the firewall starts at the project name
  • Phones at the lab-001 mark
  • Timebox: two sessions, three at most — write the end date down
Before building

Pre-commit the predictions

In the logbook, before you make any sound: which elements do you expect to nail, which do you expect to miss, and why? Writing the prediction down first is the only honest way to find out what you actually believed. The misses you failed to predict are worth double.

2 · Rebuild & verdict

Setup

The A/B rig, exactly

Do this
  • Drag the reference file onto a new audio track named REF. In clip view, switch Warp off — you are listening, not syncing.
  • Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and pull its Gain down until REF’s average level matches your rebuild bus by ear. Louder always wins, so match before judging.
  • Set Live’s tempo to the reference: click Tap in time with the kick for a few bars, or count bars against the clock and do the arithmetic.
  • Set an 8-bar loop brace over the densest bars you analyzed — the same eight bars the worksheet describes.
  • Select your rebuild tracks and group them (Cmd+G) into a group named REBUILD — one solo button for your whole side of the comparison.
Expected result

You can solo REF, then solo REBUILD, at the same perceived loudness, on the same loop, hands-free.

If it fails
SymptomMove
REF drifts against the gridWarp off means the grid is decorative for that clip — loop by time, not bars, or warp ONE downbeat only and leave the rest alone.
Everything you build sounds worse than the recordIt always does at first; that is data, not a verdict — proceed.
Logbook

The loop’s bar range and the Utility gain offset.

REF (Utility, gain-matched) → solo → master → phones REBUILD group (your tracks) → solo → master → phones

Only one is ever audible at a time — the comparison is serial, never layered, because layering hides timing and space misses.

One worksheet row = one track = one guess the build will prove or break.

Worksheet rowLive trackFirst move
Kick guessRB-KICKFind or synthesize the closest object on the Digitakt or MicroFreak per your source guess — start from the worksheet’s source column, not from a kick you already like.
Chord/stab guessRB-CHORDMicroFreak, engine per your guess; wire the chain in the worksheet’s order.
Hat/texture guessRB-HAT / RB-TEXSame move: source guess first, then the chain in worksheet order.
  1. Timebox first. Two sessions; three at the absolute most. The replica is finished when the timebox ends, not when it is perfect — you are after system accuracy, not polish. The end date is already in the logbook from the checklist above: when it arrives, render and stop, mid-miss if necessary.
  2. Build each worksheet row. Source guess → make it with your rig (MicroFreak, Digitakt, Live — their gear is irrelevant; the system is what you’re reproducing). Chain guess → wire it in the worksheet’s order. Work the mapping table above top to bottom, one track per row, gains set by the lab-001 ledger.
  3. Match it closely — everything you can hear. Same elements, same notes and voicings transcribed by ear, the same timbre as close as the rig gets, the same variation mechanism. Closeness is the measure of your hearing; every compromise you are forced into is a finding, not a failure. On RB-CHORD, also ask “what makes it change over eight bars?” and build that mechanism; if the answer is “nothing,” that worksheet row needs sharpening first.
  4. A/B at matched loudness. Thirty seconds REF solo, thirty seconds REBUILD solo, back and forth, on the DT 990s at the mark — never both at once. Matched loudness or the louder one wins automatically and the comparison lies. Listen for one layer per pass: kick only, then space, then movement, then timing.
  5. Write the miss list. Every audible difference, each assigned to the layer where it lives — use the “Where does the miss live?” table below to file it. The layer matters more than the miss: it tells you which question you answered wrong.
  6. Correct the worksheet. Re-answer the rows the rebuild proved wrong — strike the old claim, write the corrected one beside it. The corrected analysis is the deliverable that outlives the replica.
  7. Close and quarantine. Render the eight bars (loop brace selected, File → Export Audio/Video) into the logbook as study evidence, close the project, and don’t reopen it for portfolio work. Carry the miss list forward: lab 003’s hypothesis should be sharper for it.
The difference is…LayerExample
A different object entirelyMaterialTheir hat is metal; mine is noise
Same object, different place or sizeSignalTheirs sits in a room; mine is dry and close
Same sound, moves differentlyControlTheir chord opens over 16 bars; mine is static
Same sound, lands differently in timeTimeTheirs leans behind the beat; mine is on the grid
Decision check — gear miss or hearing miss?

For each item on the miss list: did the replica fail because the rig can’t make that sound, or because you didn’t notice it until you tried to build it? A gear miss is a note for the gear roadmap. A hearing miss is the gold — it is the precise edge of your current ear, located.

Pass gate — R1 complete when
  • The replica render — ≈8 bars, filed in the logbook as study
  • The Session 2 worksheet it was rebuilt from, with corrections marked
  • A/B notes at matched loudness
  • The miss list — each miss assigned to its layer, gear or hearing named
  • One sentence: what can you hear now that you couldn’t before?

No fault ritual, no full bundle — this is a half-cycle. If the replica sounds embarrassingly far from the record, the lab still passed: the distance, named layer by layer, was the deliverable.

Replication principle

The replica is disposable; the corrected analysis is not. Copy to learn what you failed to hear — then compose your own.
