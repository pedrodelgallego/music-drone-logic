Replicate: the mantra loop

Copy one clear section of Function — “Voiceprint”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a fixed vocal grain placed identically every bar over a hypnotic bed; mutes and returns of the surrounding elements do the form. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: Digitakt II main L/R → M4 in 3–4 for the bed, plus one voice into the M4 — your own through input 1 is fine, spoken at conversation level; the MicroFreak rests this lesson
  • New set saved as REP-VOICEPRINT · tempo near the record’s — write it down
  • Phones at the lab-001 mark — and mind the mic path: an open input monitoring your own voice can feed back through speakers, so track the syllable on phones
  • What you will make: a short replica of Function — “Voiceprint”. Focus on this effect: a syllable-sized voice fragment carries a whole track; the tension is in what leaves and comes back around it, never in the fragment changing.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a fixed vocal grain placed identically every bar over a hypnotic bed; mutes and returns of the surrounding elements do the form.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Function — “Voiceprint”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is the vocal fragment — and can you still hear it as a word?
    Answer

    At first, maybe - a syllable with the shape of speech. A few minutes in, it is a percussion hit that happens to be made of voice.

  2. Does the fragment itself ever change?
    Answer

    No. Same grain, same pitch, same placement, every bar - the track’s one fixed point. Whatever you remember as the voice “developing” was the space around it changing while it held still.

  3. Where does the tension come from, if nothing in the loop develops?
    Answer

    From removal and return. Elements leave; their absence builds a debt; they come back and the return lands like an event many times its actual size.

  4. How is the grain placed against the bar?
    Answer

    Identically, every bar - it is a landmark, not a melody. Because it never moves, it becomes the clock you measure everything else against: you notice the kick leaving because the grain stays.

  5. What happens at the moment an element returns?
    Answer

    It reads as bigger than it is. Nothing about the returning element changed - but the frame held still around its absence, so the re-entry carries all the weight the absence accumulated.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and yours lands on the same economy — one fixed vocal landmark over a hypnotic bed, with every event a removal or a return, at matched loudness.
  • The grain never changes — not its pitch, not its placement, not its level — from the moment it is committed.
  • The only allowed moves for the whole arrangement: track mutes, and one send amount.
  • No new material after the bed exists; if the piece feels empty, the answer is a longer absence, not another sound.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one syllable sampled and trimmed, placed on the same step of every bar over a kick-and-hat bed, and a three-to-four-minute form written entirely with mute buttons and a single send amount.

one syllable · sampled into the Digitakt II → same step · every bar · never edited → kick + hat bed → form = mutes + one send

Four minutes drawn as rows. The grain’s row never breaks — identical hit, identical place, start to finish. Every other row is interrupted and restored, and those interruptions are the entire form. Nothing in the picture changes shape; things only disappear and come back.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the bed

Phase focus: the reference and the bed. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Voiceprint” in, and loop three to four characteristic minutes.
  2. Audio Effects → Utility, trim to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. REF stays one solo-press away all lesson.
  4. Press [RECORD]. Put kick trigs on track 1 at steps 1/5/9/13, then put closed-hat trigs on track 2 on the offbeats — the lab-003 grid, kept deliberately plain.
  5. Add one quiet percussion or texture on track 3 if the record’s section carries one;
  6. nothing else.
  7. Set levels by the ledger; the bed peaks ≈ −12 dBFS into Live.
  8. Save the pattern.
  9. Put a dub-style delay (or a small dark reverb, whichever the section suggests) on a return, and send the hat to it at zero for now — this send amount is the one continuous control the arrangement will own.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Function — “Voiceprint” (Incubation, Ostgut Ton, 2013) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the bed — as the smallest Live set that proves a fixed vocal grain placed identically every bar over a hypnotic bed; mutes and returns of the surrounding elements do the form.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects a syllable-sized voice fragment carries a whole track; the tension is in what leaves and comes back around it, never in the fragment changing.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the bed into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates a fixed vocal grain placed identically every bar over a hypnotic bed; mutes and returns of the surrounding elements do the form, not just the surface of Function — “Voiceprint” (Incubation, Ostgut Ton, 2013).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, a plain hypnotic bed runs beside it, and a return sits wired at zero — a frame with nothing on it yet.

Phase 2 · ≈ 20 min

Capture one syllable

Phase focus: capture one syllable. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Arm a Live track on M4 input 1 and record ten seconds of yourself speaking a few words at conversation level, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Any words — meaning is about to be removed anyway.
  3. Zoom in and crop one syllable — consonant onset to the early part of the vowel, roughly 150–250 ms.
  4. Shorter reads as a click; longer stays a word too stubbornly.
  5. Apply a short fade-out so the cut end does not tick, then drag the crop into the Digitakt II (or a Simpler) on its own track.
  6. Tune it once against the bed — shift in semitones until it sits with the kick’s register — and set its level so it reads as an instrument, not a vocal feature.
  7. These settings are now frozen.
  8. Audition it as a one-shot a few times.
  9. It should already sound less like speech on the fifth hit than the first — the dissolve starting before the loop even runs.

Phase 3 · ≈ 15 min

Fix the landmark and wear the word out

Phase focus: fix the landmark and wear the word out. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Place the grain on one step — the same step in every bar.
  2. Against the record’s example, somewhere off the downbeat works: the grain answers the kick rather than doubling it.
  3. No p-locks, no conditional trigs, no variation of any kind on this track — every device’s “keep it interesting” feature stays off.
  4. The grain’s job is to be the thing that never moves.
  5. Run the full loop — bed plus grain — and listen for three minutes without touching anything.
  6. Write down the minute mark at which the syllable stops meaning anything to you.
  7. Compare that mark with the one from your reference pass.
  8. The two numbers should be the same order of magnitude — satiation has a speed, and you have now measured it twice.

Phase 4 · ≈ 30 min

Compose with mutes and one send

Phase focus: compose with mutes and one send. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Plan the form on paper first, as a list of exits and entries with bar numbers: “kick out at 32 · hat send rises 48–64 · kick returns at 64.” Eight to twelve events is plenty for three to four minutes.
  2. One law in the plan: the grain’s track is never muted and never sent.
  3. It plays its step in every bar of the piece, including any intro and the last bar.
  4. Record the arrangement in one take into Live: track mutes on the Digitakt II (or Live’s track activators) plus the one send amount, performed by hand.
  5. Mistakes stay — a corrected performance is no longer a performance.
  6. Listen back end to end and audit against the plan: every audible event must be a removal, a return, or the send moving.
  7. Anything else — a level ridden, a sound swapped — disqualifies the take;
  8. go again.

A three-to-four-minute piece carried by a syllable that never changes, whose every event is an exit or an entry — and a paper plan the recording can be audited against, line by line.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no touching while you judge.

  • Did the syllable stop meaning anything?
    Answer

    It must — that dissolve is the mechanism made audible, and you timed it twice: once on the record, once on your own grain. If yours still reads as a word at the end of the piece, the grain is too long or too sparse, and phase 3 has the fix. Relisten to your final take as a stranger would and notice the moment you forget a person ever said it.

  • Is every event in your piece a removal or a return?
    Answer

    Audit it against the paper plan: each audible event should match a written exit, entry, or send move, and the grain’s row should be unbroken start to finish. If an event has no line in the plan, you composed by accident; if the grain’s row broke, the landmark failed and the piece lost its clock. The record passes this audit — that economy is the lesson.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
