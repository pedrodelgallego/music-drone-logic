Replicate: sine and click as the only material

Copy one clear section of Ryoji Ikeda — Test Pattern. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: nothing in the palette but sine tones and clicks, where the only variables are level and pulse-rate. There is no timbral change to hide behind. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged; the Digitakt II carries the clicks this lesson
  • New set saved as REP-IKEDA — set a tempo only as a clock for the click trains; the pitches stay flat sines
  • High sines and sharp clicks are deceptive on the ear — they meter modestly and fatigue fast, and sharp transients can spike past where the meter shows. Phones at the lab-001 mark or lower, a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into rate and level decisions, not extra sounds
  • What you will make: a short replica of Ryoji Ikeda — Test Pattern. Focus on this effect: a piece that sounds like a measuring instrument made musical, organised by rate and loudness alone.
  • What you will turn off: reverb tails, filter sweeps, any sound with a recognisable timbre or movement inside it.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: only sine tones and clicks, where LEVEL and pulse-rate ARE the composition — no timbral change.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Ryoji Ikeda — Test Pattern. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Name every kind of sound you can hear.
    Answer

    Two families only: pure pitched tones (sines, with no overtones to give them a “timbre”) and dry clicks (impulses with no body and no tail). If you wrote a third thing, listen again — it is almost always one of those two at a different rate or level.

  2. What is actually changing from second to second?
    Answer

    How fast the clicks repeat, and how loud each element is. That is the entire vocabulary. No sound morphs into another sound; nothing is filtered open or closed. Rate and level do all the work.

  3. Why does it feel rhythmic without a drum kit?
    Answer

    Because a click train at a fixed rate is rhythm in its purest form — a metronome with no kick to dress it up. Speed the rate past roughly 20 per second and the rhythm fuses into a pitch; that boundary is one of the piece’s favourite places to sit.

  4. Is there any reverb or space?
    Answer

    Almost none. The sounds are bone dry and sit right against your ear. Dryness is part of the instrument-readout illusion — measuring devices do not have room ambience, and neither does this.

  5. Where is the “composition” if nothing changes colour?
    Answer

    In the sequence of rates and levels. The score is a list of “this sine at this level, these clicks at this rate, now switch.” With colour removed, the arrangement of rate and loudness over time is the music — there is nowhere else for it to be.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. Use the scale/length menu to run a click track faster than 1/16 so a single track can pulse at audio rates.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample — load a single dry click or impulse. Press [FX] but leave sends at zero this lesson. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync so the click clock matches Live.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control — choose a basic waveform and close the filter until only the fundamental survives, so the tone is effectively a sine. Set envelope to instant on/off and leave the LFO at zero.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same austerity — pure pitched tones and dry clicks, with nothing in between to soften them.
  • Two sound families and nothing else — no reverb, no filter movement, no third texture to rescue you.
  • Every event is a rate change or a level change; nothing changes colour on purpose.
  • Decisions are switches, not fades — a section either is at this rate and level or it isn’t.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a pure sine from the synth, a dry click train from the Digitakt, and a switching list of rates and levels that is the entire score.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a section of Test Pattern into it.
  2. Find the clearest minute — one where you can hear the clicks change rate distinctly — and loop it.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. ⌘ T for the sine, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed; name it SINE.
  6. One audio track for the clicks from the Digitakt; name it CLICK.
  7. Write in the logbook the click rate at three moments in the loop — count clicks per two seconds and halve it, or just mark “slow / fast / fused into a pitch.”
  8. Keep those three readings; phase 4 will test whether yours match.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Ryoji Ikeda — Test Pattern (2000s) and use Operator (single sine partial) plus an impulse sample on a drum rack.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the track — as the smallest Live set that proves a piece built only from sine tones and clicks where level and pulse-rate are the composition.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects rate and loudness organised into a measuring-instrument sound.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: translate The reference and the track into FM tracks (a single operator gives a clean sine), sound locks, trig conditions, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern or control path so it demonstrates sine tones and clicks where level and pulse-rate are the composition, not just the surface of Test Pattern.
  3. Keep one clear performance control — the click rate — and name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, two named tracks wait for the sine and the clicks, and three click-rate readings are on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

Make the two pure materials — sine and click

Phase focus: make the two pure materials. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: pick a basic waveform and close the filter cutoff until only the fundamental survives — the goal is a sine with no audible overtones.
  2. Set the amp envelope to instant attack and instant release so the tone snaps on and off; no fade, no tail.
  3. Zero the LFO and any filter movement — the tone must not breathe.
  4. Print one steady high sine into SINE, peaking ≈ −12 dBFS.
  5. Digitakt: load a single dry click or impulse sample into one track; turn all sends (delay, reverb) to zero.
  6. Set that track to a clearly audible repeat rate — start at 1/16 at the set tempo.
  7. Print a short pass of clicks at one steady rate into CLICK, same gain discipline.
  8. Confirm both are bone dry — solo each and listen for any tail; if there is one, kill it at the source.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: build the sine in Operator (one partial, no others) and the click as a one-sample-short impulse in a drum rack.
  2. Build the phase goal — the two pure materials — as the smallest Live set that proves only sine tones and clicks, level and rate doing the work.
  3. Keep both on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Resample each to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds against REF and change only the setting that most affects the bare, dry character.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: a single FM operator at a fixed ratio gives a clean sine; a fast envelope on a noise/impulse source gives the click.
  2. Program the two sounds so they demonstrate sine and click with no timbre to hide behind, not just the surface of Test Pattern.
  3. Keep the click rate as the one performance control; name it before recording.
  4. Record both into Live or a recorder, then compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a target-box shortcut only when the same bareness stays audible.
Expected result

A pure sine and a dry click train, both printed, both at ≈ −12 dBFS, both with no tail and no movement inside them. Neither is a piece yet; the rate and level decisions are about to make it one.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Compose with rate and level only

Phase focus: compose with rate and level only. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Write a switch list on paper first: four or five sections, each named only by its rate and level — “sine alone, soft” → “clicks 1/16, loud” → “clicks doubled to 1/32” → “clicks so fast they fuse into a pitch.”
  2. That list is the score. Nothing on it mentions colour, because there is no colour to mention.
  3. In Arrangement, place the sections as hard switches — at the boundary one rate stops and the next starts; no crossfade.
  4. Push at least one section past roughly 20 clicks per second so the rhythm fuses into a pitch — Ikeda lives on that boundary.
  5. Use level as a second axis: the same rate at two different levels reads as two different sections.
  6. The rule: if you reach for a filter sweep or a reverb to make a transition interesting, delete the reach — the interest must come from the rate or the level.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: drive the click rate from clip note-resolution and automate level per scene.
  2. Build the phase goal — compose with rate and level only — as the smallest Live set that proves level and pulse-rate are the composition.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Resample the result to PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds against REF and change only the rate or level that most affects the switch-list feel.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: use trig conditions and pattern length to switch click rates; lock the sine pitch and ride track level.
  2. Program the switch list so it demonstrates rate and level as the only variables, not just the surface of Test Pattern.
  3. Keep the click rate as the named performance control across the whole pass.
  4. Record a full pass into Live or a recorder, then compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a shortcut only when the same hard-switch mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A 2–4 minute arrangement whose entire event list is rate switches and level switches — the paper plan performed as hard cuts, with at least one moment where clicks fuse into a pitch.

If it fails

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Hold it, A/B it, and check the rate readings

Phase focus: hold it, A/B it, and check the rate readings. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play your piece end to end, no touching.
  2. Sit through it at the mark or lower; if the urge to add a pad or a reverb arrives, it is the lesson arriving.
  3. A/B one minute of yours against REF at matched loudness, judging only two things: are your materials as pure, and do your rate switches read as cleanly.
  4. Compare your three phase-1 rate readings to the rates in your own piece — did you actually use the range the record uses, or stay timid in the middle?
  5. Write in the logbook how fatiguing the high sines were versus how low the meter read.
  6. Take the scheduled break. Sharp transients and high sines tire the ear faster than they read.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Test Pattern and judge the print against it.
  2. Confirm the smallest Live set still proves sine and click, rate and level only.
  3. Use Utility to match the level against REF before judging.
  4. Keep the proof as a printed PRINT track, not only devices.
  5. Change only the one setting that most affects the bare, switching character.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: verify the FM sine and impulse hold their bareness across the pass.
  2. Confirm the program demonstrates rate and level as the composition.
  3. Keep the click rate as the named, repeatable control.
  4. Record the full pass and compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a shortcut only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A piece that holds attention with two pure sounds and a switch list, an A/B that comes down to purity and clean rate changes, and a logbook line about how hard high sines work the ear at a low meter reading.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Could a listener find a sound that is neither a sine nor a click?
    Answer

    They should not be able to, in either your piece or the record. If they can point at a “pad” or a “sweep,” something with a timbre leaked in, and that section goes back to phase 2. The whole illusion of a measuring instrument depends on there being only two materials.

  • Did the composition survive having no colour to lean on?
    Answer

    That is the test. With timbre removed, the only things carrying the piece are the sequence of rates and the sequence of levels. If your switch list was bold — real jumps, at least one fusion of clicks into pitch — it holds. If it sagged, the fix is never a new sound; it is a braver rate or level decision. Relisten to the record and notice it never reaches for colour either.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
