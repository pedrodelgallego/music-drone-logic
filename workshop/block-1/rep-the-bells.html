Replicate: the jump cut

Copy one clear section of Jeff Mills — “The Bells”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: Whole patterns swapped instantly on the bar; a shared kick holds the floor while everything above it jump-cuts. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Live set open, Digitakt II on M4 in 3–4, clock-synced to Live — the lab 001/002 wiring
  • Three empty Digitakt II patterns in one bank, reachable as A / B / C
  • What you will make: a short replica of Jeff Mills — “The Bells”. Focus on this effect: Relentless forward motion from a handful of static blocks - nothing inside any block ever develops.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: Whole patterns swapped instantly on the bar; a shared kick holds the floor while everything above it jump-cuts.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Jeff Mills — “The Bells”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many distinct blocks did you count in the looped stretch — sections you could give a one-word name to?
    Answer

    Three or four is a fair count for any short stretch: a drums-only block, the riff block, a stripped block, and recombinations of those.

  2. How does each change happen — over how many beats?
    Answer

    Over zero beats: instantly, on the bar. There is no riser, no filter sweep, no drum fill announcing the change - one block simply stops being the other.

  3. What does every block share, and what does each block swap?
    Answer

    The kick and the tempo are the constants - they make the cuts read as one track rather than a playlist. Everything above the kick is what gets swapped: the riff in and out, hats in and out, the stab pattern replaced.

  4. Is there a single fill or transition anywhere in your stretch?
    Answer

    No - and that absence is the lesson, not an accident. Where another producer would write a fill, the record simply cuts, and the surprise of the bare seam does the fill’s job better.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target: three blocks that match the record’s blocks, switched where the record switches — every change instant, on the bar.
  • No seams softened: zero fills, zero risers, zero fades — if a transition crept in, the replica failed.
  • The constant: one kick shared by all three blocks, so the montage reads as one track.
  • The rhythm of cuts: your blocks hold as long as theirs hold — the montage has its own tempo, and it is slower than your nerves.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~30 minutes

Build the three blocks as Digitakt II patterns A / B / C

Phase focus: build the three blocks as Digitakt II patterns A / B / C. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track. Drop “The Bells” onto a dedicated REF track in Live, loop your chosen stretch, gain-match it with a Utility, and keep it one solo away for the whole session.
  2. Match the shared kick first. Pick or tune a Digitakt II kick until a soloed A/B against the record’s kick is close
  3. , thenplace it identically in all three patterns — it is the one element that must never change across a cut.
  4. Build pattern A as their fullest block. Riff stand-in (a sample slot or stab), hats, kick — chase placement and character against the REF, hit by hit.
  5. Build B and C by copying A and carving. Copy the pattern, then remove or replace elements until it matches the record’s second and third blocks.
  6. Building B and C as edits of A keeps the family resemblance the record’s blocks have.
  7. Set the change length. In each pattern’s scale setup, set the pattern change length (CH.LEN) to 16 steps so a queued switch always lands on the next bar — the cut must be quantized to the bar, exactly like theirs.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Jeff Mills — “The Bells” and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — Build the three blocks as Digitakt II patterns A / B / C — as the smallest Live set that proves Whole patterns swapped instantly on the bar; a shared kick holds the floor while everything above it jump-cuts.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects Relentless forward motion from a handful of static blocks — nothing inside any block ever develops.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate Build the three blocks as Digitakt II patterns A / B / C into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates Whole patterns swapped instantly on the bar; a shared kick holds the floor while everything above it jump-cuts, not just the surface of Jeff Mills — “The Bells”.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.

Do this

  2. A×16 · B×8 · A×8 · C×16.
  3. This list is the score.
  4. Rehearse the switches. With Live’s transport running, queue each next pattern just before the bar so the change lands exactly on the downbeat.
  5. Drill the full cut list at least twice without recording.
  6. Record the performance. Arm the Digitakt II’s audio track in Live and perform the cut list start to finish, one take, mistakes included.
  7. Drop a locator at every cut in the recorded take — Create → Add Locator — so phase 3 can find the seams instantly.

Phase 3 — ~20 minutes

A/B the montage rhythm, then look at the seams

Phase focus: a/B the montage rhythm, then look at the seams. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. A/B the montage rhythm. Solo back and forth — your take, their stretch — listening only for WHERE the cuts fall and how long each block holds.
  2. Note every cut of yours that arrives early, late, or softened.
  3. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Zoom into the waveform at each locator in Arrangement view and LOOK at the seams: vertical edges, no fades, the montage visible as geometry.
  4. Count the cuts you can see and check the count against the cut list — the seam you can see is the event you heard.

Verify against the record

A/B your take against the looped stretch of “The Bells” — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate? Then answer two questions in writing.

Do your cuts land where theirs land — instantly, on the bar, with nothing announcing them?

Answer

In the record every change occupies zero beats and arrives quantized to the bar; the surprise is total because nothing telegraphs it. A good replica matches both the placement and the bareness — within a bar of their cut points, with no fill, riser, or fade anywhere near the seam. Relisten to your tightest seam against theirs and check that both are equally unannounced.

Does each block read as a distinct scene while the whole still reads as one track?

Answer

The record manages both at once: each block is nameable in one word, yet the shared kick and tempo stitch them into a single piece. If your montage reads as a playlist, the constant is too weak — the kick changed or sat at a different level across blocks. Relisten following only your kick across every seam and confirm it never flinches.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: “Matched: …” · “Missed: … (best guess why)” · “Change next: …”
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • And one more line — what the bare cut did that any transition would have undone. The jump cut is an engine of repetition you can name in your lab 003 hypothesis: blocks that never evolve, switched hard.
