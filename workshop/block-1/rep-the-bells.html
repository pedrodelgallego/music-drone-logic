Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the jump cut

Build three blocks that never evolve, then make the music out of switching between them.

The point

Arrangement as montage — whole blocks switched hard, on the bar, with no transitions and no fills. The cut itself is the event, and refusing to write a transition is itself a technique: the energy lives in the seam, not in either side of it.

The system you are replicating
Source recordJeff Mills — “The Bells”
The effectRelentless forward motion from a handful of static blocks — nothing inside any block ever develops
The mechanismWhole patterns swapped instantly on the bar; a shared kick holds the floor while everything above it jump-cuts
FidelityAs close to the record’s section as your rig allows — sounds, blocks, and cut placement included. Closeness is the measure of your hearing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Live set open, Digitakt on M4 in 3–4, clock-synced to Live — the lab 001/002 wiring
  • Three empty Digitakt patterns in one bank, reachable as A / B / C
  • Phones at the mark · 60–120 minutes clear — the blocks are quick, but the switching has to be rehearsed until the cuts land without thinking
Reference pass — 10 minutes

Describe their montage before you match it

Loop a one-to-two-minute stretch of “The Bells.” Answer in the logbook, one line each, BEFORE touching gear. Check each answer against the model only after yours is written.

  1. How many distinct blocks did you count in the looped stretch — sections you could give a one-word name to?
    Answer

    Three or four is a fair count for any short stretch: a drums-only block, the riff block, a stripped block, and recombinations of those. Each block is internally static — name it once and it never needs renaming, because nothing inside it develops. Relisten and give each block its one-word name out loud as it arrives.

  2. How does each change happen — over how many beats?
    Answer

    Over zero beats: instantly, on the bar. There is no riser, no filter sweep, no drum fill announcing the change — one block simply stops being the other. Relisten to two or three seams in a row and confirm that the change occupies no time at all.

  3. What does every block share, and what does each block swap?
    Answer

    The kick and the tempo are the constants — they make the cuts read as one track rather than a playlist. Everything above the kick is what gets swapped: the riff in and out, hats in and out, the stab pattern replaced. Relisten following only the kick across one seam and notice it never flinches.

  4. Is there a single fill or transition anywhere in your stretch?
    Answer

    No — and that absence is the lesson, not an accident. Where another producer would write a fill, the record simply cuts, and the surprise of the bare seam does the fill’s job better. Relisten to the seam you found most exciting and notice that nothing was added to make it exciting.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the montage you described, not the one you remember.

  • Sound target: three blocks that match the record’s blocks, switched where the record switches — every change instant, on the bar.
  • No seams softened: zero fills, zero risers, zero fades — if a transition crept in, the replica failed.
  • The constant: one kick shared by all three blocks, so the montage reads as one track.
  • The rhythm of cuts: your blocks hold as long as theirs hold — the montage has its own tempo, and it is slower than your nerves.

Steps

Phase 1 — ~30 minutes

Build the three blocks as Digitakt patterns A / B / C

Each block is a complete, static scene — a pattern that could loop forever without developing. The discipline is matching THEIR blocks: the same kick under all three, and each pattern carrying what the corresponding section of the record carries, nothing more. This is study replication: chase their sounds by ear before you program a single trig.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track. Drop “The Bells” onto a dedicated REF track in Live, loop your chosen stretch, gain-match it with a Utility, and keep it one solo away for the whole session.
  2. Match the shared kick first. Pick or tune a Digitakt kick until a soloed A/B against the record’s kick is close, then place it identically in all three patterns — it is the one element that must never change across a cut.
  3. Build pattern A as their fullest block. Riff stand-in (a sample slot or stab), hats, kick — chase placement and character against the REF, hit by hit.
  4. Build B and C by copying A and carving. Copy the pattern, then remove or replace elements until it matches the record’s second and third blocks. Building B and C as edits of A keeps the family resemblance the record’s blocks have.
  5. Set the change length. In each pattern’s scale setup, set the pattern change length (CH.LEN) to 16 steps so a queued switch always lands on the next bar — the cut must be quantized to the bar, exactly like theirs.

Expected result

Three saved patterns that each read as one of the record’s blocks when soloed against the REF, all sharing one kick, all set to switch on the bar.

Phase 2 — ~30 minutes

Perform the cuts where the record cuts

The montage is a performance on the pattern keys, not an arrangement drawn with a mouse. You are transcribing the record’s cut placement: which block, for how many bars, then which block next. Rehearse the sequence until your hands know it, then record the Digitakt’s output into Live in one take — a corrected montage is no longer a montage.

Do this
  1. Write the cut list. From the reference pass, note the block order and the bar counts of your looped stretch in the logbook — e.g. A×16 · B×8 · A×8 · C×16. This list is the score.
  2. Rehearse the switches. With Live’s transport running, queue each next pattern just before the bar so the change lands exactly on the downbeat. Drill the full cut list at least twice without recording.
  3. Record the performance. Arm the Digitakt’s audio track in Live and perform the cut list start to finish, one take, mistakes included.
  4. Drop a locator at every cut in the recorded take — Create → Add Locator — so phase 3 can find the seams instantly.
Expected result

One recorded take where every block change lands on a downbeat, in the record’s order, at roughly the record’s bar counts.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Cuts land mid-bar or flam against the downbeatPattern queued too late, or change length not setSet CH.LEN to 16 in every pattern and queue the switch at least a beat early
You keep cutting every 4 bars out of nervesPerforming your impatience instead of their scoreRe-record with the cut list in view — holding a block for 16 bars is part of the technique
Phase 3 — ~20 minutes

A/B the montage rhythm, then look at the seams

Two listening passes and one looking pass. The ears check the rhythm OF the cuts — not the rhythm inside the blocks — against the record. The eyes confirm the technique: in Arrangement view a jump cut is visible as a vertical seam, and any accidental softening shows up immediately as a slope.

Do this
  1. A/B the montage rhythm. Solo back and forth — your take, their stretch — listening only for WHERE the cuts fall and how long each block holds. Note every cut of yours that arrives early, late, or softened.
  2. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Zoom into the waveform at each locator in Arrangement view and LOOK at the seams: vertical edges, no fades, the montage visible as geometry. Count the cuts you can see and check the count against the cut list — the seam you can see is the event you heard.

Verify against the record

A/B your take against the looped stretch of “The Bells” — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate? Then answer two questions in writing.

Do your cuts land where theirs land — instantly, on the bar, with nothing announcing them?

Answer

In the record every change occupies zero beats and arrives quantized to the bar; the surprise is total because nothing telegraphs it. A good replica matches both the placement and the bareness — within a bar of their cut points, with no fill, riser, or fade anywhere near the seam. Relisten to your tightest seam against theirs and check that both are equally unannounced.

Does each block read as a distinct scene while the whole still reads as one track?

Answer

The record manages both at once: each block is nameable in one word, yet the shared kick and tempo stitch them into a single piece. If your montage reads as a playlist, the constant is too weak — the kick changed or sat at a different level across blocks. Relisten following only your kick across every seam and confirm it never flinches.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: “Matched: …” · “Missed: … (best guess why)” · “Change next: …”
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • And one more line — what the bare cut did that any transition would have undone. The jump cut is an engine of repetition you can name in your lab 003 hypothesis: blocks that never evolve, switched hard.
