One Voice, No Clock

The beatless twin of Lab 004: the same single MicroFreak voice, the same three minutes, but with the sequencer, the grid, and the pulse removed. Two études, one patch. The difference between them is exactly what the time grid was doing for you.

Étude · context: sustained tone · runs after Lab 004, on the same patch

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

When the clock is gone, three things must replace it: beating (two slightly detuned tones pulsing against each other), slow movement inside the tone, and volume rides. This étude makes you build all three. It also teaches something the kick-based labs cannot: sustained (held) sounds meter differently, record differently, and tire your ears differently.

How this lab works

Same voice, no edges

  • Reload the saved Lab 004 patch — engine untouched.
  • Slow the envelope until the note fades in gradually and never clicks off.
  • No sequencer, no MIDI clip, no grid. Notes are held by hand or by the Hold button.

Three held passes

  • Low, mid, high — the Lab 004 roles, sustained instead of sequenced.
  • The mid pass is tuned a few cents away from the low. The slow pulse that results — called beating — is the étude's rhythm.
  • Each pass is recorded in full. A held tone is a performance, not a loop.

Compose with level only

  • Three minutes arranged entirely by raising and lowering faders — no mutes, no cuts.
  • Every volume move spans at least thirty seconds.
  • Compare the result against your Lab 004 étude: same voice, two kinds of time.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
Crest factor The difference between a signal's loudest spikes and its average level. A kick drum is almost all gap: tall spikes with near-silence between. A held tone has almost no gap: the body sits just below the peak the whole time. Two recordings can both "peak at −12 dBFS" and feel completely different in loudness and ear fatigue. The peak numbers you calibrated in Lab 001 were based on spiky material. This étude shows you what they miss with sustained sounds.
Beating When two near-identical pitches play together, they push in and out of phase with each other. This creates a slow, wavering pulse. The pulse rate equals the detune amount: tones 1 Hz apart beat once per second. It is rhythm without a clock.
Free modulation An LFO set to run at its own speed with no sync to a tempo. Here the cycle is tens of seconds long. In a held tone, this is what keeps the sound moving rather than sitting still.

The bench comparison — do this before the music

Required

Kick loop vs held tone, same peak target

A ten-minute experiment that shows why this étude exists. Record two takes to the same −12 dBFS peak target: eight bars of a Digitakt kick pattern, then thirty seconds of the held MicroFreak tone. Then compare everything except the peak number.

Do this
  1. Record both takes, each peaking at ≈ −12 dBFS on Live's meter. Set the gain for the held tone on its settled sustain level, not its opening swell.
  2. Play each for thirty seconds at your calibrated headphone level. Note which one feels louder, and by roughly how much.
  3. Watch the meters: the kick meter dances and rests; the tone meter parks just under the peak and stays there. The tone delivers far more energy at the same peak reading.
  4. Write one ledger amendment: your personal peak target for sustained material (most people settle at −15 or lower), and the rule that gain for a drone is set on the body of the tone, not its first moment.
Expected result

The held tone feels substantially louder and more tiring than the kick loop at the same peak reading. That is why every drone session in this workshop budgets more headroom and schedules ear breaks.

Brief & system

  1. Reload the Lab 004 patch. The engine knob does not move. This étude inherits that constraint.
  2. Remove the edges. Set attack to 2 s or longer, sustain to full, release to long. If the note still has a sharp start, keep slowing the attack.
  3. Add one free LFO routing: Matrix, LFO → Timbre or Cutoff, unsynced, one cycle per 15–20 seconds, small amount. Hold a note for two minutes and confirm the tone keeps moving on its own.
  4. Record three held passes at low, mid, and high register — 3–4 minutes each. Let go after the first 30 seconds; do not adjust anything. For the mid pass, play back the low pass through your monitors and nudge the MicroFreak's fine-tune until you hear a slow beating pulse at roughly one pulse per 1–3 seconds. Write that rate down.
  5. Compose three minutes with faders only. Plan the volume story on paper first (low alone → low + mid beating → high surfaces → back to low). Ride or draw the fades. Nothing faster than 30 seconds.
  6. No reverb until the last step. Work the tone dry first. Add one send at the end only if the piece truly needs it, and note what it costs in clarity.
  7. Render — WAV, 24-bit, named LAB-004B-etude — then compare against LAB-004-etude at matched loudness. Write three sentences on what the grid had been doing.
  • Prescribed fault: record one extra pass with the input gain set to match the tone's first loud swell, not its settled sustain. Watch what happens on the meter once the body settles or the LFO pushes the level back up. Write one rule about where in a sustained note the gain decision belongs.
Pass gate — étude complete when
  • The rendered piece, three minutes, zero rhythmic events
  • The bench comparison's ledger amendment — your sustained-material peak target, in writing
  • The beat rate of the low/mid pair, named in cycles per second
  • The one prescribed-fault rule
  • Three sentences from the A/B against Lab 004 — what the clock was doing

Timebox to a handful of sessions, like Lab 004. Keep headphones at your calibrated level and take them off every fifteen minutes. Sustained tones tire your ears fast — this étude is where that habit is built.

Étude principle

Remove the clock and three things must replace it: beating, slow movement, level. If the piece works, you built all three. If it bores, one is missing — name which.
