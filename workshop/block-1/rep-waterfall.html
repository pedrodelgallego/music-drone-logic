Replicate: drift below the event

Copy one clear section of Rrose — “Waterfall”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: continuous micro-modulation - filter, pitch, sample start, level all drifting at different sub-perceptual rates; their sum reads as life, not motion. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: Digitakt II main L/R → M4 in 3–4, the lab-003 wiring — or a one-bar audio clip in Live with three slow LFO devices, if you would rather stay in the box; the MicroFreak rests this lesson
  • New set saved as REP-WATERFALL · tempo near the record’s — write it down; the Digitakt II pattern gets its own saved slot
  • Phones at the lab-001 mark — a loop that seems static invites loudness creep while you strain to hear the drift; the drift does not get easier to hear louder, only the fatigue does
  • What you will make: a short replica of Rrose — “Waterfall”. Focus on this effect: minutes of “the same bar” that never fatigues - and you cannot point at a single change.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: continuous micro-modulation - filter, pitch, sample start, level all drifting at different sub-perceptual rates; their sum reads as life, not motion.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Rrose — “Waterfall”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Is any bar identical to another?
    Answer

    No - though you cannot catch two bars differing while they pass. The notes land in the same places every time; the sound they land in never does. Relisten by skipping: play twenty seconds at minute one, then twenty at minute three.

  2. Can you point at a single event — one moment where something changed?
    Answer

    You should not be able to. Nothing arrives, nothing leaves, nothing switches - every change is already underway before you suspect it and still underway after you stop checking.

  3. How many things are moving?
    Answer

    Several at once - a filter color, a pitch shimmer, a sense of elements sliding slightly nearer and farther - and they do not move together. One quality is rising while another falls, which is why the sum never settles.

  4. Why does ten minutes of one bar not fatigue?
    Answer

    Because the ear keeps receiving new information - just never enough at once to file as an event. Fatigue comes from confirmed prediction: when a loop is exactly what you expected, attention leaves.

  5. Is the drift periodic — does the brightness come back on a schedule?
    Answer

    No. If the movers shared a cycle, the loop would have a longer super-pattern and the ear would find it within a minute. The rates are unrelated, so their combination never recurs - the bar has no larger bar above it.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between 30 seconds of REF and 30 seconds of yours lands on the same kind of life — one bar, never identical twice, with no nameable event in either.
  • The rule, enforced without mercy: any modulation you can hear as a move is too deep. Halve it.
  • Three movers minimum, at slow rates that share no common cycle — synced, integer-related rates rebuild the bar one level up and the spell collapses.
  • The pattern itself is never edited after it exists; every change for four minutes comes from the movers.

The piece’s machinery in one row: a one-bar loop left exactly alone, three LFOs at unrelated sub-perceptual rates and tiny depths aimed at cutoff, sample start, and pitch, and a four-minute print in which no bar matches any other and no move is audible.

one-bar loop · Digitakt II · frozen → 3 LFOs · slow · unrelated rates · tiny depths → cutoff + sample start + pitch (cents) → 4-minute print · no audible moves

Three movers drawn against four minutes of identical bars. Each curve alone is too slow and too shallow to register; because their periods share no common cycle, their sum — the shaded band — never repeats. The bars underneath are the same bar every time. The sound is not.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the frozen bar

Phase focus: the reference and the frozen bar. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Waterfall” in, and loop three to four characteristic minutes.
  2. Audio Effects → Utility, trim to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. REF stays one solo-press away all lesson.
  4. Press [RECORD] for grid recording and build a one-bar loop in the record’s register: kick on the quarters, a closed hat, and one short tonal stab — a chord hit or metallic tone with enough harmonics for a filter to have something to take away.
  5. Set levels by the ledger; the Digitakt II bus peaks ≈ −12 dBFS into Live.
  6. Loop it static for two full minutes and confirm it is dead — every bar identical.
  7. Write one logbook line naming the moment your attention left.
  8. This number is the “before” measurement.
  9. Save the pattern.
  10. From here, pattern edits are forbidden — the trigs you have are the trigs you keep.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Rrose — “Waterfall” (Sandwell District, 2011) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the frozen bar — as the smallest Live set that proves continuous micro-modulation — filter, pitch, sample start, level all drifting at different sub-perceptual rates; their sum reads as life, not motion.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects minutes of “the same bar” that never fatigues — and you cannot point at a single change.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the frozen bar into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates continuous micro-modulation — filter, pitch, sample start, level all drifting at different sub-perceptual rates; their sum reads as life, not motion, not just the surface of Rrose — “Waterfall” (Sandwell District, 2011).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, a one-bar loop runs perfectly still beside it, and a logbook line records how quickly the still version dies.

Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

Wire three movers at unrelated rates

Phase focus: wire three movers at unrelated rates. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Stab track, LFO 1: destination Filter Cutoff, waveform triangle, unsynced and very slow — one full breath around 50 seconds.
  2. Depth: start where you barely hear it, then halve it.
  3. Stab track, LFO 2: destination Sample Start, a different slow rate — around 70 seconds — and a depth of a few samples, enough to vary the attack’s grain, never enough to read as a different hit.
  4. Hat track, LFO 1: destination Pitch, slower still — around 90 seconds — depth a few cents, not semitones.
  5. The three periods must share no common cycle: 50 / 70 / 90 never line up inside the take.
  6. In Live instead: three LFO devices on the clip’s track, mapped to filter cutoff, clip start offset (via a Simpler holding the bar), and detune — same rates, same depths.
  7. Audition each mover solo by zeroing the other two.
  8. The pass test per mover: on any single bar you hear nothing;
  9. across a minute the quality has changed.
  10. If a bar-to-bar difference is audible, halve the depth again.

Record four minutes with your hands in your lap

Do this

  2. Adjust, then declare the patch finished.
  3. Arm the Digitakt II’s track in Live and record four minutes.
  4. Hands off — not on the encoders, not on the faders.
  5. While it records, listen the way the reference pass taught you: one dimension per minute — brightness, then grain, then pitch.
  6. Write down anything you wanted to fix instead of fixing it.
  7. The rule applies to the take retroactively: if, listening back, any modulation reads as a move you can timestamp, that mover is too deep — fix the depth and reprint.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

A/B the record, then prove the drift on screen

Phase focus: a/B the record, then prove the drift on screen. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Solo-flip 30 seconds of yours against 30 seconds of REF at matched loudness.
  2. Judge two things only: is your drift as slow, and is it as shallow?
  3. Bounce one bar from minute 1 of your take and one bar from minute 3. Drop them on two adjacent tracks, aligned to the same downbeat.
  4. Look first: zoomed in, the waveforms visibly disagree — same notes, different shapes.
  5. Then invert the phase of one (Utility → Phz-Invert) and play both: no null, just a ghostly residue.
  6. That residue is the sum of three movers nobody could hear moving.
  7. Write the logbook line: how big the residue sounds versus how invisible the modulation felt.
  8. That gap is the whole record.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your take at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no touching while you judge.

  • Could a listener name an event in your four minutes?
    Answer

    No — and “it felt alive” is the most they should manage. If they can timestamp a change, one mover is too deep or too fast, and that mover goes back to phase 2. Relisten to the record asking the same question; its changes have no timestamps either, which is why the reference pass left that logbook line empty.

  • Is minute 3 identical to minute 1?
    Answer

    Also no — and this one you prove rather than feel: the two bounced bars overlay without nulling, same notes in visibly different sound. Both answers must be no at once. “No events” with an identical minute 3 is a dead loop; a different minute 3 made of audible moves is an arrangement. The record lives exactly in between, and now so does your take.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • Carries into the lab: repetition holds attention when variation lives below the event threshold — this is Lab 003’s modulation-drift engine taken to its limit, and the depth rule (“if you can hear the move, halve it”) transfers to every slow LFO you will ever set.
