Replicate: the submerged loop

Copy one clear section of GAS — “Pop 1”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: A short chordal loop run through long, fully wet reverb, balanced over a quiet dry layer until the edges vanish; a soft kick keeps time underneath. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Live set open, MicroFreak on M4 in 1 for the chordal loop — or the slice you kept from the “Over the Ice” lesson, ready to reuse
  • Digitakt II on M4 in 3–4 for the kick, clock-synced — the lab 001/002 wiring
  • What you will make: a short replica of GAS — “Pop 1”. Focus on this effect: A dense, breathing haze that is unmistakably looping yet has no findable seam - minutes pass inside one chord of weather.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: A short chordal loop run through long, fully wet reverb, balanced over a quiet dry layer until the edges vanish; a soft kick keeps time underneath.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of GAS — “Pop 1”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Find the pulse — how loud is it, and what is it doing?
    Answer

    It is barely there - a soft, muffled four-on-the-floor sitting far below the haze, more felt than heard, and it never accents or varies. It exists only to keep time while the wash refuses to.

  2. Try to find the loop’s start — where does the material repeat from?
    Answer

    You can’t, and you are not meant to: the reverb has smeared every attack into the tail of the previous pass, so the loop has no edges left to find.

  3. What actually changes over your two or three minutes?
    Answer

    Almost nothing in the material - the change is in the weather around it: density thickening and thinning, the haze leaning brighter or darker, like a sky rather than a song. The loop is the constant; the atmosphere is the variable.

  4. Is the source of the chord nameable — strings, voices, brass?
    Answer

    No - it is orchestral in temperature but anonymous in identity, because the reverb keeps only the harmony and discards the articulation that would name the instrument. What survives submersion is pitch and colour; what drowns is the attack.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target: a chordal haze with no findable loop start, matching the record’s darkness and density.
  • The pulse survives: a faint kick under the wash — felt, never featured.
  • Same material, two worlds: your dry loop must still exist underneath, quiet — the submersion is a balance, not a replacement.
  • The numbers: the wet and dry fader positions, written down — the whole aesthetic is that balance.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Make the loop that will drown

Phase focus: make the loop that will drown. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track. Drop “Pop 1” onto a dedicated REF track in Live, loop two to three minutes, gain-match with a Utility, and keep it one solo away all session.
  2. Record a short chordal loop. One or two bars of a held minor chord on the MicroFreak — dark, slow attack, low-mid register — recorded to audio and looped.
  3. Alternatively, reuse the study slice from the “Over the Ice” lesson as your raw material;
  4. the engine works on anything chordal.
  5. Match the temperature. A/B the dry loop against the REF and chase register and darkness with the filter — their source reads orchestral and minor-hearted;
  6. yours should sit in that family before any reverb touches it.
  7. Loop it bare for a minute. Confirm the seam is plainly audible — you need to know exactly where the edge is in order to verify later that it has dissolved.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from GAS — “Pop 1” and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — Make the loop that will drown — as the smallest Live set that proves A short chordal loop run through long, fully wet reverb, balanced over a quiet dry layer until the edges vanish; a soft kick keeps time underneath.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects A dense, breathing haze that is unmistakably looping yet has no findable seam — minutes pass inside one chord of weather.
  1. Use Analog Rytm MKII for this phase: translate Make the loop that will drown into drum voices or samples, trig conditions, parameter locks, scenes, performance macros, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates A short chordal loop run through long, fully wet reverb, balanced over a quiet dry layer until the edges vanish; a soft kick keeps time underneath, not just the surface of GAS — “Pop 1”.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.

Phase 2 — ~30 minutes

Submerge it — two layers, one balance

Phase focus: submerge it — two layers, one balance. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Duplicate the loop track. Two tracks, identical clip: name them DRY and WET.
  2. Drown the wet layer. On WET, insert a long reverb — Live’s Reverb or Hybrid Reverb, decay 10 s or more — set 100 % wet.
  3. No dry signal passes this track at all.
  4. Sink the dry layer. Pull DRY far down — it whispers the chord’s pitch underneath, nothing more.
  5. Find the line. Loop both layers and move only the two faders, in single-dB steps, until the loop’s edges vanish but the harmony still reads.
  6. A/B against the REF at each step.
  7. When it sits, write both fader positions in the logbook.

Phase 3 — ~25 minutes

The faint kick, the long run, then the A/B that proves it

Phase focus: the faint kick, the long run, then the A/B that proves it. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Kick under. A plain four-on-the-floor on the Digitakt II, tuned low, transient softened, level far below the wash — A/B against the REF until yours is equally felt-not-heard.
  2. Run it long. Record four-plus minutes of the full system.
  3. Resist every urge to add an event; the only permitted motion is a slow lean of the WET level — weather, not gesture.
  4. Observe — A/B the two worlds. Solo the DRY track alone for twenty seconds, then unsolo back into the full haze.
  5. Same material, different world: write one logbook line on what the submersion did, and confirm the wet/dry fader positions are noted from phase 2.

What the listener meets over the run — the loop never changes; the weather does.

0:00
The haze established — chord readable, edges already gone, no event to wait for.
0:30
The kick fades in underneath — felt before it is noticed; the weather acquires a clock.
2:00
The only motion: the wash leans slightly denser or thinner, over a minute, like a sky.
4:00
Out — the kick gone first, then the haze left to decay on its own tail.
Expected result

Four recorded minutes of weather with a pulse, an A/B note proving the dry loop and the haze are the same material — and both fader positions on paper.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The kick reads as an event on every hitToo loud, transient too sharpLower it and soften the attack until it sinks into the wash — check against the REF at low volume
Four minutes feels unbearable to youYou are listening for events the genre refuses to provideListen once more doing something else — this music is built for the periphery, and judging it head-on is the wrong test
Verify against the record

A/B your run against “Pop 1” — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate? Then answer two questions in writing.

Can anyone find your loop’s start?

Answer

They shouldn’t — and neither should you, even knowing where it is. The phase-1 bare loop had a plainly audible seam; if the submerged version still betrays it, the reverb decay is shorter than the loop or the dry layer sits too high. Relisten to your haze trying to hum the restart point, and check that your guess drifts the way it did on the record.

Does the pulse survive the submersion — felt, but never featured?

Answer

On the record the kick is the quietest essential element: remove it and the piece collapses into ambient, raise it and the spell breaks into club techno. Yours passes if it disappears last when you turn the volume down, exactly like theirs. Relisten to both at low volume and confirm the pulse outlives the haze in each.
