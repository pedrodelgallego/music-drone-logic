Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the submerged loop

Drown a loop in reverb until its edges dissolve — repetition felt as weather, with a faint kick as the only clock.

The point

The loop is still there — it never stops — but reverb has dissolved every edge until nobody can say where it begins. Repetition becomes weather: felt constantly, locatable never. The faint kick underneath is the only clock left, and it is enough.

The system you are replicating
Source recordGAS — “Pop 1”
The effectA dense, breathing haze that is unmistakably looping yet has no findable seam — minutes pass inside one chord of weather
The mechanismA short chordal loop run through long, fully wet reverb, balanced over a quiet dry layer until the edges vanish; a soft kick keeps time underneath
FidelityAs close to the record’s section as your rig allows — density, darkness, pulse weight, and seamlessness included. Closeness is the measure of your hearing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Live set open, MicroFreak on M4 in 1 for the chordal loop — or the slice you kept from the “Over the Ice” lesson, ready to reuse
  • Digitakt on M4 in 3–4 for the kick, clock-synced — the lab 001/002 wiring
  • Phones at the mark · 60–120 minutes clear — the balance between wet and dry is found in single-dB moves, judged over whole minutes of listening
Reference pass — 10 minutes

Describe their weather before you match it

Listen to two to three minutes of “Pop 1.” Answer in the logbook, one line each, BEFORE touching gear. Check each answer against the model only after yours is written.

  1. Find the pulse — how loud is it, and what is it doing?
    Answer

    It is barely there — a soft, muffled four-on-the-floor sitting far below the haze, more felt than heard, and it never accents or varies. It exists only to keep time while the wash refuses to. Relisten at low volume and notice the pulse is the last thing to disappear; that is how deep in the mix it lives.

  2. Try to find the loop’s start — where does the material repeat from?
    Answer

    You can’t, and you are not meant to: the reverb has smeared every attack into the tail of the previous pass, so the loop has no edges left to find. You can feel that it repeats — the harmonic colour keeps returning — but no moment says “here.” Relisten trying to hum along to where you think it restarts, and notice your guess drifts every pass.

  3. What actually changes over your two or three minutes?
    Answer

    Almost nothing in the material — the change is in the weather around it: density thickening and thinning, the haze leaning brighter or darker, like a sky rather than a song. The loop is the constant; the atmosphere is the variable. Relisten and describe two moments one minute apart in weather terms, not musical ones.

  4. Is the source of the chord nameable — strings, voices, brass?
    Answer

    No — it is orchestral in temperature but anonymous in identity, because the reverb keeps only the harmony and discards the articulation that would name the instrument. What survives submersion is pitch and colour; what drowns is the attack. Relisten and notice you can sing the chord but not say what plays it.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the weather you described, not the weather you remember.

  • Sound target: a chordal haze with no findable loop start, matching the record’s darkness and density.
  • The pulse survives: a faint kick under the wash — felt, never featured.
  • Same material, two worlds: your dry loop must still exist underneath, quiet — the submersion is a balance, not a replacement.
  • The numbers: the wet and dry fader positions, written down — the whole aesthetic is that balance.

Steps

Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Make the loop that will drown

The loop itself is deliberately modest — a short chordal phrase with the orchestral darkness of the record’s source. It will spend the rest of the lesson underwater, so its job is harmonic colour, not interest. A minor voicing in a low-mid register gets you into the record’s temperature fastest.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track. Drop “Pop 1” onto a dedicated REF track in Live, loop two to three minutes, gain-match with a Utility, and keep it one solo away all session.
  2. Record a short chordal loop. One or two bars of a held minor chord on the MicroFreak — dark, slow attack, low-mid register — recorded to audio and looped. Alternatively, reuse the study slice from the “Over the Ice” lesson as your raw material; the engine works on anything chordal.
  3. Match the temperature. A/B the dry loop against the REF and chase register and darkness with the filter — their source reads orchestral and minor-hearted; yours should sit in that family before any reverb touches it.
  4. Loop it bare for a minute. Confirm the seam is plainly audible — you need to know exactly where the edge is in order to verify later that it has dissolved.
Expected result

A one-to-two-bar chordal loop in the record’s register and mood, with a clearly audible loop point — the “before” photograph.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The loop is too interesting — a riff, not a chordToo much movement in the materialStrip it to a held voicing; interest is the reverb’s job now
The dry loop is bright and synthetic next to the REFRegister too high, filter too openDrop an octave and close the filter — chase the record’s darkness before submerging
Phase 2 — ~30 minutes

Submerge it — two layers, one balance

The submersion is built as two parallel layers of the SAME loop: a fully wet reverb layer that carries the weather, and a quiet dry layer that keeps the harmony legible. The whole aesthetic lives in their balance — too dry and the seam returns, too wet and the chord dissolves into mud. You will find the line where the edges vanish but the pitch survives.

Do this
  1. Duplicate the loop track. Two tracks, identical clip: name them DRY and WET.
  2. Drown the wet layer. On WET, insert a long reverb — Live’s Reverb or Hybrid Reverb, decay 10 s or more — set 100 % wet. No dry signal passes this track at all.
  3. Sink the dry layer. Pull DRY far down — it whispers the chord’s pitch underneath, nothing more.
  4. Find the line. Loop both layers and move only the two faders, in single-dB steps, until the loop’s edges vanish but the harmony still reads. A/B against the REF at each step. When it sits, write both fader positions in the logbook.

The whole rig, drawn — one loop, two parallel fates, one balance.

chordal loop → WET · reverb 100 % wet, ≥10 s → loud — the weather → master same loop → DRY · untouched → quiet — the pitch → master Digitakt kick → faint, muffled → the only clock
Expected result

A haze in which the loop point you could plainly hear in phase 1 is no longer findable, while the chord’s pitch still reads — and two fader values in the logbook.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The seam is still findableDry layer too loud, or reverb decay shorter than the loopLengthen the decay past the loop length first, then drop DRY another dB at a time
The haze is mud — no pitch readableWet layer alone, dry buried completelyBring DRY up until the chord just becomes nameable again; the line is narrow and that is the craft
Phase 3 — ~25 minutes

The faint kick, the long run, then the A/B that proves it

The kick converts the haze from ambient into techno — but only if it stays submerged too: soft, muffled, felt. Then the section runs long, because this engine only proves itself over minutes. The observing pass is the strongest A/B in this strand: the same loop, dry then submerged, two different worlds.

Do this
  1. Kick under. A plain four-on-the-floor on the Digitakt, tuned low, transient softened, level far below the wash — A/B against the REF until yours is equally felt-not-heard.
  2. Run it long. Record four-plus minutes of the full system. Resist every urge to add an event; the only permitted motion is a slow lean of the WET level — weather, not gesture.
  3. Observe — A/B the two worlds. Solo the DRY track alone for twenty seconds, then unsolo back into the full haze. Same material, different world: write one logbook line on what the submersion did, and confirm the wet/dry fader positions are noted from phase 2.

What the listener meets over the run — the loop never changes; the weather does.

0:00
The haze established — chord readable, edges already gone, no event to wait for.
0:30
The kick fades in underneath — felt before it is noticed; the weather acquires a clock.
2:00
The only motion: the wash leans slightly denser or thinner, over a minute, like a sky.
4:00
Out — the kick gone first, then the haze left to decay on its own tail.
Expected result

Four recorded minutes of weather with a pulse, an A/B note proving the dry loop and the haze are the same material — and both fader positions on paper.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The kick reads as an event on every hitToo loud, transient too sharpLower it and soften the attack until it sinks into the wash — check against the REF at low volume
Four minutes feels unbearable to youYou are listening for events the genre refuses to provideListen once more doing something else — this music is built for the periphery, and judging it head-on is the wrong test
Verify against the record

A/B your run against “Pop 1” — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate? Then answer two questions in writing.

Can anyone find your loop’s start?

Answer

They shouldn’t — and neither should you, even knowing where it is. The phase-1 bare loop had a plainly audible seam; if the submerged version still betrays it, the reverb decay is shorter than the loop or the dry layer sits too high. Relisten to your haze trying to hum the restart point, and check that your guess drifts the way it did on the record.

Does the pulse survive the submersion — felt, but never featured?

Answer

On the record the kick is the quietest essential element: remove it and the piece collapses into ambient, raise it and the spell breaks into club techno. Yours passes if it disappears last when you turn the volume down, exactly like theirs. Relisten to both at low volume and confirm the pulse outlives the haze in each.
