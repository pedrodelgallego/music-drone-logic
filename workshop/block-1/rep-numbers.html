Replicate: the grid as a choice

Copy one clear section of Kraftwerk — “Numbers” / “Nummern”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: machine-quantized percussion and speech-synthesis voices on a strict grid; what moves is which sounds occupy which sixteenth, never when. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Digitakt II main outs → M4 inputs 3/4, clock-slaved to Live exactly as Lab 002 wired it — one clock master, transport restarted before every take
  • New set saved as REP-numbers, with Lab 002’s driver error compensation already entered
  • Phones knob at the Lab 001 mark — the A/B in phase 4 only means something at matched, moderate level
  • What you will make: a short replica of Kraftwerk — “Numbers” / “Nummern”. Focus on this effect: everything lands exactly on the clock, and that exactness is the groove - funk with zero timing offset.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: machine-quantized percussion and speech-synthesis voices on a strict grid; what moves is which sounds occupy which sixteenth, never when.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Kraftwerk — “Numbers” / “Nummern”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Clap quarter notes along for 30 seconds — does anything on the record lean against your clap?
    Answer

    Nothing. Every hit and every spoken number lands exactly where your clap lands, and the track never bends toward or away from you.

  2. If nothing is ever late, where does the funk come from?
    Answer

    From placement and accent: which sixteenths carry a sound, which of those hits is loud, and which timbre sits on which step.

  3. How many percussion sounds are there, and how full is the bar?
    Answer

    A handful - a few distinct electronic timbres, and a surprising amount of nothing. Whole sixteenths sit empty, and those gaps are doing rhythmic work: the silence after a cluster is part of the cluster.

  4. What are the voices doing, rhythmically?
    Answer

    Behaving like drums. The speech-synthesis numbers land on the grid like percussion hits - counted, placed, quantized - so the voice becomes another sequenced timbre rather than a singer floating over the beat.

  5. Is there any swing at all — are the offbeats late by even a little?
    Answer

    No. The offbeat sixteenths sit exactly halfway, not leaned - compare against “Can You Feel It,” where every offbeat is late by a set amount, and the difference is immediate.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a strict-grid electro pattern that grooves at zero timing offset — funk made of sound choice, accent, and gaps, close enough to A/B against the record’s section.
  • Nothing sits off the grid: swing reads 50%, every trig’s micro-timing reads zero, all lesson long.
  • The groove may only be changed three ways — swap a sound, change an accent, remove a hit. Nudging is forbidden.
  • The phase-4 swing copy exists to be compared, not kept — it is the control condition that proves the grid was a decision.

The record’s machinery in one row: a few electronic percussion timbres and a counting voice, all placed by a sequencer on exact sixteenths, an accent map doing the work that lateness does on other records, and empty steps doing the rest.

Digitakt II · 3–4 voices · every trig on its step → accent map · velocity per trig → subtraction · gaps as funk → Live record · Ext. In 3/4 · grid-exact

One bar of sixteenths, drawn twice. In the top lane every hit sits on its line and only the heights — the accents — and the gaps differ. In the bottom lane the offbeats have been pushed late by a swing setting: the hits left the lines, and the groove changed species. This lesson builds the top lane and visits the bottom one only to hear the difference.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the map

Phase focus: the reference and the map. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Numbers” in, and loop the 8–16 bars you chose in the reference pass.
  2. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim until the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. REF stays one solo-press away all sitting.
  4. Set Live’s tempo to the record’s by tapping — Tap in the transport bar, eight taps — then round to the nearest whole BPM.
  5. On paper, draw a 16-column grid and transcribe one bar of the section: a mark for each occupied sixteenth, per voice, with accents circled.
  6. Three or four voices is the whole kit.
  7. Mark the empty columns too — the gaps are part of the transcription, and phase 3 spends them like currency.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Kraftwerk — “Numbers” / “Nummern” (Computer World, 1981) and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the map — as the smallest Live set that proves machine-quantized percussion and speech-synthesis voices on a strict grid; what moves is which sounds occupy which sixteenth, never when.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects everything lands exactly on the clock, and that exactness is the groove — funk with zero timing offset.
  1. Use Analog Rytm MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the map into drum voices or samples, trig conditions, parameter locks, scenes, performance macros, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates machine-quantized percussion and speech-synthesis voices on a strict grid; what moves is which sounds occupy which sixteenth, never when, not just the surface of Kraftwerk — “Numbers” / “Nummern” (Computer World, 1981).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS at the right tempo, and a one-bar paper map exists: voices, occupied sixteenths, circled accents, counted gaps. The map is what phase 2 programs — not your memory of the groove.

Phase 2 · ≈ 20 min

Program the map on a strict grid

Phase focus: program the map on a strict grid. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Press [PTN], use [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to choose a bank, then press an unused [TRIG] slot.
  2. Press [PLAY]. If you hear anything, press [STOP] and choose another pattern slot.
  3. Press [TEMPO] and confirm SWING reads 50% — the no-swing value — before placing anything.
  4. Load the closest sounds you have: a dry electro kick on track 1, a short synthetic snare or clap on track 2, a tight hat or blip on track 3, and — if your section uses one — a fourth voice for the spoken-number role: any short, pitched, vocal-ish hit.
  5. Press [SRC], turn DATA ENTRY knob D to open the sample list, audition with the active track key, and press [YES] to load the sample.
  6. Press [RECORD] for grid recording and place the trigs from the paper map, voice by voice.
  7. Trust the map over your memory.
  8. Hold each trig once and confirm its micro-timing reads zero — a leftover nudge from an old jam would quietly un-make the lesson.
  9. Zero every delay and reverb send on all tracks — the record is dry, and a tail is a timing smear by another name.
  10. Save the pattern.
  11. Loop it against REF and correct placement only: same sixteenths occupied, same sixteenths empty.
  12. Accents wait for phase 3.

A saved, bone-dry pattern whose occupied sixteenths match the record’s map exactly, with swing at 50% and every trig’s micro-timing at zero. It sounds flat and stiff next to the record — correct: the groove arrives in phase 3, and not via timing.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Make it groove without touching time

Phase focus: make it groove without touching time. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Build the accent map: hold each trig and set its velocity/level so the circled hits from the paper map land hard and the rest sit back.
  2. On the record the loud–soft contour is the bounce — copy theirs first, judge later.
  3. Audition sound swaps against REF: if a voice refuses to groove, the timbre is wrong, not the timing.
  4. A shorter, brighter hat on the same steps changes the feel more than any nudge could.
  5. Subtract: mute one hit at a time and listen to what the new gap does.
  6. If the bar gets funkier, the hit was clutter — the record’s bars are emptier than they feel.
  7. The rule, enforced without mercy: any urge toward micro-timing or swing is answered with a sound choice, an accent change, or a deletion.
  8. Those are the only three moves.
  9. Loop against REF in 10-second flips until the two grooves are the same species.
  10. Press [FUNC] + [YES] again to update the temporary save.

Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

Print it twice — strict, then swung — and A/B

Phase focus: print it twice — strict, then swung — and A/B. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Create an audio track: ⌘ ⌥ I, Audio From Ext. In 3/4, Monitor Off, armed.
  2. Confirm the input peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — the Digitakt II’s Main Volume is the only level control on this path.
  3. Stop, press play — the transport restart — and record 8 bars of the strict pattern.
  4. Rename the clip GRID.
  5. Press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D until SWING reads ≈ 58%.
  6. Change nothing else.
  7. Record 8 bars onto the same track and rename the clip SWUNG.
  8. A/B the two prints, 10 seconds each, then each against REF.
  9. Write one line on what the grid version has that the swung one lost — name it in plain words, not genre words.
  10. Zoom to sample level on one offbeat hat in each print and measure its distance from the grid line: GRID ≈ 0 ms, SWUNG tens of ms.
  11. The ruler confirms what the ear filed.
  12. Press [TEMPO], turn DATA ENTRY knob D until SWING reads 50%, then press [FUNC] + [YES] to save. The strict pattern is the deliverable.

Expected result

Two prints of one pattern, separated by one number. The swung copy lopes and relaxes; the strict copy snaps, and its snap is what matches the record. The logbook line names the loss — usually some version of “the offbeats stopped hitting and started leaning.”

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your GRID print at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Is anything in your print off the grid?
    Answer

    No — and you can prove it twice. The ruler: at sample zoom every hit sits on its line, the same distance (near zero, with compensation entered) at bar 1 and bar 8. The ear: clap along and nothing leans. If either test finds an offset, it came from your chain, not your choices — back to the Lab 002 loopback. Relisten to the record with the same clap test; it passes for the same reason.

  • Where does the funk come from, if nothing is late?
    Answer

    From the map and the contour: which sixteenths are occupied, which are empty, which hits are accented, and what each timbre is. You demonstrated it in phase 3 — the pattern went from stiff to grooving while no event moved — so the funk cannot live in the timing, because the timing never changed. Relisten to one bar of “Numbers” on loop and describe its groove using only the words which and how hard; the description will be complete.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • Carries into the lab: the grid is not the absence of a timing decision — it is one. Choosing zero offset takes the same calibrated chain as choosing any other number; Lab 002 is what makes the choice real instead of accidental.
