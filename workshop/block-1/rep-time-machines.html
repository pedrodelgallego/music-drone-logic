Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: one tone, slow phase

Copy one clear section of Coil — “7-Methoxy-β-Carboline: (Telepathine)”, from Time Machines. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: two oscillators tuned a few cents apart; their drifting phase relationship is the only motion - no filter automation, no events. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged
  • New set saved as REP-TIME-MACHINES — set any tempo you like; this lesson is measured in minutes, not bars
  • M4 phones knob at the lab-001 mark or lower — sustained material fatigues faster than rhythm; take a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes, then file it and stop — most of that time is spent holding still and listening, by design
  • What you will make: a short replica of Coil — “7-Methoxy-β-Carboline: (Telepathine)”, from Time Machines. Focus on this effect: a single held synthesizer tone that survives - and warps - ten minutes of attention.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: two oscillators tuned a few cents apart; their drifting phase relationship is the only motion - no filter automation, no events.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Coil — “7-Methoxy-β-Carboline: (Telepathine)”, from Time Machines. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many distinct sounds are playing?
    Answer

    One. There is one tone, and everything else you hear - the swell, the wah, the slow churn - is interference happening inside it, not a second sound arriving beside it.

  2. What is the fastest change you can hear?
    Answer

    The slow wah of the beating - a swell and dip taking seconds per cycle, and nothing faster anywhere. No transient, no tremolo, no pulse. That ceiling on speed is a decision, and it is the slowest ceiling in this whole block.

  3. Does the tone ever restart?
    Answer

    No. After the first onset there is never another attack - no re-strike, no edit, no event of any kind. That absence is the active ingredient: attacks are how the ear keeps time, and with none arriving, time goes soft.

  4. Is the movement performed or built-in?
    Answer

    Built-in. Two oscillators tuned slightly apart drift against each other continuously, with no hand on anything - the motion is a physical consequence of the tuning, set once and then left alone.

  5. How long did the excerpt feel versus how long it was?
    Answer

    Check the clock - most people undershoot badly, guessing two minutes for four or more. That gap between felt time and clock time is not a side effect; duration perception is the material being composed.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your tone lands on the same kind of patience — one held tone whose interior beats at a comparable slow rate, at matched loudness, with nothing else moving at all.
  • Held tone at ≈ −12 dBFS — gain set on the sustain, never on the first strike.
  • One pitch, two slightly detuned layers — if you add a second pitch or a filter sweep to keep interest, the lesson is being dodged.
  • The take is 8–10 minutes long, hands off — duration is not padding here; it is the instrument.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “7-Methoxy-β-Carboline: (Telepathine)” into it.
  2. Live Loop three to four minutes from somewhere in the middle — the tone fully established, the beating clear.
  3. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loop peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Live REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. Live ⌘ T once; rename: DRONE, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
  6. Live One source track is the whole session.
  7. Logbook Write the beat rate in the logbook: how many cycles of the slow wah you hear per second — or per several seconds.
  8. Logbook That number, not the tempo, is this lesson’s clock.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one named, routed, armed track waits for the tone, and the record’s beat rate is on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 20 min

The patch — one pitch, two tunings

Phase focus: the patch — one pitch, two tunings. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, Paraphonic ON.
  2. MicroFreak Envelope: attack anywhere from instant to slow, sustain full.
  3. MicroFreak Cutoff parked dark-ish, resonance low — and it stays parked;
  4. MicroFreak no filter movement at all, the whole lesson.
  5. MicroFreak Hold ONE pitch on two voices and detune one against the other a few cents, using the fine-tune and oscillator controls.
  6. MicroFreak Aim for an audible beat of roughly 0.5–1 cycle per second between the layers.
  7. MicroFreak If the paraphony fights you — MicroFreak paraphony shares one oscillator pitch, so true per-voice detune may not be reachable — take the reliable route instead: record two takes of the same single note onto two tracks, the second take nudged a few cents with fine tune.
  8. MicroFreak Two layers a few cents apart is the machine, however you get there.
  9. Logbook Tune by ear against your logbook number: too-fast flutter means too far apart, no swell at all means too close.
  10. Logbook Walk the fine tune until the wah breathes at the record’s patience.
  11. Logbook Set the M4 input-1 gain so the held tone peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — on the sustain, not on any strike.
Expected result

One pitch, two layers, beating slowly at roughly the record’s rate, with both hands free and nothing left to perform. The patch is finished when there is nothing left for you to do.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Beating too fast — seasick flutter instead of a slow wahOscillators too far apart in hertzBring the detune under ≈ 2 Hz; the swell should take a second or more per cycle
No beating at all — the tone just sits therePerfectly tuned, or a clean octave apart — phase has nothing to drift againstNudge the fine tune a few cents and listen for the swell to appear
Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

Hold it — hands off, all the way through

Phase focus: hold it — hands off, all the way through. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

  • Safety: ten minutes of sustained tone is fatiguing material. Phones at the mark or lower · the 15-minute break still applies even mid-take · never turn up to find interest; the interest is in the beating, not the level.
Do this
  1. MicroFreak Start the tone — weight the keys or use the MicroFreak’s Hold so your hands can leave — and record 8–10 minutes into DRONE (both tracks at once if you took the two-take route).
  2. MicroFreak Hands off everything: no filter, no fader, no nudge.
  3. MicroFreak The patch was finished in phase 2; the performance is the not-performing.
  4. MicroFreak Sit through the entire take at the mark or lower, listening, not waiting.
  5. MicroFreak The urge to “help” the tone with a filter sweep is the lesson arriving;
  6. Logbook resist it and write down the minute-mark when the urge hit.
  7. Logbook Confirm afterward that the take held ≈ −12 dBFS on the sustain throughout — the held tone is the signal here, not any strike at the start.
Expected result

Eight to ten unbroken minutes of one tone beating slowly, untouched after the first onset — and somewhere in the middle, the moment where it stopped being long and started being deep.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The tone dies of interest within secondsToo dark or too pure — the beating has no harmonic content to act onAdd a hair of Timbre / harmonics so the interference has something to move through
Peaks fine at the start, then the meter sagsGain set on the attack instead of the sustainRe-set the input gain while the tone holds, then record again
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

A/B and the time test

Phase focus: a/B and the time test. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live + Logbook Before looking at the clock or the Arrangement ruler, write down how long you think the take ran.
  2. Live + Logbook Then check, and log both numbers side by side.
  3. Live A/B one minute of REF against one minute of your take, at matched loudness.
  4. Live Judge only two things: does the interior move at the same patience, and does nothing else move at all?
  5. Logbook If your beating is faster or slower than the record’s, name the fix in hertz in the logbook — you now know the arithmetic — even if you don’t re-record today.
  6. Logbook Write one sentence on where the motion came from — with the words “played” and “automated” banned from the sentence.
Expected result

An A/B that comes down to patience and stillness — the record’s actual materials — and a logged gap between felt time and clock time that proves the mechanism worked on you.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The A/B is impossible to judge — yours always sounds smallerLoudness not matched; level is impersonating depthRe-trim both to ≈ −12 dBFS peaks and judge the interiors only
Your felt time matched the clock exactlyYou watched the ruler, or the take was too short for the effect to take holdHide the clock, record the full ten minutes, and sit the whole take again
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your take at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • There is no sequencer anywhere in this set. Where is the rhythm coming from?
    Answer

    The tuning itself — the beating is interference, a property of the two pitches, not a performance. Nothing is playing the rhythm; the rhythm is what two frequencies a hair apart are. If you hear no rhythm at all, the detune has drifted to zero or to a clean interval, and a few cents of fine tune brings it back. Relisten to the record asking who is playing the swell; the answer is no one.

  • Did your 10 minutes feel like 10 minutes?
    Answer

    Almost never — without attacks the ear loses its grid, and the take feels shorter, or longer, or simply unmeasured. That warping is the record’s actual subject; the tone is only the carrier. And it works only at duration — an 8-bar version cannot do it, which is why the take had to be ten minutes and why nothing was allowed to arrive. Relisten to your logged guess versus the clock, and keep both numbers; they are the lesson’s real output.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
