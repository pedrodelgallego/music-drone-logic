Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the human clock

Program the motorik beat machine-tight, then play the eighths yourself for two minutes without fixing anything — and hear what a few milliseconds of breathing buys that a grid cannot.

The point

A human can be the clock — motorik is discipline performed, not quantized, and the aliveness is a few milliseconds of breathing inside total commitment.

The system you are replicating
Source recordNeu! — “Hallogallo”
The effectsteady eighths that drive for ten minutes — tight, never stiff, never wandering
The mechanisma drummer holding the pulse live — ms-sized breathing around a fixed tempo
FidelityAs close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Launchpad connected and seen by Live, pads playing a drum rack — test one pad before the sitting starts
  • Digitakt II main outs → M4 inputs 3/4, clock-slaved to Live as in the previous lessons
  • New set saved as REP-hallogallo
  • Phones knob at the Lab 001 mark; buffer at your playable threshold from Lab 002 — you will be performing through software monitoring
  • Timebox: one sitting, 60–120 minutes — the two-minute performance will take several attempts, and the attempts are the lesson
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to two or three minutes of “Hallogallo” and choose an 8–16 bar stretch where the drums are most exposed — that section is what you will replicate. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Check each answer against the reveal only after you have written your own.

  1. Are the eighth notes perfectly even, or do they breathe?
    Answer

    They breathe — by amounts so small you feel them rather than hear them. A machine would place every stroke identically; these strokes scatter a few milliseconds either side of perfect, and that scatter is what keeps ten minutes of the same figure alive. Relisten to thirty seconds of hats only and notice that you cannot point at a single uneven stroke, yet the whole never goes cold.

  2. Does the tempo itself ever wander?
    Answer

    No — and that is the trick. The breathing happens around the pulse, not in it: individual strokes drift milliseconds, but the underlying tempo holds like a rail for the entire track. Tap quarters with your foot through a full minute and check whether your foot ever has to correct; it should not. Breathing without wandering is the exact target of this lesson.

  3. What is missing that a drummer would normally play?
    Answer

    Almost everything — fills, crashes, big dynamic arcs, anything that announces a section. The drummer refuses every ornament a drummer is taught to add, and that refusal is the style: motorik drives precisely because nothing interrupts the pulse. Relisten across a section change and notice how little the drums acknowledge it.

  4. Why does ten minutes of one beat drive instead of bore?
    Answer

    Because it is a performance, not a loop — no two bars are clones, even though every bar is the same figure. The ms-level variation gives the ear something microscopically new in every bar while the commitment gives the body something absolutely reliable. Relisten and ask of any two bars: same figure, yes — same bar, never.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: the motorik figure at the record’s tempo, with eighths that breathe like theirs — alive at the millisecond scale, immovable at the bar scale.
  • Side by side, your quantized version sounds like a diagram of the record; your played version sounds like the record.
  • You held two minutes without stopping and without fixing — the discipline, performed.
Phase 1 · ~25 min

Program the motorik beat — quantized first

First the diagram: the record’s figure, machine-tight. This version is not the goal — it is the control condition. Everything the played version has and this one lacks is, by definition, the human clock.

Do this
  1. Drop “Hallogallo” into Live on a track named REF, loop your 8–16 bars, gain-match with a Utility, and set Live’s tempo to the record’s by tapping along.
  2. Build a drum rack with the closest sounds to their kit — a dry kick, a soft snare, a closed hat that can take eighths without ringing.
  3. Program the figure on the grid: kick and snare in the motorik shape, hats on every eighth. Quantized, no swing, no micro-timing.
  4. Loop it against REF and correct until the figure is theirs. Name this track GRID.

A correct, machine-tight motorik loop that is recognizably the record’s figure — and audibly not the record. Against REF it sounds drawn with a ruler. Keep it; the comparison is the lesson.

Phase 2 · ~30 min

Play the eighths live — two minutes, no fixing

Now you become the clock. The kick and snare keep playing from GRID; the eighths come from your hands, on the pads, recorded without quantization. The rule that makes this a lesson instead of a take: two unbroken minutes, and nothing gets fixed afterwards.

Launchpad pads · hat played as eighths → Live MIDI track · quantize OFF → A/B vs GRID track
Do this
  1. Create a MIDI track named PLAYED on the same drum rack, armed, with record quantization off: Edit → Record Quantization → None.
  2. Mute the hat lane in GRID — kick and snare keep running as your frame.
  3. Practice the eighths on one pad for a minute or two with the metronome off, riding the kick and snare instead. Use two alternating fingers if one tires.
  4. Record two unbroken minutes of played eighths. If you stumble, keep going — Dinger did not stop, and recovering inside the pulse is the skill. No editing, no quantizing, no re-recording single bars afterwards.
  5. Take three attempts at most, then keep the best whole take. Choosing a take is allowed; repairing one is not.
Expected result

A two-minute take that is honestly yours: a few audible wobbles, recoveries, and long stretches where the eighths sit deep in the kick-and-snare frame. It will feel harder than it sounds — total commitment at the eighth-note level is a physical discipline, and that discovery belongs in the logbook.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Your eighths land consistently late, every strokePlaying against a late monitor path — you hear late, so you play lateLower the buffer to your Lab 002 playable threshold, or monitor the pads through a near-zero path
The take collapses after 30 secondsTempo too fast for your hands today, or one finger doing all the workAlternate two fingers; practice longer before recording — the record’s tempo stays, your readiness moves
Phase 3 · ~20 min

Compare the clock to the grid — then choose

Now the experiment pays off: the same figure twice, one drawn, one performed. You A/B them against each other and against the record, and you decide — as the record’s makers did — which clock this music wants.

Do this
  1. Loop 16 bars. Listen to GRID hats with the frame for a minute, then switch to PLAYED hats. Write one word for each.
  2. A/B each version against REF. Which one is closer to the record’s aliveness? Not which is tighter — which is closer.
  3. Try a blend: GRID hats quiet underneath, PLAYED hats on top. Sometimes the rail plus the breathing beats either alone.
  4. Choose — grid, played, or blend — and write one sentence defending the choice. The defense matters more than the choice.
Expected result

A clear, repeatable preference. Most ears land on the played take or the blend for this record — the grid version is more correct and less true. If you chose the grid, that is a legitimate finding too: write down what the played take would need before you trusted it.

Phase 4 · ~15 min

Observe — the cloud, not the line

Last, the word “breathing” becomes a picture. You zoom into your played take and measure where the strokes actually landed. The quantized track is a line; a living take is a cloud — and the cloud has a shape you can describe in numbers.

Do this
  1. Set the ruler to minutes:seconds:milliseconds, as in Lab 002, and zoom into eight consecutive played eighths in the PLAYED clip.
  2. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Measure each note’s offset from its grid line, in ms, noting early as minus and late as plus. Write all eight numbers down.
  3. Look at the spread: the offsets should scatter a few ms both ways around zero — a cloud centered on the pulse. Compare against the GRID clip, where every offset is exactly zero — a line.
  4. Write the sentence in the logbook: my cloud is about ___ ms wide, centered on ___. If the center sits noticeably off zero, that is your monitoring latency or your habit — name which.
Expected result

Eight small numbers scattered both ways around zero — the measured shape of “alive”. The cloud is the feel: wide enough to breathe, centered enough to never wander. That is the human clock, stated as engineering.

Verify against the record

A/B your chosen version against the REF section, then answer:

  • Can you hear your take breathe without ever hearing it wander? Loop a minute and keep your foot on quarters the whole way.
    Answer

    The foot is the wander-detector: if it ever has to correct, the pulse moved, and the take is wandering rather than breathing. Breathing shows up differently — the strokes shimmer around your steady foot without ever pulling it. If your foot corrected, the fix is more practice at the frame, not editing; relisten to the record with the same foot test and feel how its eighths never once tug the quarter.

  • 10 seconds of your take, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate to say which is the machine? Does your played take drive the way theirs does?
    Answer

    If the friend hesitates, you have performed discipline rather than programmed it — that is the lesson passed. If they point at yours instantly, the giveaway is usually dynamics rather than timing: a real drummer’s strokes vary slightly in loudness as well as placement, and pads played with one stiff finger sound flat even when the timing clouds correctly. Write which dimension gave you away in the miss list, then relisten to their hats for loudness breathing, not just timing breathing.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • What carries into the lab: the cloud is a legitimate clock — when a later lab asks for “tight”, you now ask back: line-tight or cloud-tight?
