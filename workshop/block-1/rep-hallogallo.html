Replicate: the human clock

Copy one clear section of Neu! — “Hallogallo”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Neu! — “Hallogallo”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Are the eighth notes perfectly even, or do they breathe?
    Answer

    They breathe - by amounts so small you feel them rather than hear them.

  2. Does the tempo itself ever wander?
    Answer

    No - and that is the trick. The breathing happens around the pulse, not in it: individual strokes drift milliseconds, but the underlying tempo holds like a rail for the entire track.

  3. What is missing that a drummer would normally play?
    Answer

    Almost everything - fills, crashes, big dynamic arcs, anything that announces a section.

  4. Why does ten minutes of one beat drive instead of bore?
    Answer

    Because it is a performance, not a loop - no two bars are clones, even though every bar is the same figure.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: the motorik figure at the record’s tempo, with eighths that breathe like theirs — alive at the millisecond scale, immovable at the bar scale.
  • Side by side, your quantized version sounds like a diagram of the record; your played version sounds like the record.
  • You held two minutes without stopping and without fixing — the discipline, performed.

Phase 1 · ~25 min

Program the motorik beat — quantized first

Phase focus: program the motorik beat — quantized first. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Drop “Hallogallo” into Live on a track named REF, loop your 8–16 bars, gain-match with a Utility, and set Live’s tempo to the record’s by tapping along.
  2. Build a drum rack with the closest sounds to their kit — a dry kick, a soft snare, a closed hat that can take eighths without ringing.
  3. Program the figure on the grid: kick and snare in the motorik shape, hats on every eighth.
  4. Quantized, no swing, no micro-timing.
  5. Loop it against REF and correct until the figure is theirs.
  6. Name this track GRID.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Neu! — “Hallogallo” and use Drum Rack, MIDI clips, Groove Pool, and clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — Program the motorik beat — quantized first — as the smallest Live set that proves a drummer holding the pulse live — ms-sized breathing around a fixed tempo.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects steady eighths that drive for ten minutes — tight, never stiff, never wandering.
  1. Use Analog Rytm MKII for this phase: translate Program the motorik beat — quantized first into drum voices or samples, trig conditions, parameter locks, scenes, performance macros, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates a drummer holding the pulse live — ms-sized breathing around a fixed tempo, not just the surface of Neu! — “Hallogallo”.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.

A correct, machine-tight motorik loop that is recognizably the record’s figure — and audibly not the record. Against REF it sounds drawn with a ruler. Keep it; the comparison is the lesson.

Phase 2 · ~30 min

Play the eighths live — two minutes, no fixing

Phase focus: play the eighths live — two minutes, no fixing. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Launchpad pads · hat played as eighths → Live MIDI track · quantize OFF → A/B vs GRID track
Do this
  1. Create a MIDI track named PLAYED on the same drum rack, armed, with record quantization off: Edit → Record Quantization → None.
  2. Mute the hat lane in GRID — kick and snare keep running as your frame.
  3. Practice the eighths on one pad for a minute or two with the metronome off, riding the kick and snare instead.
  4. Use two alternating fingers if one tires.
  5. Record two unbroken minutes of played eighths.
  6. If you stumble, keep going — Dinger did not stop, and recovering inside the pulse is the skill.
  7. No editing, no quantizing, no re-recording single bars afterwards.
  8. Take three attempts at most, then keep the best whole take.
  9. Choosing a take is allowed; repairing one is not.

Phase 3 · ~20 min

Compare the clock to the grid — then choose

Phase focus: compare the clock to the grid — then choose. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Loop 16 bars.
  2. Listen to GRID hats with the frame for a minute, then switch to PLAYED hats.
  3. Write one word for each.
  4. A/B each version against REF.
  5. Which one is closer to the record’s aliveness?
  6. Not which is tighter — which is closer.
  7. Try a blend: GRID hats quiet underneath, PLAYED hats on top.
  8. Sometimes the rail plus the breathing beats either alone.
  9. Choose — grid, played, or blend — and write one sentence defending the choice.
  10. The defense matters more than the choice.

Phase 4 · ~15 min

Observe — the cloud, not the line

Phase focus: observe — the cloud, not the line. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Set the ruler to minutes:seconds:milliseconds, as in Lab 002, and zoom into eight consecutive played eighths in the PLAYED clip.
  2. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Measure each note’s offset from its grid line, in ms, noting early as minus and late as plus.
  3. Write all eight numbers down.
  4. Look at the spread: the offsets should scatter a few ms both ways around zero — a cloud centered on the pulse.
  5. Compare against the GRID clip, where every offset is exactly zero — a line.
  6. Write the sentence in the logbook: my cloud is about ___ ms wide, centered on ___. If the center sits noticeably off zero, that is your monitoring latency or your habit — name which.

Verify against the record

A/B your chosen version against the REF section, then answer:

  • Can you hear your take breathe without ever hearing it wander? Loop a minute and keep your foot on quarters the whole way.
    Answer

    The foot is the wander-detector: if it ever has to correct, the pulse moved, and the take is wandering rather than breathing. Breathing shows up differently — the strokes shimmer around your steady foot without ever pulling it. If your foot corrected, the fix is more practice at the frame, not editing; relisten to the record with the same foot test and feel how its eighths never once tug the quarter.

  • 10 seconds of your take, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate to say which is the machine? Does your played take drive the way theirs does?
    Answer

    If the friend hesitates, you have performed discipline rather than programmed it — that is the lesson passed. If they point at yours instantly, the giveaway is usually dynamics rather than timing: a real drummer’s strokes vary slightly in loudness as well as placement, and pads played with one stiff finger sound flat even when the timing clouds correctly. Write which dimension gave you away in the miss list, then relisten to their hats for loudness breathing, not just timing breathing.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • What carries into the lab: the cloud is a legitimate clock — when a later lab asks for “tight”, you now ask back: line-tight or cloud-tight?
