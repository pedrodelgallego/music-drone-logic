Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the deliberate double

Copy one clear section of Lee “Scratch” Perry — Super Ape. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: one sound + a delayed copy, gap chosen by ear. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordLee “Scratch” Perry — Super Ape

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • MicroFreak or Digitakt II into the M4, wired as in Lab 001
  • New set saved as REP-super-ape
  • Phones knob at the Lab 001 mark
  • What you will make: a short replica of Lee “Scratch” Perry — Super Ape. Focus on this effect: the room feels made of late copies.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: one sound + a delayed copy, gap chosen by ear.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Lee “Scratch” Perry — Super Ape. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Pick one snare hit — how many times does it come back?
    Answer

    On most Super Ape sections a thrown snare comes back three to five times before it sinks under the band, each repeat clearly its own event.

  2. Are the repeats the same loudness, or fading?
    Answer

    Fading - every repeat lands quieter than the one before it, at a steady rate set by the echo’s feedback.

  3. Does any doubled sound feel hollow, and does any feel wide?
    Answer

    Both happen on this record, and the difference is gap size. Hollow is the very small gap - a comb-filtered copy sitting almost on top of the original.

  4. Where does each echo sit — behind the dry sound, or beside it?
    Answer

    Behind - the repeats are quieter and duller than the dry hit, so they read as depth, not as a second instrument beside the first. The copy never competes with the source for the front of the image.

  5. Is the smear constant, or thrown on specific hits?
    Answer

    Thrown. The echo is performed at the desk - a hand pushes a send up on chosen hits and pulls it back down, so some snares bloom and the next identical snare stays dry.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: at small gaps the double colors the sound (hollow or thick); at larger gaps it becomes space; at the largest it is a separate musical event. You should be able to name which regime the record sits in — and put yours in the same one.
  • Your echoes land where the record’s land: same gap, same fade, same hits thrown.
  • The ride on the copy’s fader sounds like their hand, not a preset.

The whole build is one routing, repeated with different numbers. Keep this picture in your head for the rest of the sitting:

source track · dry → ⌘D → duplicate · track delay ___ ms · ≈−6 dB · panned → sum on the Master → phones
Phase 1 · ~25 min

Reference in, phrase transcribed

Phase focus: reference in, phrase transcribed. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Drop the Super Ape track into Live on a track named REF and loop the 8–16 bars you chose in the reference pass.
  2. Live Gain-match REF with a Utility so it sits at your working level, and keep it one solo away for the whole sitting.
  3. Digitakt II + MicroFreak Find the closest Digitakt II sounds to their kit — or the closest MicroFreak patch to their stab — and record the section’s pattern, hit for hit, dry, onto an audio track.
  4. Live Loop yours against REF and correct: fix the notes first, then the hits, then the tones.
  5. Live Expect three or four passes — the second listen always finds something the first missed.
  6. Live Stop when the phrase is theirs, not approximately theirs.
  7. Live A dry bar of yours next to a dry bar of theirs should differ in tone, not in content.
Expected result

One dry audio track holding the record’s phrase in your sounds, looping in time against REF, with neither louder than the other when you flip solos.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Yours always sounds worse than REFLevels not matched — louder reads as betterRe-trim the Utility on REF until flipping solos changes tone, not size
The phrase never settles after many passesSection too long or too dense to hold in the earCut the loop to 4 bars, match those, then extend back out
Phase 2 · ~15 min

Build the double and see the comb

Phase focus: build the double and see the comb. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Duplicate the dry track with ⌘ D.
  2. Live You now have two identical copies playing in unison — the dub double, ready to be placed where the record places it.
  3. Live Open the track delay field on the copy — it lives at the bottom of the mixer;
  4. Live enable it via the track delay display if it is hidden.
  5. Live Type 1 ms into the copy’s track delay and play both tracks together.
  6. Live Listen for the hollowing.
  7. Live Drop a Spectrum on the Master and look at the comb — evenly spaced notches across the whole spectrum.
  8. Live Slide the track delay between 1 and 5 ms and watch the notches move closer together and further apart while the hollowness shifts color.
Expected result

An unmistakable hollow, phasey version of your phrase, and a Spectrum display showing regular notches that move as you change the delay. This is Lab 002’s fault, displayed as a picture you chose.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
No hollowness, just louderTrack delay not applied — field hidden or typed on the wrong trackEnable the track delay display; confirm the copy’s field reads 1.00 ms, not the original’s
Notches invisible on SpectrumSource too sparse — short hits don’t hold the analyzer stillLoop a sustained note or dense bar while you watch, then go back to the phrase
Phase 3 · ~15 min

Map the gap regimes

Phase focus: map the gap regimes. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Step the copy’s track delay through the series 1 ms → 5 ms → 15 ms → 40 ms → 90 ms.
  2. Logbook At each value, play the full phrase — not one hit — and write one word for what you hear: hollow?
  3. Logbook thicker?
  4. Logbook wider?
  5. Logbook echo?
  6. Live Do not skip values; you need the whole map to find where their echoes live on it.
  7. Live Then walk back down the series and find your own two boundary values: the gap where “hollow” becomes “wide”, and the gap where “wide” becomes “a second hit”.
  8. Logbook Write both numbers down.
Expected result

A five-word map plus two personal boundary numbers in the logbook. From the reference pass you already know which regime the record sits in — for the dub throws, the third — so you now know roughly where on this axis the next phase will land.

Phase 4 · ~25 min

Match their gap, fade, and placement

Phase focus: match their gap, fade, and placement. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Solo REF, pick one snare throw, and tap the delay time in ms against the record’s repeats — adjust until your echo lands where their echo lands.
  2. Live Match the fade: set the copy’s level so your repeats die away at their rate.
  3. Live Flip between yours and theirs every few seconds; long listens drift, short ones compare.
  4. Live Sit the copy under the original — about 6 dB down is the usual neighborhood on Super Ape, but the record is the ruler, not the number.
  5. Live Pan the copy where theirs sits — behind the dry sound or beside it, as you answered in the reference pass.
  6. Live Do at least three full A/B passes of the whole section before you accept the settings.
  7. Live Each pass, change at most one thing.
Expected result

Your doubled phrase sits in the same regime, at the same depth, fading at the same rate as the record’s. Write the gap in ms and the copy’s level in dB — those two numbers are the system, captured.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The echo never sits “behind” — it competesCopy too loud or too brightPull the copy down until it disappears, then bring it up until it just reads; darken it if it still pokes out
Gap sounds right on the snare, wrong on everything elseYou matched one hit, not the sectionRe-check against two other hits in the loop; pick the gap that serves the section, as the record does
Phase 5 · ~20 min

Throw the hits — the hand on the fader

Phase focus: throw the hits — the hand on the fader. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Relisten to your section of REF and mark, bar by bar, which hits get thrown and which stay dry — you already sketched this in the reference pass.
  2. Live Ride the copy’s fader against REF for 2 minutes — fade the double in on the hits they throw and out on the hits they leave dry.
  3. Live Repeat the ride at least twice more.
  4. Live The first pass is aiming; the later passes are timing — getting the push to land on the hit, not after it.
  5. Live Record your best ride into Live so it exists as audio you can A/B.
Expected result

A recorded pass where the smear comes and goes on the same hits as the record’s — a hand audible in the fader, not a preset. That hand is the rest of the Perry system, and the record tells you where it moves.

Verify against the record

Loop your section against REF — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — then answer:

  • Would a friend hesitate to say which is which? If not, name the first thing that gives yours away.
    Answer

    The usual giveaways, in order: the fade rate of the repeats, then the tone of the copy — on the record each repeat comes back slightly darker, while a clean digital double comes back identical. If yours sounds “too perfect”, that is the tell. Name one concrete thing, fix only that, and A/B again; “it just sounds different” is not an answer your next pass can use.

  • What ms gap matched their echoes, and at what level did the fade match? Write both numbers.
    Answer

    There is no universal right pair — but your gap should sit in the echo regime, past your own ≈35 ms boundary from Phase 3, and your copy’s level should be far enough down that it reads behind the dry hit, around the −6 dB neighborhood. The point of writing both numbers is that a feel becomes a setting: next time you want “Perry space”, you dial it in instead of hunting for it.
