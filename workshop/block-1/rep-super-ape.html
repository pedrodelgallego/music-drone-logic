Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the deliberate double

Make two copies of one sound, milliseconds apart, and listen as the gap grows from a fault into a space.

The point

Two copies of one sound, milliseconds apart, move from “fault” to “space” as the gap grows — dub is built on choosing the gap.

The system you are replicating
Source recordLee “Scratch” Perry — Super Ape
The effectthe room feels made of late copies
The mechanismone sound + a delayed copy, gap chosen by ear
FidelityAs close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • MicroFreak or Digitakt into the M4, wired as in Lab 001
  • New set saved as REP-super-ape
  • Phones knob at the Lab 001 mark
  • Timebox: one sitting, 60–120 minutes — most of the extra time goes into matching passes against the record, not into more building
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to about one minute of Super Ape and choose the 8–16 bars where the echoes are most characteristic — that section is what you will replicate. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Check each answer against the reveal only after you have written your own.

  1. Pick one snare hit — how many times does it come back?
    Answer

    On most Super Ape sections a thrown snare comes back three to five times before it sinks under the band, each repeat clearly its own event. Count one throw at a time; if you lose count, the repeats are overlapping into a wash — pick a sparser moment, because a countable throw is the one you can replicate.

  2. Are the repeats the same loudness, or fading?
    Answer

    Fading — every repeat lands quieter than the one before it, at a steady rate set by the echo’s feedback. That fade is what lets the repeats read as space instead of clutter: if they came back at full loudness the room would fill up and stop sounding like a room. Relisten to one throw and hum the loudness staircase.

  3. Does any doubled sound feel hollow, and does any feel wide?
    Answer

    Both happen on this record, and the difference is gap size. Hollow is the very small gap — a comb-filtered copy sitting almost on top of the original. Wide or thick is the slightly larger gap, where the copy still fuses with the source but fattens it. Listen for a horn or voice that suddenly thickens — that is a double too small to hear as an echo.

  4. Where does each echo sit — behind the dry sound, or beside it?
    Answer

    Behind — the repeats are quieter and duller than the dry hit, so they read as depth, not as a second instrument beside the first. The copy never competes with the source for the front of the image. If an echo seems to sit beside the dry sound, check whether it is panned away — placement is a mixing decision Perry made, and one you will copy.

  5. Is the smear constant, or thrown on specific hits?
    Answer

    Thrown. The echo is performed at the desk — a hand pushes a send up on chosen hits and pulls it back down, so some snares bloom and the next identical snare stays dry. That selectivity is the giveaway that the smear is an instrument being played, not an effect left switched on. Relisten and mark which hits get thrown in your chosen section.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: at small gaps the double colors the sound (hollow or thick); at larger gaps it becomes space; at the largest it is a separate musical event. You should be able to name which regime the record sits in — and put yours in the same one.
  • Your echoes land where the record’s land: same gap, same fade, same hits thrown.
  • The ride on the copy’s fader sounds like their hand, not a preset.

The whole build is one routing, repeated with different numbers. Keep this picture in your head for the rest of the sitting:

source track · dry → ⌘D → duplicate · track delay ___ ms · ≈−6 dB · panned → sum on the Master → phones
Phase 1 · ~25 min

Reference in, phrase transcribed

Before any doubling exists, you need two things side by side in one Live set: the record itself, gain-matched, and your own dry performance of its phrase. Everything later is a comparison, and a comparison needs both sides at the same level — otherwise the louder one always sounds better and your ear lies to you.

Do this
  1. Drop the Super Ape track into Live on a track named REF and loop the 8–16 bars you chose in the reference pass.
  2. Gain-match REF with a Utility so it sits at your working level, and keep it one solo away for the whole sitting.
  3. Find the closest Digitakt sounds to their kit — or the closest MicroFreak patch to their stab — and record the section’s pattern, hit for hit, dry, onto an audio track.
  4. Loop yours against REF and correct: fix the notes first, then the hits, then the tones. Expect three or four passes — the second listen always finds something the first missed.
  5. Stop when the phrase is theirs, not approximately theirs. A dry bar of yours next to a dry bar of theirs should differ in tone, not in content.
Expected result

One dry audio track holding the record’s phrase in your sounds, looping in time against REF, with neither louder than the other when you flip solos.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Yours always sounds worse than REFLevels not matched — louder reads as betterRe-trim the Utility on REF until flipping solos changes tone, not size
The phrase never settles after many passesSection too long or too dense to hold in the earCut the loop to 4 bars, match those, then extend back out
Phase 2 · ~15 min

Build the double and see the comb

Now make the second copy. At 0 ms apart, two identical tracks just sum into one louder sound; the instant you delay one of them, every frequency where the late copy arrives upside-down cancels — that cancellation pattern is the comb from Lab 002’s fault ritual, except this time you chose it. Seeing it on an analyzer turns a vague “hollowness” into a picture with a number behind it.

Do this
  1. Duplicate the dry track with ⌘ D. You now have two identical copies playing in unison — the dub double, ready to be placed where the record places it.
  2. Open the track delay field on the copy — it lives at the bottom of the mixer; enable it via the track delay display if it is hidden.
  3. Type 1 ms into the copy’s track delay and play both tracks together. Listen for the hollowing.
  4. Drop a Spectrum on the Master and look at the comb — evenly spaced notches across the whole spectrum.
  5. Slide the track delay between 1 and 5 ms and watch the notches move closer together and further apart while the hollowness shifts color.
Expected result

An unmistakable hollow, phasey version of your phrase, and a Spectrum display showing regular notches that move as you change the delay. This is Lab 002’s fault, displayed as a picture you chose.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
No hollowness, just louderTrack delay not applied — field hidden or typed on the wrong trackEnable the track delay display; confirm the copy’s field reads 1.00 ms, not the original’s
Notches invisible on SpectrumSource too sparse — short hits don’t hold the analyzer stillLoop a sustained note or dense bar while you watch, then go back to the phrase
Phase 3 · ~15 min

Map the gap regimes

One number — the gap — moves the double through three different perceptual worlds. Below roughly 10 ms the two copies fuse into one colored, hollow sound; up to roughly 35 ms they still fuse but read as thicker and wider; past that the ear gives up on fusing them and hears a second event — an echo. Dub lives in the third regime. You are about to walk the whole axis so you know the territory before you go hunting for Perry’s spot on it.

Do this
  1. Step the copy’s track delay through the series 1 ms → 5 ms → 15 ms → 40 ms → 90 ms.
  2. At each value, play the full phrase — not one hit — and write one word for what you hear: hollow? thicker? wider? echo? Do not skip values; you need the whole map to find where their echoes live on it.
  3. Then walk back down the series and find your own two boundary values: the gap where “hollow” becomes “wide”, and the gap where “wide” becomes “a second hit”. Write both numbers down.
Expected result

A five-word map plus two personal boundary numbers in the logbook. From the reference pass you already know which regime the record sits in — for the dub throws, the third — so you now know roughly where on this axis the next phase will land.

Phase 4 · ~25 min

Match their gap, fade, and placement

Now hunt for the record’s actual numbers. This is the slowest phase and the one that teaches the most: every A/B pass against REF sharpens what you can hear. Expect to circle — set a gap, compare, adjust, compare again — and expect the level and pan of the copy to matter as much as the time.

Do this
  1. Solo REF, pick one snare throw, and tap the delay time in ms against the record’s repeats — adjust until your echo lands where their echo lands.
  2. Match the fade: set the copy’s level so your repeats die away at their rate. Flip between yours and theirs every few seconds; long listens drift, short ones compare.
  3. Sit the copy under the original — about 6 dB down is the usual neighborhood on Super Ape, but the record is the ruler, not the number.
  4. Pan the copy where theirs sits — behind the dry sound or beside it, as you answered in the reference pass.
  5. Do at least three full A/B passes of the whole section before you accept the settings. Each pass, change at most one thing.
Expected result

Your doubled phrase sits in the same regime, at the same depth, fading at the same rate as the record’s. Write the gap in ms and the copy’s level in dB — those two numbers are the system, captured.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The echo never sits “behind” — it competesCopy too loud or too brightPull the copy down until it disappears, then bring it up until it just reads; darken it if it still pokes out
Gap sounds right on the snare, wrong on everything elseYou matched one hit, not the sectionRe-check against two other hits in the loop; pick the gap that serves the section, as the record does
Phase 5 · ~20 min

Throw the hits — the hand on the fader

The last piece of the Perry system is not a setting — it is a performance. The echo is pushed onto chosen hits and pulled off the rest, and that choosing is what makes the record breathe. You finish by playing the copy’s fader like they played the send.

Do this
  1. Relisten to your section of REF and mark, bar by bar, which hits get thrown and which stay dry — you already sketched this in the reference pass.
  2. Ride the copy’s fader against REF for 2 minutes — fade the double in on the hits they throw and out on the hits they leave dry.
  3. Repeat the ride at least twice more. The first pass is aiming; the later passes are timing — getting the push to land on the hit, not after it.
  4. Record your best ride into Live so it exists as audio you can A/B.
Expected result

A recorded pass where the smear comes and goes on the same hits as the record’s — a hand audible in the fader, not a preset. That hand is the rest of the Perry system, and the record tells you where it moves.

Verify against the record

Loop your section against REF — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — then answer:

  • Would a friend hesitate to say which is which? If not, name the first thing that gives yours away.
    Answer

    The usual giveaways, in order: the fade rate of the repeats, then the tone of the copy — on the record each repeat comes back slightly darker, while a clean digital double comes back identical. If yours sounds “too perfect”, that is the tell. Name one concrete thing, fix only that, and A/B again; “it just sounds different” is not an answer your next pass can use.

  • What ms gap matched their echoes, and at what level did the fade match? Write both numbers.
    Answer

    There is no universal right pair — but your gap should sit in the echo regime, past your own ≈35 ms boundary from Phase 3, and your copy’s level should be far enough down that it reads behind the dry hit, around the −6 dB neighborhood. The point of writing both numbers is that a feel becomes a setting: next time you want “Perry space”, you dial it in instead of hunting for it.
