Replicate: a drone that moves only in the long form

Copy one clear section of Kevin Drumm — Imperial Distortion. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a near-static drone whose only movement is tiny dynamic change measured over the whole piece, not within any moment. Glance at ten seconds and nothing happens; sit through ten minutes and it has travelled somewhere. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged; the Digitakt II rests this lesson
  • New set saved as REP-DRUMM — no tempo worth setting; this lesson is measured in dynamic change per minute
  • Soft sustained drones are deceptive — they meter low and load the ear over time, and you’ll be tempted to “improve” the stillness with a move. Phones at the lab-001 mark or lower, a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into drawing one very long dynamic curve, not adding events
  • What you will make: a short replica of Kevin Drumm — Imperial Distortion. Focus on this effect: a drone you cannot catch moving that is clearly somewhere new by the end.
  • What you will turn off: events, rhythm, audible modulation, any gesture you could point at and call a moment.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a near-static drone that moves only through tiny long-form dynamic changes.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Kevin Drumm — Imperial Distortion, then skip ahead several minutes and listen again. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. In any ten-second window, what is changing?
    Answer

    Almost nothing. Within a short window the drone is effectively frozen — same pitch, same colour, same level. That stillness is deliberate; the change is too slow to appear inside ten seconds.

  2. Compare the start of your minute to a point several minutes later — is it the same?
    Answer

    No. It’s a little louder, a little softer, or differently weighted. The movement only becomes visible across the long form. The piece travels; you just never see a single step.

  3. Is the “movement” pitch, colour, or level?
    Answer

    Mostly level — a tiny long-form dynamic change. Pitch stays put and colour barely shifts; what differs between the start and the end is largely how loud and how present the drone is. The dynamics are the journey.

  4. Could you point to a single “event”?
    Answer

    No. There are no events with addresses — no hits, no swells you can time, no entrances. The change is a slope so gentle it never crosses the threshold of “something happened.”

  5. Where is the composition if nothing moves moment to moment?
    Answer

    In the single long dynamic arc across the whole piece. Held a near-static drone, the only score available is the shape of its loudness over many minutes — that arc is the composition, and it is invisible at any one instant.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Live 12 automation: automate Audio Effects → Utility → Gain and draw a single long breakpoint curve across the entire Arrangement — longer than any LFO will reach.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control. Choose a warm, soft engine, hold one note, set a long attack/release, and freeze everything else — no LFO, no filter movement, no audible modulation.
  • Digitakt II: idle this lesson — there is no rhythm here.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same stillness — a soft drone you cannot catch moving, that is clearly elsewhere by the end.
  • One drone and nothing else — no second part, no rhythm, no audible modulation to fake life.
  • Every “event” is a point on one long dynamic curve; pitch and colour stay put.
  • The change is invisible in ten seconds and undeniable across ten minutes — if you can hear it move, it was too fast.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one warm soft drone held still, a single long dynamic curve drawn across the whole arrangement, and a mix kept gentle so the only travel is in the loudness over many minutes.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a long stretch of Imperial Distortion into it.
  2. Loop a five-minute section — long enough that the start and the end clearly differ.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loudest point peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. ⌘ T for the drone, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed; name it DRONE.
  6. Write in the logbook a level guess for the section’s start and its end — “starts mid, ends a touch fuller,” or a rough dB.
  7. Keep those two numbers; phase 3 will draw the curve between them.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Kevin Drumm — Imperial Distortion (2008) and use a soft pad source into Utility for the long curve.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the track — as the smallest Live set that proves a near-static drone moving only through tiny long-form dynamic changes.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B the start and end of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects the long-form travel.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: a warm sustained FM patch ridden by a slow level macro, into main-out recording.
  2. Program the source so it demonstrates a near-static drone moving only in the long form, not just the surface of Imperial Distortion.
  3. Keep one clear performance control — the long level curve — and name it before recording.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a target-box shortcut only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A five-minute reference loop at ≈ −12 dBFS where the start and end audibly differ, one named, routed, unity-gain drone track waiting, and two level readings on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The drone — soft, warm, and frozen

Phase focus: the drone. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: pick a warm, soft engine and hold one note — no chord, no paraphony.
  2. Set a long attack so the drone fades in with no edge, and full sustain so it simply persists.
  3. Freeze everything: LFO off, filter still, no audible modulation. The drone must not breathe on its own.
  4. Print 6–8 minutes of the held drone into DRONE, peaking ≈ −12 dBFS.
  5. Use the MicroFreak’s Hold so your hands can leave for the full take.
  6. Solo it and confirm: across any ten seconds it should be indistinguishable from a freeze. If you hear it wobble, find and kill the modulation.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: build a soft, static pad in Wavetable or Operator with no LFO and no filter movement.
  2. Build the phase goal — the frozen drone — as the smallest Live set that proves the drone holds still on its own.
  3. Keep it on a named track, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Resample to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds against REF and confirm both are equally still.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: a warm low-index FM patch with long envelopes and no LFO gives a frozen drone.
  2. Program it so it demonstrates a near-static drone, not just the surface of Imperial Distortion.
  3. Keep level as the one named control for later.
  4. Record the drone into Live or a recorder, then compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a shortcut only when the same frozen character stays audible.
Expected result

A 6–8 minute printed drone that is effectively frozen — no wobble, no edge, no audible movement of any kind. On its own it should feel motionless; the long curve will give it the only journey it gets.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Compose with one long dynamic curve

Phase focus: compose with one long dynamic curve. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Put a Audio Effects → Utility on DRONE and automate its Gain — this single curve is the entire composition.
  2. Draw one long arc across the whole take by hand: rise gently to a peak around the middle and ease back, or a slow steady climb from the start level to the end level you logged. No LFO is slow enough; draw it.
  3. The total dynamic range should be small — a handful of dB — but spread across many minutes so no instant reads as a move.
  4. Resist adding a second curve or a second event. One arc only; the discipline is the lesson.
  5. The rule: scrub to any ten-second window and you should not be able to tell which way the curve is going. Scrub from start to end and the difference should be obvious.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: automate Utility Gain with one hand-drawn arc across the whole arrangement.
  2. Build the phase goal — one long dynamic curve — as the smallest Live set that proves the long form is the only movement.
  3. Keep the drone on a named track, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Resample to PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B the start and end against REF and adjust only the curve shape.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: record one slow level-macro automation across the pattern as the single arc.
  2. Program it so it demonstrates movement only in the long form, not just the surface of Imperial Distortion.
  3. Keep the level macro as the named, repeatable control.
  4. Record a full pass into Live or a recorder, then compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a shortcut only when the same invisible-in-ten-seconds mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A 6–8 minute arrangement whose entire event list is one long dynamic arc — invisible in any short window, clearly travelled from end to end.

If it fails

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Hold it, A/B it, and check the two ends

Phase focus: hold it, A/B it, and check the two ends. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play your whole take end to end, no touching, at the mark or lower.
  2. If the urge to add an event to “rescue” the stillness arrives, it is the lesson arriving.
  3. A/B ten seconds of yours against REF — both should feel equally frozen in that window.
  4. Now A/B the start of yours against the end of yours: the difference should be clear, the same way the record’s ends differ.
  5. Write in the logbook how few dB the whole arc spanned versus how large the long-form journey felt.
  6. Take the scheduled break. Soft drones fatigue slowly and quietly.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: judge the print against REF at matched level.
  2. Confirm the smallest Live set still proves movement only in the long form.
  3. Use Utility to match the level against REF before judging.
  4. Keep the proof as a printed PRINT track.
  5. Adjust only the single curve that carries the journey.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: verify the frozen drone and the one level arc hold across the pass.
  2. Confirm the program demonstrates the long-form arc as the whole composition.
  3. Keep the level macro as the named, repeatable control.
  4. Record the full pass and compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a shortcut only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A piece that is frozen in any ten seconds yet clearly somewhere new by the end, an A/B that comes down to stillness-in-the-small and travel-in-the-large, and a logbook line about how few dB the journey actually spanned.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Could a listener name a single event?
    Answer

    They should not be able to, in your piece or the record. If they can point at a moment and say “there,” a passage of the curve was too steep and goes back to phase 3. The record has no events with addresses, and neither should yours.

  • Did the piece travel without ever appearing to move?
    Answer

    That is the whole test. Frozen in the small, travelled in the large — the only score is one long dynamic arc, and its job is to be invisible at every instant yet undeniable end to end. If the journey didn’t register, the fix is never a new event; it is a slightly wider or slightly longer curve. Relisten to the record and notice you can never catch it moving either.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
