Replicate: music your monitoring decides

Copy one clear section of Thomas Köner — Permafrost. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: filtered low rumble beds, glacially layered, with no events - depth read entirely through level relationships and playback capability. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged; the Digitakt II rests this lesson. Also on the bench: your laptop’s built-in speakers and your phone — they are instruments in phase 4
  • New set saved as REP-PERMAFROST — no tempo worth setting; this lesson is measured in minutes per move
  • Sustained low rumble is deceptive — it meters modestly, loads the ear and the body heavily, and is felt before it reads as loud. Phones at the lab-001 mark or lower, a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into layering and into listening on three systems, not extra devices
  • What you will make: a short replica of Thomas Köner — Permafrost. Focus on this effect: vast, physical low-frequency weather on a capable playback path; near-silence on a laptop - the same file, two different pieces of music.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: filtered low rumble beds, glacially layered, with no events - depth read entirely through level relationships and playback capability.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Thomas Köner — Permafrost. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is the highest-pitched sound you can hear?
    Answer

    Almost nothing above the low mids - some distant air and hiss at most. Nearly all of the energy sits below ≈ 200 Hz, where most music puts only its foundation; here the foundation is the building.

  2. How many events happen in three minutes?
    Answer

    None you could name. There are no hits, no entrances, no edges - only slow weather: one layer of rumble leaning into another over minutes. What feels like “something happened” is a balance having shifted while you weren’t watching.

  3. Where does the music register — in the ear, or in the body?
    Answer

    In the body first.

  4. Now the laptop minute: what remained of the piece?
    Answer

    A thin hiss and a faint shadow - perhaps nothing at all. A small speaker physically cannot move enough air to reproduce energy this low, so it simply doesn’t: the file plays, the meter would still move, and the music is gone.

  5. So which instrument is doing the most work in this piece?
    Answer

    The playback system. The record commits everything to a register that only a capable monitoring chain can deliver, so whether the piece exists at all is decided after the file ends - in the speaker, not in the studio.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece on the DT 990s lands on the same register and the same patience — slow low weather, no events, at matched loudness. And on the laptop, both should vanish the same way.
  • Everything lives below ≈ 200 Hz — no melodic element, no percussion, no bright detail to rescue the small speakers.
  • No events — every change is a level relationship drifting, and a move you can hear happen was too fast.
  • Levels are set on the phones at the mark and verified on the weakest path you own — both listenings are part of the work.

The piece’s machinery in one row: two beds of low rumble made by nearly closing a low-pass on a simple source, drifted by a very slow LFO, printed, and layered at careful levels — then judged on three playback systems that disagree about whether it exists.

MicroFreak · low-pass nearly closed · very slow LFO → two printed beds · BED A + BED B → layered at careful levels · ≈ −12 dBFS → three playback paths · phones / laptop / phone speaker

The same file on three playback paths, drawn as what each one can physically reproduce. The bars are the piece’s energy, piled up at the low end; the hollow bars are the part of the music each speaker silently discards. The file never changes — only the third panel calls it silence.

Your rig
Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a track from Permafrost into it.
  2. Find a characteristic three to four minutes — at least two layers of rumble audibly present — and loop them.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. ⌘ T once; rename: DRONE, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
  6. One source track for now — it becomes two in phase 2.
  7. Write in the logbook how many layers you believe you hear in the loop, and what the lowest one is doing.
  8. Keep the lines; phase 4 will ask the same questions on a different speaker.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Thomas Köner — Permafrost (1993) and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the track — as the smallest Live set that proves filtered low rumble beds, glacially layered, with no events — depth read entirely through level relationships and playback capability.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects vast, physical low-frequency weather on a capable playback path; near-silence on a laptop — the same file, two different pieces of music.
  1. Use Analog Four MKII for this phase: translate The reference and the track into one analog synth track, parameter locks, slides, performance macros, and Overbridge or main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates filtered low rumble beds, glacially layered, with no events — depth read entirely through level relationships and playback capability, not just the surface of Thomas Köner — Permafrost (1993).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one named, routed, unity-gain track waits for the first bed, and a layer count is on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 20 min

The beds — low rumble, printed twice

Phase focus: the beds — low rumble, printed twice. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog on a very low note, or the Noise oscillator type — either source works;
  2. the filter does the real shaping.
  3. Close the low-pass cutoff until almost nothing is left — no pitch edge, no brightness, only weight and slow grain.
  4. If you can whistle along to it, it is still too open.
  5. Route the LFO to cutoff: slowest rate available, small amount — the rumble should swell and recede over tens of seconds, never wobble.
  6. Envelope: slow attack, full sustain.
  7. Set the M4 input-1 gain on the held rumble, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — check after a full minute of holding;
  8. sustained material has no strike to fool you, but LFO swells move the peak.
  9. Print 4–5 minutes into DRONE; rename the track BED A.
  10. Use Hold so your hands can leave.
  11. ⌘ T, rename BED B, Ext. In 1, and print a second 4–5 minute take — a different low note or a different LFO rate, same gain.
  12. Two weathers, not one weather twice.

Phase 3 · ≈ 15 min

Layer the beds at careful levels

Phase focus: layer the beds at careful levels. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play both beds together.
  2. On the phones at the mark, set BED B a few dB under BED A — near, under, not beside: the lower bed should be felt as floor, not heard as a second voice.
  3. Plan two or three relationship drifts on paper, in plain words: “A carries, B is floor” → “they merge” → “B has quietly become the carrier.” That sentence chain is the score.
  4. Ride the two faders across 4–5 minutes of Arrangement, each move spread over at least a minute, automation recorded.
  5. If you can hear a move happen, it was too fast — redo that passage slower.
  6. Keep the sum peaking ≈ −12 dBFS — two low beds stack more weight than the meter admits, and the headroom belongs to phase 4’s comparisons.

Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

The monitoring audition — three systems, one file

Phase focus: the monitoring audition — three systems, one file. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Export or simply play your 4–5 minutes on the DT 990s at the mark, end to end.
  2. Logbook, two lines: what carries the piece, and where you feel it.
  3. Play the same audio on the laptop’s built-in speakers at a comfortable level.
  4. Two more lines: what survived, what vanished.
  5. Watch Live’s meter while it plays — write down what it claims.
  6. Play it once more on the phone’s speaker.
  7. Two final lines.
  8. Expect to be describing near-silence; describe it anyway.
  9. Repeat the three-system pass with one minute of REF — the record fails on the small speakers in exactly the same way, which is your evidence the failure is physics, not your mix.
  10. The rule, written into the logbook: levels are set on the phones at the mark, and every piece is verified once on the weakest path you own — so the day a piece must survive a small speaker, the loss is known instead of discovered.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge. Then answer for the small-speaker passes.

  • Where did the piece go on the small speaker?
    Answer

    Nowhere — and everywhere you can no longer reach. The energy is still in the file, complete; the speaker discarded it because it physically cannot move enough air at those frequencies. The piece did not get quieter, it got filtered, and what the filter removed was nearly all of it. Relisten to the record on the laptop and on the phones back to back; the difference is not a volume difference, and hearing that is the lesson.

  • What did the meter say while the music vanished?
    Answer

    ≈ −12 dBFS, steady, same as always. The meter reads the file before the speaker gets it, so it cannot see the loss — it reported a healthy signal during your near-silent phone pass. A meter tells you what you sent; only a monitoring path tells you what arrived. Relisten to one laptop minute with the meter in view and let the two reports disagree in front of you.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
