Replicate: the path is the instrument

Copy one clear section of Emptyset — Demiurge. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: minimal sources driven into nonlinear stages; the structure is changes in how hard each stage is pushed, at pinned output loudness. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged; the Digitakt II rests this lesson
  • New set saved as REP-DEMIURGE — set a tempo and keep it; this lesson, unlike its siblings, is measured in bars
  • Saturated, limited material is dense — almost no gaps for the ear to rest in — and it tires you long before it reads as loud on any meter. Phones at the lab-001 mark, never above it, a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into the drive plan and its compensation, not extra devices
  • What you will make: a short replica of Emptyset — Demiurge. Focus on this effect: massive, physical tracks built from almost no material - the weight and grit arrive from the signal path being pushed, not from anything added.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: minimal sources driven into nonlinear stages; the structure is changes in how hard each stage is pushed, at pinned output loudness.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Emptyset — Demiurge. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many sound sources can you count?
    Answer

    Fewer than it feels like - a low pulse, something sine-like underneath, and very little else. What reads as a large arrangement is one skeleton wearing different amounts of distortion.

  2. Where does the dirt come from?
    Answer

    Not from the sources - they are clean and dull on their own. The grit, body, and snarl are what nonlinear stages produce when a signal hits them too hard: saturation bending the wave, limiting flattening it.

  3. What actually changes between sections?
    Answer

    How hard the stages are being pushed. The pattern barely changes; a new section arrives as a new amount of drive - more of the wave bending, more harmonics, less gap between hits. Relisten across one section boundary and ask what was added.

  4. Does the track get louder as it gets bigger?
    Answer

    Barely. The output is pinned - limiting holds the ceiling while saturation fills the space under it, so “bigger” arrives as density at constant level: more spectrum occupied, more of each moment full.

  5. Could this music be written as notes on paper?
    Answer

    No - the notes are trivial and the piece lives elsewhere. The score, if one existed, would be a row of drive amounts: how hard, into which stage, for how long.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your eight bars lands on the same bareness underneath and the same grown mass on top — density rising while loudness stays put, at matched loudness.
  • One low pulse pattern and nothing else — no second voice, no pad, no element to rescue you.
  • Every event is a drive amount changing; the pattern itself never changes.
  • The output is pinned — if a bar got louder instead of denser, the compensation failed and the passage goes back.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one low pulse pattern printed dry at nominal, a Saturator into a Limiter in series, and eight bars of automation that only raises drive — gain-compensated so the output never moves while the density grows.

MicroFreak · one low pulse pattern · printed dry → Saturator · drive automated → Limiter · ceiling pinned → mix · loudness constant · density grows

The same pulse pattern three times, drawn against a ceiling that never moves. Nothing is added between panels — the bars are the same hits, pushed harder at each stage — and the third panel feels enormous while the meter reads it almost the same as the first.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a track from Demiurge into it.
  2. Find a characteristic two to three minutes — at least one clear move from barer to denser — and loop them.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. ⌘ T once; rename: PULSE, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
  6. Write in the logbook where in the loop the track is barest and where it is densest, as timestamps.
  7. Phase 4 will put a meter against both.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Emptyset — Demiurge (2011, Subtext) and use Live audio/MIDI tracks, Utility, automation, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the track — as the smallest Live set that proves minimal sources driven into nonlinear stages; the structure is changes in how hard each stage is pushed, at pinned output loudness.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects massive, physical tracks built from almost no material — the weight and grit arrive from the signal path being pushed, not from anything added.
  1. Use Syntakt for this phase: translate The reference and the track into machine tracks, trig conditions, parameter locks, the FX block, macros, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates minimal sources driven into nonlinear stages; the structure is changes in how hard each stage is pushed, at pinned output loudness, not just the surface of Emptyset — Demiurge (2011, Subtext).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one named, routed, unity-gain track waits for the pulse, and a barest/densest pair of timestamps is on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The skeleton — one low pulse pattern, printed dry

Phase focus: the skeleton — one low pulse pattern, printed dry. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this

  2. MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, Wave toward square so the tone is a hollow pulse, one low note.
  3. Filter open just enough to keep the click of each attack;
  4. no LFO, nothing moving in the patch.
  5. Program a sparse one-bar pattern on the MicroFreak sequencer — four to six hits with real gaps between them.
  6. Boring is correct: this is a skeleton waiting for muscle.
  7. Set the M4 input-1 gain to the ledger nominal — peaks ≈ −12 dBFS in Live, clean, no amber.
  8. Watch the peaks over a full running bar: a pulse is all strike, and a single hit will fool you.
  9. Record 8 bars of the running pattern into PULSE, then disarm.
  10. This printed clip is the only source material of the lesson.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

The chain — write with drive amounts

Phase focus: the chain — write with drive amounts. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. On PULSE: Audio Effects → Saturator, then Audio Effects → Limiter directly after it.
  2. Set the Limiter Ceiling ≈ −12 dB — the output is now pinned, and nothing upstream can raise it.
  3. Plan the eight bars on paper first, in drive amounts and plain words: bars 1–2 “clean skeleton” → 3–4 “edges appear” → 5–6 “the saturator carries” → 7–8 “limiter leaned on, full mass.” That sentence chain is the score.
  4. Automate Saturator Drive across the eight bars to perform the plan — from ≈ 0 dB rising toward +18 to +24 dB.
  5. Pair every Drive rise with a fall in the Saturator’s Output so the limiter is fed, not slammed;
  6. let the limiter take only the last few dB.
  7. The rule, enforced without mercy: loudness stays put.
  8. A/B bar 1 against bar 8 at the mark — if bar 8 is louder rather than denser, compensate further and redo the passage.
  9. Optional, straight from lab-001’s clipping tour: print one alternative take with the M4 input-1 gain deliberately into amber — analog drive at the first stage, chosen and logged in the ledger as an exception.
  10. Compare its grain against the Saturator’s; they are different instruments.

A/B it, and read the meter against your ears

Phase focus: a/B it, and read the meter against your ears. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this

  2. Logbook, one line: at which bar does the skeleton stop sounding unfinished and start sounding like intent?

  5. the gap between the two numbers is the lesson.
  6. Take the scheduled break.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your eight bars at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Can you hear which stage is being pushed at a given moment?
    Answer

    You should — lab-001’s clipping tour gave you the table, and this record is the table set to music. Input clipping is a hard, brittle crackle riding the strikes, baked into the take. The Saturator is warmth and added spectrum that grows smoothly as drive rises. The limiter is felt rather than heard — sustain swelling, transients flattening, the gaps between hits closing up. Relisten to the record naming the stage out loud at each section change; when the naming stops hesitating, the diagnosis table has become an ear.

  • Does the loudest-feeling bar meter louder?
    Answer

    Barely — a dB or two, perhaps nothing. The limiter pins the output, so what grew was density: more harmonics, less gap, more of every moment occupied. The ear reads that as “louder” because spectrum and duty cycle are most of what loudness is; the meter reads level, which you deliberately held still. Relisten to your barest and densest bars once more with the meter in view, and let the disagreement settle in.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
