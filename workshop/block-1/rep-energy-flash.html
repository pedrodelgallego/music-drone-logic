Replicate: One Riff, All Menace

Copy one clear section of Joey Beltram — “Energy Flash”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: filter, level and density ridden as pressure - the notes never change. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • MicroFreak ¼″ out → M4 in 1; Digitakt II connected and level-checked.
  • New set saved as REP-energy-flash, tempo 126 — then warp REF and adjust by ear.
  • Cutoff, resonance and envelopes are explained in plain words in the Lab 004 concepts table.
  • What you will make: a short replica of Joey Beltram — “Energy Flash”. Focus on this effect: one two-bar riff heard as a full arrangement.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: filter, level and density ridden as pressure - the notes never change.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Joey Beltram — “Energy Flash”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Hum the main riff. Now hum the second riff.
    Answer

    You can’t - there is only one. A single two-bar riff is the entire melodic content of the track, and everything you remember as “another part” is that riff under different pressure.

  2. What actually changes between minute one and minute three — the notes, or the pressure on them?
    Answer

    The pressure. The filter opens, the level leans forward, the drums get denser - while the riff’s notes stay identical from start to finish. Relisten to one escalation and try to name the knob that made it instead of the note.

  3. Where does the menace come from — the riff itself, or what is done to it?
    Answer

    Both, but mostly the second: a dark riff played once is just a sound; looped under slowly rising pressure it becomes a threat. The repetition tells you it will not stop, and the pressure tells you it is getting closer.

  4. Besides the riff and the drums, what else exists? Count the parts.
    Answer

    Very little - a sparse stab here, a vocal fragment there, a handful of accents. The record is brutally economical; most “extra parts” you counted are the riff at another filter state.

  5. Does the riff ever leave — and what does its absence do?
    Answer

    It thins or drops for short stretches, and those absences are the track’s breathing: they reset your ear so the return hits harder than any addition could. Absence is also pressure.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target — what success sounds like:
  • The riff, transcribed by ear, close enough that a friend hearing yours then theirs would hesitate.
  • The patch dark and heavy — menace from weight, not brightness.
  • Every escalation traceable to an automation lane, never to a new clip.
  • Drums plain and pounding where theirs pound — no fills the record doesn’t make.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.

The whole rig for this lesson — one riff into one dark patch; the Digitakt II supplies only the drums and the stab the section actually uses.

Set the reference

Do this

  2. Find 8–16 bars that contain one complete lean-in — quiet state through peak — and loop them.

  4. Keep REF one solo away for the whole session.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Joey Beltram — “Energy Flash” and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.

  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects one two-bar riff heard as a full arrangement.
  1. Use Analog Four MKII for this phase: translate Set the reference into one analog synth track, parameter locks, slides, performance macros, and Overbridge or main-out recording.

Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the looped section at matched volume, then answer:

  • Hum the riff from memory — how many riffs did your replica need?
    Answer

    One. If your section needed a second riff to stay interesting, the record disagrees — it runs longer than your replica on a single one. Delete the extra, push the automation harder, and relisten to how the record raises stakes without ever adding notes.

  • Take your loudest moment: can you trace it entirely to filter and level automation rather than new material?
    Answer

    Every escalation in the record is a pressure move — the same two bars with the filter further open and the level leaning in. If your peak needed a new clip, it broke the system; rebuild it from the lanes. Relisten to the record’s biggest moment and confirm it is still the same two bars.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines:
  • Matched: what your replica got closest to — the riff, the darkness, the timing of the lean-ins; start with the escalation that landed nearest the record’s.
  • Missed: what the record does that yours doesn’t (best guess why).
  • Change next: the one thing you would do differently on the next attempt.
