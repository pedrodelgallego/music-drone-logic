Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: One Riff, All Menace

One two-bar riff, one dark patch, and an automation lane — replicate a section of “Energy Flash” closely enough to feel how pressure, not notes, arranges a record.

The point

One dark riff can carry an entire track when intensity — filter, level, density — does all the arranging. Menace is repetition plus pressure.

The system you are replicating
Source recordJoey Beltram — “Energy Flash”
The effectone two-bar riff heard as a full arrangement
The mechanismfilter, level and density ridden as pressure — the notes never change
Fidelityas close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • MicroFreak ¼″ out → M4 in 1; Digitakt connected and level-checked.
  • New set saved as REP-energy-flash, tempo 126 — then warp REF and adjust by ear.
  • Cutoff, resonance and envelopes are explained in plain words in the Lab 004 concepts table.
  • Timebox: 60–120 minutes. The riff is only two bars, so transcription is quick — matching the dark weight of the patch and placing the pressure where the record places it are the slow parts. Stop when the escalation lands, not before.
Listen first

Reference pass — 10 minutes

Listen to about one minute of “Energy Flash”. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear — and write your answer before opening each reveal.

  1. Hum the main riff. Now hum the second riff.
    Answer

    You can’t — there is only one. A single two-bar riff is the entire melodic content of the track, and everything you remember as “another part” is that riff under different pressure. Relisten to a full minute and try to catch a second riff arriving; it never does.

  2. What actually changes between minute one and minute three — the notes, or the pressure on them?
    Answer

    The pressure. The filter opens, the level leans forward, the drums get denser — while the riff’s notes stay identical from start to finish. Relisten to one escalation and try to name the knob that made it instead of the note.

  3. Where does the menace come from — the riff itself, or what is done to it?
    Answer

    Both, but mostly the second: a dark riff played once is just a sound; looped under slowly rising pressure it becomes a threat. The repetition tells you it will not stop, and the pressure tells you it is getting closer. Relisten and notice the exact moment the loop stops feeling like music and starts feeling like warning.

  4. Besides the riff and the drums, what else exists? Count the parts.
    Answer

    Very little — a sparse stab here, a vocal fragment there, a handful of accents. The record is brutally economical; most “extra parts” you counted are the riff at another filter state. Relisten and recount, asking of each new arrival: is this new material, or the same riff under new pressure?

  5. Does the riff ever leave — and what does its absence do?
    Answer

    It thins or drops for short stretches, and those absences are the track’s breathing: they reset your ear so the return hits harder than any addition could. Absence is also pressure. Relisten to the bar just before a return and feel how loaded the silence is.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

  • Sound target — what success sounds like:
  • The riff, transcribed by ear, close enough that a friend hearing yours then theirs would hesitate.
  • The patch dark and heavy — menace from weight, not brightness.
  • Every escalation traceable to an automation lane, never to a new clip.
  • Drums plain and pounding where theirs pound — no fills the record doesn’t make.

Steps

The whole rig for this lesson — one riff into one dark patch; the Digitakt supplies only the drums and the stab the section actually uses.

MIDI clip (riff) → MicroFreak → M4 in 1 → Live: MF PRINT (record) + Digitakt drums + stab → own tracks
Phase 1

Set the reference

Every judgement in this lesson — the riff’s notes, the patch’s darkness, the timing of the pressure — is made against the record, so the record goes on the bench first, looped and gain-matched. Pick a stretch that contains one full escalation, because the escalation is what you are replicating.

Do this
  1. Drop “Energy Flash” onto an audio track named REF.
  2. Find 8–16 bars that contain one complete lean-in — quiet state through peak — and loop them.
  3. Gain-match with a Utility on REF.
  4. Keep REF one solo away for the whole session.
Expected result

A clean loop of the section whose pressure curve you will be matching, at an honest level.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The loop stumbles at the seamLoop brace not on bar boundariesSnap the brace to whole bars; warp the clip to the set tempo if needed
REF always sounds “better” in A/BIt’s just louderPull the Utility down until levels genuinely match
Phase 2

Transcribe the riff, match the dark patch

The two-bar riff is the entire melodic content of the record, so it gets transcribed by ear, note by note. Then the MicroFreak gets dressed dark: a raw analog-style engine, low octave, the filter mostly closed. The grid below is a rhythm scaffold only — your ear supplies the true notes and placements.

Filled = note, bright = accented, dim = ghost — rhythm only; the pitches come from your transcription.

Do this
  1. Transcribe the two-bar riff by ear into a two-bar Live MIDI clip, MIDI To → MicroFreak.
  2. Solo REF, hum it, place it, compare; revise until the riffs chase each other.
  3. Dress dark: V.Analog engine, low octave, LP filter, cutoff around 30 %, resonance around 20 %, Paraphonic OFF.
  4. Envelope: fast attack, medium decay; shape Wave and Timbre toward the record’s weight.
  5. A/B against REF until it is as close to the record’s riff as the MicroFreak gets.
Expected result

Your riff and theirs looping together — same notes, same gait, recognisably the same family of menace.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The riff sounds bouncy, not menacingOctave too high or filter too openDrop an octave, close the cutoff, lengthen the decay slightly — darkness is register plus filter
The notes are right but the line feels thinMissing low weight in the patchConfirm Paraphonic OFF, pick a richer wave position, and check the M4 gain against your ledger
Phase 3

Add the sparse stab and percussion

The section uses only a handful of other elements — pounding drums and an occasional stab. They exist to give the riff something to press against, so they get built plain, placed exactly where the record places them, and nothing more.

Do this
  1. Program the Digitakt kick and hats where the record puts them — plain hits on the 16-step grid, no fills the record doesn’t make.
  2. Build the stab on its own Digitakt track — a single short hit, tuned and placed where the section places it.
  3. A/B levels against REF; their drums pound but never bury the riff.
Expected result

The riff sitting on a plain, pounding bed — every other element countable on one hand.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The drums sound busier than REF’sFills and ghost hits added from habitMute everything the record doesn’t do — economy is the assignment
The stab swallows the riffLevel set by enthusiasm, not by the recordA/B against REF — their stab is an accent, not a lead
Phase 4

Arrange by intensity only

This is the record’s arranging move: the riff clip never changes, and the section is built entirely from filter and level automation plus drum density. No new parts are allowed — when the music needs to grow, you press harder instead of adding. Match where the record leans in and where it breathes.

  • Safety — before drawing the peaks: filter opening and level rising compound each other, and the loudest automation peak will be much hotter than the state you mixed at. Headphones at the mark or lower — never turn up to inspect. Draw the peaks, then play them at the set level before listening closely.
Do this
  1. In Arrangement view, lay the riff clip across the full section length — one clip, looped, never edited.
  2. Draw automation on the synth track: a cutoff lane and a level lane, rising where the record leans in, pulling back where it breathes.
  3. Add density only with Digitakt mutes — hats in and out where theirs come and go; no new patterns.
  4. Play the whole section against REF and move your lean-ins until they land where theirs land.
Expected result

A full section in which the only difference between any two bars is automation values and drum density — and it still escalates.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The escalation feels flatAutomation drawn as one straight rampMove in stages — hold a plateau, then step up; listen to where REF holds its breath
You keep wanting to add a partThe itch is the lessonRaise pressure instead — open the filter five percent more and see if the urge survives
Phase 5

Observe the lane, compare, export

Now make the lesson visible. On the synth track sit two objects: a note clip that is one unbroken loop, and an automation lane drawn like a skyline. The lane is the arrangement — the clip never changed. Look at both, write the contrast down, then verify against the record and print.

Do this
  1. Look at the synth track: flat, identical clip below; rising and falling automation above. Write one logbook line on the contrast — the lane is the arrangement.
  2. Mark with locators where your escalations land and where REF’s land; measure the gaps.
  3. Export the section — File → Export Audio/Video, WAV, named REP-energy-flash.
Expected result

Paired locators a few seconds apart at worst, one written line about the lane, and a finished WAV.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A peak in the audio has no automation under itIt came from a clip edit or an added partUndo the edit and rebuild that moment with the lanes — the constraint is the lesson
The export contains REFReference track not muted at renderMute REF, re-export, and listen to the file before filing it
Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the looped section at matched volume, then answer:

  • Hum the riff from memory — how many riffs did your replica need?
    Answer

    One. If your section needed a second riff to stay interesting, the record disagrees — it runs longer than your replica on a single one. Delete the extra, push the automation harder, and relisten to how the record raises stakes without ever adding notes.

  • Take your loudest moment: can you trace it entirely to filter and level automation rather than new material?
    Answer

    Every escalation in the record is a pressure move — the same two bars with the filter further open and the level leaning in. If your peak needed a new clip, it broke the system; rebuild it from the lanes. Relisten to the record’s biggest moment and confirm it is still the same two bars.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines:
  • Matched: what your replica got closest to — the riff, the darkness, the timing of the lean-ins; start with the escalation that landed nearest the record’s.
  • Missed: what the record does that yours doesn’t (best guess why).
  • Change next: the one thing you would do differently on the next attempt.
