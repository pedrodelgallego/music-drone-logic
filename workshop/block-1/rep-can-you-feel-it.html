Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: swing you can set

Program the record’s pattern dead straight, then step the swing amount until the offbeats lean exactly as far as theirs — and learn that the feel is a number someone chose.

The point

Swing is a number — a rule that delays every offbeat by a chosen amount. The feel is adjustable, and the record chose its setting.

The system you are replicating
Source recordMr. Fingers — “Can You Feel It”
The effectevery offbeat hat leans late by the same chosen amount
The mechanisma machine swing setting — one rule applied to every offbeat
FidelityAs close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Digitakt II main outs → M4 inputs 3/4, clock-slaved to Live as in the previous lessons
  • MicroFreak audio out → M4 input 1, for the bassline
  • New set saved as REP-can-you-feel-it
  • Phones knob at the Lab 001 mark
  • Timebox: one sitting, 60–120 minutes — most of it goes into the swing-stepping passes, and the passes are the lesson
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to about one minute of “Can You Feel It” and choose the 8–16 bars where the offbeat lean is most obvious — that section is what you will replicate. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Check each answer against the reveal only after you have written your own.

  1. Which hits lean — and which hold the frame?
    Answer

    The offbeat hats lean; the kick and the bass hold the frame. Every hit on a downbeat sits on the grid, and every hit between downbeats arrives a chosen amount late — that split is the whole definition of swing. Relisten with your foot on the quarters: the kick agrees with your foot, the hats consistently land just behind the halfway point between taps.

  2. Roughly how much do they lean — a subtle lilt, or a hard shuffle?
    Answer

    A lilt — well short of a shuffle. The offbeats are unmistakably late once you listen for them, but a casual ear just hears “warm” rather than “swung”. On the percentage scale you will use later, that puts it in the mid-to-upper fifties rather than the sixties; you will find the exact value by stepping. Relisten and commit to one word for the size before the gear gets a vote.

  3. Is the lean identical every bar?
    Answer

    Yes — machine swing is. A drum machine applies the same delay to every offbeat by rule, so the lean cannot vary unless someone changes the setting. That perfect sameness is what separates dialed swing from a drummer’s swing, which breathes. Relisten to eight bars of hats only: the distance behind the beat never changes by a hair.

  4. Does the bassline swing with the hats, or sit straight?
    Answer

    Listen to where its notes start: the bass moves mostly on and around the downbeats, so it reads as part of the straight frame, while the hats do the leaning above it. That contrast matters — if everything swung, nothing would. Relisten following only the bass for four bars, then only the hats, and feel how the groove lives in the gap between the two.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: the record’s pattern and bassline, with every offbeat leaning by one rule — and the rule set to the record’s amount.
  • The lean is identical every bar, because a setting applies it — you chose the number by ear, against the record.
  • You can name the number, and you heard what one step above and below it feels like.
Phase 1 · ~30 min

Transcribe and program the section — dead straight

Swing only means something against the straight version of the same pattern, so you build that first: drums and bassline, every hit on the grid, swing at 50% — which is no swing at all. The distance between this version and the record is exactly the number you are about to find.

Do this
  1. Drop “Can You Feel It” into Live on a track named REF and loop the 8–16 bars you chose in the reference pass. Gain-match it with a Utility and keep it one solo away all sitting.
  2. Set Live’s tempo to the record’s by ear — tap Tap along with REF until the metronome agrees.
  3. Transcribe their drum pattern by ear on the Digitakt — kick, hats, clap — picking the closest sounds to their kit. Confirm the per-track swing is at 50% (no swing) on every track.
  4. Transcribe the bassline on the MicroFreak — a few notes, looping; sequence it from a Live MIDI clip so it locks to the grid. Match their patch roughly: soft attack, round and warm.
  5. Loop yours against REF and correct voice by voice until the pattern is the record’s. Keep everything straight — the lean comes in Phase 2, as one setting.

One plausible transcription shape — yours may differ; the grid is the method, not the answer. The hats are marked dim on purpose: they sit on those offbeat cells now, but at the record’s setting they live a rule-sized distance after them. The grid cannot draw that — the swing setting will.

Expected result

A dry, straight version of the record’s section in your sounds. Against REF it should already be recognizably the same music — and noticeably stiffer, because their offbeats lean and yours do not yet. That stiffness is the number you are about to find.

Phase 2 · ~25 min

Step the swing amount and feel each number

Now the experiment: the same pattern at four swing settings, in order. You are not hunting for the record yet — you are calibrating your ear to what each number feels like, so that the record’s setting will be recognizable when you pass it.

Do this
  1. Pick one swing method and stick to it. Digitakt: per-track swing on the hat track (and any other offbeat voice). Live: a groove from the Groove Pool applied to your clips, Timing at full. One method, not both.
  2. Mute REF. Play your loop at 50% for a few bars, then step to 54%, then 58%, then 62%.
  3. At each stop, listen for at least eight bars and write one word for the feel — your word, not the manual’s. Do not skip a stop and do not rush one; the differences are small until they are suddenly not.
  4. Note the setting where the loop stops feeling stiff — and the setting where it starts feeling drunk. The record lives between those two.

Expected result

Four distinct feels from one pattern and one knob: flat, warming, rolling, staggering — in your own words. The realization to write down: nothing about the pattern changed. The feel is the number.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix

Some hits lean, others stay straightSwing applied on one Digitakt track but not the others carrying offbeatsSet the same swing value on every track that has offbeat trigs — one rule for the kit
Phase 3 · ~20 min

Find the record’s setting by ear

Now the match. You A/B your loop against the record and adjust the one number until the two leans agree. The answer is found, not guessed — expect many short passes, and expect the last few steps to be argued in single percent.

Do this
  1. Unmute REF and loop it against your pattern. Start from the Phase 2 setting that felt closest.
  2. A/B a few bars at a time: are your offbeats leaning less than theirs, the same, or more? Step the swing by one or two percent and listen again.
  3. When you think you have it, overshoot by one step on purpose, hear the difference, and come back. A match you cannot overshoot was found by luck.
  4. Write the final number in the logbook before Phase 4 touches anything.
Expected result

One setting where your loop and the record lean together — the stiffness gone, the stagger never arrived. You can name the number, and you know what one step either side of it sounds like.

Phase 4 · ~20 min

Record it and measure the rule in ms

Last, the claim “swing is a number” becomes a measurement. You print the loop twice — once at 50%, once at your matched setting — and put the ms ruler on the same offbeat hat in both takes. The difference is the rule, in milliseconds.

Digitakt II · swing ___% → main outs → M4 in 3/4 → Live record · Ext. In 3/4 · Monitor Off
Do this
  1. Restart the transport — the Lab 002 reset rule — then record 8 bars at swing 50% into Live (Ext. In 3/4, Monitor Off).
  2. Set the matched swing value and record the same 8 bars again, onto a second track, after another transport restart.
  3. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Set the ruler to minutes:seconds:milliseconds, as in Lab 002, and zoom both takes to the same offbeat hat.
  4. Measure each hat’s distance from the grid line and subtract: the 50% hat sits on the line, the swung hat sits ___ ms after it. Check a second offbeat in another bar — the difference must be identical, because a rule put it there.
Expected result

One number in milliseconds, the same at every offbeat you check. Swing demystified: the warmth you matched by ear is that number, applied by rule — found with your ears, stated as engineering.

Verify against the record

A/B your matched recording against the REF section, then answer:

  • Is your lean identical every bar — and is it the same lean as theirs? Loop 4 bars of each and listen only to the hats.
    Answer

    Identical every bar should be automatic — a setting applied it, and machine swing cannot wander. So if the two loops still differ, the miss is the amount, not the consistency: your number is a step or two from theirs. Go back to single-percent steps around your setting rather than doubting the method, and relisten to hats only until the two leans land together.

  • 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate to say which is which? Do both move your hips the same way?
    Answer

    At the right setting the body answers before the ear does — the same lilt shows up in your shoulders for both loops. If the friend would not hesitate, the giveaway is usually timbre or the bassline’s phrasing rather than the swing number, because the number is the easiest part to match exactly. Name the real difference out loud and write it in the miss list, then relisten asking only: do the offbeats of both loops land together?

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • What carries into the lab: swing is a parameter with a value, not a vibe — and from now on you set it on purpose, like a buffer size.
