Clock, Latency & Monitoring

When you play a note through the computer, you hear it a little later than you played it. This lab measures exactly how much later, teaches you the three different ways to listen while recording, and shows you the two timing problems that quietly ruin takes.

Bench lab · S1 · E2 · full cycle · new concepts: 5

Start here
  • Open the bench configuration from Lab 001, with phones at the marked position.
  • Save a new set as LAB-002-latency.
  • Begin with the buffer-size feel test, then measure the loopback; do not trust Live's reported latency until the cable test confirms it.
  • Expected time: two sessions of 60–90 minutes.
The point

You can never remove the computer’s delay. But if you measure it once and choose how you listen on purpose, it stops causing problems. Players who skip this step are playing against a delay they don’t know about.

How this lab works

1 · Feel it, then measure it

  • Play the synth at four buffer sizes and write down what each delay feels like under your hands (Step 01)
  • Then run one cable from an output straight back into an input and measure the real delay in milliseconds (Step 02)

2 · Choose what you hear

  • Try the three listening paths your rig offers and notice what each one trades away (Step 03)
  • For each path, prove what actually gets recorded — it is not always what you heard (Step 04)

3 · Trust the clock, then break the listening

  • Run the drum machine from Live’s tempo for 64 bars and measure whether it stays in place (Step 05)
  • Then switch on two listening paths at once — on purpose — and learn the hollow sound that mistake makes (fault ritual)

New concepts — in plain words

Every technical term in this lab is defined below. Come back here any time a word is unclear.

ConceptIn plain words
Latency The gap between doing something — hitting a key — and hearing it through the computer. It is always there. The only question is how big it is and whether you know the number.
Buffer size How many audio samples the computer collects before processing them. A bigger buffer (more samples) is stable but adds more delay. A smaller buffer is faster but can cause crackles if the computer can't keep up.
Round trip The full journey: into the interface, through the computer, back out to your ears. Measured in milliseconds (thousandths of a second). This is the number a player actually feels.
Direct vs software monitoring Two ways to hear yourself while recording. Direct monitoring: the interface sends your input straight to your headphones — no delay, but no computer effects either. Software monitoring: you hear the signal after it passes through the computer — with effects, but late by the full round-trip delay. You must pick one before you record.
Comb filtering What you hear when the same sound reaches your ears twice, a few milliseconds apart. Certain frequencies cancel out, making the sound hollow and phasey — like talking into a spinning fan. This is what happens when two monitoring paths are both switched on at the same time.
MIDI clock A timing signal Live sends over USB to keep hardware like the Digitakt playing at the right tempo. It is coarse — good enough for tempo, but not precise enough to place individual hits exactly on the beat.
MIDI notes vs CC Two different message types sent over the same USB cable. A MIDI note says “play this pitch now.” A CC (short for Control Change) says “this knob is at value X.” Mapping is how you decide which CC controls which knob inside Live.
buffer latency driver error compensation monitoring paths comb filtering midi clock jitter

01 Lee “Scratch” Perry — Super Ape the deliberate double

Why this record: dub music is built on deliberate timing overlap — copies of sounds are layered slightly apart until the overlap fills the space of the record. Any track works.

Listen for
  • Echoes of the snare drum that come back slightly late and slowly fade away
  • Moments where a doubled voice or horn suddenly sounds wider — or hollow
  • The feeling that the room itself is made of late copies
After — verify

Pick one echo you heard. Could you tell it was a separate, later copy of the sound — not the sound itself? If not, relisten to one verse and count the repeats of a single snare hit.

05 Neu! — “Hallogallo” the human clock

Why this record: a drummer holding a steady tempo for ten minutes — tight enough to lock the track, but not so rigid it sounds mechanical. No machine is involved. The small variations of a few milliseconds are what a quantized grid cannot produce.

Listen for

07 Ricardo Villalobos — “Easy Lee” micro-swing

Why this record: each percussion sound has its own small timing offset — one lands slightly early, one slightly late, one dead on. Together they create a rolling groove. The feel is built voice by voice, not by applying one setting to the whole pattern.

Why this record: everything lands exactly on the clock — and that exactness is the groove. The energy comes from which sixteenth-note slots are filled and how hard, not from anything landing early or late.

Listening · Before the bench

Write the hypothesis before touching gear

One sentence in the logbook

Aim for: tightness is a decision, not an accident. Perry chose the smear. Plastikman chose the grid. Dilla chose late. Heard chose a swing number. Neu! chose a human clock. Rashad chose early. Villalobos chose a different small offset per voice. Kraftwerk chose exactness. D’Angelo chose a different amount of lateness per layer. LFO chose a note only some systems can play. Ten records, ten decisions — and all ten knew exactly where their sounds sat. This lab gives you that same knowledge for your own rig.

Overview — three clocks in the room

Sound through a computer is always a little late. The interface collects a chunk of audio, the computer processes it, and the result comes back out. Every stage adds delay. The delay never reaches zero. So the question is never whether your audio is late — it is how late, and whether you know the number.

There are three clocks running in your room, and they do not agree. The audio clock is the interface’s internal timer. It ticks 48,000 times a second, placing every recorded sample precisely. MIDI clock is a coarser timing signal sent over USB — good enough to keep the Digitakt at the right tempo, but not precise enough to place a single hit exactly on the beat. The third clock is your hands: they adapt to whatever they hear. If they hear late, they play late, and the take keeps that drag.

Procedure

Step 01

The buffer experiment — feel the number first

Before measuring anything, feel the delay from the playing side. The numbers will make more sense once your hands know what they mean.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak audio out → M4 input 1, as wired in Lab 001. Create one audio track, open its routing with ⌘ ⌥ I, set input to Ext. In 1, Monitor In — that is software monitoring, so the signal makes the full round trip through the computer before you hear it.
  2. Turn the M4’s hardware blend knob fully toward computer playback — you must hear only the path through Live, or this experiment measures nothing.
  3. Check the Options menu: Reduced Latency When Monitoring stays OFF for this entire lab. It changes the delay you are about to feel and measure; leave it off so every number in this lab was taken under the same conditions.
  4. ⌘ , → Audio. Set Buffer Size to 1024. Close the preferences and play a fast line of short, separated notes on the MicroFreak.
  5. Repeat at 256, then 128, then 64. At each size, play the same line and write what it feels like in your own words — not the spec-sheet word, your word.
  6. Note the buffer at which the synth stops feeling like an instrument and starts feeling like a remote control. Then set the buffer back to 256 — the working size for the rest of the lab.
Expected result

At 1024 samples the delay is obvious — you can hear the note arrive after you press the key. Somewhere between 256 and 64 there is a point where your hands stop noticing. That point is personal: it is the largest buffer at which you can comfortably perform through software monitoring. If 64 samples causes crackles or dropouts, your computer has hit its limit at that setting — write that down too.

Step 02

Loopback — measure the real round trip

The interface reports its own delay to Live, but that number is usually a little low because parts of the signal path are invisible to it. This test measures the true delay by sending a click out of the interface and straight back in through a cable, recording it, and checking how late it landed. Live has a field where you enter the correction.

Do this
  1. Mute or turn down the monitors, and leave the phones knob at the mark. During this test you will be listening to nothing — the cable does the listening.
  2. Take the ¼″ TS patch cable: M4 rear line output 3 → M4 rear line input 3. The signal now leaves the computer, passes through both converters, and comes straight back.
  3. Make a click track: a MIDI track with a one-bar clip, quarter notes, driving a short sharp sound — Simpler with a click or rim sample, attack at zero.
  4. Open the click track’s routing with ⌘ ⌥ I and set its Audio To to Ext. Out 3 only — not Master. If the click also reaches Master, you create a second copy of the signal and, with the loopback patched, a path that can feed back on itself. The loopback chain never touches Master.
  5. If Ext. Out 3 does not appear in the chooser: ⌘ , → Audio → Output Config → enable mono outputs 3 & 4. (Do the same in Input Config if Ext. In 3 is missing.)
  6. Create an audio track: ⌘ ⌥ I, Audio From Ext. In 3, Monitor Off, armed. Record eight bars of the click.
  7. Now measure. Right-click the Arrangement beat-time ruler and switch the time display to minutes : seconds : milliseconds. Zoom to maximum on the first recorded click. Drag-select from the grid line to the very first edge of the click in the waveform, and read the selection length in ms. Convert back if you want samples: samples = ms × 48 at 48 kHz.
  8. Compare your measured offset against Live’s reported input + output latency in ⌘ , → Audio → Latency. The shortfall is your driver error — the part of the journey the interface’s report missed.
  9. Enter that difference in Driver Error Compensation — same Latency section; remember it is greyed out unless the external device is selected.
  10. Re-record the eight bars and re-measure. Repeat until the recorded click sits on the grid line. Then unpatch the cable and write the final numbers into the table below.
Expected result

At 256 samples / 48 kHz, expect a round trip in the low-to-mid teens of milliseconds. The minimum possible is 2 × 256 ÷ 48 ≈ 10.7 ms. Converter and driver overhead add a little more on top. If your result is far from that range, check the configuration first before suspecting the M4. After entering the compensation value, the recorded click should land on the grid line within a few samples.

Why this works

The minimum delay is simple to calculate: round trip ≈ 2 × buffer ÷ sample rate. One buffer fills on the way in and one on the way out. The converters and driver add a bit more on top — and that extra is exactly what the loopback test reveals. Re-measure whenever you change the buffer size, sample rate, or driver. The compensation value only applies to the configuration it was measured on.

Step 03

Monitoring modes — the three paths

While recording, your rig gives you exactly three ways to hear the source. Each makes a different trade-off: speed, effects, or accuracy. The signal is the same — but what reaches your ears is not.

MicroFreak → M4 blend knob → phones · near-zero ms · dry MicroFreak → M4 → USB → Live + FX → USB → M4 → phones · full round trip: ___ ms Digitakt own outs → M4 in 3/4 → blend → phones · near-zero ms, while a different USB copy records
Do this
  1. Path (a): turn the M4 blend knob fully toward input, set the MicroFreak track’s Monitor to Off. Play. This is direct monitoring — the immediate now, with no effects.
  2. Path (b): blend knob fully toward playback, Monitor In, drop one obvious effect (a delay, a saturator) on the track. Play. This is software monitoring — the processed now, late by your Step 02 number.
  3. Path (c): blend toward input, run a Digitakt pattern, arm a stereo track on Ext. In 3/4 with Monitor Off. You hear the box directly; the USB copy records in silence behind your back.
  4. After each path, write one line: what was instant, what was late, what was processed.

Step 05

MIDI clock test — 64 bars of truth

MIDI clock keeps the Digitakt at the right tempo but does not place individual hits precisely. This test measures how much offset there is — and whether it stays fixed or grows over time.

Do this
  1. Make Live the clock master — the one device that sets the tempo for everything else: ⌘ , → Link, Tempo & MIDI → MIDI Ports → enable Sync on the Digitakt II output port row.
  2. On the Digitakt: SETTINGS → MIDI CONFIG → SYNC → CLOCK RECEIVE and TRANSPORT RECEIVE on; send nothing. Press play in Live and confirm the Digitakt’s pattern starts and its screen shows Live’s tempo.
  3. Program a Digitakt kick on quarter notes, one bar, nothing else. Main outs → M4 inputs 3/4, as wired.
  4. In Live, build a reference click: a one-bar MIDI clip triggering a sharp click sound on quarter notes, rendered or frozen to an audio track — this copy never leaves the computer, so its timing is exact.
  5. Record the Digitakt’s audio (Ext. In 3/4, Monitor Off) for 64 bars alongside the reference click.
  6. Measure the gap between the two with the Step 02 technique — ms time ruler, maximum zoom, drag-select from the click’s first edge to the kick’s first edge — at bar 1 and again at bar 64.
Expected result

A fixed small offset between click and kick is latency — you can measure it, compensate for it, and work with it. If the offset at bar 64 is larger than at bar 1, that is drift: the gap is growing over time. No compensation setting fixes drift. Drift means the clock setup is wrong — two devices both trying to be the master, a bad USB hub, or an outdated driver.

Step 06 · Control path

The other cable — notes, CC, and clock

The USB cable carries three types of MIDI message: a note (“play this pitch now”), a CC (“this knob is at value X”), and clock (“tick”). This lab has covered clock. This step proves the other two, because Lab 003’s performance work depends on them.

Do this
  1. Create a MIDI track (⌘ ⇧ T), set its output to the MicroFreak port, channel 1, Monitor In. Play the Launchpad pads — the MicroFreak sounds: that is the NOTE path, through Live.
  2. Now the CC/mapping path: press ⌘ M — Live tints everything mappable. Click a mappable control (a track volume, or a device dial), then move a Launchpad fader or knob; the assignment appears in the mapping browser on the left.
  3. Set the Min/Max range for the mapping in that browser row — a mapped knob with a chosen range is a performance decision made in advance.
  4. Press ⌘ M again to exit map mode, then move the hardware control and watch the mapped dial follow.
Expected result

The pads trigger the synth through Live’s note path. The mapped dial follows the hardware control within the range you set. You should now be able to say which of the three message types is doing what.

Fault ritual · Monitoring

The doubled-monitoring comb filter

Predict

Before creating the fault, write down what you expect to hear: two copies of the same signal arriving milliseconds apart, both reaching your headphones at once. What should that sound like? (The concepts table already told you.)

Create

On the MicroFreak input, enable both listening paths at once: turn the M4’s blend knob toward direct input and set the Live track’s Monitor to In.

Observe

Hold a long note and write down what you hear: hollow, phasey, like the sound is coming through a tube. Sweep the filter cutoff slowly and notice that the hollow notches stay fixed in frequency while the tone moves through them.

Diagnose

Two copies of the same signal are reaching your headphones — one via direct monitoring, one delayed by the full round trip through software. At frequencies where the delayed copy arrives with reversed polarity, the two copies cancel out. This creates a row of notches spread across the spectrum (called comb filtering). Nothing is broken. Both paths are working — they just should not both be on at once.

Fix

Pick one mode: blend knob to playback for software monitoring, or Monitor Off for direct. The hollow sound disappears the moment only one copy of the signal reaches your headphones.

Write the rule

One monitoring path per source, declared before recording. File the full entry: symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule.

Going deeper — clock depth

The 64-bar test tells you whether your offset is fixed (latency) or growing (drift). Here are a few more timing concepts you will encounter as your rig grows.

The clock vocabulary you will keep using
JitterTiny, random variation in exactly when each clock tick lands. Drift is the average offset growing in one direction over time. Jitter is random wobble around a fixed average. To spot it: instead of measuring bar 1 vs bar 64, measure the click-to-kick gap on many individual bars. If the offsets vary randomly without trending, that is jitter. If they march steadily in one direction, that is drift.
PDC — plugin delay compensationSome plugins need a moment to look ahead before they can process audio (for example, a look-ahead limiter). Live automatically delays every other track to keep everything in time. This keeps your mix aligned, but it adds to the total round-trip delay you feel when monitoring through software — which is why a heavy plugin chain can make the rig feel slower under your hands.
Word clockA dedicated sync signal that keeps the audio clock — the 48,000-times-a-second internal timer — exactly matched between digital devices, sample for sample. Not needed on this single-interface setup (the M4 is the only audio clock), but the first thing to check if you add a second digital device and hear clicks or pops at the transitions.
Ableton LinkA tempo-sync method that works over a local network instead of a cable. It keeps tempo and bar position in sync across multiple apps and devices without requiring one to be the master. Like MIDI clock, it syncs musical time, not individual sample placement. Use it to lock a phone app or a second laptop to your session, not to tighten a single recorded take.

The four signal domains

This lab’s cables carry two kinds of thing: audio you can hear, and messages that control machines. There are four signal domains in total. Confusing them causes half of all “why is nothing working” problems. Know which domain a cable carries before you plug it in.

Audio, MIDI, CV, gate — what each one is
AudioThe sound itself — a varying voltage (analog) or a stream of samples (digital). This is what travels through the ¼″ jacks into the M4 and gets recorded. If you can hear it, it is audio.
MIDIDigital messages, not sound: “play this pitch now” (note), “this knob is at value X” (CC), “tick” (clock). On this rig, MIDI travels over USB. Plugging a MIDI cable into an audio input produces silence — wrong domain.
CV — control voltageA continuous voltage used to control something — pitch, filter cutoff, an LFO shape — rather than to be heard. It is the analog predecessor of MIDI CC. Instead of a number on a wire, the control value is the actual voltage level. Patching it to an audio input produces a thump or buzz, not music.
Gate / triggerA simple on/off voltage: a gate stays high for the length of a note; a trigger is an instant pulse. These are the modular world’s equivalents of a MIDI note-on message — they signal timing, not pitch.
  • Where this goes: Block 1 work is almost entirely in the audio and MIDI domains. CV and gate are the language of modular synthesis — Block 6 uses voltages directly, where pitch, gate, and modulation travel as physical signals on cables rather than messages over USB. The rule to carry forward: match the signal domain to the jack, every time.

Bench principle

You cannot remove latency — you can only decide where it lives. Tightness is not something you summon at the moment of recording. It is a routing decision you make before pressing record.
