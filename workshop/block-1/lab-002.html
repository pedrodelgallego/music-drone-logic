Block 1 · Lab 002 · Bench

Clock, Latency & Monitoring

When you play a note through the computer, you hear it a little later than you played it. This lab measures exactly how much later, teaches you the three different ways to listen while recording, and shows you the two timing problems that quietly ruin takes.

Bench lab · S1 · E2 · full cycle · new concepts: 5

listen → hypothesis → draw the path → calibrate → build → fault → diagnose → print → delta
Start here
  • Open the bench configuration from Lab 001, with phones at the marked position.
  • Save a new set as LAB-002-latency.
  • Begin with the buffer-size feel test, then measure the loopback; do not trust Live's reported latency until the cable test confirms it.
  • Expected time: two sessions of 60–90 minutes.
The point

You can never remove the computer’s delay — but if you measure it once and choose how you listen on purpose, it stops mattering. Players who don’t choose are playing along to a delay they don’t know.

How this lab works

1 · Feel it, then measure it

  • Play the synth at four buffer sizes and write down what each delay feels like under your hands (Step 01)
  • Then run one cable from an output straight back into an input and measure the real delay in milliseconds (Step 02)

2 · Choose what you hear

  • Try the three listening paths your rig offers and notice what each one trades away (Step 03)
  • For each path, prove what actually gets recorded — it is not always what you heard (Step 04)

3 · Trust the clock, then break the listening

  • Run the drum machine from Live’s tempo for 64 bars and measure whether it stays in place (Step 05)
  • Then switch on two listening paths at once — on purpose — and learn the hollow sound that mistake makes (fault ritual)
Pass standard
Minimum passLoopback latency measured, monitoring modes compared, one doubled-monitoring fault diagnosed, and one system diff written.

New concepts — in plain words

Every technical word in this lab is defined here first. Come back to this table whenever a term feels slippery.

ConceptIn plain words
Latency The delay between doing something — hitting a key — and hearing the result through the computer. It is always there; the only question is how big it is, and whether you know.
Buffer size How big a chunk of audio the computer processes at once. Big chunks are safe but late, like a courier who waits for a full van before driving. Small chunks arrive faster, but the courier can drop packages — you hear that as crackles.
Round trip The full journey: into the interface, through the computer, back out to your ears. Measured in milliseconds (thousandths of a second). This is the number a player actually feels.
Direct vs software monitoring Two ways to hear yourself while recording. Direct: the interface sends your input straight to the headphones — immediate, but without any computer effects. Software: you hear the signal after it has been through the computer — with effects, but late. Choosing one is choosing which “now” you listen to.
Comb filtering What happens when you hear the same sound twice, a few milliseconds apart: certain frequencies cancel out, and the sound turns hollow and phasey, like talking into a fan. It is the signature sound of two listening paths being on at once.
MIDI clock The metronome signal Live sends over USB so that hardware plays at the same tempo. It keeps the Digitakt at the right speed, but it is coarse: it does not place individual hits precisely.
MIDI notes vs CC Two different sentences down the same cable: a note says “play this pitch now”; a CC says “this knob is now at value X”. Mapping is deciding which knob a CC sentence steers.
buffer latency driver error compensation monitoring paths comb filtering midi clock jitter

01 Lee “Scratch” Perry — Super Ape the deliberate double

Why this record: dub is built on timing smear — copies of sounds are layered slightly apart, on purpose, until the smear becomes the space of the record. Any track works.

Listen for
  • Echoes of the snare drum that come back slightly late and slowly fade away
  • Moments where a doubled voice or horn suddenly sounds wider — or hollow
  • The feeling that the room itself is made of late copies
After — verify

Pick one echo you heard. Could you tell it was a separate, later copy of the sound — not the sound itself? If not, relisten to one verse and count the repeats of a single snare hit.

05 Neu! — “Hallogallo” the human clock

Why this record: a drummer being a clock for ten minutes — tight enough to drive, alive enough to breathe. No machine involved: the steadiness is a performance, and the few milliseconds it breathes are what a grid cannot give you.

Listening · Before the bench

Write the hypothesis before touching gear

One sentence in the logbook

Aim it at: tightness is a choice with a path behind it. Perry chose the smear, Plastikman chose the grid, Dilla chose late, Heard chose a swing number, Neu! chose a human clock, Rashad chose early, Villalobos chose a different tiny offset per voice — seven records, seven timing decisions, and all seven knew exactly where every copy and every hit sat. This lab gives you that knowledge for your own rig.

Overview — three clocks in the room

Sound through a computer is always a little late. The interface collects a chunk of audio, the computer works on it, and the result comes back out — every stage adds delay, and the delay never reaches zero. So the question is never whether your audio is late. It is by how much — and whether you know the number or are only hoping.

It matters because there are three clocks running in your room, and they do not agree. The audio clock is the interface’s heartbeat: it ticks 48,000 times a second, and everything recorded through it is placed with extreme precision. MIDI clock is a coarse metronome message sent over USB: good enough to hold the Digitakt at the right tempo, not good enough to place a single hit. The third clock is your hands: they adapt to whatever they hear — so if they hear late, they play late, and the take keeps that drag forever.

Procedure

Step 01

The buffer experiment — feel the number first

Before measuring anything, learn what the delay feels like from the playing side. Numbers mean more once your hands have met them.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak audio out → M4 input 1, as wired in Lab 001. Create one audio track, open its routing with ⌘ ⌥ I, set input to Ext. In 1, Monitor In — that is software monitoring, so the signal makes the full round trip through the computer before you hear it.
  2. Turn the M4’s hardware blend knob fully toward computer playback — you must hear only the path through Live, or this experiment measures nothing.
  3. Check the Options menu: Reduced Latency When Monitoring stays OFF for this entire lab. It changes the delay you are about to feel and measure; leave it off so every number in this lab was taken under the same conditions.
  4. ⌘ , → Audio. Set Buffer Size to 1024. Close the preferences and play a fast line of short, separated notes on the MicroFreak.
  5. Repeat at 256, then 128, then 64. At each size, play the same line and write what it feels like in your own words — not the spec-sheet word, your word.
  6. Note the buffer at which the synth stops feeling like an instrument and starts feeling like a remote control. Then set the buffer back to 256 — the working size for the rest of the lab.
Expected result

At 1024 the delay is unmistakable — the note arrives after the key. Somewhere between 256 and 64 there is a threshold where your hands stop noticing. That threshold is personal and real: it is the largest buffer at which you can honestly perform through software monitoring. If 64 samples produces crackles or dropouts, that is your machine telling you its floor — write that down too.

Step 02

Loopback — measure the real round trip

The interface tells Live how long its delay is — but that report is usually a little short of the truth, because parts of the journey are invisible to it. This test measures the real delay with one cable: send a click out of the interface, straight back into it, record it, and see how late it lands. Live has a field for exactly that correction.

Do this
  1. Mute or turn down the monitors, and leave the phones knob at the mark. During this test you will be listening to nothing — the cable does the listening.
  2. Take the ¼″ TS patch cable: M4 rear line output 3 → M4 rear line input 3. The signal now leaves the computer, passes through both converters, and comes straight back.
  3. Make a click track: a MIDI track with a one-bar clip, quarter notes, driving a short sharp sound — Simpler with a click or rim sample, attack at zero.
  4. Open the click track’s routing with ⌘ ⌥ I and set its Audio To to Ext. Out 3 only — not Master. If the click also reaches Master, you create a second copy of the signal and, with the loopback patched, a path that can feed back on itself. The loopback chain never touches Master.
  5. If Ext. Out 3 does not appear in the chooser: ⌘ , → Audio → Output Config → enable mono outputs 3 & 4. (Do the same in Input Config if Ext. In 3 is missing.)
  6. Create an audio track: ⌘ ⌥ I, Audio From Ext. In 3, Monitor Off, armed. Record eight bars of the click.
  7. Now measure. Right-click the Arrangement beat-time ruler and switch the time display to minutes : seconds : milliseconds. Zoom to maximum on the first recorded click. Drag-select from the grid line to the very first edge of the click in the waveform, and read the selection length in ms. Convert back if you want samples: samples = ms × 48 at 48 kHz.
  8. Compare your measured offset against Live’s reported input + output latency in ⌘ , → Audio → Latency. The shortfall is your driver error — the part of the journey the interface’s report missed.
  9. Enter that difference in Driver Error Compensation — same Latency section; remember it is greyed out unless the external device is selected.
  10. Re-record the eight bars and re-measure. Repeat until the recorded click sits on the grid line. Then unpatch the cable and write the final numbers into the table below.
Expected result

At 256 samples / 48 kHz, expect a round trip in the low-to-mid teens of milliseconds. The arithmetic floor is 2 × 256 ÷ 48 ≈ 10.7 ms; converter and driver overhead sits on top of that. The exact number is yours to measure — if your result is far outside that neighbourhood, suspect the configuration, not the M4. After compensation, the recorded click should sit on the grid line within a few samples.

Why this works

The delay’s floor is simple arithmetic: round trip ≈ 2 × buffer ÷ sample rate — the chunk has to be filled once on the way in and once on the way out. The converters and the driver add a little more on top, and that extra is what the loopback exposes. Re-measure whenever the buffer size, sample rate, or driver changes — the compensation value is only valid for the configuration it was measured on.

Logbook

The loopback table, plus the configuration it belongs to: measured at ___ samples, 48 kHz, Reduced Latency When Monitoring off. A compensation number without its configuration is a future bug.

Step 03

Monitoring modes — the three paths

While you record a source, your rig offers exactly three ways to hear it. Each one trades speed against effects against truth. One signal, three nows:

MicroFreak → M4 blend knob → phones · near-zero ms · dry MicroFreak → M4 → USB → Live + FX → USB → M4 → phones · full round trip: ___ ms Digitakt own outs → M4 in 3/4 → blend → phones · near-zero ms, while a different USB copy records
Do this
  1. Path (a): turn the M4 blend knob fully toward input, set the MicroFreak track’s Monitor to Off. Play. This is direct monitoring — the immediate now, with no effects.
  2. Path (b): blend knob fully toward playback, Monitor In, drop one obvious effect (a delay, a saturator) on the track. Play. This is software monitoring — the processed now, late by your Step 02 number.
  3. Path (c): blend toward input, run a Digitakt pattern, arm a stereo track on Ext. In 3/4 with Monitor Off. You hear the box directly; the USB copy records in silence behind your back.
  4. After each path, write one line: what was instant, what was late, what was processed.
PathWhat you hearWhat gets printedWhen to use
(a) M4 direct monitoringblend knob toward input The input straight from the interface, near-zero delay — but dry: none of Live’s FX The dry signal, on the grid once compensation is set Recording a source dry; any time playing feel matters more than hearing FX
(b) Live software monitoringtrack monitor In / Auto The signal through Live’s full chain — with the full round-trip delay The dry input; FX are live on playback, printed only if you resample When FX are part of the instrument and the buffer is low enough to play against
(c) Digitakt’s own outputsanalog L/R through M4 inputs 3/4 The box itself, no added delay — but not the copy a USB-audio recording into Live captures Whichever copy you armed: the analog inputs, or a separate USB stream Jamming and programming on the Digitakt; verify the printed copy afterwards
Expected result

Path (a) and path (b) are unmistakably different under the fingers — (b) is the Step 01 feeling with a number now attached to it. Path (c) is the trap: it feels perfect because you are listening to the box itself, while the thing being recorded is a copy you have not heard yet.

Step 05

MIDI clock test — 64 bars of truth

MIDI clock keeps the Digitakt at the right tempo; it does not place individual hits. This test measures how loose it is — and, more importantly, whether the looseness stays constant or grows over time.

Do this
  1. Make Live the clock master — the one device that sets the tempo for everything else: ⌘ , → Link, Tempo & MIDI → MIDI Ports → enable Sync on the Digitakt II output port row.
  2. On the Digitakt: SETTINGS → MIDI CONFIG → SYNC → CLOCK RECEIVE and TRANSPORT RECEIVE on; send nothing. Press play in Live and confirm the Digitakt’s pattern starts and its screen shows Live’s tempo.
  3. Program a Digitakt kick on quarter notes, one bar, nothing else. Main outs → M4 inputs 3/4, as wired.
  4. In Live, build a reference click: a one-bar MIDI clip triggering a sharp click sound on quarter notes, rendered or frozen to an audio track — this copy never leaves the computer, so its timing is exact.
  5. Record the Digitakt’s audio (Ext. In 3/4, Monitor Off) for 64 bars alongside the reference click.
  6. Measure the gap between the two with the Step 02 technique — ms time ruler, maximum zoom, drag-select from the click’s first edge to the kick’s first edge — at bar 1 and again at bar 64.
Expected result

A constant small offset between click and kick — that is latency: knowable, correctable, livable. If the offset at bar 64 is meaningfully larger than at bar 1, that is drift — the gap growing over time — and no compensation field fixes drift. Drift means the clock chain itself is wrong: two devices both trying to be the master, a flaky USB hub, a stale driver.

Step 06 · Control path

The other cable — notes, CC, and clock

The USB cable carries three different sentences — a note (“play this pitch now”), a CC (“this knob is now at value X”), and clock (“tick”). This lab has covered clock; this step proves the other two, because Lab 003’s performance riding depends on them.

Do this
  1. Create a MIDI track (⌘ ⇧ T), set its output to the MicroFreak port, channel 1, Monitor In. Play the Launchpad pads — the MicroFreak sounds: that is the NOTE path, through Live.
  2. Now the CC/mapping path: press ⌘ M — Live tints everything mappable. Click a mappable control (a track volume, or a device dial), then move a Launchpad fader or knob; the assignment appears in the mapping browser on the left.
  3. Set the Min/Max range for the mapping in that browser row — a mapped knob with a chosen range is a performance decision made in advance.
  4. Press ⌘ M again to exit map mode, then move the hardware control and watch the mapped dial follow.
Expected result

Notes from the pads sound the synth through Live’s path; the mapped dial follows the hardware with the range you chose; and you can say out loud which of the three message types is doing what.

Fault ritual · Monitoring

The doubled-monitoring comb filter

Predict

Write the expected symptom before creating the fault: two copies of one signal, milliseconds apart, added together at your headphones. What should that sound like? (Hint: the concepts table already told you.)

Create

On the MicroFreak input, enable both listening paths at once: turn the M4’s blend knob toward direct input and set the Live track’s Monitor to In.

Observe

Play a long held note and describe what you hear in the logbook: hollow, phasey, comb-filtered — like the sound is arriving through a tube. Sweep the filter cutoff and notice that the hollow notches stay in the same places while the tone moves through them.

Diagnose

Two copies of the same signal, offset by the software path’s round-trip delay, added together at your headphones. At every frequency where the delayed copy arrives upside-down relative to the first, the two cancel — leaving a comb of notches across the whole spectrum. Nothing is broken; two healthy paths are simply both on.

Fix

Choose one mode. Blend knob to playback for software monitoring, or Monitor Off for direct. The hollowness vanishes the instant only one copy survives.

Write the rule

One monitoring path per source, declared before recording. File the full entry: symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule.

Bench principle

You cannot remove latency — only decide where it lives. Tightness is not a feel you summon at the take; it is a routing decision made before it.
