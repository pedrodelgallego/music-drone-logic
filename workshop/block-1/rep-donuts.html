Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: late on purpose, reliably

Take a tight pattern and move only the snare late — by the same amount, every bar — until lateness becomes groove.

The point

Groove is lateness that repeats exactly — a missed hit wanders, a placed hit is late by the same amount every bar.

The system you are replicating
Source recordJ Dilla — Donuts
The effectthe snare leans back, identically, bar after bar
The mechanismone element nudged late by a fixed amount, the rest on the grid
FidelityAs close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Digitakt II main outs → M4 inputs 3/4, clock-slaved to Live as in the previous lesson
  • New set saved as REP-donuts, or continue from REP-spastik
  • Phones knob at the Lab 001 mark
  • Timebox: one sitting, 60–120 minutes — finding their exact offset takes repeated A/B passes, and the passes are the lesson
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to about one minute of Donuts, pick one track, and choose the 8–16 bars where the snare’s lean is most obvious — that section is what you will replicate. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Check each answer against the reveal only after you have written your own.

  1. Which drum is late — and is it the same drum every bar?
    Answer

    The snare — and yes, the same drum every bar. The kick and hats hold the frame so the snare has something to lean against; if everything were late, nothing would be. Relisten and check the kick against your tapping foot: it lands where you expect, which is exactly what makes the snare’s lean audible.

  2. Roughly how late does it feel — a hair, or almost a stumble?
    Answer

    A hair — tens of milliseconds, closer to a lean than a stumble. It is small enough that a casual listener only feels it as “lazy” or “drunk”, not small enough to vanish. You will find the exact number with your nudge key later; for now, commit to one word for the size and see if the gear agrees with you.

  3. Does the lateness ever change amount?
    Answer

    No — within a section the amount is fixed. The snare is not drifting around behind the beat; it is parked at one distance behind it, and that fixedness is what your ear reads as confidence. If the amount seems to change, you have probably crossed into a different loop or section — choose one section and stay in it.

  4. Why does it read as groove and not as sloppiness? (Hint: because it repeats exactly.)
    Answer

    Because the lateness is identical every bar. Randomness reads as error; repetition reads as intent — the ear forgives almost any placement once it can predict it. A drummer who misses lands somewhere different each time; Dilla’s snare lands in the same wrong-but-right place over and over. Relisten to eight bars and follow only the snare: the lean never wavers.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: only the snare moves — and it lands where the record’s snare lands; kick and hats stay machine-tight.
  • The lateness is identical every bar — reliable, not random, and the same amount as theirs.
  • Pushed one step past the match it stumbles; you found that edge and came back to their offset.
Phase 1 · ~30 min

Reference in, pattern transcribed — on the grid first

The lean only means something against a straight frame, so you build the straight version first: the record’s pattern, every hit on the grid, including the snare. Starting tight and then moving one element is the whole method — it is also how you will know, later, exactly how far you moved.

Do this
  1. Drop your chosen Donuts track into Live on a track named REF and loop the 8–16 bars you chose in the reference pass.
  2. Gain-match it with a Utility so it sits at your working level, and keep it one solo away for the whole sitting.
  3. Transcribe their drum pattern by ear — not a generic tight pattern, theirs: pick the closest Digitakt sounds to their kit and program every kick, snare, and hat where you hear it.
  4. Loop yours against REF and correct, voice by voice, until the pattern is the record’s. Expect several passes — solo each voice against the record at least once.
  5. Keep everything on the grid, dry, no swing — including the snare. The lean comes later, as one deliberate move.

Expected result

A dry, machine-tight version of the record’s pattern in your sounds. Against REF it should already be recognizably the same music — and noticeably stiffer. That stiffness is the gap this lesson closes.

Phase 2 · ~25 min

Nudge the snare and find their offset

Now the one deliberate move. You push only the snare late, in small equal steps, comparing against the record after every step, until your snare sits where theirs sits. The amount must be the same on every snare trig — the groove lives in that sameness, not in the lateness itself.

Do this
  1. Pick one nudge method and stick to it. Digitakt: hold each snare trig and press the right arrow key to nudge its micro-timing late, click by click. Live: record the pattern, turn snap off, and drag only the snare hits. One method, not both.
  2. Loop yours against REF and nudge the snare later, one click or a few ms at a time. Apply the same number of clicks to every snare trig before you listen again.
  3. After each step, A/B for a few bars: is your snare still ahead of theirs, level with it, or past it? Adjust and repeat — expect many small passes; the offset is found, not guessed.
  4. Stop when your snare sits where theirs sits — leaning back by the same amount, bar after bar. Keep kick and hats on the grid; on the record, only the snare leans.
Expected result

A loop that suddenly relaxes — the stiffness from Phase 1 melts at one specific offset, and at that offset your snare and theirs land together when both play. Note the click count or ms value; you will verify it with a ruler in Phase 4.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The lean changes from bar to barSnare trigs carry different micro-timing amountsHold each snare trig and set every one to the same value; one number for all
The whole pattern feels late, not just the snareNudged the wrong trigs, or used both methods at onceZero everything, recheck kick and hats sit at zero, redo the nudge on snare trigs only
Phase 3 · ~10 min

Overshoot once to feel the edge

A match you cannot overshoot is a match you found by luck. Pushing past the record’s offset — until the lean collapses into a stumble — proves there is an edge, and that their snare sits deliberately on the groove side of it. Then you come back.

Do this
  1. Push one step past the match — one more click, or another 10 ms — and listen to a few bars.
  2. Keep going, a step at a time, until the snare stops reading as a lean and starts reading as a mistake. Write down where that happened.
  3. Come back to the setting that matched the record. Listen once more to confirm the relaxation returns.
Expected result

Two numbers in the logbook: the matched offset and the stumble point. Now you know their offset is a placement, not an accident — it sits a measurable distance inside the edge.

Phase 4 · ~20 min

Record it and measure the groove in ms

Last, the claim “identical every bar” becomes a measurement. You print two minutes of the matched version through the calibrated path, then put a ruler on two different bars. If the two numbers are equal, the lateness is engineering; if they differ, something in the chain is adding its own timing on top of yours.

Digitakt II · snare trigs +___ ms → main outs → M4 in 3/4 → Live record · Ext. In 3/4 · Monitor Off
Do this
  1. Restart the transport — the Lab 002 reset rule — then record 2 minutes of the matched version into Live (Ext. In 3/4, Monitor Off).
  2. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Set the ruler to minutes:seconds:milliseconds, as in Lab 002, and zoom to two snare hits in different bars.
  3. Measure both offsets from the grid in ms — they must be equal. Write the number; that number is their groove, found by your ear and stated as engineering.
Expected result

Two equal ms readings, bars apart — the picture the timeline diagram promised. The kick sits on its lines; the snare sits the same fixed distance after its lines, every time.

Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the REF section, then answer:

  • Does your snare land in the same late place as theirs, bar after bar? Loop 4 bars of each and listen only to the snares.
    Answer

    Listening to snares only is the honest test, because the rest of the kit masks small timing differences. Four bars of theirs should sound like one snare placement repeated four times — and four bars of yours should sound the same way, at the same distance. If yours wanders where theirs repeats, the miss is consistency, not amount: fix the per-trig values before touching the offset again.

  • 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate to say which is which? Play both to your own hips: do they move you the same way?
    Answer

    The hips are a legitimate instrument here: a matched offset produces the same lean in the body, and a missed one produces a polite nod instead of a drag. If the friend would not hesitate, the giveaway on this lesson is usually timbre — your clean sounds against their dusty samples — rather than placement. Say which it is out loud and write it in the miss list; a timing miss and a tone miss have different fixes, and naming the right one is the skill.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • What carries into the lab: “on the grid” is a choice, not a default — and Lab 003’s groove will be built on choices like this.
