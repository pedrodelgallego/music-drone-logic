Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: late on purpose, reliably

Copy one clear section of J Dilla — Donuts. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: one element nudged late by a fixed amount, the rest on the grid. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating

Before you start
  • Digitakt II main outs → M4 inputs 3/4, clock-slaved to Live as in the previous lesson
  • New set saved as REP-donuts, or continue from REP-spastik
  • Phones knob at the Lab 001 mark
  • What you will make: a short replica of J Dilla — Donuts. Focus on this effect: the snare leans back, identically, bar after bar.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: one element nudged late by a fixed amount, the rest on the grid.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of J Dilla — Donuts. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Which drum is late — and is it the same drum every bar?
    Answer

    The snare - and yes, the same drum every bar. The kick and hats hold the frame so the snare has something to lean against; if everything were late, nothing would be.

  2. Roughly how late does it feel — a hair, or almost a stumble?
    Answer

    A hair - tens of milliseconds, closer to a lean than a stumble. It is small enough that a casual listener only feels it as “lazy” or “drunk”, not small enough to vanish.

  3. Does the lateness ever change amount?
    Answer

    No - within a section the amount is fixed. The snare is not drifting around behind the beat; it is parked at one distance behind it, and that fixedness is what your ear reads as confidence.

  4. Why does it read as groove and not as sloppiness? (Hint: because it repeats exactly.)
    Answer

    Because the lateness is identical every bar. Randomness reads as error; repetition reads as intent - the ear forgives almost any placement once it can predict it.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: only the snare moves — and it lands where the record’s snare lands; kick and hats stay machine-tight.
  • The lateness is identical every bar — reliable, not random, and the same amount as theirs.
  • Pushed one step past the match it stumbles; you found that edge and came back to their offset.
Phase 1 · ~30 min

Reference in, pattern transcribed — on the grid first

Phase focus: reference in, pattern transcribed — on the grid first. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Drop your chosen Donuts track into Live on a track named REF and loop the 8–16 bars you chose in the reference pass.
  2. Live Gain-match it with a Utility so it sits at your working level, and keep it one solo away for the whole sitting.
  3. Digitakt II Transcribe their drum pattern by ear — not a generic tight pattern, theirs: pick the closest Digitakt II sounds to their kit and program every kick, snare, and hat where you hear it.
  4. Digitakt II + Live Loop yours against REF and correct, voice by voice, until the pattern is the record’s.
  5. Digitakt II + Live Expect several passes — solo each voice against the record at least once.
  6. Digitakt II Keep everything on the grid, dry, no swing — including the snare.
  7. Digitakt II The lean comes later, as one deliberate move.

Expected result

A dry, machine-tight version of the record’s pattern in your sounds. Against REF it should already be recognizably the same music — and noticeably stiffer. That stiffness is the gap this lesson closes.

Phase 2 · ~25 min

Nudge the snare and find their offset

Phase focus: nudge the snare and find their offset. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Pick one nudge method and stick to it.
  2. Digitakt II Digitakt II: hold each snare trig and press the right arrow key to nudge its micro-timing late, click by click.
  3. Digitakt II + Live Live: record the pattern, turn snap off, and drag only the snare hits.
  4. Digitakt II + Live One method, not both.
  5. Live Loop yours against REF and nudge the snare later, one click or a few ms at a time.
  6. Live Apply the same number of clicks to every snare trig before you listen again.
  7. Live After each step, A/B for a few bars: is your snare still ahead of theirs, level with it, or past it?
  8. Live Adjust and repeat — expect many small passes; the offset is found, not guessed.
  9. Live Stop when your snare sits where theirs sits — leaning back by the same amount, bar after bar.
  10. Live Keep kick and hats on the grid; on the record, only the snare leans.
Expected result

A loop that suddenly relaxes — the stiffness from Phase 1 melts at one specific offset, and at that offset your snare and theirs land together when both play. Note the click count or ms value; you will verify it with a ruler in Phase 4.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The lean changes from bar to barSnare trigs carry different micro-timing amountsHold each snare trig and set every one to the same value; one number for all
The whole pattern feels late, not just the snareNudged the wrong trigs, or used both methods at onceZero everything, recheck kick and hats sit at zero, redo the nudge on snare trigs only
Phase 3 · ~10 min

Overshoot once to feel the edge

Phase focus: overshoot once to feel the edge. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Push one step past the match — one more click, or another 10 ms — and listen to a few bars.
  2. Keep going, a step at a time, until the snare stops reading as a lean and starts reading as a mistake.
  3. Logbook Write down where that happened.
  4. Logbook Come back to the setting that matched the record.
  5. Live Listen once more to confirm the relaxation returns.
Expected result

Two numbers in the logbook: the matched offset and the stumble point. Now you know their offset is a placement, not an accident — it sits a measurable distance inside the edge.

Phase 4 · ~20 min

Record it and measure the groove in ms

Phase focus: record it and measure the groove in ms. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Digitakt II · snare trigs +___ ms → main outs → M4 in 3/4 → Live record · Ext. In 3/4 · Monitor Off
Do this
  1. Live Restart the transport — the Lab 002 reset rule — then record 2 minutes of the matched version into Live (Ext. In 3/4, Monitor Off).
  2. Live Observe — see it, not just hear it. Set the ruler to minutes:seconds:milliseconds, as in Lab 002, and zoom to two snare hits in different bars.
  3. Live Measure both offsets from the grid in ms — they must be equal.
  4. Logbook Write the number; that number is their groove, found by your ear and stated as engineering.
Expected result

Two equal ms readings, bars apart — the picture the timeline diagram promised. The kick sits on its lines; the snare sits the same fixed distance after its lines, every time.

Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the REF section, then answer:

  • Does your snare land in the same late place as theirs, bar after bar? Loop 4 bars of each and listen only to the snares.
    Answer

    Listening to snares only is the honest test, because the rest of the kit masks small timing differences. Four bars of theirs should sound like one snare placement repeated four times — and four bars of yours should sound the same way, at the same distance. If yours wanders where theirs repeats, the miss is consistency, not amount: fix the per-trig values before touching the offset again.

  • 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate to say which is which? Play both to your own hips: do they move you the same way?
    Answer

    The hips are a legitimate instrument here: a matched offset produces the same lean in the body, and a missed one produces a polite nod instead of a drag. If the friend would not hesitate, the giveaway on this lesson is usually timbre — your clean sounds against their dusty samples — rather than placement. Say which it is out loud and write it in the miss list; a timing miss and a tone miss have different fixes, and naming the right one is the skill.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • What carries into the lab: “on the grid” is a choice, not a default — and Lab 003’s groove will be built on choices like this.
