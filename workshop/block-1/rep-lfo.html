Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the note your speakers decide

Copy one clear section of LFO — “LFO”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a sub-register bassline with a brief higher blip as its advertisement, placed tight on the grid against a dry beat. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the Lab 002 wiring; Live’s Operator stands in for the sub if you prefer to stay in the box
  • New set saved as REP-lfo, tempo around the record’s — tap it from REF in phase 1
  • Your laptop speaker and a phone within reach — the lesson auditions one print on three playback paths, and hunting for the phone mid-pass breaks the comparison
  • Safety: sustained sub on headphones is deceptive — it loads the ear while metering and sounding modest. Phones at the Lab 001 mark or lower, a real break every 20 minutes, and never raise the level to “find” the bass
  • What you will make: a short replica of LFO — “LFO”. Focus on this effect: a bass hook whose fundamental does not exist on small speakers; the record changes identity with the playback path.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a sub-register bassline with a brief higher blip as its advertisement, placed tight on the grid against a dry beat.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of LFO — “LFO”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. On the headphones — what is the loudest element of the track?
    Answer

    The bass, and it is not close. The sub carries the track from below - more weight than the kick, more presence than the bleeps - and some of what it does arrives as pressure rather than tone.

  2. Now the laptop speaker — where did the bassline go?
    Answer

    It is gone. The fundamental sits below what the laptop’s driver can move, so the loudest element of the record is simply absent - what remains is the beat, the bleeps, and a thin ghost where the hook was.

  3. On the small speaker, what tells you a bass note happened at all?
    Answer

    The brief brighter blip riding the bass - a flick of harmonics at each note’s start - plus the rhythm-shaped hole where the weight should be.

  4. Is the bass loose or tight against the beat?
    Answer

    Tight - dead on the grid against a dry beat. Sub demands that: the ear barely localizes very low frequencies in time, so a late sub does not read as groove, it reads as mud.

  5. Why would anyone put the hook where most speakers cannot play it?
    Answer

    Because the record was aimed at big systems - club and sound-system playback, where 50 Hz is not a gap but the main event - and it hedged the bet with the blip for everything smaller.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a sub hook around 40–60 Hz with a short brighter top layer, against a dry beat — balanced so the hook reads on the DT 990s, the laptop, and the phone, without the sub crushing the path that can play it.
  • The sub stays tight on the grid — in this register, placement error is mud, not feel.
  • The blip is an advertisement, not a melody: short, quiet on the good path, decisive on the small ones.
  • Levels are set by the meter and the ledger, never by whichever speaker you happen to be hearing.

The record’s machinery in one row: a sine-heavy bassline whose fundamental sits around 40–60 Hz, a short brighter blip at each note’s start, a dry drum-machine beat holding the grid, and a balance struck in the knowledge that most speakers will never play the fundamental.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference, three ways

Phase focus: the reference, three ways. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak + Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “LFO” in, loop the most bass-exposed 8–16 bars, and trim with a Utility to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  2. Live Tap Live’s tempo from the loop.
  3. Live + Logbook Drop Audio Effects → Spectrum on REF and read where the bass fundamental sits — note the Hz number in the logbook.
  4. Live + Logbook Play one minute on the DT 990s at the mark.
  5. Logbook One logbook line: what the track is about on this path.
  6. Recorder Play the same minute on the laptop speaker, then the phone speaker, with Spectrum still in view.
  7. Recorder One line per path — and note that the meter and Spectrum keep showing the bass that the room no longer contains.
  8. Live Keep REF one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
Expected result

Three logbook lines describing what sounds like three different records, a Hz number for the record’s fundamental, and the lesson’s central disagreement witnessed: the meter reads the print while each speaker decides the experience.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The bass seems present on the laptop tooYou are hearing the blip and the upper harmonics, not the fundamental — or the room is flatteringWatch Spectrum’s low shelf while listening; the region below ≈ 80 Hz is what the laptop cannot give you
No clear fundamental visible on SpectrumLooking at a section where the bass rests, or the analyzer’s averaging is too fastLoop the hook itself and slow Spectrum’s averaging until the low peak holds still
Phase 2 · ≈ 20 min

The sub voice — one octave below comfort

Phase focus: the sub voice — one octave below comfort. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, filter cutoff low so the wave is nearly pure — weight, no edge.
  2. MicroFreak (In the box: an Operator sine.) No LFO, no movement in the patch.
  3. Logbook Write a two-bar bassline in the record’s register — notes between E1 and B1 (≈ 41–62 Hz).
  4. Live Verify the real pitch with Spectrum or Live’s Tuner; octave mistakes are the rule down here, not the exception.
  5. Live Short notes, clean releases — the hook is rhythmic, and overlapping sub notes turn into a continuous rumble that no path survives.
  6. Live Set the M4 input-1 gain on a held note, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — the Lab 001 ledger practice;
  7. Live the ear is the wrong instrument for this register, so trust the meter for level.
  8. Digitakt II + Live Record the line into a track named SUB, tight on the grid — quantize the take, no swing.
Expected result

A printed sub line at ≈ −12 dBFS whose Spectrum peak sits where the logbook says the record’s does — felt clearly on the DT 990s, and (check now) essentially absent from the laptop speaker. That absence is correct and is the problem phase 3 solves.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The line sounds an octave too high — bass you hear, not feelWrong octave at the keyboard; in this register the ear misjudgesCheck the note name in Tuner or the MIDI clip; the fundamental belongs at ≈ 41–62 Hz
Sub audible on the laptop alreadyFilter open too far — harmonics carrying the note upstairsClose the cutoff until the laptop loses the note; purity now is what makes phase 3 mean something
Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

The blip and the beat

Phase focus: the blip and the beat. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Duplicate the SUB MIDI to a new track named BLIP, same notes one or two octaves up.
  2. Live Same patch family with the filter more open, or a second Operator with a brighter wave.
  3. Live Make it short: fast attack, decay well under the note length — a flick at each note’s front, not a doubled melody.
  4. Live If it sings, shorten it.
  5. Digitakt II + Live Build a dry, minimal beat — kick and a hat or clap, Digitakt II per the Lab 002 path or a Drum Rack — every hit on the grid, no sends.
  6. Digitakt II + Live The beat is the frame, not the subject.
  7. Digitakt II + Live Balance on the DT 990s first: blip well under the sub — present at note starts, never the lead.
  8. Logbook Set the blip’s fader by ear on the good path, then write the dB value down.
  9. Live Loop everything against REF at matched loudness and adjust until the two systems — yours and theirs — read as the same kind of machine.
Expected result

On the headphones: a sub hook with a discreet bright edge, over a dry beat — close kin to the record’s section. The blip barely registers as a separate element on this path; its real audience is the speakers that are about to be consulted.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The blip reads as its own melody on the good pathToo long or too loud — it has stopped advertising and started performingShorten the decay first, then lower the fader; the order matters
Sub and kick fight — the low end pumps or blursBoth occupying the same register at the same momentsShorten the kick’s tail or move the sub notes off the kick’s steps; down here, two tenants never share a room well
Phase 4 · ≈ 25 min

The three-path audition and the re-balance

Phase focus: the three-path audition and the re-balance. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Recorder Render 8 bars: select the loop, File → Export Audio/Video, and get the file somewhere the phone can play it.
  2. Recorder Audition at comparable, moderate loudness: DT 990s at the mark, then laptop, then phone speaker.
  3. Logbook Per path, one logbook line — survives: ___ · gone: ___ · the hook reads: yes/no.
  4. Recorder Re-balance for the misses: if the hook fails on the phone, raise the blip a dB or two or brighten it slightly — never raise the sub to fix a small speaker, it cannot help and it wrecks the good path.
  5. Recorder If the sub now overloads the headphone path — fatigue, bloat, the kick disappearing — pull the SUB fader, not the input gain;
  6. Recorder the ledger’s print level stays correct.
  7. Recorder Re-render and re-audition.
  8. Live + Logbook Two or three rounds; the deliverable is one balance whose three logbook lines all end in the hook reads: yes.
  9. Live + Logbook Take the scheduled break — sustained sub fatigue arrives without announcing itself, and a tired ear will mis-balance the next round.
Expected result

One print whose identity holds across three playback paths: full weight on the DT 990s, and on the laptop and phone the blip and the beat still spell out the same hook. The per-path table in the logbook is the artifact — a monitoring decision written down.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Fixing the phone keeps breaking the headphonesRe-balancing the sub instead of the blip — the two paths share that fader’s consequencesFreeze the sub’s level; only the blip’s level and brightness negotiate between paths
Each audition gives a different verdict at different volumesLoudness unmatched between paths — the louder path always flattersSet each device to a comparable moderate level before judging, and keep it there across rounds
Verify against the record

Play your final print and the REF section back to back on all three paths — matched, moderate loudness, judging one question per path.

  • On the smallest speaker, what tells you the bass note happened?
    Answer

    The blip and the hole — exactly the pair the record uses. The bright flick names each note’s position and roughly its pitch, and the rhythm-shaped absence underneath says something heavy is missing. If nothing on the phone tells you the note happened, the blip is too quiet, too dark, or too short — the advertisement never shipped. Relisten to “LFO” on the phone and then to yours: both should fail in the same eloquent way, gesturing at the same missing floor.

  • Does the meter agree that the sub is the loudest element?
    Answer

    Yes — solo the SUB track against the others and the meters say it plainly, and Spectrum shows its fundamental towering over the mix. The same element is simultaneously the loudest thing in the print and inaudible on the laptop, and both facts are true, because the meter reads the file and the speaker decides the room. That gap is the entire reason “what I hear vs what is printed” needed a lab. Relisten on the laptop with the meters in view one last time, and let the two instruments disagree in front of you.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1 — file the per-path survival table with them.
  • Carries into the lab: monitoring is part of the instrument. Lab 002’s “what I hear vs what is printed” table has a third column this record forces open — what they will hear — and a balance that ignores it is a mix for an audience of one.
