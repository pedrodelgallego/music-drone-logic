Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: The Tiny Variation

Copy one clear section of Terrence Dixon — “Minimalism”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: one parameter nudged per pass - a single delta at a time, nothing else. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordTerrence Dixon — “Minimalism”
The effectone looped bar heard as continuous forward motion
The mechanismone parameter nudged per pass — a single delta at a time, nothing else

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Digitakt II outs → M4 ins 1/2; MicroFreak ¼″ out → M4 in 3 if the section’s motif needs it.
  • New set saved as REP-minimalism, tempo 128 — then warp REF and adjust by ear.
  • P-locks are explained on the Digitakt II: hold a trig, turn a knob — the change belongs to that step only.
  • What you will make: a short replica of Terrence Dixon — “Minimalism”. Focus on this effect: one looped bar heard as continuous forward motion.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: one parameter nudged per pass - a single delta at a time, nothing else.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Terrence Dixon — “Minimalism”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Name the one changing parameter — in one word.
    Answer

    If you needed a sentence, you described the effect rather than the cause - the record moves by nudging a single timbral control, pass after pass, and one word (filter, tone, decay…) should cover your best hearing of it.

  2. How many elements live inside the looped bar? Count them.
    Answer

    A handful - a kick, a couple of percussion voices, a small tonal motif; the bar is countable on one hand. The poverty of material is deliberate: with this little, a tiny delta is a visible event.

  3. How often does the delta arrive — every bar, every two?
    Answer

    Roughly once per bar or two - a steady drip, never a flood. The rate is the record’s pulse of change: regular enough to pull you forward, slow enough that each nudge registers.

  4. Does any genuinely new element ever enter the section?
    Answer

    Essentially no - the bar you hear at the start is the bar you hear at the end, with minutes of single-parameter drift between. What feels like an arrival is an old element pushed past a threshold.

  5. Why does it hypnotise instead of bore — what is the patience doing?
    Answer

    The unchanged loop convinces your ear nothing is happening, so attention relaxes - and then each tiny delta lands on an open ear with the force of an event. Patience manufactures the sensitivity that makes minimal change audible.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target — what success sounds like:
  • The one-bar loop, rebuilt by ear — the record’s inventory, nothing extra.
  • One delta per pass or two, each traceable to a single named parameter.
  • Minutes of runtime in which nothing is ever added — and it still moves forward.
  • The record’s patience: your deltas arrive at its drip rate, not your nerves’.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.

The whole rig for this lesson — one looped bar, one knob’s worth of change per pass, one long take.

Phase 1

Set the reference

Phase focus: set the reference. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Drop “Minimalism” onto an audio track named REF.
  2. Live Loop a one-to-two-minute stretch — long enough to contain many passes of the bar and their deltas.
  3. Live Gain-match with a Utility on REF.
  4. Live Keep REF one solo away for the whole session.

A clean long loop of the section whose drift you will be matching, at an honest level.

Phase 3

Set up the delta engine

Phase focus: set up the delta engine. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

  • Safety — before the long take: single deltas are small, but they accumulate — twenty filter nudges in the same direction can end somewhere much louder and sharper than where you mixed. Headphones at the mark or lower — never turn up to inspect. Decide the parameter’s ceiling now, write it down, and stop nudging when you reach it.
Do this
  1. Live + Logbook Name the delta parameter in the logbook — one word, the same word you wrote in the reference pass.
  2. Live + Logbook Decide the move size: small enough that one delta is barely noticeable, large enough that five in a row are undeniable.
  3. Live + Logbook Rehearse two minutes unrecorded: nudge at the top of a bar — a p-lock value or one knob — then hands off for the rest of the pass.
  4. Logbook Decide and write down the ceiling and floor for the parameter — the take ends inside them.
Expected result

A named parameter, a calibrated move size, written limits — and hands that have practised staying still.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Each delta is inaudible even in pairsMove size too timidDouble it — the record’s nudges are small, not invisible
You touched a second knob in rehearsalHabit — every other lesson rewarded itPut one finger on the chosen control and sit on the other hand; the constraint is physical before it is musical
Phase 4

Run it for minutes — the long take

Phase focus: run it for minutes — the long take. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Arm DT PRINT — Audio From → Ext. In → 1/2 — and record from before the first pass.
  2. Live Run the loop for at least three minutes, one delta per bar or two — match the drip rate you tapped out against REF.
  3. Live Log each delta as you go: a tally mark and the new value, nothing fancier.
  4. Live Change nothing else — no mutes, no fills, no second parameter, no stopping to fix;
  5. Live a flawed full take beats a perfect fragment.
Expected result

One long take in which the only events are single deltas — and which still, unmistakably, goes somewhere.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The take feels static even after minutesDeltas all in one direction at one rate — a ramp, not a driftListen to REF’s drip: it hesitates, holds, occasionally steps back; let the deltas breathe the same way
You added something at minute twoThe itch wonKeep the take, mark the moment, and record one more pass — knowing the itch arrives at minute two is half of beating it

Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the looped section at matched volume, then answer:

  • Name your changing parameter in one word — and is it the same word you wrote for the record?
    Answer

    One word should cover both — that is the proof you replicated the mechanism and not just the mood. If your word and the record’s word differ, you may still have made something good, but you studied a different engine. Relisten to two consecutive passes of the record and re-test your word against them.

  • Pick any minute of your take: is every audible change accounted for by exactly one line of the delta list?
    Answer

    It should be — in the record, every event for minutes at a time is one parameter crossing a threshold, and your list is the same claim in writing. An audible change with no line behind it means something else moved. Relisten to the record’s most eventful minute and notice it, too, would fit on three lines.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines:
  • Matched: what your replica got closest to — the bar, the drip rate, the patience; start with the delta that landed nearest the record’s.
  • Missed: what the record does that yours doesn’t (best guess why).
  • Change next: the one thing you would do differently on the next attempt.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1. Carries into étude 004: 004’s rule that the system, not your hands, carries the variation is this same minimal-delta engine moved inside the patch.
