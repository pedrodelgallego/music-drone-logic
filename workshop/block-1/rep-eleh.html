Replicate: level as composition

Copy one clear section of Eleh — Floating Frequencies / Intuitive Synthesis. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: two or three pure tones, each on its own minutes-long amplitude envelope, rising and falling past each other. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged; the Digitakt II rests this lesson
  • New set saved as REP-ELEH — no tempo worth setting; this lesson is measured in minutes per move
  • Pure low tones at sustained level are deceptive — they meter modestly and load the ear heavily, and low frequencies are felt before they read as loud. Phones at the lab-001 mark or lower, a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into riding and listening, not extra devices
  • What you will make: a short replica of Eleh — Floating Frequencies / Intuitive Synthesis. Focus on this effect: a piece whose only events are level changes too slow to catch, on tones too pure to hide anything.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: two or three pure tones, each on its own minutes-long amplitude envelope, rising and falling past each other.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Eleh — Floating Frequencies / Intuitive Synthesis. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many tones are sounding?
    Answer

    Two, or three at most - and you will overcount, because pure tones fuse into one sound when their balance is even and split apart again as it shifts. What reads as a new tone arriving is usually an old tone coming forward.

  2. What is the only thing that ever changes?
    Answer

    Level. Every “event” on this record is a loudness relationship shifting - one tone moving in front of another. Nothing changes pitch, and nothing changes color except as a side-effect of the balance moving.

  3. How fast does a change complete?
    Answer

    Minutes. A rise begins below the threshold of noticing and finishes after you have stopped waiting for it.

  4. Why do such pure tones not feel thin?
    Answer

    Because low pure tones load the room and the body - they arrive in the chest before they arrive as “loud,” and with nothing else in the spectrum the ear magnifies whatever is there.

  5. Where is the “mix” in this record?
    Answer

    It is the composition. On most records balance is presentation - a way of serving material written elsewhere. Here there is no elsewhere: which tone is in front is the entire content, so the mixing and the writing are one act.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same bareness and the same patience — two pure tones trading places over minutes, at matched loudness.
  • Two or three tones and nothing else — no effects tail, no filter movement, no third element to rescue you.
  • Every event is a level relationship shifting; nothing changes pitch and nothing changes color on purpose.
  • Moves complete in minutes — if you can hear a move happen, it was too fast.

The piece’s machinery in one row: two nearly pure low tones printed to their own tracks, two faders ridden past each other at minute speed, and a mix at nominal where the balance — not the level — is the score.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a track from Floating Frequencies / Intuitive Synthesis into it.
  2. Find the most characteristic three to four minutes — two tones audibly trading places — and loop them.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. ⌘ T once; rename: DRONE, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed.
  6. One source track for now — it becomes two in phase 2.
  7. Write in the logbook how many simultaneous tones you believe you hear, at three different timestamps in the loop.
  8. Keep the three numbers; phase 4 will explain why they disagree.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Eleh — Floating Frequencies / Intuitive Synthesis (2006) and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the track — as the smallest Live set that proves two or three pure tones, each on its own minutes-long amplitude envelope, rising and falling past each other.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects a piece whose only events are level changes too slow to catch, on tones too pure to hide anything.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: translate The reference and the track into FM tracks, sound locks, trig conditions, LFOs, macros, and Overbridge or main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates two or three pure tones, each on its own minutes-long amplitude envelope, rising and falling past each other, not just the surface of Eleh — Floating Frequencies / Intuitive Synthesis (2006).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one named, routed, unity-gain track waits for the tone, and three tone counts are on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The tone — one note, nearly pure, printed twice

Phase focus: the tone — one note, nearly pure, printed twice. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, one low note — no paraphony, no chord.
  2. Close the filter cutoff low enough that the wave is nearly pure;
  3. stop when the tone has no audible edge left, only weight.
  4. Zero filter movement, zero LFO — nothing in the patch may move.
  5. Envelope: attack slow, sustain full.
  6. The note should fade in like a light coming up and then simply exist.
  7. Set the M4 input-1 gain on the held tone, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — a steady sine is all sustain;
  8. there is no strike to fool you, but check the gain after a full minute of holding anyway.
  9. Print 4–5 minutes of one steady tone into DRONE; rename the track TONE A.
  10. Use the MicroFreak’s Hold so your hands can leave.
  11. ⌘ T, rename TONE B, Ext. In 1, and print a second 4–5 minute take a fifth or an octave away — same patch, same gain, different note.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Compose with the faders only

Phase focus: compose with the faders only. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Put a Audio Effects → Utility on each take if you want a trim, or use clip gain plus the fader — but make the ride itself on the fader, so the move is a hand gesture, not a number typed into a box.
  2. Plan three or four level relationships on paper first, in plain words: “A in front, B underneath” → “equals” → “B in front, A felt not heard.” That sentence chain is the score.
  3. Ride the two faders across 4–5 minutes of Arrangement, each move spread over at least a minute.
  4. Record the automation — ⌘ M to MIDI-map the faders to hardware, or use Arrangement automation recording;
  5. either route works.
  6. The rule, enforced without mercy: if you can hear a move happen, it was too fast.
  7. Redo that passage slower.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Hold it, A/B it, and read the meter against your ears

Phase focus: hold it, A/B it, and read the meter against your ears. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play your 4–5 minutes end to end, no touching.
  2. Sit through it at the mark or lower; if the urge to add a third element arrives, it is the lesson arriving.
  3. A/B one minute of yours against REF at matched loudness, judging only two things: are your changes as slow, and are your tones as bare?
  4. The meter lesson: watch Live’s track meters during your quietest passage and your fullest.
  5. Write in the logbook how small the dB difference reads versus how large the musical difference feels.
  6. Take the scheduled break.
  7. Low tones are felt before they read as loud — the fatigue arrives without announcing itself.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Could a listener name your events?
    Answer

    They should not be able to. The most a listener should manage is “it was different later” — the scene changed, but no one saw the stagehands. If they can point at a moment and say “there,” a ride was too fast, and that passage goes back to phase 3. Relisten to the record asking the same question; its events have no addresses either.

  • Did the loudest moment read as loud on the meter?
    Answer

    Barely. A few dB of pure-tone balance reads as a scene change to the ear while the meter shrugs — level and loudness are different quantities, measured by different instruments. This is exactly why the ledger’s numbers need ears behind them: the meter said almost nothing happened, and you just heard a whole piece happen. Relisten to your quietest and fullest passages once more with the meter in view, and let the disagreement settle in.
