Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: level as composition

Print two nearly pure low tones and compose a piece using nothing but their faders, the way Floating Frequencies / Intuitive Synthesis does — and hear the gain ledger’s subject, level, turn into the music itself.

The point

The fader is the score — two or three pure tones whose only events are loudness relationships shifting, over minutes, on material too bare to hide anything.

The system you are replicating
Source recordEleh — Floating Frequencies / Intuitive Synthesis (2006)

The mechanismtwo or three pure tones, each on its own minutes-long amplitude envelope, rising and falling past each other
Fidelityas close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged; the Digitakt rests this lesson
  • New set saved as REP-ELEH — no tempo worth setting; this lesson is measured in minutes per move
  • Pure low tones at sustained level are deceptive — they meter modestly and load the ear heavily, and low frequencies are felt before they read as loud. Phones at the lab-001 mark or lower, a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into riding and listening, not extra devices
Reference pass — 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to three or four minutes of Floating Frequencies / Intuitive Synthesis — this record refuses short windows, because its events take minutes to happen at all. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Open each model answer only after your own line exists.

  1. How many tones are sounding?
    Answer

    Two, or three at most — and you will overcount, because pure tones fuse into one sound when their balance is even and split apart again as it shifts. What reads as a new tone arriving is usually an old tone coming forward. Relisten and write a tone count at three different moments; watch the counts disagree with each other.

  2. What is the only thing that ever changes?
    Answer

    Level. Every “event” on this record is a loudness relationship shifting — one tone moving in front of another. Nothing changes pitch, and nothing changes color except as a side-effect of the balance moving. Relisten and try to find one event that is not a level change; the failure to find one is the answer.

  3. How fast does a change complete?
    Answer

    Minutes. A rise begins below the threshold of noticing and finishes after you have stopped waiting for it. Relisten and try to time one full rise from first suspicion to settled; you will likely give up before it finishes, which is the answer.

  4. Why do such pure tones not feel thin?
    Answer

    Because low pure tones load the room and the body — they arrive in the chest before they arrive as “loud,” and with nothing else in the spectrum the ear magnifies whatever is there. Bareness reads as presence, not absence, when the register is low enough. Relisten at the mark and notice where in your body the lowest tone registers first.

  5. Where is the “mix” in this record?
    Answer

    It is the composition. On most records balance is presentation — a way of serving material written elsewhere. Here there is no elsewhere: which tone is in front is the entire content, so the mixing and the writing are one act. Relisten and try to describe the piece without describing a balance; it cannot be done.

Build only after the answers exist — you are about to copy the system you just described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same bareness and the same patience — two pure tones trading places over minutes, at matched loudness. Closeness is the measure of your hearing.
  • Two or three tones and nothing else — no effects tail, no filter movement, no third element to rescue you.
  • Every event is a level relationship shifting; nothing changes pitch and nothing changes color on purpose.
  • Moves complete in minutes — if you can hear a move happen, it was too fast.

The piece’s machinery in one row: two nearly pure low tones printed to their own tracks, two faders ridden past each other at minute speed, and a mix at nominal where the balance — not the level — is the score.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Every slowness judgment in this lesson is a comparison against the record at matched loudness — and pure low tones meter low while feeling large, so an untrimmed reference would mislead in both directions at once. Physically, the Utility puts the record at nominal, where the only thing left to compare is how its balances move.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a track from Floating Frequencies / Intuitive Synthesis into it.
  2. Find the most characteristic three to four minutes — two tones audibly trading places — and loop them.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  4. ⌘ T once; rename: DRONE, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed. One source track for now — it becomes two in phase 2.
  5. Write in the logbook how many simultaneous tones you believe you hear, at three different timestamps in the loop. Keep the three numbers; phase 4 will explain why they disagree.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, one named, routed, unity-gain track waits for the tone, and three tone counts are on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The tone — one note, nearly pure, printed twice

The record’s material is almost nothing: a low tone with no edges. Physically, closing a low-pass on a simple wave strips its harmonics away toward a sine — the filter here is not an effect but a purifier, set once and never moved. The patch must be boring on purpose, because anything interesting in the tone would compete with the only thing this piece is about.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, one low note — no paraphony, no chord. Close the filter cutoff low enough that the wave is nearly pure; stop when the tone has no audible edge left, only weight.
  2. Zero filter movement, zero LFO — nothing in the patch may move. Envelope: attack slow, sustain full. The note should fade in like a light coming up and then simply exist.
  3. Set the M4 input-1 gain on the held tone, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — a steady sine is all sustain; there is no strike to fool you, but check the gain after a full minute of holding anyway.
  4. Print 4–5 minutes of one steady tone into DRONE; rename the track TONE A. Use the MicroFreak’s Hold so your hands can leave.
  5. ⌘ T, rename TONE B, Ext. In 1, and print a second 4–5 minute take a fifth or an octave away — same patch, same gain, different note.
Expected result

Two printed takes on two tracks — minutes of nearly pure low tone each, a fifth or an octave apart, both at ≈ −12 dBFS. Neither is a piece yet; the balance between them is about to become the piece.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Compose with the faders only

Now the writing happens, and the pen is the fader. Physically, nothing is generated or processed in this phase — the same two tones play throughout, and the only variable is how many dB of one reach the mix relative to the other. A plan on paper comes first, because a ride this slow cannot be improvised: by the time you notice a move was wrong, it has been wrong for a minute.

Do this
  1. Put a Audio Effects → Utility on each take if you want a trim, or use clip gain plus the fader — but make the ride itself on the fader, so the move is a hand gesture, not a number typed into a box.
  2. Plan three or four level relationships on paper first, in plain words: “A in front, B underneath” → “equals” → “B in front, A felt not heard.” That sentence chain is the score.
  3. Ride the two faders across 4–5 minutes of Arrangement, each move spread over at least a minute. Record the automation — ⌘ M to MIDI-map the faders to hardware, or use Arrangement automation recording; either route works.
  4. The rule, enforced without mercy: if you can hear a move happen, it was too fast. Redo that passage slower.
Expected result

A 4–5 minute arrangement whose entire event list is two fader lines crossing — the paper plan performed at minute speed, with no audible moment where any single move can be pointed at.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
The piece feels static rather than slowLevel moves too small — the relationships never clearly changeWiden the dynamic range of the plan; the front tone should be clearly in front
A move pops out as an audible eventThe ride was spread over less than a minuteSlow the hand — redo the passage with the move stretched past the minute mark
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Hold it, A/B it, and read the meter against your ears

The record’s last ingredient is your patience as a listener: the piece only works if you sit through it the way an audience would. Physically nothing new happens — you play the arrangement end to end, compare a minute of it against the record, and then put the meter and your ears side by side and let them disagree.

Do this
  1. Play your 4–5 minutes end to end, no touching. Sit through it at the mark or lower; if the urge to add a third element arrives, it is the lesson arriving.
  2. A/B one minute of yours against REF at matched loudness, judging only two things: are your changes as slow, and are your tones as bare?
  3. The meter lesson: watch Live’s track meters during your quietest passage and your fullest. Write in the logbook how small the dB difference reads versus how large the musical difference feels.
  4. Take the scheduled break. Low tones are felt before they read as loud — the fatigue arrives without announcing itself.
Expected result

A piece that holds attention with two tones and a balance, an A/B that comes down to slowness and bareness, and a logbook line about the gap between measured level and musical weight — the lab-001 lesson seen from the other side.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
Hiss becomes audible in the quiet passagesRecord level too low at the source — the noise floor was printed into the takeRe-print with the synth and preamp pushed per the ledger — never rescue it with the fader
Listening fatigue arrives before the piece endsLevel crept above the mark — low tones load faster than they readReturn to the mark, take the break now, and judge again afterward
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Could a listener name your events?
    Answer

    They should not be able to. The most a listener should manage is “it was different later” — the scene changed, but no one saw the stagehands. If they can point at a moment and say “there,” a ride was too fast, and that passage goes back to phase 3. Relisten to the record asking the same question; its events have no addresses either.

  • Did the loudest moment read as loud on the meter?
    Answer

    Barely. A few dB of pure-tone balance reads as a scene change to the ear while the meter shrugs — level and loudness are different quantities, measured by different instruments. This is exactly why the ledger’s numbers need ears behind them: the meter said almost nothing happened, and you just heard a whole piece happen. Relisten to your quietest and fullest passages once more with the meter in view, and let the disagreement settle in.
