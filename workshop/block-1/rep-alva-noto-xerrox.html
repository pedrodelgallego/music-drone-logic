Replicate: one faint source, slow swells

Copy one clear section of Alva Noto — Xerrox Vol. 1. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a single faint source — almost too quiet to register — shaped entirely by slow level swells. The source barely changes; the envelope does all the composing. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating

The effecta piece so quiet and so still it seems to materialise out of the room, then recede — movement you feel more than hear
The mechanismminimal microsound where one faint source is shaped entirely by slow level swells

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged; the Digitakt II rests this lesson
  • New set saved as REP-XERROX — no tempo worth setting; this lesson is measured in long slow envelopes
  • Very quiet material is deceptive — you will be tempted to turn it up to “find” it, and then the swells become shouts. Set the monitor once at the lab-001 mark and judge the music at that level, not louder; a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into drawing slow envelopes, not adding sources
  • What you will make: a short replica of Alva Noto — Xerrox Vol. 1. Focus on this effect: a piece that seems to materialise out of the room and recede, with one faint source and nothing else.
  • What you will turn off: extra layers, busy modulation, anything that makes the source “interesting” on its own — the interest must come from the swell.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: one faint source shaped entirely by slow level swells.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Alva Noto — Xerrox Vol. 1 at a fixed, quiet level. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many distinct sources are sounding?
    Answer

    One, mostly — a faint, slightly grainy texture with no clear pitch or attack. It can seem like several because it swells in and out, but it is usually a single source coming forward and receding, not new layers arriving.

  2. What is doing the “moving”?
    Answer

    Level. The source barely changes its content; what changes is how loud it is, on a swell so slow it feels like the room is breathing rather than like a fader moving. The envelope is the composition.

  3. How quiet is the quietest part?
    Answer

    Near silence — the source can drop so low you’re not sure it’s still there. That uncertainty is intended: the piece works at the threshold of audibility, where presence and absence blur.

  4. Does the source itself ever change colour?
    Answer

    Barely. There may be a faint grain or a slow filtering, but the dominant gesture is the swell, not a timbre change. If you heard a big colour shift, you were probably hearing a level swell reveal something that was always there.

  5. Where is the “composition” in something this still?
    Answer

    In the shape and timing of the swells — when the source rises, how far, how long it holds, how it recedes. With the source held nearly constant, the envelope is the score; nothing else is moving enough to be.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Live 12 automation: click the track title and choose a parameter from the lane menu; draw breakpoints by hand for envelopes longer than any LFO will reach, or use Audio Effects → Utility → Gain as the automation target.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control. Choose a quiet, textured engine (noise-ish or a soft wavetable), set a long slow attack and release, and leave the LFO either off or at a very slow rate so the source stays nearly still.
  • Digitakt II: idle this lesson — there is no rhythm here. If you must use it, only as a quiet sustained sample with its sends at zero.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same quietness and the same patience — one faint source breathing in and out over minutes.
  • One source and nothing else — no second layer, no busy modulation, no reverb wash to fake the depth.
  • Every event is a level swell; the source itself stays nearly constant.
  • Swells complete over tens of seconds to minutes — if you can hear a fader “move,” it was too fast.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one faint textured source held nearly constant, a single very slow level envelope drawn by hand, and a mix kept low so the swell is the entire content.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a section of Xerrox Vol. 1 into it.
  2. Find a stretch where a single swell rises and recedes clearly — three to four minutes — and loop it.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the loudest swell peaks ≈ −12 dBFS; the quiet parts will read far below.
  4. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson, at this fixed level.
  5. ⌘ T for the source, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed; name it SOURCE.
  6. Write in the logbook the timing of the swell you chose — when it starts to rise, when it peaks, when it’s gone — in seconds.
  7. Keep those numbers; phase 3 will rebuild that exact shape.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Alva Noto — Xerrox Vol. 1 (2000s) and use a quiet grain or wavetable source into Utility for the swell.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the track — as the smallest Live set that proves one faint source shaped entirely by slow level swells.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects the materialise-and-recede quality.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: a soft FM source with long envelopes, ridden by macro level, into main-out recording.
  2. Program the source so it demonstrates one faint source shaped by slow level swells, not just the surface of Xerrox Vol. 1.
  3. Keep one clear performance control — the swell level — and name it before recording.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a target-box shortcut only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

The record loops with its loudest swell at ≈ −12 dBFS, one named, routed, unity-gain source track waits, and the timing of one swell is on paper in seconds.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The source — one faint texture, held nearly still

Phase focus: the source. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: pick a quiet, slightly grainy engine — a soft wavetable or a noise-leaning source — and one held note.
  2. Set a long slow attack and a long release so the source has no sharp edge.
  3. Either leave the LFO off, or set it so slow it adds only a faint shimmer — the source must not be busy.
  4. Print 4–5 minutes of the source held at a steady, modest level into SOURCE — peaking ≈ −12 dBFS at the recorder so you have headroom to swell from.
  5. Use the MicroFreak’s Hold so your hands can leave.
  6. Solo it and confirm: on its own, this should be almost boring — a faint, even texture. The boredom is correct; the swell makes it music.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: build the source from a quiet grain/granular patch or a soft Wavetable pad with no internal movement.
  2. Build the phase goal — the faint source — as the smallest Live set that proves the source can be carried by level alone.
  3. Keep it on a named track, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Resample to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds against REF and change only the setting that most affects the faint, even texture.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: a low-index FM patch with slow envelopes gives a faint, nearly static source.
  2. Program the source so it demonstrates a faint texture carried by level, not just the surface of Xerrox Vol. 1.
  3. Keep level as the one named performance control.
  4. Record the source into Live or a recorder, then compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a shortcut only when the same faint character stays audible.
Expected result

A printed take of one faint, even texture — minutes long, with no sharp edge and no busy movement inside it. On its own it should feel almost too plain; that is exactly right.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Compose with the swell only

Phase focus: compose with the swell only. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Put a Audio Effects → Utility on SOURCE and automate its Gain — this is your swell.
  2. Draw the envelope by hand in Arrangement; no LFO is slow enough. Rebuild the shape you logged in phase 1: rise, peak, recede, with a near-silent valley between swells.
  3. Make the rise and the fall take tens of seconds each — long enough that no single instant reads as “the fader moved.”
  4. Plan two or three swells across the take in plain words on paper first: “emerge slowly → hold just present → recede to near-silence → emerge again, a little louder.”
  5. Let the valleys be genuinely quiet — the source dropping to the edge of audibility is the point, not a mistake to fix.
  6. The rule, enforced without mercy: if you can hear the swell happen as a “move,” it was too fast. Stretch it.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: automate Utility Gain on the source by hand for the swells.
  2. Build the phase goal — compose with the swell only — as the smallest Live set that proves level shapes the whole piece.
  3. Keep the source on a named track, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Resample to PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds against REF and change only the swell shape that most affects the materialise-and-recede feel.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: ride a macro mapped to level, recorded as automation, for each swell.
  2. Program the swells so they demonstrate level as the composition, not just the surface of Xerrox Vol. 1.
  3. Keep the level macro as the named, repeatable control across the pass.
  4. Record a full pass into Live or a recorder, then compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a shortcut only when the same slow-swell mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A 4–5 minute arrangement whose entire event list is two or three hand-drawn swells — the source emerging from near-silence and receding, with no audible moment where the fader can be pointed at.

A swell pops out as an audible eventThe rise or fall was spread over too few secondsSlow it — redraw the breakpoints so each swell takes tens of seconds
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Hold it, A/B it, and resist turning it up

Phase focus: hold it, A/B it, and resist turning it up. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play your 4–5 minutes end to end, no touching, at the lab-001 mark.
  2. If the urge to turn it up to “find” the source arrives, that is the lesson arriving — the quietness is the work.
  3. A/B one minute of yours against REF at matched loudness, judging only two things: are your swells as slow, and is your source as faint and plain.
  4. Watch the meter during a full swell: note how few dB the rise actually spans versus how large the musical arrival feels.
  5. Write that gap in the logbook — it is the lab-001 lesson seen from the quiet end.
  6. Take the scheduled break. Quiet listening invites creeping the monitor up; reset it after the break.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: judge the print against REF at the fixed quiet level.
  2. Confirm the smallest Live set still proves one faint source shaped by level.
  3. Use Utility to match the level against REF before judging.
  4. Keep the proof as a printed PRINT track.
  5. Change only the one swell that most affects the materialise-and-recede feel.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: verify the faint source and level macro hold across the pass.
  2. Confirm the program demonstrates level as the whole composition.
  3. Keep the level macro as the named, repeatable control.
  4. Record the full pass and compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a shortcut only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A piece that holds attention with one faint source and a few slow swells, an A/B that comes down to slowness and faintness, and a logbook line about how few dB a swell spans versus how large the arrival feels.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Could a listener point to a second source?
    Answer

    They should not be able to — in your piece or the record. If they can name a “second layer,” you added one instead of trusting the swell. The whole effect depends on one source coming forward and receding, so a second source goes back to phase 2 and gets deleted.

  • Did the swell carry the piece without a colour change to help?
    Answer

    That is the test. With the source held plain, the only thing composing is the envelope — its shape, depth, and timing. If your swells were bold and slow, the piece breathes. If it sagged, the fix is never a new sound; it is a deeper or slower swell. Relisten to the record: its source barely changes colour, and yet it never feels static.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
