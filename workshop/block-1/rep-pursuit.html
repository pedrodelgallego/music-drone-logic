Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: overdrive on purpose

Transcribe the bass line of a “Pursuit” section, drive it to the record’s amount on purpose, and put the deliberate version next to a real accident so your ears learn the difference.

The point

Distortion is a decision with an address — you choose what distorts, where in the chain, and how much; an accident chooses for you.

The system you are replicating
Source recordGesaffelstein — “Pursuit”
The effectdistortion as the lead sound, clean elements underneath
The mechanismdrive applied to chosen elements, at a chosen stage, by a chosen amount
Fidelityas close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, Digitakt II → M4 inputs 3/4, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged
  • New set saved as REP-PURSUIT, tempo 124
  • M4 phones knob at the lab-001 mark — it stays there for the whole lesson
  • Timebox: 60–120 minutes, then file it and stop — the extra time goes into transcription and matching passes, not extra devices
Reference pass — 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to about one minute of “Pursuit” — at least twice, once for the driven sound and once for everything around it. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Open each model answer only after your own line exists.

  1. What is distorted?
    Answer

    The synth bass — the riff that carries the track — is the driven element; the grit is its identity, not a coating on the mix. Relisten to a single note: the buzz starts and stops with the note, which tells you the distortion lives on that sound’s own channel, not across the whole record.

  2. What stays clean?
    Answer

    The kick — and most of the percussion frame around it. It thuds rather than buzzes, and it keeps the track readable underneath the aggression. If you could not point at it, relisten and follow the kick alone for eight bars; its cleanness is what makes the bass sound so violent by contrast.

  3. Is the distortion constant — or does it move?
    Answer

    Constant from note to note — the same grit density every time the riff repeats, which is the fingerprint of a decision rather than an accident. It changes only when the arrangement changes, never within a phrase. Relisten hunting for one note that is grittier than its neighbours; you will struggle to find it.

  4. Is the low end distorted too, or protected?
    Answer

    Protected. The kick’s fundamental stays clean, and even the driven bass keeps its note readable under the harmonics — the grit lives in the mids and top of that sound. A record this aggressive with a distorted low end would collapse into mud; relisten for how solid the bottom stays while everything above it snarls.

  5. Is the driven sound actually louder — or just denser?
    Answer

    Denser. At matched loudness the driven bass does not sit above the rest of the mix — it is thicker, with harmonics stacked on top of the same note. That is exactly the difference the gain-compensated Saturator A/B will let you hear in your own version: more spectrum, same level.

Build only after the answers exist — you are about to copy the system you just described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your sketch lands on the same riff with the same grit density — as close to the record’s bass as MicroFreak + Saturator get. Closeness is the measure of your hearing.
  • Denser and more aggressive, not louder — the Saturator’s output is gain-compensated so the A/B is fair, not flattered by the louder-is-better trap.
  • More harmonics on top, the note still readable underneath — pushed exactly as far as the record pushes it.
  • Consistent grit from note to note — the record’s decision, matched, not a wander.

The same riff at three addresses. Row one is the clean signal you will record; row two is the record’s move — distortion at a chosen device, gain-compensated; row three is the accident you will recreate at the end, where the converter, not you, decides.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the tracks

Every grit judgment you make in this lesson is a comparison against the record, so the record has to live in the session at the same loudness as your build. Physically, the Utility trim is what keeps the A/B honest — a louder version always sounds “better”, and this lesson dies if loudness gets to vote. Two destination tracks are enough: the bass and the clean ground under it.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Pursuit” into it.
  2. Find the most characteristic 8–16 bars for deliberate drive — the stretch where the driven bass carries everything over a clean kick — and loop them.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim its gain so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, matched to the build you are about to make. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  4. ⌘ T twice; rename with ⌘ R: BASS CLEAN and KICK.
  5. ⌘ ⌥ I to show In/Out. BASS CLEAN: Ext. In 1. KICK: Ext. In 3/4. Faders at 0 dB.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS and two named, routed, unity-gain tracks wait for audio. A solo-press flips you between the record and silence.

If it fails

No meter movement when testing an inputExt. In channel does not match the physical jackMicroFreak on 1, Digitakt on 3/4 — the lab-001 wiring table
Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

The bass, recorded clean

The record’s bass is a driven sound, but you record it clean — because the lesson is that the drive is a separate, later decision with its own address. Physically, what goes to disk here is the MicroFreak at the ledger gain, nothing hot anywhere; the grit will be added inside Live where you can A/B it fairly. The transcription is the slow part: same notes, same octave, same rhythm, off the record, by ear.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, Paraphonic OFF, cutoff ≈ 35 %, resonance ≈ 15 %, attack 0, decay medium — starting points.
  2. Solo REF and transcribe the riff by ear — same notes, same octave, same rhythm; the page cannot give the notes, hearing them is the exercise. Get the rhythm first, then the pitches; most of the riff’s menace is rhythm.
  3. Check the octave explicitly — play your candidate riff an octave up and an octave down against the record and keep the one that sits where the record sits.
  4. Make at least three solo-flip passes, nudging cutoff and decay one at a time, until the clean tone is as close as V.Analog gets to the note under the record’s grit.
  5. Set the M4 input-1 gain so peaks sit ≈ −12 dBFS — the lab-001 ledger value — and record the 8 bars into BASS CLEAN.
Expected result

An 8-bar clean take of the record’s riff, peaking ≈ −12 dBFS — recognisably the same line, just naked. It should sound politely wrong: right notes, none of the record’s violence. The violence comes next, on purpose.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
The riff never sits right against the recordWrong octave — the most common transcription missRe-test an octave up and down before touching any other parameter
Tone is buzzy before any drive existsCutoff or resonance well above the starting pointsPull both back toward ≈ 35 % / ≈ 15 % — the clean take must be clean for the A/B to mean anything
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Drive it — to the record’s amount

Now the decision gets made, at a chosen address: a Saturator on a duplicate of the clean track. Physically, the device generates new harmonics above the note — that is all distortion is — and the Output pulled down by the same amount the Drive went up keeps loudness out of the comparison. The record decides the amount; your job is to keep flipping until the grit density matches.

Do this
  1. Select BASS CLEAN, ⌘ D, rename the copy BASS DRIVEN. Identical audio, two addresses.
  2. On BASS DRIVEN, drop in Audio Effects → Saturator. Push Drive up — start at +18 dB.
  3. Pull Saturator’s Output down by roughly the same amount, and A/B against BASS CLEAN with the track activators until both play at matched loudness — same meter peaks, same perceived level.
  4. Solo-flip against REF and correct the Drive until the grit density matches the record’s bass; the record, not the default, decides the number. Re-compensate the Output after every Drive change, and make at least three flip-and-correct passes — the first one always overshoots.
  5. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Drop Audio Effects → Spectrum after the Saturator. Toggle the device on and off and watch new harmonics stack above the fundamental while the meter level stays matched. Note in the logbook roughly where the energy appeared — low-mids? top? — and whether that is where the record’s bass carries its energy too.
Expected result

Two tracks with identical audio and one decision between them: the driven copy is denser and more aggressive at the same loudness, with the note still readable underneath — and the flip against REF lands on the same grit.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
The driven version simply sounds “better” in every A/BOutput not pulled down — the louder-is-better trap is votingGain-compensate until both tracks meter the same, then judge again
Grit is harsher than the record at every Drive settingThe record wants less than you think — density, not amountHalve the Drive and walk it up in small steps, flipping against REF each step
Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

The clean ground and the sketch

The record’s aggression only works because something clean holds the floor — the kick is the contrast that makes the drive legible. Physically, this track stays untouched: no drive anywhere on its path, level set so it sits under the bass exactly as far as the record sets it. Then the sketch stretches your matched drive across the same changes the record makes, which is where a wrong Drive setting gets exposed.

Do this
  1. Digitakt: press REC, audition for the slot closest to the record’s kick, and place trigs where the record places them — 1, 5, 9, 13 is the likely shape; REF confirms it.
  2. Set Main Volume so KICK sits as far below the bass as the record’s kick does, peaking ≈ −12 dBFS or under, and record 8 bars — clean, no drive anywhere on this track.
  3. In Arrangement view, build a 2-minute sketch — mirror the section. Loop and tile the clips: BASS DRIVEN carries the riff the whole way; KICK holds the ground; BASS CLEAN stays muted, kept only for comparison.
  4. Drop elements in and out where the record’s section does, so the drive has to stay musical across the same changes.
  5. Play the sketch once end to end against the record’s section, listening only for moments where your grit stops working — note the bar numbers.
Expected result

A 2-minute sketch where the driven bass stays consistent and musical across the record’s changes, over a clean kick. If the grit was right in the 8-bar loop, it is still right at bar 60.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
The kick buzzes tooThe Digitakt is overdriving the fixed 3/4 line inputsLower Main Volume — on this pair the instrument’s knob is the record level
The sketch loses energy at the dropsYour mutes do not mirror the record’s entries and exitsCopy the record’s arrangement moves bar by bar; the drive needs the same scaffolding
Phase 5 · ≈ 25 min

The accident, side by side

To prove the record’s drive is a decision, you now make the version nobody decides: the same riff clipping the M4’s converter. Physically this is a different event in a different place — the wave arrives at the converter already too big and gets flattened before recording, permanently, with no device and no undo. Hearing the two distortions back to back is what files the difference into your ears.

  • Safety: this phase is deliberately hot. Phones at the mark or lower · record only a few seconds · reset the gain immediately afterwards · never turn the phones up to inspect a harsh sound.
Do this
  1. Recreate the accident. New audio track BASS CLIPPED, Ext. In 1. Raise the M4 input-1 gain until the red clip LED fires on every note — exactly the lab-001 step 07 fault — and record a few seconds of the same riff.
  2. Reset the gain to nominal immediately after.
  3. Place them side by side. Drag the clipped clip into the timeline directly after a Saturator passage, match its clip gain so loudness is comparable, and listen through once at the mark.
  4. Listen a second time, attention only on the note edges — where the Saturator rounds, the converter truncates.
  5. Write the sentence. One sentence in the logbook naming the difference between the Saturator version and the input clip — where each one lives, and which one you chose.
Expected result

The clipped take sounds harsh, positional, and unmusical next to both the record’s drive and yours — same riff, no address, nobody’s decision. The logbook sentence names the difference in your own words.

If it fails
SymptomLikely causeFix
The clip LED never firesMicroFreak output or input gain not actually hotConfirm the rear volume is at the ledger value, then keep raising the input-1 gain — briefly, phones at the mark
The clipped take sounds like the Saturator to youJudging loudness instead of character, or listening too loudMatch the clip gains, return phones to the mark, and compare only the note edges
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your sketch at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Could a friend, hearing 10 seconds of your bass then 10 of the record’s, say which is which? The longer they hesitate, the better your ears did.
    Answer

    If they hesitate, your riff, octave, and grit density are all close. An instant tell usually means note or octave errors before drive errors — the ear catches a wrong pitch faster than a wrong harmonic. Re-transcribe in phase 2 before reaching for the Drive knob again.

  • Is the input clip still obviously different — harsh, positional, unmusical — next to both the record’s drive and yours? Name the difference in one sentence: what distorts, where in the chain, and who decided.
    Answer

    It should be — and the sentence writes itself from the diagram: the Saturator distorts one chosen track inside Live, gain-compensated and repeatable; the clipped take distorted at the M4’s converter, before recording, permanently, on everything passing that input. The record’s drive has an address; the accident has none. Relisten to the handoff between the two clips — the moment the address disappears is audible.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • Carries into the lab: “too hot on purpose, for this sound” is a defensible level decision.
