Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: overdrive on purpose

Copy one clear section of Gesaffelstein — “Pursuit”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: drive applied to chosen elements, at a chosen stage, by a chosen amount. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: drive applied to chosen elements, at a chosen stage, by a chosen amount.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Gesaffelstein — “Pursuit”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is distorted?
    Answer

    The synth bass - the riff that carries the track - is the driven element; the grit is its identity, not a coating on the mix.

  2. What stays clean?
    Answer

    The kick - and most of the percussion frame around it. It thuds rather than buzzes, and it keeps the track readable underneath the aggression.

  3. Is the distortion constant — or does it move?
    Answer

    Constant from note to note - the same grit density every time the riff repeats, which is the fingerprint of a decision rather than an accident. It changes only when the arrangement changes, never within a phrase.

  4. Is the low end distorted too, or protected?
    Answer

    Protected. The kick’s fundamental stays clean, and even the driven bass keeps its note readable under the harmonics - the grit lives in the mids and top of that sound.

  5. Is the driven sound actually louder — or just denser?
    Answer

    Denser. At matched loudness the driven bass does not sit above the rest of the mix - it is thicker, with harmonics stacked on top of the same note.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your sketch lands on the same riff with the same grit density and low-end weight.
  • Denser and more aggressive, not louder — the Saturator’s output is gain-compensated so the A/B is fair, not flattered by the louder-is-better trap.
  • More harmonics on top, the note still readable underneath — pushed exactly as far as the record pushes it.
  • Consistent grit from note to note — the record’s decision, matched, not a wander.

The same riff at three addresses. Row one is the clean signal you will record; row two is the record’s move — distortion at a chosen device, gain-compensated; row three is the accident you will recreate at the end, where the converter, not you, decides.

Phase 1 · ≈ 15 min

The reference and the tracks

Phase focus: the reference and the tracks. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Pursuit” into it.
  2. Live Find the most characteristic 8–16 bars for deliberate drive — the stretch where the driven bass carries everything over a clean kick — and loop them.
  3. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim its gain so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, matched to the build you are about to make.
  4. Live REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. Live ⌘ T twice; rename with ⌘ R: BASS CLEAN and KICK.
  6. Live ⌘ ⌥ I to show In/Out.
  7. Live BASS CLEAN: Ext. In 1.
  8. Live KICK: Ext. In 3/4.
  9. Live Faders at 0 dB.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS and two named, routed, unity-gain tracks wait for audio. A solo-press flips you between the record and silence.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
REF later wins every A/B effortlesslyUtility never trimmed — the file is mastered loudTrim until the looped section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, same as your takes
No meter movement when testing an inputExt. In channel does not match the physical jackMicroFreak on 1, Digitakt II on 3/4 — the lab-001 wiring table
Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

The bass, recorded clean

Phase focus: the bass, recorded clean. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, Paraphonic OFF, cutoff ≈ 35 %, resonance ≈ 15 %, attack 0, decay medium — starting points.
  2. Live Solo REF and transcribe the riff by ear — same notes, same octave, same rhythm;
  3. Live the page cannot give the notes, hearing them is the exercise.
  4. Live Get the rhythm first, then the pitches; most of the riff’s menace is rhythm.
  5. Live Check the octave explicitly — play your candidate riff an octave up and an octave down against the record and keep the one that sits where the record sits.
  6. MicroFreak Make at least three solo-flip passes, nudging cutoff and decay one at a time, until the clean tone carries the note under the record’s grit.
  7. MicroFreak Set the M4 input-1 gain so peaks sit ≈ −12 dBFS — the lab-001 ledger value — and record the 8 bars into BASS CLEAN.
Expected result

An 8-bar clean take of the record’s riff, peaking ≈ −12 dBFS — recognisably the same line, just naked. It should sound politely wrong: right notes, none of the record’s violence. The violence comes next, on purpose.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The riff never sits right against the recordWrong octave — the most common transcription missRe-test an octave up and down before touching any other parameter
Tone is buzzy before any drive existsCutoff or resonance well above the starting pointsPull both back toward ≈ 35 % / ≈ 15 % — the clean take must be clean for the A/B to mean anything
Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Drive it — to the record’s amount

Phase focus: drive it — to the record’s amount. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Select BASS CLEAN, ⌘ D, rename the copy BASS DRIVEN.
  2. Live Identical audio, two addresses.
  3. Live On BASS DRIVEN, drop in Audio Effects → Saturator.
  4. Live Push Drive up — start at +18 dB.
  5. Live Pull Saturator’s Output down by roughly the same amount, and A/B against BASS CLEAN with the track activators until both play at matched loudness — same meter peaks, same perceived level.
  6. Live Solo-flip against REF and correct the Drive until the grit density matches the record’s bass;
  7. Live the record, not the default, decides the number.
  8. Live Re-compensate the Output after every Drive change, and make at least three flip-and-correct passes — the first one always overshoots.
  9. Live Observe — see it, not just hear it. Drop Audio Effects → Spectrum after the Saturator.
  10. Live Toggle the device on and off and watch new harmonics stack above the fundamental while the meter level stays matched.
  11. Logbook Note in the logbook roughly where the energy appeared — low-mids?
  12. Logbook top?
  13. Logbook — and whether that is where the record’s bass carries its energy too.
Expected result

Two tracks with identical audio and one decision between them: the driven copy is denser and more aggressive at the same loudness, with the note still readable underneath — and the flip against REF lands on the same grit.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The driven version simply sounds “better” in every A/BOutput not pulled down — the louder-is-better trap is votingGain-compensate until both tracks meter the same, then judge again
Grit is harsher than the record at every Drive settingThe record wants less than you think — density, not amountHalve the Drive and walk it up in small steps, flipping against REF each step
Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

The clean ground and the sketch

Phase focus: the clean ground and the sketch. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Digitakt II Digitakt II: press REC, audition for the slot closest to the record’s kick, and place trigs where the record places them — 1, 5, 9, 13 is the likely shape;
  2. Live REF confirms it.
  3. Digitakt II Set Main Volume so KICK sits as far below the bass as the record’s kick does, peaking ≈ −12 dBFS or under, and record 8 bars — clean, no drive anywhere on this track.
  4. Live In Arrangement view, build a 2-minute sketch — mirror the section.
  5. Live Loop and tile the clips: BASS DRIVEN carries the riff the whole way;
  6. Live KICK holds the ground; BASS CLEAN stays muted, kept only for comparison.
  7. Live Drop elements in and out where the record’s section does, so the drive has to stay musical across the same changes.
  8. Logbook Play the sketch once end to end against the record’s section, listening only for moments where your grit stops working — note the bar numbers.
Expected result

A 2-minute sketch where the driven bass stays consistent and musical across the record’s changes, over a clean kick. If the grit was right in the 8-bar loop, it is still right at bar 60.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The kick buzzes tooThe Digitakt II is overdriving the fixed 3/4 line inputsLower Main Volume — on this pair the instrument’s knob is the record level
The sketch loses energy at the dropsYour mutes do not mirror the record’s entries and exitsCopy the record’s arrangement moves bar by bar; the drive needs the same scaffolding
Phase 5 · ≈ 25 min

The accident, side by side

Phase focus: the accident, side by side. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

  • Safety: this phase is deliberately hot. Phones at the mark or lower · record only a few seconds · reset the gain immediately afterwards · never turn the phones up to inspect a harsh sound.
Do this
  1. Live Recreate the accident. New audio track BASS CLIPPED, Ext. In 1.
  2. Live Raise the M4 input-1 gain until the red clip LED fires on every note — exactly the lab-001 step 07 fault — and record a few seconds of the same riff.
  3. Live Reset the gain to nominal immediately after.
  4. Live Place them side by side. Drag the clipped clip into the timeline directly after a Saturator passage, match its clip gain so loudness is comparable, and listen through once at the mark.
  5. Live Listen a second time, attention only on the note edges — where the Saturator rounds, the converter truncates.
  6. Live + Logbook Write the sentence. One sentence in the logbook naming the difference between the Saturator version and the input clip — where each one lives, and which one you chose.
Expected result

The clipped take sounds harsh, positional, and unmusical next to both the record’s drive and yours — same riff, no address, nobody’s decision. The logbook sentence names the difference in your own words.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The clip LED never firesMicroFreak output or input gain not actually hotConfirm the rear volume is at the ledger value, then keep raising the input-1 gain — briefly, phones at the mark
The clipped take sounds like the Saturator to youJudging loudness instead of character, or listening too loudMatch the clip gains, return phones to the mark, and compare only the note edges
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your sketch at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Could a friend, hearing 10 seconds of your bass then 10 of the record’s, say which is which? The longer they hesitate, the better your ears did.
    Answer

    If they hesitate, your riff, octave, and grit density are all close. An instant tell usually means note or octave errors before drive errors — the ear catches a wrong pitch faster than a wrong harmonic. Re-transcribe in phase 2 before reaching for the Drive knob again.

  • Is the input clip still obviously different — harsh, positional, unmusical — next to both the record’s drive and yours? Name the difference in one sentence: what distorts, where in the chain, and who decided.
    Answer

    It should be — and the sentence writes itself from the diagram: the Saturator distorts one chosen track inside Live, gain-compensated and repeatable; the clipped take distorted at the M4’s converter, before recording, permanently, on everything passing that input. The record’s drive has an address; the accident has none. Relisten to the handoff between the two clips — the moment the address disappears is audible.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • Carries into the lab: “too hot on purpose, for this sound” is a defensible level decision.
