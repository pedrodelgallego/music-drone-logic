Replicate: One Bassline, Six Minutes

One rubbery bassline, per-trig accents, and the mute buttons — replicate a section of “Losing Control” closely enough to feel how emphasis and subtraction replace new material. This drill installs the Digitakt’s entire variation vocabulary: tune locks, velocity accents, filter locks, conditional trigs, and mute-mode arrangement.

The point

One bassline survives six minutes when accents, filter p-locks, and dropouts are the vocabulary — the story is told by emphasis and subtraction, never by new notes.

Before you start
  • Digitakt outs → M4 ins 3/4, level-checked; MicroFreak ¼″ out reachable for one sampling pass.
  • New set saved as REP-losing-control, tempo 128 — then warp REF and adjust by ear.
  • P-locks and accents live on the Digitakt: hold a trig, turn a knob — the change belongs to that step only. The whole drill is variations on that one gesture.
  • Timebox: 60–120 minutes. The bassline is one bar, so transcription is quick — building six honest variants and performing the mutes with the record’s restraint are the slow parts. Stop when the dropouts land, not before.
  • Session one (phases 1–4): set the reference, sample the note, play the line as tune locks, place the accents. Stop when your bar and the record’s chase each other.
  • Session two (phases 5–9): filter locks, conditional trigs, the six variants, the mute performance, and the export. Start by re-listening to REF before touching the box.
Listen first

Reference pass — 10 minutes

Listen to about two minutes of “Losing Control”. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear — and write your answer before opening each reveal.

  1. How many basslines does the track use?
    Answer

    One — a single one-bar line, looped for the entire record. Everything that feels like a second bassline is the same line with different steps accented or different steps missing. Relisten to any two minutes and try to catch a genuinely new bar arriving; it never does.

  2. What actually varies — the notes, or the emphasis on them?
    Answer

    The emphasis. Bell’s vocabulary is accents that lean on different steps, a filter that opens on some hits and not others, and elements muted in and out — while the pitches stay fixed. Relisten to one transition and name the emphasis move that made it instead of a note.

  3. When does the hi-hat enter, and what does its entry do?
    Answer

    Late — and because so little else has happened, a closed hat entering reads as a major event. The record teaches that in a sparse system the cost of every addition is enormous, so each one buys minutes of interest. Relisten to the bars on either side of the entry and feel how the pulse seems to double.

  4. Did the bassline’s notes ever change — even once?
    Answer

    No. Six minutes, one bar of pitches, untouched — the discipline is the whole point. Any moment you were sure a note moved was an accent shifting weight or a step dropping out. Relisten to the moment you doubted and find the emphasis doing the work.

  5. What happens at the dropouts — what leaves, and what does its absence set up?
    Answer

    Whole elements vanish for a few bars — the bassline thins to a skeleton or the drums step out — and the absence loads the return: the unchanged line comes back sounding new. Subtraction is the record’s loudest gesture. Relisten to one dropout and notice your attention spike at the return.

  6. Listen for a note that is not always there — a quiet hit that appears on some passes of the loop and not others. Found one?
    Answer

    They are there — ghost hits that flicker in and out across passes, too regular to be mistakes and too irregular to be every bar. That intermittency is exactly what a conditional trig reproduces: a step that plays one pass in four, or a quarter of the time. Relisten to four consecutive bars of the bassline and tally which hits appear in all four.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

  • Sound target — what success sounds like:
  • The one-bar bassline, transcribed by ear, rubbery and round — close enough that a friend hearing yours then theirs would hesitate.
  • Six-plus distinct one-bar variants, every one of them the same notes — only accents, p-locks and gaps differ.
  • At least two ghost steps on conditions, so the loop never plays identically twice.
  • Dropouts that land where the record’s land, and a hat entry that feels like an event.
  • The spoken phrase is out of scope — the bassline vocabulary is the study.

Steps

The whole rig for this lesson — one sampled bass note becomes the Digitakt’s entire melodic vocabulary; the mutes are the arrangement.

MicroFreak (one note) → sampled into Digitakt → bass track + drums (p-locks, accents, conditions, mutes) → M4 in 3/4 → Live: DT PRINT (record)
Phase 1

Set the reference

Every judgement in this lesson — the bassline’s notes, the rubber of the tone, the placement of the dropouts — is made against the record, so the record goes on the bench first, looped and gain-matched. Pick a stretch that contains at least one dropout and one return, because those are what you are replicating.

Do this
  1. Drop “Losing Control” onto an audio track named REF.
  2. Find 8–16 bars that contain one dropout and its return, and loop them.
  3. Gain-match with a Utility on REF.
  4. Keep REF one solo away for the whole session.
Expected result

A clean loop of the section whose emphasis moves you will be matching, at an honest level.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The loop stumbles at the seamLoop brace not on bar boundariesSnap the brace to whole bars; warp the clip to the set tempo if needed
REF always sounds “better” in A/BIt’s just louderPull the Utility down until levels genuinely match
The chosen stretch has no dropoutSection picked for the groove, not the lessonScrub later into the record — the biggest subtractions live past the halfway mark
Phase 2

Sample one bass note — the only sound you get

The classic Elektron move: one clean sample, retuned per step, becomes the whole melodic instrument. You sample a single sustained bass hit — the MicroFreak through the Digitakt’s inputs, or any clean bass single-note sample from the +Drive — and from this point on, every pitch in the bassline is a TUNE lock on that one sample. The discipline of one source is not a limitation; it is why every variant later will sound like the same line wearing different weights.

Do this
  1. Dial a round bass note on the MicroFreak: V.Analog, low octave, LP filter mostly closed, fast attack, short decay, Paraphonic OFF.
  2. Connect the MicroFreak’s ¼″ out to the Digitakt’s audio inputs and sample one clean, sustained note — a few semitones above the line’s register, so downward tuning stays clean. (No MicroFreak handy? Load any clean single bass note from the +Drive instead.)
  3. Trim the sample tight: start point right at the attack, no silence in front, length just past the decay.
  4. Assign it to one Digitakt track — this is the bass track for the rest of the drill.
  5. Play the track’s trig key chromatically up and down a few steps of tune to confirm the sample retunes without artefacts in the range you need.
Expected result

One bass note on one track that sounds round and rubbery at several tunings — a one-sample instrument, ready to carry the whole transcription.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A click at the start of every hitSample start point sits before or after the true attackNudge the start point until the attack is clean; re-trim rather than fading
Pitched-down steps sound muddySource note sampled too low to retune cleanlyResample the MicroFreak a few semitones higher and tune down to the line’s register
Hiss or hum baked into the sampleSampling level too low, or a noisy cable runRe-sample hotter at the Digitakt’s inputs with a direct cable — a noisy source multiplies across every step
Phase 3

Transcribe the line, play it as tune locks

The one-bar bassline is the entire melodic content of the record, so it gets transcribed by ear, note by note — and played not as separate samples but as TUNE parameter locks: hold each trig and turn the tune knob on the SRC page to that step’s pitch. One sample, many notes. This single gesture — hold a trig, turn a knob, the change belongs to that step only — is the foundation every later phase builds on. Do this
  1. Press REC to enter grid recording on the bass track.
  2. Place the line’s rhythm as trigs on the 16-step grid, by ear against REF.
  3. For each trig: hold the trig key and turn TUNE on the SRC page to that step’s pitch — the value blinks to show it belongs to this step only. Release, next trig.
  4. Solo REF, hum it, place it, compare; revise until the lines chase each other.
  5. Shape the rubber on the track — filter and amp decay — until it is as close to the record’s tone as the box gets. These track-level settings are the baseline that the per-step locks will deviate from.
Expected result

Your bar and theirs looping together — same notes, same bounce, recognisably the same family of rubber. Every pitch is a tune lock; the track’s own tune setting never moved.

Phase 4

Accents — find which notes the record leans on

The first vocabulary move. The record does not play its bar flat: certain steps are pushed — leaned on — and that lean is most of the groove. Listen to REF and mark which hits feel heavier; then put the lean in with velocity locks: hold the trig, turn velocity, that step alone gets louder. Same notes, new weight.

Do this
  1. Loop REF and tap along; mark in the logbook which steps of the bar carry the push — usually two or three, often the downbeat and one late step.
  2. On the bass track, hold each pushed step’s trig and raise its velocity above the others — start with a clearly audible difference, then refine.
  3. Optionally pull the un-pushed steps slightly down instead — emphasis can be made by lowering the surroundings, exactly as the record’s dropouts do at full scale.
  4. A/B against REF: the two bars should lean in the same places.
Expected result

The bar grooves instead of marching — the same notes, but the weight falls where the record’s weight falls.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Every step got louderVelocity turned without holding the trig — a track edit, not a step lockRestore the track value, then hold the trig while turning
The accent is inaudible in the loopVelocity difference too small to survive playbackWiden the gap between accented and plain steps until the lean is felt, then trim back
An accent jumps past your mixed levelAccent stacked on an already-hot stepLower it — and keep the phones at the mark; never turn up to inspect
Phase 5

Filter locks — the squelchy notes that open

The second move. On the record some hits are brighter — the filter opens on them and not on their neighbours, and those are the squelchy notes your ear keeps catching. Same gesture, different page: hold the trig, turn filter cutoff, and one note opens while the bar around it stays closed.

  • Safety — before the filter p-locks: an accented trig with the filter p-locked open can jump well past the level you mixed at, and resonance sharpens the jump. Headphones at the mark or lower — never turn up to inspect. Audition each new p-lock once at the set level before looping it.
Do this
  1. Loop REF and find the opened notes — the hits with audible squelch on top of the rubber.
  2. Hold each of those trigs and raise filter cutoff on the filter page for that step only; a touch of resonance sharpens the squelch.
  3. Audition once at the set level, then loop and compare placement against REF.
  4. Check that the opened steps still sit inside the bar — if one dominates every pass, close it part-way.
Expected result

A bar where two or three notes glint and the rest stay matte — emphasis by brightness, on the record’s steps, with the pitches untouched.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A locked note leaps out painfullyCutoff plus resonance plus accent stacked on one stepBack the cutoff lock off; if it still bites, drop that step’s velocity lock
The lock won’t clear back to matteThe cutoff lock is stored on the trigHold the trig and remove it, then re-place it — a fresh trig carries no locks
The variant sounds like a new basslineA pitch got locked along the wayHold each trig and check TUNE against the transcription — emphasis only, the notes are sacred
Phase 6

Conditional trigs — steps that sometimes play

The third move, and the cheapest variation on the box: a ghost note that does not play every pass. Hold a trig and set its trig condition on the TRIG page — 1:4 means it plays on the first of every four pattern loops; 25% means it plays a quarter of the time, at random. The loop stops being identical twice in a row, and nothing new was added. This is exactly how Lab 003 keeps its grid alive without new elements.

Do this
  1. Add one or two quiet extra trigs in the bar’s gaps — ghost candidates, velocity locked low.
  2. Hold each ghost trig and set its condition on the TRIG page: start with 1:4 for one, 25% for the other.
  3. Let the pattern loop at least eight times without touching anything, and listen for the ghosts arriving and missing.
  4. Compare with your reference-pass tally of the record’s flickering hits — match the feel of “sometimes”, not an exact schedule.
Expected result

A bar that breathes across passes — most loops identical, then a ghost lands and the ear wakes up. The pattern is alive with zero new material.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The conditional trig never seems to fireLoop count — 1:4 needs four full passes before it playsBe patient: let the pattern run and count loops; restart the pattern to reset the count
The ghost plays every single passThe condition didn’t take, or it’s set on the wrong trigHold the trig and read its condition on the TRIG page; re-set it and verify the value while holding
When the ghost lands, it shoutsNo velocity lock on the ghost stepHold the trig and pull its velocity well below the plain steps — ghosts whisper
Phase 7

Micro-variation — six variants, one change each

Now the vocabulary compounds. Duplicate the pattern and change exactly one lock in the copy — one accent moved, one filter lock added, one step removed, one condition changed. Repeat until six-plus variants exist. The discipline of one change per copy is the drill: it is what makes the differences felt before they are noticed, and it is the same one-variable rule Lab 003 enforces across the whole build.

Do this
  1. Copy the bassline pattern to a new pattern slot — same trigs, same pitches, same locks.

Phase 8

The mute performance — arrange three minutes by subtraction

Now the vocabulary gets spoken. Drums go in plain — kick, a late hat — and the section is performed from the Digitakt’s mute mode: variants chained, elements muted in and out, dropouts placed where the record places them. Nothing new enters; subtraction is the only big gesture available, exactly as on the record — and exactly the move Lab 003’s build step 10 demands at full track scale.

Do this
  1. Program a plain kick pattern and a closed hat on their own Digitakt tracks — no fills the record doesn’t make.
  2. Arm DT PRINT in Live — Audio From → Ext. In → 3/4, Monitor Off, listening through one declared path only.
  3. Enter the Digitakt’s mute mode, so the track keys mute and unmute instead of triggering — this is your performance surface for the whole take. All mutes happen here, none in Live.
  4. Record a continuous three-minute take: chain or switch the variants, and perform entries and exits of bass, hat, and the variant patterns where the record performs its own.
  5. Hold the hat back for minutes, then let it enter as the event it is on the record.
  6. Place at least two dropouts where REF places its own — mute the bass entirely, count the bars, and let the unchanged line return.
Expected result

One continuous take in which the same bar of notes reads as a developing track, with the hat entry and the dropouts landing at the record’s moments — and every arrangement decision made from the mute buttons.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The mutes don’t appear in the recordingYou muted in Live instead of on the box, or vice versa — the recorded signal didn’t pass through the surface you mutedPick one mute surface and stay on it: the Digitakt’s mute mode, printed as audio through the recording
The three minutes feel staticVariants changed too rarely, or mutes too timidListen to how often REF shifts weight — it is more often than you think; mark the shifts and match their rate
You keep wanting a second basslineThe itch is the lessonReach for a dropout instead — subtraction buys the interest you wanted to buy with addition
Tracks trigger instead of mutingYou left mute mode mid-takeRe-enter mute mode and watch for its display state before touching the track keys again
Phase 9

Observe the vocabulary, compare, export

Now make the lesson visible. Count your distinct one-bar variants — the aim is six or more from one pattern — and write what differs in each. The list is short and the differences are tiny; that a track lived on it for three minutes is the observation. Then verify against the record and print.

Do this
  1. Complete the blank variants table from phase 7 in the logbook — one line per variant on what differs: accent, filter lock, gap, condition. Aim for 6+; confirm none of them changes a note.
  2. Count the conditional ghosts: how many steps in the final take never play twice the same way? Two or more is the target.
  3. Mark with locators where your dropouts and hat entry land and where REF’s land; measure the gaps.
  4. Export the section — File → Export Audio/Video, WAV, named REP-losing-control.
Expected result

A written vocabulary of 6+ variants, at least two living conditions, paired locators a few seconds apart at worst, and a finished WAV.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
You can only describe three variantsSeveral copies drifted into duplicatesGo back to phase 7 and push the duplicates apart — one emphasis move each is enough
The export contains REFReference track not muted at renderMute REF, re-export, and listen to the file before filing it
The take sounds thin next to REFLevels drifted during the performanceGain-match the export against REF before judging — loudness is not the lesson here
Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the looped section at matched volume, then answer:

  • Play your take start to finish — did the bassline’s notes ever change?
    Answer

    They must not have — the record holds one bar of pitches for six minutes, and your replica holds them for three. If a note moved, find the variant that moved it and strip the p-lock back to emphasis only. Relisten to the record’s most dramatic minute and confirm it, too, is the same notes.

  • Take your most interesting bar: can you trace it entirely to an accent, a p-lock, a condition, or an absence rather than new material?
    Answer

    Every event in the record is one of those four — a step leaning harder, the filter opening on certain hits, a ghost that only sometimes lands, or something gone. If your best bar needed new material, it broke the system; rebuild that moment from the vocabulary. Relisten to the bar after a dropout and notice that the record’s biggest moment is made of absence.

  • Six minutes from one bar — where does the record put its biggest single event?
    Answer

    In a removal, not an addition — the bass dropping out is the chorus. The track’s peak of attention is the moment the most familiar element vanishes, and the return after the gap lands harder than any new sound could. Relisten for the longest silence-shaped gap in the record and note what re-enters after it; then check whether your own take’s biggest moment is also a subtraction.

  • Could a listener tell your variants apart blind — and should they be able to?
    Answer

    Not consciously — and that is the pass condition. The record’s variants are felt as “something shifted”, never as “a new pattern started”. If a friend can name the exact change, the variant is too loud a move; if three minutes feel identical, the moves are too small. Relisten to any two adjacent minutes of REF and notice you sense development without being able to point at it.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines:
  • Matched: what your replica got closest to — the rubber, the variant set, the dropout placement; start with the emphasis move that landed nearest the record’s.
  • Missed: what the record does that yours doesn’t (best guess why).
  • Change next: the one thing you would do differently on the next attempt.
