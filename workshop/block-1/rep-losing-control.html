The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: per-step accents, filter emphasis, mutes and dropouts - the notes never change. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Digitakt II outs → M4 ins 3/4, level-checked; MicroFreak ¼″ out reachable for one sampling pass.
  • New set saved as REP-losing-control, tempo 128 — then warp REF and adjust by ear.
  • P-locks and accents live on the Digitakt II: hold a trig, turn a knob — the change belongs to that step only. The whole drill is variations on that one gesture.
  • Session one (phases 1–4): set the reference, sample the note, play the line as tune locks, place the accents. Stop when your bar and the record’s chase each other.
  • Session two (phases 5–9): filter locks, conditional trigs, the six variants, the mute performance, and the export. Start by re-listening to REF before touching the box.
  • What you will make: a short replica of DBX (Daniel Bell) — “Losing Control”. Focus on this effect: one one-bar bassline heard as six minutes of development.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: per-step accents, filter emphasis, mutes and dropouts - the notes never change.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of DBX (Daniel Bell) — “Losing Control”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many basslines does the track use?
    Answer

    One - a single one-bar line, looped for the entire record. Everything that feels like a second bassline is the same line with different steps accented or different steps missing.

  2. What actually varies — the notes, or the emphasis on them?
    Answer

    The emphasis. Bell’s vocabulary is accents that lean on different steps, a filter that opens on some hits and not others, and elements muted in and out - while the pitches stay fixed.

  3. When does the hi-hat enter, and what does its entry do?
    Answer

    Late - and because so little else has happened, a closed hat entering reads as a major event. The record teaches that in a sparse system the cost of every addition is enormous, so each one buys minutes of interest.

  4. Did the bassline’s notes ever change — even once?
    Answer

    No. Six minutes, one bar of pitches, untouched - the discipline is the whole point. Any moment you were sure a note moved was an accent shifting weight or a step dropping out.

  5. What happens at the dropouts — what leaves, and what does its absence set up?
    Answer

    Whole elements vanish for a few bars - the bassline thins to a skeleton or the drums step out - and the absence loads the return: the unchanged line comes back sounding new. Subtraction is the record’s loudest gesture.

  6. Listen for a note that is not always there — a quiet hit that appears on some passes of the loop and not others. Found one?
    Answer

    They are there - ghost hits that flicker in and out across passes, too regular to be mistakes and too irregular to be every bar.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target — what success sounds like:
  • The one-bar bassline, transcribed by ear, rubbery and round — close enough that a friend hearing yours then theirs would hesitate.
  • Six-plus distinct one-bar variants, every one of them the same notes — only accents, p-locks and gaps differ.
  • At least two ghost steps on conditions, so the loop never plays identically twice.
  • Dropouts that land where the record’s land, and a hat entry that feels like an event.
  • The spoken phrase is out of scope — the bassline vocabulary is the study.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.

Phase 4

Accents — find which notes the record leans on

Phase focus: accents — find which notes the record leans on. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Loop REF and tap along; mark in the logbook which steps of the bar carry the push — usually two or three, often the downbeat and one late step.
  2. On the bass track, hold each pushed step’s trig and raise its velocity above the others — start with a clearly audible difference, then refine.
  3. Optionally pull the un-pushed steps slightly down instead — emphasis can be made by lowering the surroundings, exactly as the record’s dropouts do at full scale.
  4. A/B against REF: the two bars should lean in the same places.

Phase 5

Filter locks — the squelchy notes that open

Do this

Phase 6

Conditional trigs — steps that sometimes play

Phase focus: conditional trigs — steps that sometimes play. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Add one or two quiet extra trigs in the bar’s gaps — ghost candidates, velocity locked low.
  2. Hold each ghost trig and set its condition on the TRIG page: start with 1:4 for one, 25% for the other.
  3. Let the pattern loop at least eight times without touching anything, and listen for the ghosts arriving and missing.
  4. Compare with your reference-pass tally of the record’s flickering hits — match the feel of “sometimes”, not an exact schedule.

Phase 8

The mute performance — arrange three minutes by subtraction

Phase focus: the mute performance — arrange three minutes by subtraction. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Program a plain kick pattern and a closed hat on their own Digitakt II tracks — no fills the record doesn’t make.
  2. Arm DT PRINT in Live — Audio From → Ext. In → 3/4, Monitor Off, listening through one declared path only.
  3. Enter the Digitakt II’s mute mode, so the track keys mute and unmute instead of triggering — this is your performance surface for the whole take.
  4. All mutes happen here, none in Live.
  5. Record a continuous three-minute take: chain or switch the variants, and perform entries and exits of bass, hat, and the variant patterns where the record performs its own.
  6. Hold the hat back for minutes, then let it enter as the event it is on the record.
  7. Place at least two dropouts where REF places its own — mute the bass entirely, count the bars, and let the unchanged line return.

Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the looped section at matched volume, then answer:

  • Play your take start to finish — did the bassline’s notes ever change?
    Answer

    They must not have — the record holds one bar of pitches for six minutes, and your replica holds them for three. If a note moved, find the variant that moved it and strip the p-lock back to emphasis only. Relisten to the record’s most dramatic minute and confirm it, too, is the same notes.

  • Take your most interesting bar: can you trace it entirely to an accent, a p-lock, a condition, or an absence rather than new material?
    Answer

    Every event in the record is one of those four — a step leaning harder, the filter opening on certain hits, a ghost that only sometimes lands, or something gone. If your best bar needed new material, it broke the system; rebuild that moment from the vocabulary. Relisten to the bar after a dropout and notice that the record’s biggest moment is made of absence.

  • Six minutes from one bar — where does the record put its biggest single event?
    Answer

    In a removal, not an addition — the bass dropping out is the chorus. The track’s peak of attention is the moment the most familiar element vanishes, and the return after the gap lands harder than any new sound could. Relisten for the longest silence-shaped gap in the record and note what re-enters after it; then check whether your own take’s biggest moment is also a subtraction.

  • Could a listener tell your variants apart blind — and should they be able to?
    Answer

    Not consciously — and that is the pass condition. The record’s variants are felt as “something shifted”, never as “a new pattern started”. If a friend can name the exact change, the variant is too loud a move; if three minutes feel identical, the moves are too small. Relisten to any two adjacent minutes of REF and notice you sense development without being able to point at it.
