Replicate: one drone, imperceptible filtering

Copy one clear section of Éliane Radigue — Jetsun Mila. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: one continuous synth drone that evolves only through filtering and beating so slow you never catch a change — there are no discrete events at all, just a single sound transforming under you. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 input 1, DT 990 Pro in the phones jack — the lab-001 wiring, unchanged; the Digitakt II rests this lesson
  • New set saved as REP-RADIGUE — no tempo worth setting; this lesson is measured in filter movement per minute
  • Sustained low-mid drones are deceptive — they load the ear over long listening, and the slow beating can mask fatigue. Phones at the lab-001 mark or lower, a real break every 15 minutes
  • Timebox: 60–90 minutes, then file it and stop — the time goes into riding the filter slowly, not adding parts
  • What you will make: a short replica of Éliane Radigue — Jetsun Mila. Focus on this effect: one unbroken tone that seems alive, with no event you can point to.
  • What you will turn off: retriggers, rhythm, any second part, any modulation fast enough to be heard as a wobble.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: one continuous drone evolving through imperceptibly slow filtering and beating, with no discrete events.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Éliane Radigue — Jetsun Mila, then to a point several minutes later. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many separate sounds start and stop?
    Answer

    None. Nothing starts and nothing stops — there is one continuous sound for the whole stretch. There are no entrances, no hits, no discrete events to count. What you’re hearing is one drone changing, not many sounds arriving.

  2. What are the two kinds of slow change you can hear?
    Answer

    Filtering — the colour of the drone slowly opening and closing — and beating — a faint, slow pulsing where two nearly-identical tones drift in and out of phase. Both move far below the speed of an obvious LFO.

  3. Can you catch the moment a change “happens”?
    Answer

    No. The changes are imperceptible in the moment — you only know something shifted by comparing now to a memory of a minute ago. That is the entire technique: change you can detect over time but never witness.

  4. Is the beating performed or built into the tuning?
    Answer

    Built in. Two oscillators tuned a hair apart beat at a rate set by their pitch difference — tune them closer and the pulse slows, further and it speeds up. The beating is a property of the sound, not a fader being moved.

  5. Where is the composition if there are no events?
    Answer

    In the slow trajectory of the filter and the beat rate over the whole piece. With one continuous sound and no events, the only score is how its colour and its pulse drift across many minutes — that drift is the music.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Live 12 automation: automate the Auto Filter cutoff (or the MicroFreak’s cutoff via MIDI map) with a single very long hand-drawn curve; no built-in LFO will move this slowly.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control. Hold one note continuously; use the Cutoff for the slow filter trajectory. For beating, layer two near-identical tones tuned a hair apart (a second take, or the paraphonic engine detuned slightly).
  • Digitakt II: idle this lesson — there is no rhythm here.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your piece lands on the same continuity — one unbroken sound that is somewhere new minutes later, with no event between.
  • One continuous drone and nothing else — no retriggers, no second part, no rhythm.
  • The two movements are slow filtering and slow beating; nothing else changes on purpose.
  • The changes are imperceptible in the moment and obvious over minutes — if you can hear a change “happen,” it was too fast.

The piece’s machinery in one row: one continuous drone made of two tones tuned a hair apart so they beat, a single very slow filter trajectory drawn across the whole take, and a mix where nothing ever starts or stops.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag a long stretch of Jetsun Mila into it.
  2. Loop a five-minute section where the colour clearly drifts from start to end, and the beating is audible.
  3. Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so it peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. ⌘ T for the drone, Ext. In 1, fader at 0 dB, armed; name it DRONE.
  6. Write in the logbook two things: roughly how the colour differs between the loop’s start and end (brighter? darker?), and the rough beat rate (one pulse every how many seconds).
  7. Keep those readings; phases 2 and 3 will reproduce them.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Éliane Radigue — Jetsun Mila (1980s) and use two detuned oscillators (Operator or Wavetable) into Auto Filter.
  2. Build the phase goal — The reference and the track — as the smallest Live set that proves one continuous drone evolving through slow filtering and beating, with no discrete events.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B the start and end of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects the slow filter drift.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: two detuned FM operators give the beat; a slowly automated filter or index gives the colour drift; record main-out.
  2. Program it so it demonstrates one continuous drone with no events, not just the surface of Jetsun Mila.
  3. Keep one clear performance control — the slow filter trajectory — and name it before recording.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a target-box shortcut only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A five-minute reference loop at ≈ −12 dBFS with audible beating and a colour that differs end to end, one named drone track waiting, and the colour and beat-rate readings on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The drone — one continuous tone that beats

Phase focus: the drone. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak: hold one low-mid note continuously — no retrigger, full sustain.
  2. Build the beating: either use the paraphonic engine with two voices detuned a hair apart, or print a second take a few cents off the first. Two tones almost the same pitch.
  3. Set the detune so the pulse matches the rate you logged — closer tuning = slower pulse, wider = faster. Aim for one pulse every few seconds.
  4. Print 6–8 minutes of the continuous beating drone into DRONE, peaking ≈ −12 dBFS.
  5. Use the MicroFreak’s Hold so the tone never stops for the whole take.
  6. Solo it and confirm: it is one unbroken sound with a slow pulse, and nothing about it starts or stops.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: two oscillators (Operator/Wavetable) detuned a few cents on one held MIDI note give a continuous beating drone.
  2. Build the phase goal — the beating drone — as the smallest Live set that proves one continuous tone can pulse without any event.
  3. Keep it on a named track, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Resample to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B the beat rate against REF and adjust the detune until it matches.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: two FM operators detuned a few cents on one sustained note give the beat.
  2. Program it so it demonstrates one continuous beating drone, not just the surface of Jetsun Mila.
  3. Keep the filter trajectory as the one named control for later.
  4. Record the drone into Live or a recorder, then compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a shortcut only when the same continuous, beating character stays audible.
Expected result

A 6–8 minute continuous drone with a slow built-in pulse at roughly the record’s beat rate — one unbroken sound, no entrances, no retriggers.

Phase 3 · ≈ 25 min

Compose with one slow filter trajectory

Phase focus: compose with one slow filter trajectory. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Put an Audio Effects → Auto Filter on DRONE and automate the cutoff by hand across the whole take — this single trajectory is the colour composition.
  2. Rebuild the colour drift you logged: open the filter slowly toward the brighter passage, ease it back, over many minutes. No LFO is slow enough; draw it.
  3. The total filter travel can be large, but spread it so no ten-second window reveals which way it’s going.
  4. Optionally let the beat rate drift too: a very slow automation of one oscillator’s tuning will speed or slow the pulse over time — a second imperceptible movement.
  5. Do not add any event. No swell you could time, no entrance. Two slow continuous changes only.
  6. The rule: scrub to any ten seconds and you should not be able to tell the filter is moving; scrub from start to end and the colour change should be obvious.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: hand-draw one long Auto Filter cutoff curve, optionally a second slow detune automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — one slow filter trajectory — as the smallest Live set that proves the colour drift carries the piece.
  3. Keep the drone on a named track, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Resample to PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B the start and end against REF and adjust only the filter curve.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: automate a filter or FM index slowly across the pattern as the single colour trajectory.
  2. Program it so it demonstrates evolution through slow filtering, no discrete events, not just the surface of Jetsun Mila.
  3. Keep the filter trajectory as the named, repeatable control.
  4. Record a full pass into Live or a recorder, then compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a shortcut only when the same imperceptible-change mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A 6–8 minute continuous drone whose only changes are one slow filter trajectory and a slow pulse — invisible in any short window, clearly transformed end to end, with no event between.

Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

Hold it, A/B it, and test the two timescales

Phase focus: hold it, A/B it, and test the two timescales. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Play your whole take end to end, no touching, at the mark or lower.
  2. If the urge to add an event to mark a section arrives, it is the lesson arriving — there are no sections here.
  3. A/B ten seconds of yours against REF: both should sound like one continuous sound that isn’t doing anything in particular right now.
  4. A/B the start of yours against the end: the colour and perhaps the pulse should clearly differ, the way the record’s do.
  5. Write in the logbook the felt duration versus the clock — drones distort time; note the gap.
  6. Take the scheduled break. Continuous low-mid tones fatigue quietly.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: judge the print against REF at matched level.
  2. Confirm the smallest Live set still proves one continuous drone, no events.
  3. Use Utility to match the level against REF before judging.
  4. Keep the proof as a printed PRINT track.
  5. Adjust only the filter or detune trajectory that carries the change.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: verify the beating drone and the slow filter trajectory hold across the pass.
  2. Confirm the program demonstrates slow filtering and beating as the only movement.
  3. Keep the filter trajectory as the named, repeatable control.
  4. Record the full pass and compare with REF at matched level.
  5. Use a shortcut only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A piece that is one continuous sound in any ten seconds yet clearly transformed end to end, an A/B that comes down to continuity and slow drift, and a logbook line about how the drone bent your sense of time.

Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your piece at matched loudness — phones at the mark or lower, no level riding while you judge.

  • Could a listener point to anything starting or stopping?
    Answer

    They should not be able to, in your piece or the record. If they can name an entrance or an event, something discrete leaked in and goes back to phase 3. The whole effect is one unbroken sound — no addresses, no edges.

  • Did filtering and beating alone make it feel alive?
    Answer

    That is the test. With no events, the only life available is the slow filter drift and the built-in beat. If your drone breathed and travelled without ever appearing to move, it holds. If it felt dead, the fix is never an event; it is a clearer beat or a wider, slower filter trajectory. Relisten to the record — it is one sound the entire time, and never still.
