Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: the dragged pocket

Copy one clear section of D’Angelo — “Playa Playa”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: each layer - snare, bass, keys, vocal - sits its own few-to-tens of milliseconds behind the kick’s grid, and keeps that offset for the length of the song. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating

The effectthe whole band leans back off the beat without ever falling over — sloppy-sounding, perfectly consistent

Before you start
  • The Lab 002 wiring as-is: Digitakt II main outs → M4 inputs 3/4 if you build the beat there, MicroFreak → M4 input 1 for the bass — or build the whole pocket inside Live; the lesson lives in the milliseconds, not the boxes
  • New set saved as REP-playa-playa, with Lab 002’s driver error compensation already entered — an unknown latency under a lesson about deliberate latency would poison every number
  • Phones knob at the Lab 001 mark
  • What you will make: a short replica of D’Angelo — “Playa Playa”. Focus on this effect: the whole band leans back off the beat without ever falling over - sloppy-sounding, perfectly consistent.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: each layer - snare, bass, keys, vocal - sits its own few-to-tens of milliseconds behind the kick’s grid, and keeps that offset for the length of the song.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of D’Angelo — “Playa Playa”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Put your foot on the kick. Is the band late, or is the kick late?
    Answer

    The kick holds the frame and everything else hangs behind it. Your foot locks to the kick without effort - it is the reference everything leans against - while snare, bass, and keys arrive after the place your body expects them.

  2. Follow only the snare for eight bars. Does its lateness ever change?
    Answer

    No - it hangs back by the same amount every single bar. That constancy is the difference between a pocket and a mistake: a sloppy player’s lateness wanders, this snare’s lateness is a fixed number.

  3. Are all the layers late by the same amount?
    Answer

    No - and this is the record’s engine. The bass sits in its own place behind the kick, the snare in another, the keys and vocal in others still: together apart.

  4. Why doesn’t it fall apart?
    Answer

    Because the frame is steady and the offsets are fixed. The kick never wavers, and each layer repeats its own distance bar after bar, so within a few bars your ear learns the map of gaps and starts riding it like swing.

  5. What would quantizing this record do to it?
    Answer

    Erase it. Pull every layer onto the kick’s grid and the notes, the sounds, and the song all survive - but the record doesn’t, because the content was the set of distances you just removed. What would remain is a stiff, ordinary funk track.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a pocket beat where every solo’d layer sounds almost lazy against the click, but the sum leans back as one body — and where three prints (on grid / equally late / each differently late) make the record’s choice audible.
  • The kick stays on the grid — something must hold the frame the others lean against.
  • Every offset is one fixed number for the whole take: set it, write it down, never ride it.
  • The groove lives between layers — no layer gets random variation inside itself.

The record’s machinery in one row: a kick holding the grid, each other layer placed a distinct fixed number of milliseconds behind it, and those numbers never changing for the length of the song — a pocket you could write down as a table.

straight pocket · kick on the grid → per-track delay · one ms number per layer → three prints · A / B / C → A/B vs the record · which one breathes

Two beats under the ruler. The kick sits on each grid line; keys, snare, and bass land behind it at three different fixed distances — and in the second bar the distances repeat exactly. The drag is a table of numbers wearing a loose shirt.

Phase 1 · ≈ 20 min

The reference and the straight pocket

Phase focus: the reference and the straight pocket. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), drag “Playa Playa” in, and loop the 8–16 bars you chose.
  2. Live Audio Effects → Utility, trim to peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  3. Live Set Live’s tempo by tapping along to REF — Voodoo sits slow;
  4. Live take the eight taps twice and trust the second pass.
  5. Digitakt II + MicroFreak Build a four-layer pocket on separate tracks: KICK and SNARE plus a hat (Drum Rack, or the Digitakt II recorded per the Lab 002 path), BASS (MicroFreak into input 1, or Operator), and KEYS — one soft chord stab, two or three changes per bar at most.
  6. Digitakt II Keep every note exactly on the grid — quantized, no swing, no humanize.
  7. Digitakt II Boring is the point; this is the zero column.
  8. Live + Logbook Loop it against REF once and write one logbook line on the difference in feel.
  9. Live + Logbook “Stiff” is expected; name something more specific if you can.
Expected result

A four-layer pocket at the record’s tempo, fully on the grid, peaking ≈ −12 dBFS per layer — musically close to the record in notes and sounds, and nothing like it in feel. That gap is what the next phase closes with numbers.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Hardware layers already sound loose before any offsets existUnknown latency on the recording path — compensation unset or staleRe-run Lab 002’s loopback at the current buffer and enter the difference; this lesson needs a clean zero
The straight version already grooves and nothing feels missingTempo too fast — at speed, stiffness hidesRe-tap against REF; the record’s slowness is what exposes placement
Phase 2 · ≈ 20 min

Drag each layer by its own number

Phase focus: drag each layer by its own number. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Show the Track Delay field: in Session View, enable the D selector in the mixer-section column on the right edge.
  2. Live Each track gets a small delay field, in ms.
  3. Live Enter first guesses, kick untouched at 0: KEYS +10, SNARE +18, BASS +28.
  4. Digitakt II Alternative Digitakt II method: in grid recording, hold each [TRIG] key and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to nudge its micro-timing — same physics, coarser steps.
  5. Live Play against REF in 10-second flips and tune each number by a few ms at a time.
  6. Live Judge with your foot on the kick: each layer should hang, none should sound like an error.
  7. Live Keep the hat at 0 or nearly — something beyond the kick should stay close to the frame, or the lean loses its reference.
  8. Logbook When the lean breathes, freeze the numbers and write the table in the logbook: layer, direction, amount.
  9. Logbook The groove is now a document.
Expected result

The same MIDI and the same sounds as phase 1, now leaning back as one body — and a written table of three or four small numbers that fully specifies the feel. Mute the offsets (set them to 0) and back to hear the pocket appear and disappear at will.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A layer sounds like a flam or a mistake, not a leanOffset too large — past ≈ 40 ms an offset starts reading as a separate eventPull the number back a few ms at a time until “wrong” turns into “lazy”
The drag is inaudibleOffsets too small, or all layers near the same number — a shared shift cancels itselfSpread the numbers apart; the audible thing is the difference between layers
Phase 3 · ≈ 20 min

Print three versions — grid, shared lean, true pocket

Phase focus: print three versions — grid, shared lean, true pocket. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Print A — on grid: set every Track Delay to 0 and render 8 bars (⌘ R over the time selection works, or record the master to an audio track).
  2. Live Name it A-GRID.
  3. Live Print B — equally late: kick at 0, every other layer at the same number — use your snare value.
  4. Live Render 8 bars as B-SHARED.
  5. Logbook Print C — each differently late: restore the phase-2 table exactly from the logbook.
  6. Logbook Render 8 bars as C-POCKET.
  7. Live Line the three clips up on one track, matched in level, and loop them back to back.
  8. Logbook One line in the logbook per print: what the lean does, in plain words.
  9. Logbook Notice what B actually is: a single fixed gap between the kick and everyone else — one offset, audible as a kind of permanent flam, with none of C’s depth.
Expected result

Three prints that share every note and every sound. A is stiff. B leans, but as one rigid plate — the band shifted, not breathing. C breathes: layers at different distances read as depth in time, the way layers at different levels read as depth in space.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
B and C sound the sameC’s numbers too close together — the table collapsed into one shared offsetSpread C’s table: keys near the frame, bass furthest back, snare between
A and B sound the sameB’s shared number too small to read at this tempoRaise B to your largest phase-2 value; the comparison needs an audible shared lean
Phase 4 · ≈ 15 min

A/B against the record and read the offsets like a ruler

Phase focus: a/B against the record and read the offsets like a ruler. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live A/B each print against REF, 10 seconds each, levels matched with a Utility until neither is obviously louder.
  2. Live Phones at the mark.
  3. Logbook Rank the three prints by how well they match the record’s feel and write the ranking down before reading anyone else’s opinion — including this page’s.
  4. Live Loop C for eight bars with your foot on the kick and check each layer’s lean for constancy, the way the reference pass checked the record’s.
  5. Logbook At sample zoom, measure one snare’s distance from the grid in bar 1 and bar 8 of C — the two distances should match your logbook number, both times.
  6. Logbook File the delay table next to the miss list.
  7. Logbook A groove you can re-enter from a table is a groove you own.
Expected result

C ranks closest to the record, the lean in C proves constant by ear and by ruler, and the logbook holds a table of milliseconds that turns “feel” into a setting you can recall, vary, and reason about.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
C still sounds stiff next to the recordOnly timing was copied — the record’s players also shape attacks and lengthsSoften the snare’s attack, lengthen the bass notes slightly; placement is necessary, not sufficient
C sounds drunk in the bad wayAn offset is being modulated, or a layer has humanize/random timing left onOffsets are fixed numbers — strip any randomization; the record’s looseness is constant
Verify against the record

Flip between REF and your three prints at matched loudness — phones at the mark, foot on the kick.

  • Which print breathes like the record, and why that one?
    Answer

    C — the per-layer table. A has no lean at all; B leans as a single rigid plate, because one shared offset is just the whole band moved, a flam against the kick with nothing inside it. C has interior distances: bass behind snare behind keys, each constant, which the ear reads as a body leaning rather than a block shifted. Relisten to the record after C and notice they answer the foot-tap the same way.

  • Can you hear that the offsets are constant, not random?
    Answer

    Yes — by the same test the reference pass used on the record: follow one layer for eight bars and its lateness never changes size. Random offsets would read as sloppy playing within two bars; constant ones read as intent, because the ear learns the map and starts trusting it. The ruler agrees: bar 1 and bar 8 measure the same ms. Relisten to one verse of “Playa Playa” and then to C, asking only “does the distance wander?” — both answer no.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1 — and keep the delay table with them.
  • Carries into the lab: latency you place on purpose is groove; latency you don’t know about is mud. Same milliseconds, same physics — the only difference is whether the number is in your logbook or hiding in your signal path, and Lab 002 exists to leave it nowhere to hide.
