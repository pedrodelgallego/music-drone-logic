Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: ahead of the beat

Build a fast straight grid, then lean chosen hits early — by the same amount, every time — until the track falls forward the way footwork does.

The point

Lean events early against a fast grid and the music feels like it is falling forward — anticipation as energy, the exact mirror of lateness as groove.

The system you are replicating
Source recordDJ Rashad — “Let It Go”
The effectclaps and toms arrive before you expect, tumbling in triplets at 160
The mechanismchosen hits nudged early by a fixed amount, against a straight fast grid
FidelityAs close to the record’s section as your rig allows; closeness is the measure of your hearing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Digitakt II main outs → M4 inputs 3/4, clock-slaved to Live as in the previous lessons
  • New set saved as REP-let-it-go, tempo at 160
  • Phones knob at the Lab 001 mark
  • Timebox: one sitting, 60–120 minutes — at 160 BPM the A/B windows are short, so expect many of them; the passes are the lesson
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Describe the system before you copy it

Listen to about one minute of “Let It Go” and choose the 8–16 bars where the tumble is most obvious — that section is what you will replicate. Answer in the logbook, one line each, before touching gear. Check each answer against the reveal only after you have written your own.

  1. How fast is the grid — and is the grid itself straight?
    Answer

    Around 160 BPM, and yes — the grid underneath is dead straight. Footwork’s wildness rides on a rigid frame: the kicks and the sub hold machine time while everything above them leans and tumbles. Tap quarters with your foot through eight bars and notice that the foot never has to correct, no matter how unstable the surface feels.

  2. What tumbles — which sounds carry the falling-forward feeling?
    Answer

    The claps, the toms, and the vocal chops — figures that move in triplets against the straight grid and arrive a touch before you expect them. The kick pattern is syncopated but planted; the tumble lives in the layer above it. Relisten and sort each sound into one of two bags: holds the frame, or tumbles over it.

  3. The early hits — do they arrive the same amount early every time?
    Answer

    Yes — the earliness repeats exactly, which is what makes it placement rather than rushing. A rushing player gets progressively earlier until the band drags them back; these hits lean the same distance ahead, cycle after cycle, like the mirror image of Dilla’s snare. Relisten to one clap across eight bars and check that its lean never grows.

  4. Why does it read as urgency and not as a mistake?
    Answer

    Because the earliness is consistent and the frame never moves — the ear hears a fixed, repeated relationship and reads intent, exactly as it does with deliberate lateness. Early-by-rule pulls you forward; early-by-accident just sounds like stumbling, because it cannot be predicted. Relisten and feel your body tip slightly ahead of the kick: that tip is the design.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

Steps

  • Sound target: a straight 160 frame that never moves, with claps and stabs tumbling in triplets and leaning early — by the record’s amount.
  • The earliness is identical every cycle — placed, not rushed, and the frame underneath stays machine-tight.
  • A/B’d against the straight version, yours falls forward the way the record does.
Phase 1 · ~25 min

The frame — a straight grid at 160

Earliness only reads against something that refuses to move, so you build the refusal first: the record’s kick-and-sub frame, dead on the grid, at the record’s tempo. No leans yet — a hit can only be early relative to a frame that exists.

Do this
  1. Drop “Let It Go” into Live on a track named REF and loop the 8–16 bars you chose. Gain-match it with a Utility and keep it one solo away all sitting.
  2. Set Live to 160 BPM and confirm against REF — tap along and adjust by 0.1 BPM until 16 bars stay aligned.
  3. Transcribe the kick pattern by ear on the Digitakt — footwork kicks are syncopated, not four-on-the-floor; find where theirs actually land. Add the sub if your section has one, locked to the kicks.
  4. Keep every trig on the grid: no swing, no micro-timing. Loop against REF and correct until the frame is theirs.

Expected result

A straight, planted 160 frame in your sounds that locks to the record’s kicks. It will sound like the record’s skeleton — correct and strangely calm, because everything that makes the record fall forward is still missing.

Phase 2 · ~25 min

The tumble — triplets over the straight grid

Now the layer that tumbles: claps, toms, and stabs on triplet positions, rolling three-against-four over the straight frame. Still no leaning — first the triplet figures have to be theirs, on their positions, before anything moves early.

Do this
  1. On the Digitakt, set the clap and tom tracks to a triplet scale (per-track SCALE), or program them in Live on a 1/8T grid — one method, not both.
  2. Transcribe the record’s clap and tom figures onto triplet positions, listening phrase by phrase against REF. Footwork figures repeat in short cells: find the cell, then its repeats.
  3. Add the stab or vocal-chop rhythm the same way if your section has one — closest sound you have, placement over timbre.
  4. Loop the whole thing against REF: the three-against-four roll should already be unmistakable, even though nothing leans yet.
Expected result

The record’s tumbling figures, machine-placed: triplets rolling over the straight kicks. Against REF it is recognizably the same music — but it sits flat where theirs pulls forward. That missing pull is Phase 3.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The triplets land with the kicks instead of against themClaps programmed on the 16th grid, not a triplet scaleCheck the track’s scale or the clip’s grid setting — three even hits per beat, not “close enough” 16ths
The roll sounds right alone but wrong with the frameTriplet track and frame track at different scales or lengths, drifting per cycleConfirm both pattern lengths resolve to the same bar count and restart the pattern together
Phase 3 · ~25 min

Lean the chosen hits early

Now the one deliberate move — in the opposite direction from the Dilla lesson. You pick the hits that tumble hardest on the record and nudge them early, the same amount each, comparing against the record after every step. Anticipation is a placement, and you are about to find theirs.

Do this
  1. Pick one nudge method and stick to it. Digitakt: hold each chosen trig and press the left arrow to nudge its micro-timing early, click by click. Live: snap off, drag the chosen hits left by 10–20 ms. One method, not both.
  2. Choose the hits to lean by ear against REF — usually the claps and the first hit of each tom figure, not every triplet. The record leans chosen hits; leaning everything just shifts the layer.
  3. Nudge the chosen hits early in small equal steps, applying the same number of clicks to each before listening again. A/B after every step: still behind their lean, level with it, or past it?
  4. Stop when your hits arrive where theirs arrive — the same touch ahead, every cycle. Keep the kicks and sub untouched on the grid.
Expected result

At one specific lean the loop tips forward — the flat Phase 2 version starts falling the way the record falls. Note the click count or ms value; Phase 4 puts a ruler on it.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
It reads as sloppy, not urgentDifferent lean amounts on different hits — earliness that cannot be predictedHold each leaned trig and set every one to the same value; one number for the lean
The whole track just sounds shiftedEvery hit leaned, including the frameZero the kicks and sub back to the grid; lean only the chosen tumble hits
Phase 4 · ~20 min

A/B the whole section, record it, measure the lead

Last, the proof in both directions: straight vs early through the full section, then a ruler on the earliness. On the ms timeline an early hit is a hit that lands before its grid line — the Dilla diagram mirrored. If the lead is the same number of ms at two different bars, it is placed; if it varies, it is rushed.

Do this
  1. A/B the section once more, both ways: your straight Phase 2 version (micro-timing temporarily zeroed, or muted A/B clips) against your leaned version. Write one word for what the lean adds.
  2. Restart the transport — the Lab 002 reset rule — then record 2 minutes of the leaned version into Live (Ext. In 3/4, Monitor Off).
  3. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Set the ruler to minutes:seconds:milliseconds, as in Lab 002, and zoom to the same leaned clap in two different bars.
  4. Measure each one’s lead before its grid line, in ms. Identical = placed. Write the number — a negative offset, chosen on purpose, is this record’s engine.
Expected result

Two equal ms leads, bars apart — the mirror image of the Donuts measurement, sitting on the other side of the grid line. Anticipation, stated as engineering: the same fixed distance, just early instead of late.

Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the REF section, then answer:

  • Do your leaned hits arrive the same touch early as theirs, cycle after cycle? Loop 4 bars of each and follow only the claps.
    Answer

    Four bars of their claps should sound like one placement stamped four times, ahead of the beat by a fixed hair — and four bars of yours should do the same, at the same distance. If yours pull further and further forward, that is rushing, and the fix is per-trig consistency, not a smaller lean. Relisten alternating four bars of each, asking one question only: does the lean ever grow?

  • 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — does each one tip your body forward the same way? Would a friend hesitate to say which is which?
    Answer

    The forward tip is the test that matters: a matched lean produces the same physical anticipation, like the half-step you take when a bus brakes. If the friend would not hesitate, the giveaway at this tempo is usually the triplet figures’ density or your sample choices, not the lean itself — footwork’s chopped vocals are hard to fake from a stock kit. Name the real gap in the miss list, then relisten asking only whether both versions fall forward at the same rate.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • What carries into the lab: the grid has two sides — late relaxes, early propels, and both are placements measured in the same milliseconds.
