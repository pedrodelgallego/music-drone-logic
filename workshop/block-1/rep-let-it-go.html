Replicate: ahead of the beat

Copy one clear section of DJ Rashad — “Let It Go”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: chosen hits nudged early by a fixed amount, against a straight fast grid. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Digitakt II main outs → M4 inputs 3/4, clock-slaved to Live as in the previous lessons
  • New set saved as REP-let-it-go, tempo at 160

  • What you will make: a short replica of DJ Rashad — “Let It Go”. Focus on this effect: claps and toms arrive before you expect, tumbling in triplets at 160.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.

  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of DJ Rashad — “Let It Go”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How fast is the grid — and is the grid itself straight?
    Answer

    Around 160 BPM, and yes - the grid underneath is dead straight. Footwork’s wildness rides on a rigid frame: the kicks and the sub hold machine time while everything above them leans and tumbles.

  2. What tumbles — which sounds carry the falling-forward feeling?
    Answer

    The claps, the toms, and the vocal chops - figures that move in triplets against the straight grid and arrive a touch before you expect them. The kick pattern is syncopated but planted; the tumble lives in the layer above it.

  3. The early hits — do they arrive the same amount early every time?
    Answer

    Yes - the earliness repeats exactly, which is what makes it placement rather than rushing.

  4. Why does it read as urgency and not as a mistake?
    Answer

    Because the earliness is consistent and the frame never moves - the ear hears a fixed, repeated relationship and reads intent, exactly as it does with deliberate lateness.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a straight 160 frame that never moves, with claps and stabs tumbling in triplets and leaning early — by the record’s amount.
  • The earliness is identical every cycle — placed, not rushed, and the frame underneath stays machine-tight.
  • A/B’d against the straight version, yours falls forward the way the record does.

Phase 1 · ~25 min

The frame — a straight grid at 160

Phase focus: the frame — a straight grid at 160. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Drop “Let It Go” into Live on a track named REF and loop the 8–16 bars you chose.
  2. Gain-match it with a Utility and keep it one solo away all sitting.
  3. Set Live to 160 BPM and confirm against REF — tap along and adjust by 0.1 BPM until 16 bars stay aligned.
  4. Transcribe the kick pattern by ear on the Digitakt II — footwork kicks are syncopated, not four-on-the-floor;
  5. find where theirs actually land.
  6. Add the sub if your section has one, locked to the kicks.
  7. Keep every trig on the grid: no swing, no micro-timing.
  8. Loop against REF and correct until the frame is theirs.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from DJ Rashad — “Let It Go” and use Drum Rack, MIDI clips, Groove Pool, and clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — The frame — a straight grid at 160 — as the smallest Live set that proves chosen hits nudged early by a fixed amount, against a straight fast grid.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects claps and toms arrive before you expect, tumbling in triplets at 160.
  1. Use Analog Rytm MKII for this phase: translate The frame — a straight grid at 160 into drum voices or samples, trig conditions, parameter locks, scenes, performance macros, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates chosen hits nudged early by a fixed amount, against a straight fast grid, not just the surface of DJ Rashad — “Let It Go”.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.

Phase 2 · ~25 min

The tumble — triplets over the straight grid

Phase focus: the tumble — triplets over the straight grid. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. On the Digitakt II, use [TRK] + the clap or tom [TRIG] key to select that track. Set that track to a triplet scale in the per-track SCALE page. Or program them in Live on a 1/8T grid — one method, not both.
  2. Transcribe the record’s clap and tom figures onto triplet positions, listening phrase by phrase against REF.
  3. Footwork figures repeat in short cells: find the cell, then its repeats.
  4. Add the stab or vocal-chop rhythm the same way if your section has one — closest sound you have, placement over timbre.
  5. Loop the whole thing against REF: the three-against-four roll should already be unmistakable, even though nothing leans yet.

Expected result

The record’s tumbling figures, machine-placed: triplets rolling over the straight kicks. Against REF it is recognizably the same music — but it sits flat where theirs pulls forward. That missing pull is Phase 3.

Phase 3 · ~25 min

Lean the chosen hits early

Phase focus: lean the chosen hits early. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Pick one nudge method and stick to it.
  2. Digitakt II: hold each chosen trig and press the left arrow to nudge its micro-timing early, click by click.
  3. Live: snap off, drag the chosen hits left by 10–20 ms.
  4. One method, not both.
  5. Choose the hits to lean by ear against REF — usually the claps and the first hit of each tom figure, not every triplet.
  6. The record leans chosen hits; leaning everything just shifts the layer.
  7. Nudge the chosen hits early in small equal steps, applying the same number of clicks to each before listening again.
  8. A/B after every step: still behind their lean, level with it, or past it?
  9. Stop when your hits arrive where theirs arrive — the same touch ahead, every cycle.
  10. Keep the kicks and sub untouched on the grid.

At one specific lean the loop tips forward — the flat Phase 2 version starts falling the way the record falls. Note the click count or ms value; Phase 4 puts a ruler on it.

Two equal ms leads, bars apart — the mirror image of the Donuts measurement, sitting on the other side of the grid line. Anticipation, stated as engineering: the same fixed distance, just early instead of late.

Verify against the record

A/B your recording against the REF section, then answer:

  • Do your leaned hits arrive the same touch early as theirs, cycle after cycle? Loop 4 bars of each and follow only the claps.
    Answer

    Four bars of their claps should sound like one placement stamped four times, ahead of the beat by a fixed hair — and four bars of yours should do the same, at the same distance. If yours pull further and further forward, that is rushing, and the fix is per-trig consistency, not a smaller lean. Relisten alternating four bars of each, asking one question only: does the lean ever grow?

  • 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — does each one tip your body forward the same way? Would a friend hesitate to say which is which?
    Answer

    The forward tip is the test that matters: a matched lean produces the same physical anticipation, like the half-step you take when a bus brakes. If the friend would not hesitate, the giveaway at this tempo is usually the triplet figures’ density or your sample choices, not the lean itself — footwork’s chopped vocals are hard to fake from a stock kit. Name the real gap in the miss list, then relisten asking only whether both versions fall forward at the same rate.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • What carries into the lab: the grid has two sides — late relaxes, early propels, and both are placements measured in the same milliseconds.
