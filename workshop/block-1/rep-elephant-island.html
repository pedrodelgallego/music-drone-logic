Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: Pattern Logic as Variation

Copy one clear section of Sleeparchive — “Elephant Island”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a single short percussive synth voice; the sequencer - rests, retrigs, displacement - does all the writing; arrangement is pattern-block substitution. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Gear: Digitakt II outs → M4 ins 3/4, the lab-001 wiring; the MicroFreak rests this lesson — or stay in the box entirely with one Operator sine-click
  • New set saved as REP-ELEPHANT-ISLAND, tempo 130 — then warp REF and adjust by ear
  • Short bright blips are sharp on the ear, and a retrig stacks several of them into one beat. Phones at the lab-001 mark; audition every retrig burst once at the set level before looping it
  • What you will make: a short replica of Sleeparchive — “Elephant Island”. Focus on this effect: clinical minimal techno from what sounds like one tiny blip voice - all development is combinatorial, and nothing about the sound itself ever changes.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a single short percussive synth voice; the sequencer - rests, retrigs, displacement - does all the writing; arrangement is pattern-block substitution.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Sleeparchive — “Elephant Island”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many voices can you count?
    Answer

    Very few - drums and one tiny blip, and the blip is the lead. You will overcount, because the same blip in a different rhythm reads as a new sound arriving.

  2. Does the blip’s sound ever change?
    Answer

    No. Not the pitch, not the length, not the brightness - across the whole track the voice is frozen, like a single key on a machine pressed in different orders. Relisten hunting for one timbral move; finding none is the answer.

  3. Then where does the development come from?
    Answer

    The grid. Which steps fire, which rest, which stutter into a retrig - every “new idea” is a redistribution of the same event in time. The sequencer is doing all the writing.

  4. How does the track get from one section to the next?
    Answer

    Block substitution. Nothing morphs and nothing fades - one pattern is swapped for another at a bar line, clean as a switch being thrown.

  5. Could you write one section’s difference from another as a sentence about steps?
    Answer

    You can - and that is the test of having heard it. “The blip now lands on 1 and 11 instead of 1, 5, 9, 13, and step 11 stutters.” If your sentence needs adjectives instead of step numbers, relisten to the two sections until it doesn’t.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your print lands on the same clinical economy — one frozen blip whose every change is a trig decision, at matched loudness.
  • The blip’s timbre is locked: no parameter locks on SRC, filter, or amp — trig placement, retrigs, and conditions only.
  • Four patterns, each exactly one trig-logic sentence away from the last.
  • Arrangement is pattern chaining — block substitution at bar lines, no fades, no morphs.

The track’s machinery in one row: one short blip on one Digitakt II track with its timbre declared frozen, four patterns that differ only in trig logic, and a chain that swaps them block by block into a single printed take.

one blip · single-cycle or sine-click → Digitakt II track 1 · timbre frozen → patterns A–D · trigs, retrigs, conditions → chain · block substitution · M4 in 3/4 → Live · DT PRINT · 3 min

Four patterns, one voice. Every square is the same sound at the same settings — the only thing that differs between panels is the firing logic, and that logic is the entire piece.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the track

Phase focus: the reference and the track. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “Elephant Island” into it.
  2. Live Loop 8–16 bars spanning one section change — you need a before and an after on the same screen.
  3. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Live REF stays one solo-press away all lesson.
  5. Live ⌘ T, rename DT PRINT, Audio From → Ext. In → 3/4, Monitor Off, fader at 0 dB.
  6. Logbook In the logbook, write the blip’s rhythm at two different sections as step numbers — your first two trig-logic sentences.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS across a section change, the print track is routed and silent, and two step-number sentences are on paper.

Phase 2 · ≈ 15 min

The blip — one voice, then frozen

Phase focus: the blip — one voice, then frozen. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Digitakt II + Live Load a single-cycle square or sine from the +Drive onto Digitakt II track 1 — or sample one short Operator sine-click from Live if you prefer the box-only route.
  2. Live Tune it to the record’s register by ear against REF — a small, exact pitch, more instrument than percussion.
  3. Live Amp: attack 0, decay very short, sustain 0 — the note should be over before you can describe it.
  4. Live Filter open or barely tamed.
  5. Logbook Write the track’s SRC, filter, and amp settings in the logbook and rule a line under them: frozen.
  6. Logbook No parameter locks on any of them for the rest of the lesson.
  7. Logbook Set the M4 input gain on a looping four-square of the blip, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
Expected result

One tiny voice that matches the record’s register and dryness, its settings written down and declared untouchable — a fixed object the sequencer is about to write with.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
A click or thump at the start of every hitSample start point before the true zero crossingNudge the start point until the attack is clean; trim, don’t fade
The blip rings on like a toneAmp decay too long for the materialShorten the decay until the note is a point, not a line — the record’s voice is over instantly
Phase 3 · ≈ 30 min

Four patterns by trig logic only

Phase focus: four patterns by trig logic only. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Digitakt II Pattern A — the statement: transcribe the record’s main blip figure from your phase-1 sentence onto the 16-step grid.
  2. Live This is the reference point everything else displaces.
  3. Digitakt II Pattern B — displacement: copy A to the next pattern slot, then move exactly two trigs one step each.
  4. Logbook Write the sentence first: “B is A with 5 → 6 and 13 → 14.”
  5. Digitakt II Pattern C — retrig: copy A, then hold one trig and set a retrig on the TRIG page — one step stutters into a burst.
  6. Digitakt II Audition it once at the set level before looping.
  7. Digitakt II Pattern D — rests: copy A and delete trigs down to a skeleton of two;
  8. Digitakt II give one survivor a 1:4 condition so the emptiness itself varies by pass.
  9. Digitakt II + Live Loop each pattern against REF; the family resemblance to the record’s sections should be plain.
  10. Digitakt II + Logbook If a pattern needs a knob outside the TRIG page, it is the wrong idea — write a different sentence.
Expected result

Four patterns, four written sentences, one unchanged sound — each pattern audibly a different piece of music made from the same frozen object.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Two patterns sound identical in the loopThe displacement is too small to read at tempoMove a trig that carries weight — a downbeat or its neighbour — not a step nobody was listening to
A pattern sounds like a new instrumentA parameter lock on SRC, filter, or amp crept inHold each trig and inspect; strip every lock that isn’t trig logic — the timbre is frozen
Phase 4 · ≈ 20 min

Chain the blocks and print three minutes

Phase focus: chain the blocks and print three minutes. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live Plan the chain on paper as blocks: A×8 · B×8 · A×4 · C×8 · D×8 · A×8 is a starting shape — adjust the proportions to what REF taught you.
  2. Digitakt II If the blip needs a clock under it, one plain quarter-note kick on a second Digitakt II track is allowed — programmed once, never varied;
  3. Digitakt II the lesson stays on the blip track.
  4. Digitakt II + Live Arm DT PRINT and record ≈ 3 minutes in Arrangement, switching patterns by queueing the next one a bar early — the swaps must land exactly on bar lines.
  5. Live Play the print end to end, then A/B a section change of yours against one of REF’s at matched loudness: same suddenness, same cleanness?
  6. Live Export: File → Export Audio/Video, WAV, named REP-ELEPHANT-ISLAND.
Expected result

Three minutes of architecture from one frozen blip — sections that are patterns, transitions that are substitutions, and a printed WAV whose first and last minute contain the identical voice.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Transitions stumble or arrive mid-barThe next pattern was queued after the bar had turnedQueue early — press the next pattern during the preceding bar and let the box wait
The three minutes feel like one minute three timesThe chain leans on A too long between substitutionsShorten the blocks; the record swaps more often than you think — recount its changes
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your print at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Is every variation in your print explainable as a trig-logic statement?
    Answer

    It must be. Go block by block: “B is A with two steps moved right; C is A with step 9 retriggered; D is two survivors and a 1:4 ghost.” If any block needs a sentence about tone, an illegal lock crept in — find it on the trig and strip it. Relisten to the record’s sections and run the same reduction on them; every one of them reduces.

  • Did the timbre ever change?
    Answer

    It must not have. Solo the blip and A/B the first and last minute of your print: same pitch, same length, same brightness — the voice identical while the music is not. That gap between a frozen sound and a moving piece is the entire lesson. Relisten to the record’s first and final minutes back to back and confirm it holds the same discipline for its whole run.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R1.
  • Carries into the lab: when the voice is fixed, the sequencer is the synthesis — Lab 004’s A and B patterns, one note apart, are this grid logic applied to a richer voice.
