Measurement Tools

Two tools make invisible problems visible. A spectrum analyzer shows the shape of your sound across all frequencies. A correlation meter tells you whether your stereo mix will survive being summed to mono. This lab teaches you to use both proactively — reaching for them before a problem, not after something already sounds wrong.

The point

Later in this workshop you will encounter phase problems by accident — that hollow comb-filter sound when two monitoring paths are both on. Here you learn it on purpose. You put the meter up first, read what healthy looks like, create a phase problem deliberately, then watch the meter confirm what your ears suspect. A measurement you trust finds a fault in seconds that ears alone would chase for an hour. It also catches the one fault ears in headphones miss entirely: a mix that sounds wide and full in stereo but disappears in mono.

1 · New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
Spectrum analyzer A live graph of your sound. Frequency runs left (low) to right (high). Height shows how much energy is at each frequency. It turns "sounds muddy" into "there is a 3 dB hump at 250 Hz" — a vague impression into a specific number.
Spectral tilt The overall slope of the spectrum from low to high frequencies. Most finished music tilts gently downward — more energy at the low end, less at the high end. A flat or upward tilt often sounds thin or harsh. An overly steep downward tilt sounds dull. The tilt gives you the broad tonal character of a sound at a glance.
Resonance · mud · masking Three problems the spectrum reveals. A resonance is a narrow spike where one frequency rings louder than the rest. Mud is a broad buildup in the low-mids (around 200–500 Hz) that makes everything sound thick and unclear. Masking is when two sounds pile up in the same frequency band, so one buries the other.
Correlation meter A single needle that scores how similar your left and right channels are. +1 means they are identical — perfectly mono-safe. 0 means they are unrelated — wide, but still safe. −1 means they are exact mirror-opposites — when summed to mono they cancel each other out, and the sound disappears. This is the most important meter for checking stereo safety.
Mono collapse Summing left and right into a single channel — what a phone speaker, a club PA's mono sub, or a Bluetooth speaker does to your mix. Any element near −1 correlation cancels out in that sum and disappears. The correlation meter predicts this before it happens.
spectrum analyzer spectral tilt resonance · mud · masking correlation meter mono collapse

2 · Listening — read with your eyes and ears at once

Before building anything, put the meters on a few finished tracks and learn what healthy looks like. The goal is to connect what you hear with what the meters show, so the readings start to mean something.

01 Your known-good reference — on the spectrum what a healthy tilt looks like

Drop Spectrum on the master and play your reference. Watch the broad shape, not the dancing detail.

Listen / look for
  • The downward tilt — strong lows, gently falling highs — and how smooth it is.
  • No lone spikes towering over their neighbours (those would be resonances).
  • How full but uncluttered the low-mids are: present, not piled up into mud.
After — verify

Sketch the tilt as a single line. That sketch is your target shape — step 03 turns it into a written characterization.

02 The same reference — on the correlation meter what mono-safe looks like

Now watch the correlation needle through the same track.

Look for
  • The needle living mostly between 0 and +1 — wide where it wants width, but rarely if ever dipping below 0.
  • That the bass holds near +1 (low end is kept mono in almost every good master).
  • How brief any dips toward 0 are — width that never tips into cancellation.
After — verify

Sum the reference to mono (Utility, Width 0 %). Nothing should vanish — confirming a needle that stayed above −1 predicts a safe collapse. This is the behaviour your own mix must match.

03 A deliberately over-widened track what danger looks like

Find or make a track with a heavy stereo widener on it. Watch the needle swing toward −1.

Look for
  • The needle plunging below 0 — toward −1 — on the widened element.
  • The huge stereo impression in headphones that the meter is warning you about.
After — verify

Collapse to mono: the widened element thins or disappears. You just watched the meter predict a fault before you heard it — the whole skill of this lab, in one move.

Before building, write one sentence describing what you expect to learn. For example: a problem I can measure is a problem I can fix in seconds — and the correlation meter catches the one fault my ears in headphones will never warn me about. The bench will test it.

4 · Procedure

Step 01

Insert both meters last on the master

Goal: a permanent set of meters on the master that reads exactly what leaves your system.

Do this
  1. On the master, add Spectrum, then a correlation meter, then a Utility — in that order, last in the chain.
  2. Set Spectrum to a readable scale (logarithmic frequency, a sensible averaging so the display is stable, not jittery).
  3. Confirm the correlation meter shows a scale from +1 through 0 to −1.
  4. Leave the Utility at Width 100 % for now; it is your mono switch later.
Expected result

Two live meters and a mono switch, reading the master output. This chain stays on your master template from now on — there from the start, not added after something goes wrong.

Step 02

Characterize the known-good reference

Goal: a written description of what a healthy track looks like on both meters — your reference point for everything that follows.

Do this
  1. Play the reference through the chain. On Spectrum, write down: the tilt (downward / flat / rising), any standing resonance (frequency + how prominent), and the low-mid fullness (mud or clean).
  2. On the correlation meter, write down: where the needle lives (the band it occupies), how low it dips, how often.
  3. Toggle the Utility to Width 0 % and back: note what, if anything, changes on mono collapse.

Step 03

Build a test signal and read it clean

Goal: a healthy stereo signal you understand on both meters — the clean starting point you will then deliberately break.

Do this
  1. Make a sustained stereo source: a MicroFreak pad printed and lightly stereo-spread, or a synth pad — something with body across the spectrum.
  2. Read it: note its tilt and where its correlation needle sits. A naturally stereo pad should read near 0 to +0.7 and survive mono fine.
  3. Fill the "before" column for this clean baseline.
Decision checkpoint

Can you state your test signal's tilt and correlation number from the meters, without guessing from how it sounds? If you are reading the needle's position rather than relying on your impression of width, you are ready to break it.

5 · The fault & diagnosis

Step 04

Fault ritual — create a phase problem, then measure it

The required fault: a mix that sounds good in stereo but collapses in mono. You create it on purpose, see it on the meters, fix it, and watch the meters recover.

predict → widen → read both meters → collapse to mono → fix → confirm recovery
  • Predict: write down what you expect the correlation needle to do, and what you expect to happen to the spectrum when you collapse to mono.
  • Create (choose one): put an aggressive stereo widener on the test signal and push it hard — or duplicate the signal, detune the copy a few cents, and pan the pair hard left and right. Both methods create out-of-phase stereo content.
  • Measure in stereo: note how wide it sounds, and that the correlation needle has dropped toward −1.
  • Collapse to mono: set Utility Width to 0 %. Watch the spectrum: the affected frequencies drop into comb notches — regular dips where the two channels cancel each other out — and the element thins or disappears. The needle, the spectrum, and your ears should all agree.
  • Fix: remove the widener, or mono the low end and reduce the detune and panning, until the needle climbs back above 0 and the comb notches are gone from the mono spectrum.
  • Confirm: re-read both meters in mono. Recovery on the meters is the proof, not your impression. Fill the "after" column.

6 · Pass gate & report bundle

The report bundle — filed in the logbook
  • Reference characterization — the "good" column, tilt + correlation profiled
  • Before reading — the widened test signal's correlation dip and the comb notches in its mono spectrum (screenshot or written values)
  • After reading — the same signal fixed, needle back above 0, notches gone
  • The completed before/after table
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule, and the meter reading that proves the fix
  • The master diagnostic chain saved into your master template (Spectrum + correlation + Utility, last in chain)
  • System diff sentence — "I thought my mix was wide; the meter showed it was cancelling."
Pass gate

A before/after correlation and spectrum reading showing a phase problem found and fixed by measurement, not by ear alone. The "before" reading shows negative correlation and comb notches on mono collapse. The "after" reading shows the needle back above 0 and the notches gone. "It sounds better now" does not pass. "Correlation went from −0.6 to +0.3 and the 1.2 kHz / 2.4 kHz comb notches disappeared in mono" passes.

Pass standard
Minimum passThe bundle above exists; the before/after readings document a phase fault found and fixed on the meters.
Solid passYou can read tilt, resonance, mud, and masking off the spectrum and the correlation band off the needle without prompting; the diagnostic chain lives on your master template.
Advanced transferYou reach for the correlation meter before a problem — checking mono safety on every wide element as you place it, not after a collapse.
  1. Which fault did the meter catch that your ears in headphones had not flagged?
  2. How far did the correlation needle move between the broken and fixed states — and was the audible mono difference proportional?
  3. What in the reference's spectrum surprised you when you saw it measured?
  4. Where will you now place these meters by default — and at what point in a build will you first glance at the correlation needle?
Bench principle

A problem you can measure is a problem you can fix in seconds — put the meter up first, and let the needle catch the fault your ears would miss.
