Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: One Bassline, Whole Record

Copy one clear section of Adonis — “No Way Back”. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: one synth-bass riff on famously cheap gear, a bare 707/808-style drum pattern, vocal interjections - the variation is the riff’s filter and accents, and what drops out. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordAdonis — “No Way Back” (1986, Trax)
The effecta single squelching bassline serves as melody, groove, and hook for six minutes — economy so total there is nothing else to listen to, and nothing else needed

Before you start
  • Gear: MicroFreak → M4 in 1, Digitakt II outs → M4 ins 3/4 — the lab-001 wiring table, both rigs; the bass and the jack each get their own track
  • New set saved as REP-NO-WAY-BACK, tempo 120 — then warp REF and adjust by ear
  • A resonant filter opening on an accented note jumps well past the level you mixed at. Phones at the lab-001 mark, never higher; audition every new filter position once at the set level before looping it
  • What you will make: a short replica of Adonis — “No Way Back”. Focus on this effect: a single squelching bassline serves as melody, groove, and hook for six minutes.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: one synth-bass riff on famously cheap gear, a bare 707/808-style drum pattern, vocal interjections - the variation is the riff’s filter and accents, and what drops out.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Adonis — “No Way Back”. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Count the pitched elements — instruments playing notes. How many?
    Answer

    One. The bassline is the only thing in the track with pitches; everything else is drum machine and voice. What feels like a lead arriving partway through is the same riff with the filter further open.

  2. Where is the melody?
    Answer

    In the bass. There is no line on top - the riff’s pitches are the only tune the record has, and they are the tune you leave humming. Relisten and hum along; the thing you hum is the bassline, because there is nothing else to hum.

  3. What changes about the riff across the track?
    Answer

    Its dress, never its notes - brightness, bite, which hits lean hardest. The pitches loop unchanged for six minutes while the filter and the accents redistribute the attention across them.

  4. What do the vocal shouts do?
    Answer

    They mark time. The interjections are punctuation - they flag a section turning the way a fill or a new layer would on a richer record, and they cost nothing. They are events, not melody.

  5. What is the track’s biggest event made of?
    Answer

    Absence. The drums step out, or the bass does, and the return of the unchanged riff lands harder than any addition could. On a record this bare a dropout is a cliff edge.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
  • Sound target: a solo-flip between REF and your take lands on the same total economy — one squelching riff over a bare jack, at matched loudness.
  • One pitched element, total — the MicroFreak riff. If something else seems to need pitches, the riff is not working hard enough yet.
  • The drums stay bare — kick, hat, clap, and no fills the record doesn’t make.
  • Arrangement is mutes plus one filter move; nothing new ever enters.

The track’s machinery in one row: one 8–16-step bass riff from a single oscillator through a single filter, a bare drum-machine pattern underneath, and three to four minutes arranged with mute buttons and one filter move.

MicroFreak · one osc · one filter · the riff → M4 in 1 · BASS → Digitakt II · bare jack · M4 in 3/4 · DRUMS → Live · mutes + one filter move · 3–4 min print

The same bar three times. Nothing is added between panels — the filter and the mute buttons reassign the riff’s job, and that reassignment is the whole arrangement.

Phase 1 · ≈ 10 min

The reference and the tracks

Phase focus: the reference and the tracks. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Live ⌘ T, rename the track REF (⌘ R), and drag “No Way Back” into it.
  2. Live Find 8–16 bars containing one dropout and one clear filter change on the riff, and loop them.
  3. Live Drop Audio Effects → Utility on REF and trim so the section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS.
  4. Live REF stays one solo-press away for the whole lesson.
  5. Live ⌘ T twice; rename BASS (Ext. In 1) and DRUMS (Ext. In 3/4), faders at 0 dB.
  6. Logbook Write the riff’s rhythm in the logbook as you hear it — which steps fire, which lean.
  7. Logbook This sketch is what phase 2 transcribes.
Expected result

The record loops at ≈ −12 dBFS, two named and routed tracks wait for the rigs, and a first rhythm sketch is on paper.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
REF always wins the A/BIt’s just louder — Utility never trimmedPull it down until the looped section peaks ≈ −12 dBFS, same as your tracks
No meter movement on either inputExt. In channels don’t match the physical jacksMicroFreak on 1, Digitakt II on 3/4 — the lab-001 wiring table
Phase 2 · ≈ 25 min

The riff — one oscillator, one filter, transcribed

Phase focus: the riff — one oscillator, one filter, transcribed. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. MicroFreak MicroFreak: Type → V.Analog, Paraphonic OFF, LP filter, Cutoff ≈ 30 %, Resonance ≈ 45 % — present, not whistling.
  2. MicroFreak Envelope: attack 0, decay short, sustain low.
  3. MicroFreak In the Matrix, route Env → Cutoff, a small-to-medium amount — that route is the squelch;
  4. MicroFreak tune it until each note spits and closes.
  5. MicroFreak + Live MIDI track, MIDI To → MicroFreak, channel 1: transcribe the riff by ear into a one-bar clip, 8–16 steps, mostly one low octave.
  6. Live Loop REF, hum it, place it, revise until the two lines chase each other.
  7. Live Set the M4 input-1 gain on the looping riff, peaks ≈ −12 dBFS — check it with the filter at its most open, not its most polite.
  8. MicroFreak + Logbook Write the patch’s five numbers in the logbook: cutoff, resonance, decay, env-amount, octave.
Expected result

Your bar and the record’s loop together — same notes, same bounce, recognisably the same family of squelch — from one oscillator and one filter.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Every note is the same dull thud — no squelchEnv → Cutoff amount at zero, or cutoff already openClose the cutoff to ≈ 30 % and raise the Matrix amount until the filter audibly spits per note
The line drones instead of bouncingDecay too long, sustain too highShorten the amp decay and drop sustain — the rubber lives in the release of each hit
Phase 3 · ≈ 15 min

The jack — a drum pattern with no opinions

Phase focus: the jack — a drum pattern with no opinions. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Digitakt II Digitakt II: kick on the quarters, closed hat on the off-beats, a clap or snare on 2 and 4 — three tracks, plain 707/808-style hits from the +Drive.
  2. Digitakt II No fills, no ghost notes, no flourishes the record doesn’t make.
  3. Digitakt II If a step feels clever, remove it.
  4. Digitakt II + Live Match the Digitakt II’s clock to Live (the lab-003 sync setting) so the jack and the riff lock.
  5. Live Balance DRUMS against BASS by ear against REF — on the record the bass sits in front of the kick, not under it.
Expected result

A bare jack that you stop noticing within a bar — which is correct — with the riff sitting in front of it the way the record’s does.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The drums swallow the riffDRUMS balanced for excitement, not for the recordSolo REF, hear where its kick sits relative to the bass, and pull yours to match
The pattern feels busy next to REFExtra steps crept inDelete until only kick, off-beat hat, and the 2/4 hit remain — bare is the spec
Phase 4 · ≈ 25 min

Arrange with mutes and one filter move

Phase focus: arrange with mutes and one filter move. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Plan the take on paper in plain words: where the filter opens, where it closes back, where the drums drop, where the bass stands alone.
  2. Three or four moves total — the record is patient.
  3. Live Arm BASS and DRUMS, Monitor Off on both, and record a continuous 3–4 minute take in Arrangement.
  4. Digitakt II + MicroFreak During the take: ride the cutoff slowly across the whole arc — its most open pass is your chorus — and perform the drum entries and exits from the Digitakt II’s mute mode.
  5. Live Place at least one full dropout and let the unchanged riff return.
  6. Live Afterward, cut — don’t add.
  7. Live If a stretch sags, remove something from it and let the return do the work, exactly as the record would.
  8. Live A/B against REF at matched loudness, then export: File → Export Audio/Video, WAV, named REP-NO-WAY-BACK.
Expected result

Three to four minutes in which one bar of notes reads as a developing track — the filter’s arc as the foreground story, the mutes as the form, and not one new element anywhere.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The take feels staticMutes too timid, filter arc spread too thinCount how often REF shifts weight — it is more often than you think; mark its shifts and match their rate
A filter peak jumps past your mixed levelResonance and an open cutoff stacking on accented notesBack the resonance off a notch and keep the phones at the mark — never turn up to inspect
Verify against the record

Solo-flip between REF and your export at matched loudness — phones at the mark, no level riding while you judge.

  • Count the pitched elements in your take. How many?
    Answer

    One, or the replication failed. If a pad, a chord, or a second line crept in, delete it and listen to what the riff does in the space it leaves — the record proves the space was never empty. Relisten to the record with the same census: six minutes, one pitched element, and no gap where a second one belongs.

  • Where does the hook live when the bass is the bass and the lead?
    Answer

    In the filter and the accents. The hook is not a separate part — it is the same notes at their brightest, which is why the riff’s most-opened pass works as the chorus. If your hook needed new material, the filter move was too shy to carry the foreground. Relisten to your take a day later and notice which pass you hum: the open one.

  • Logbook — three-line miss list. Matched: where your version produces the record’s effect. Missed: where it doesn’t — and your best guess why. Change next: the one move you’d try first.
