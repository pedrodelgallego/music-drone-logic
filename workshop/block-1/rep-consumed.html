Replicate: arrangement by removal

Copy one clear section of Plastikman — Consumed. Build the main parts, record a short take, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: The full loop is the ceiling; arrangement is removal and re-entry, planned and executed by mutes. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

Before you start
  • Digitakt II synced to Live (lab 002’s clock setup), MicroFreak on its input
  • Pen and paper for the removal chart — it is transcribed from the record before anything is performed
  • What you will make: a short replica of Plastikman — Consumed. Focus on this effect: A piece that breathes for minutes at a time, where every change feels enormous.
  • What you will turn off: extra parts, decoration, and shortcuts that hide whether the mechanism works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: The full loop is the ceiling; arrangement is removal and re-entry, planned and executed by mutes.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Plastikman — Consumed. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is absent RIGHT NOW (pick any moment)?
    Answer

    At almost any moment, something that played earlier is gone - the hats, a percussion layer, the 303’s upper movement, sometimes the kick itself.

  2. When something left, did you notice at the moment of exit or only later?
    Answer

    Almost always later. The exits are placed so quietly that you register the new emptiness a few bars after the element left - you feel the room change before you can say why.

  3. Does anything return — and is its return bigger than its first entrance?
    Answer

    Yes, and the return is always the bigger event. The absence taught you the element’s weight, so when it re-enters it carries the memory of every bar it was gone.

  4. How much of the minute is “full” vs “emptied”?
    Answer

    Far more is emptied than full. The complete wall is a point the record visits, not a state it rests in - most of the minute, at least one element is missing.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target: the arrangement is mutes only — no fades, no edits, no new sounds — and every mute lands where the record’s own exit lands.
  • Elements: the 303 figure and the drum parts are transcribed from the section by ear.
  • Opening: the removal that makes the record feel like it OPENED does the same in your take.
  • Return: the record’s re-entries land as bigger events in yours too, where they land in theirs.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II timing: press [TEMPO] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to set swing. 50% is no swing. In grid recording, hold a trig and press [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to check or zero micro-timing.
  • Digitakt II sound and sync: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Press [FX] to reach delay/reverb/chorus sends. Press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync; use [YES] or [LEFT]/[RIGHT] to toggle receive settings.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter if your device has a standby state, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson explicitly tells you to restart.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~35 minutes

Transcribe the wall

Phase focus: transcribe the wall. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track. Drop Consumed onto a dedicated REF track in Live.
  2. Find the stretch where the most elements play at once and loop 8–16 bars of it — that is your transcription source.
  3. Gain-match it with a Utility so comparisons are honest, and keep it one solo away for the whole session.
  4. Transcribe the 303 figure on the MicroFreak. Note for note — pitches, lengths, where it slides and bites.
  5. Chase the sound after the notes: engine, filter, decay.
  6. A/B it solo against the REF before moving on.
  7. Transcribe the drum parts on the Digitakt II. Kick, hat, percussion — whatever is actually in the section, with their weight, decay, and placement checked against REF.
  8. A/B each element solo against the REF before adding the next.
  9. Confirm the wall matches. Loop your full set next to the REF — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs.
  10. It should feel like the same full, stable maximum.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Plastikman — Consumed (title track) and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — Transcribe the wall — as the smallest Live set that proves The full loop is the ceiling; arrangement is removal and re-entry, planned and executed by mutes.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects A piece that breathes for minutes at a time, where every change feels enormous.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate Transcribe the wall into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates The full loop is the ceiling; arrangement is removal and re-entry, planned and executed by mutes, not just the surface of Plastikman — Consumed (title track).
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A full loop that A/Bs against the record’s densest stretch as the same wall — same elements, same weight — before a single thing has been removed.

Phase 3 — ~30 minutes

Rehearse, then perform the mutes

Phase focus: rehearse, then perform the mutes. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

all elements (MicroFreak + Digitakt II) → mutes only — no fades, no edits → master
Do this
  1. Set up the mutes. Digitakt II track mute mode (or FUNC + trig), or Launchpad track mutes for the Live side.
  2. Every element on the chart needs one reachable button.
  3. Rehearse the chart once without recording — just to get your hands used to which button is which element.
  4. Record the performance. Arm recording and execute the record’s chart with mutes only, start to finish.
  5. No fades, no new sounds, no edits — if you miss one of their cues, the miss stays.
  6. Hold the emptiest bar as long as they do. Resist the urge to bring things back early — the record holds it longer than feels comfortable, and that discomfort is the material.
  7. Rebuild by re-entry, in their order. Bring the elements back exactly where and in the order the record does.
  8. Notice what each return does — that is data for the verify step.

Verify against the record

A/B your three minutes against the same stretch of Consumed — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate? Then answer two questions in writing.

Did your exits and returns land where the record’s do?

Answer

Check the diff, not your memory: every row of the chart either landed or it did not. The most common pattern is exits on time and returns early — the bare stretches feel far longer from inside the performance than they sound from outside, so the hands rush the rebuild. If your returns came early, the miss list entry is about nerve, not hearing.

Did the removal that makes their track feel like it OPENED do the same in yours?

Answer

It should — that is the record’s whole trick, and matching it is the proof you heard it. The opening is usually the exit of a heavy, constant element: when it leaves, the space around everything that remains suddenly becomes audible, and the track feels bigger with less in it. If your version of that mute felt like a loss instead of an opening, the element you removed was not carrying the weight theirs carries — go back and check the wall.
