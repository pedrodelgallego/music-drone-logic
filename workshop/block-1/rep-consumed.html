Block 1 · Replication lesson

Replicate: arrangement by removal

Build a full loop, then compose three minutes using nothing but the mute buttons.

The point

When the full loop is the maximum, the only composition tool left is taking things away — and absence becomes the event. The piece is not what plays; it is the shape of what is missing, minute by minute.

The system you are replicating
Source recordPlastikman — Consumed (title track)
The effectA piece that breathes for minutes at a time, where every change feels enormous
The mechanismThe full loop is the ceiling; arrangement is removal and re-entry, planned and executed by mutes
FidelityAs close to the record’s section as your rig allows — sounds, chords, patterns, and moves included. Closeness is the measure of your hearing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Digitakt synced to Live (lab 002’s clock setup), MicroFreak on its input
  • Pen and paper for the removal chart — it is transcribed from the record before anything is performed
  • Phones at the mark · 60–120 minutes clear — transcribing the elements by ear is slow work, and the mute performance needs at least one full rehearsal before the take
Reference pass — 10 minutes

Describe their section before you match it

Listen to about one minute of Consumed. Answer in the logbook, one line each, BEFORE touching gear. Check each answer against the model only after yours is written.

  1. What is absent RIGHT NOW (pick any moment)?
    Answer

    At almost any moment, something that played earlier is gone — the hats, a percussion layer, the 303’s upper movement, sometimes the kick itself. The honest answer names a specific element, and the moment you can do that, you are hearing the record as carved rather than assembled.

  2. When something left, did you notice at the moment of exit or only later?
    Answer

    Almost always later. The exits are placed so quietly that you register the new emptiness a few bars after the element left — you feel the room change before you can say why. That delay is the design: the removal works on you before you notice it working.

  3. Does anything return — and is its return bigger than its first entrance?
    Answer

    Yes, and the return is always the bigger event. The absence taught you the element’s weight, so when it re-enters it carries the memory of every bar it was gone. Nothing about the element itself changed — only what you now know about losing it.

  4. How much of the minute is “full” vs “emptied”?
    Answer

    Far more is emptied than full. The complete wall is a point the record visits, not a state it rests in — most of the minute, at least one element is missing. If your answer came out mostly “full,” relisten naming what is absent at each moment instead of what plays.

Build only after the answers exist — you replicate the section you described, not the one you remember.

  • Sound target: the arrangement is mutes only — no fades, no edits, no new sounds — and every mute lands where the record’s own exit lands.
  • Elements: the 303 figure and the drum parts are transcribed from the section by ear, as close as the rig allows.
  • Opening: the removal that makes the record feel like it OPENED does the same in your take.
  • Return: the record’s re-entries land as bigger events in yours too, where they land in theirs.

Steps

Phase 1 — ~35 minutes

Transcribe the wall

Subtraction only means something if the maximum you subtract from is theirs. This phase builds the full loop element by element, by ear — the slowest, most honest work in the lesson. Each element is A/B’d solo against the record before the next one is allowed in, because an error baked into the wall now poisons every mute later.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track. Drop Consumed onto a dedicated REF track in Live. Find the stretch where the most elements play at once and loop 8–16 bars of it — that is your transcription source. Gain-match it with a Utility so comparisons are honest, and keep it one solo away for the whole session.
  2. Transcribe the 303 figure on the MicroFreak. Note for note — pitches, lengths, where it slides and bites. Chase the sound after the notes: engine, filter, decay. A/B it solo against the REF before moving on.
  3. Transcribe the drum parts on the Digitakt. Kick, hat, percussion — whatever is actually in the section, with their sounds chased as close as the rig gets. A/B each element solo against the REF before adding the next.
  4. Confirm the wall matches. Loop your full set next to the REF — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs. It should feel like the same full, stable maximum.
Expected result

A full loop that A/Bs against the record’s densest stretch as the same wall — same elements, same weight — before a single thing has been removed.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
One element sticks out as obviously not theirsThe sound was chased against the full mix instead of soloFix that element solo against the REF first — you cannot subtract from the wrong wall
Your wall has more parts than you can point to in theirsSingle sounds split into invented layersMute your additions one at a time while the REF plays; keep only what the record confirms
Phase 2 — ~20 minutes

Chart the record’s exits and returns on paper

The removal chart is a transcription of their mutes, not a plan of your own — every row is copied from the record by ear. Scrub the minutes around your looped section and write down which element leaves at which bar, how long it stays gone, and what returns in what order. This piece of paper is the score you perform in the next phase.

Do this
  1. Scrub the REF across the minutes around your looped section, listening only for exits and returns — ignore everything else.
  2. Write the chart FROM the record: one row per span of bars, naming what leaves or returns in that span. Use the worksheet below as the template.
  3. Mark the emptiest span — the barest point of the section — and count how long the record holds it. That number goes on the chart.

Fill from the section’s actual exits — this column is the worksheet.

bars 1–16
Full wall — every element in. This is the maximum; nothing is added after it.
bars 17–32
First exit: ………… leaves. Note whether you caught it at the exit or only bars later.
bars 33–48
Second exit: ………… leaves; ………… is still gone.
bars 49–64
Emptiest span: what remains — and for how many bars does the record hold it?
bars 65–80
First return: ………… re-enters. Is it bigger than its first entrance?
bars 81–96
Rebuild order: ………… then ………… — copy their sequence exactly.
Expected result

A chart specific enough that someone else could perform the section from it — every exit, every return, every hold, with bar numbers.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
Every span on your chart looks the sameThe scrubbed stretch is too short — you stayed inside one state of the arrangementWiden the scrub to the minutes before and after your loop; the exits live at the edges
The chart is full of things being addedCharting entrances out of habit — this record exitsRelisten asking only “what just left?”; in Consumed nearly every event is a removal
Phase 3 — ~30 minutes

Rehearse, then perform the mutes

The chart now becomes a performance. Your hands learn which button is which element, then you execute the record’s exits and returns in one recorded pass — mutes only. The signal path below is the whole instrument: nothing between the elements and the master except the mute buttons.

all elements (MicroFreak + Digitakt) → mutes only — no fades, no edits → master
Do this
  1. Set up the mutes. Digitakt track mute mode (or FUNC + trig), or Launchpad track mutes for the Live side. Every element on the chart needs one reachable button.
  2. Rehearse the chart once without recording — just to get your hands used to which button is which element.
  3. Record the performance. Arm recording and execute the record’s chart with mutes only, start to finish. No fades, no new sounds, no edits — if you miss one of their cues, the miss stays.
  4. Hold the emptiest bar as long as they do. Resist the urge to bring things back early — the record holds it longer than feels comfortable, and that discomfort is the material.
  5. Rebuild by re-entry, in their order. Bring the elements back exactly where and in the order the record does. Notice what each return does — that is data for the verify step.
Expected result

Three minutes whose every change is a mute or unmute landing on the record’s own cue — including an emptiest stretch held as long as theirs.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
You brought elements back early at the emptiest pointThe discomfort of the bare loop wonRe-take with the hold length written large on the chart — count the bars out loud if needed
Wrong element muted mid-takeHands not yet mapped to elementsOne more unrecorded rehearsal; name each button out loud as you press it
Phase 4 — ~15 minutes

Listen back and write the diff

The chart was the plan; the recording is what your hands actually did. Putting them side by side turns the performance into data — the diff between the record’s mutes and yours is your hearing, on paper, the same plan-vs-reality move as the labs’ system diff.

Do this
  1. Listen back once, hands off. Follow the chart with your eyes while you listen and mark where your recording deviates from the record’s sequence of exits and returns.
  2. Observe — see it, not just hear it. Put the removal chart you transcribed next to what you actually performed and write the diff: which of the record’s mutes you missed, which unplanned ones you made.
Expected result

A written diff — missed mutes, unplanned mutes, early returns — short enough to fit under the chart and honest enough to feed the miss list.

Verify against the record

A/B your three minutes against the same stretch of Consumed — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs — would a friend hesitate? Then answer two questions in writing.

Did your exits and returns land where the record’s do?

Answer

Check the diff, not your memory: every row of the chart either landed or it did not. The most common pattern is exits on time and returns early — the bare stretches feel far longer from inside the performance than they sound from outside, so the hands rush the rebuild. If your returns came early, the miss list entry is about nerve, not hearing.

Did the removal that makes their track feel like it OPENED do the same in yours?

Answer

It should — that is the record’s whole trick, and matching it is the proof you heard it. The opening is usually the exit of a heavy, constant element: when it leaves, the space around everything that remains suddenly becomes audible, and the track feels bigger with less in it. If your version of that mute felt like a loss instead of an opening, the element you removed was not carrying the weight theirs carries — go back and check the wall.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: “Matched: …” · “Missed: … (best guess why)” · “Change next: …”
