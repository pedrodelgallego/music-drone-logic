Process & pattern — when the rule writes the rhythm

Three listening sessions about where rhythm comes from when nobody is placing beats by taste: a process that runs, a grammar of variation, a refusal to repeat, a hand that keeps polymetric time for decades. Every clock decision you make in this block starts from what you hear here.

The point

A rhythm is never just a pattern — it is a pattern plus a clock, a length, a rule for change, and a moment of reset. These sessions teach you to hear all four, because in Block 3 you become the person designing them.

Listening is the loop

These sessions are not homework beside the labs — they are the first step of the operating loop. Every timing decision you make in this block is stolen from here, and the questions below are the same five System Spec questions the labs use, plus a sixth.

  • Programme: complete — the briefs, references, and method below are final in shape; the worksheets are included below.
  • Reference overlap: these four works also appear in the lab accordions of 009–012, authored in parallel. That is by design — programme pages reuse lab references; the programme hears them as one comparison, the labs hear them one facet at a time.

New concepts — in plain words

Four terms carry this page. Here is what each one means, before any of them is used.

ConceptIn plain words
Process music Music where the composer designs a rule and the rule writes the notes. The decisions move up a level: instead of choosing this beat or that one, you choose what kind of beats the system is allowed to produce — then you listen to it work, like everyone else.
Phasing Two copies of the same pattern, one running slightly faster or slightly longer than the other. They drift apart, pass through every possible offset, and eventually realign. The composition is the journey between the two moments of unison.
Polymeter vs polyrhythm Polymeter: patterns of different lengths sharing one pulse — a 3-step loop against a 4-step loop, both counting the same beat, realigning every 12. Polyrhythm: different pulses sharing one length — three evenly spaced hits against four, inside the same bar. The labs use both; the worksheet must say which one it is hearing.
Variation grammar The ruleset that decides how a repeating pattern is allowed to change: which steps can move, which sounds can swap, what odds govern each event. A groove with a grammar never plays the same bar twice and never plays a wrong one — the grammar, not the bar, is what the artist composed.

How to listen — the six-question frame

Every work in this programme gets the same six questions, answered in writing in your logbook. A few lines per question is enough; vagueness is the only wrong answer.

  1. Inputs — what raw material enters the system?
  2. Transforms — what changes it, in what order?
  3. Feedback / variation — what keeps it alive over time?
  4. Constraints — what prevents clutter?
  5. Human role — what is performed, decided, curated?
  6. Likely audio path — what had to be routed, recorded, mixed, or performed for this to exist — and what was the clock: a sequencer, a tape machine, a conductor, a drummer’s arm?

The method is three passes per work. First pass: just listen. No notes, no pausing. Second pass: answer questions 1–5. Third pass: question 6 only. In this block the sixth question has a sharper edge: you are guessing at pattern lengths, clock sources, and rules you cannot see. Count where you can — bars between repeats, voices that realign — because counting is the engineering pass of this programme.

Two further prompts run alongside the six questions, asked once per work after the third pass. The context prompt: who made this, for whom, and with access to what — a conservatory, a stolen afternoon on borrowed gear, a hand drum, a studio nobody else could book? At whose expense did it become possible, and whose labour or tradition does the rule borrow from? A process is never neutral; the clock you steal arrives with a history, and naming it keeps the theft honest. The score prompt: what would this rhythm look like written down — a grid of steps, a circle of strokes, a phase diagram, a page of conventional notation? Try to sketch one bar of it. Notation matters here because it is the same act as programming a sequencer: it forces you to commit to a length, a position, and a rule, and it exposes exactly the parts of the rhythm you cannot yet count. The sketch that defeats you is the row R3 will test first.

Session 1 — Records: four process philosophies

Four answers to the same question — where does the next bar come from? Listen to each with the six-question frame, then compare the philosophies. The differences are the lesson; any one record alone would only teach its own surface.

01 Steve Reich — “Drumming Part I” the process is the score

Four pairs of tuned bongos and a single pattern, built up one beat at a time — beats substituted for rests until the full figure stands — then pushed out of phase, one player accelerating gently against another until they lock a beat apart. Nothing is hidden: the whole piece is the audible execution of a rule you can write in two sentences. This is the block’s founding document — the clearest recording ever made of a process doing the composing.

Listen for
  • The build-up: how the pattern is constructed by substitution, one added beat at a time, before any phasing begins
  • The phase move itself — the blurred, unstable stretch while one drummer pushes ahead, and the audible click when the new alignment locks
  • Resultant patterns: melodies neither player is playing, which appear only between the two parts
After — verify

Could you tell when a phase shift was in progress versus locked? In progress sounds like a flam slowly widening; locked snaps back into a clean composite groove. If every section sounded equally steady, relisten to any transition and follow just one drum pitch through it — the wobble is short, and it is the whole mechanism.

02 Jlin — “Erotic Heat” variation instead of a loop

From Dark Energy (2015) — footwork at 160 BPM built almost entirely from drums, with nothing that behaves like a loop. Where most dance music states a bar and repeats it, Jlin states a vocabulary and never stops re-deciding it: kicks land as phrases, not anchors, and the pattern you just learned is already gone. It sits in this programme as the modern proof that a groove can be all grammar and no loop — relentless variation that still hits like a machine.

Listen for
  • How long any pattern actually survives before it is changed — count bars between exact repeats, if you can find one at all
  • The kick as melody: pitch and placement doing the work a bassline would normally do
  • What stays fixed so the constant variation reads as one piece — the tempo, the palette, the phrase lengths
After — verify

What is the invariant? Not a loop — the palette and the pulse. The sounds and the 160 BPM grid never waver, which is exactly what lets every bar differ without the track falling apart. If it felt chaotic rather than composed, relisten to one minute and track only the kick: its phrases repeat in spirit, never in letter.

03 Autechre — “Flutter” the anti-quantization statement

From the Anti EP (1994), released against the UK Criminal Justice Bill, which targeted gatherings playing music “wholly or predominantly characterised by the emission of a succession of repetitive beats.” Autechre’s answer was engineering: a track whose rhythm is designed so that no two bars repeat — the sleeve advised DJs to keep a lawyer and a musicologist on hand to confirm its non-repetitive nature. A legal constraint turned into a compositional system; the constraint is the piece.

Listen for
  • Non-repetition as a discipline: try to clap along and notice exactly where your prediction fails, bar after bar
  • What still makes it danceable — the steady tempo and the stable sound palette underneath the ever-changing placement
  • The serene pads against the restless drums: one layer holds time so the other can refuse it
After — verify

Is it actually unpredictable? No — it is non-repeating, which is different. The events come from a constrained vocabulary on a fixed grid; only their arrangement never recurs. If you heard randomness, relisten to thirty seconds twice: the second listen feels familiar, which proves there is a grammar even though there is no loop.

04 Burnt Friedman & Jaki Liebezeit — Secret Rhythms polymeter played by hand

Friedman’s electronics and Can drummer Jaki Liebezeit playing cyclical patterns in unequal meters — by hand, for whole pieces, with a steadiness most sequencers would envy. Liebezeit spent decades refining a way of drumming that treats the pattern as a circle rather than a bar line. It sits in this programme as the counter-example: everything the other three works do with machines or scores, done here by a human being who has made himself the clock.

Listen for
  • The cycle lengths: count the drum pattern’s period against the electronics’ period, and find where they meet again
  • Machine-tight but breathing — what tiny timing and dynamic differences tell you a hand is playing, not a sequencer
  • Who follows whom: when the electronics and the drums drift, which one corrects?
After — verify

Who is the clock? Liebezeit — the electronics are arranged around his cycle, not the other way round. If you couldn’t tell the drummer from the machine, relisten to one track and follow a single repeated drum stroke: its level breathes slightly with each pass, which no p-lock did. That breath is the data point this whole programme turns on.

Session 2 — Samples: pattern length and variation grammar

Session 2 · Deconstruction

Chart one process piece, voice by voice

Pick one process piece — one of the Session 1 records, or another work whose rhythm is rule-made. Loop a short passage and chart it voice by voice: for each voice, the pattern length (in steps or beats — count it, don’t estimate it) and the variation grammar (what changes between passes, and what rule seems to decide the change: probability, rotation, substitution, phase). Finish with the sixth question for the whole passage: what clock and what sequencing logic had to exist for this to exist?

Note

You will be wrong, and being wrong in writing is the point. The hardest call in this block is telling a rule from a performance — is that displacement a probability firing, or a hand pushing? A guess on paper can be corrected; an impression cannot.

This worksheet is a blueprint

In replication lab R3 you will reimplement this passage as sequencer rules on the Digitakt — pattern lengths, conditions, p-locks — and find out, empirically, which rules you guessed right. Write each row as if someone will have to program a sequencer from it. Someone will: you.

After — verify

Every row should have a counted length and a named rule — a voice with a length but no grammar, or a grammar with no length, is half a row. If a row resists the chart entirely, mark it low confidence; R3 will test exactly that row first. The worksheet template is the table above; the columns are enough to run the session now.

Session 3 — Video: who is the clock?

Session 3 · Video

Reich ensemble footage, watched twice

Find footage of a Reich ensemble performing Drumming, Piano Phase, or Clapping Music — any ensemble, any era. Watch it twice: once as an audience member, then again watching only the hands, the glances, and the moments of drift. The suggestions below are searches, not curated picks — choose footage where you can actually see the players’ hands.

Search: Steve Reich Drumming ensemble livewatch for who holds steady while another player accelerates
Search: Piano Phase live performancewatch for the phase move itself — two hands, one grid, one drifting
Search: Clapping Music performancewatch for the rotation: the displacement is discrete, not gradual
  1. Who is the clock — which player holds the pulse, and how can you tell from the body, not the sound?
  2. The drift — how is a phase move executed: gradual acceleration, or a counted jump? What do the players watch while it happens?
  3. Pre-planned vs ridden — what was decided before the performance, and what is being decided in the room?
  4. Failure risk — what would a timing failure sound like here, and what could the ensemble do to recover?
Watch for the clock

Every system in this block must declare a master clock, and this footage is where that requirement comes from: even an ensemble of equals has one. Find the player the others listen to — then notice that the role can move during the piece. Your Digitakt and Live have the same negotiation, settled in a menu instead of a glance.

After — verify

Can you say who the clock was without rewatching — and at which moment, if any, the role changed hands? If you can’t answer, rewatch ten minutes watching only one player’s hands and the eyes of the player beside them.

Deliverable — the stolen principle

File all worksheets in the logbook. Then write down one stolen principle — a structural idea, never material — before you build anything with it. It will be applied and judged at capstone C2. Naming it in advance makes it a commitment instead of a story you tell afterwards.

Pass standard
Minimum passThree sessions filed, six-question notes exist, the pattern-length worksheet exists with counted lengths, and one stolen principle is named.
Solid passThe stolen principle is structural enough that another person could build from it without copying any pattern.
Advanced transferYou can hear the same principle operating in a work from another block’s territory — a drone, a dub mix, a field recording organized in time.
  • Valid: “compose the rule, then obey it completely” — constraint as the piece, stolen from Autechre’s move, not their bars
  • Valid: “the realignment point is the drop” — form from where unequal cycles meet, not from arrangement markers
  • Invalid: “bongos phasing like Reich’s” — that is material, not structure
