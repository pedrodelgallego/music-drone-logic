Replicate: Aleksi Perälä — “Colundi Sequence”

Copy the reference's working mechanism, not its style. Build a short original study with Live 12, Digitakt II, MicroFreak, and the MOTU M4, then A/B it against the reference at matched loudness.

The point

This drill teaches one thing: a timing rule. Here the rule is that a fixed alternative tuning — a table of specific frequencies chosen before any notes are written — can be the engine of a piece. Commit to the grid first, then let a sequence walk through it. The reference gives you the clue; the build proves whether you understood it.

Before you start
  • Live 12 set saved as REP-PERALA-COLUNDI.
  • Digitakt II connected by USB for sync and by main outs to MOTU M4 inputs 3/4.
  • MicroFreak output connected to MOTU M4 input 1 — this lesson lives on the synth, so the MicroFreak is your main source.
  • MOTU M4 headphones at the Lab 001 mark; Live tracks set to Monitor Off while recording hardware.
  • Reference on a Live audio track named REF, with Utility inserted for loudness matching.
  • What you will make: a 60-90 second original study where a sequence walks through a fixed microtonal tuning grid, and that grid alone gives the music its alien shimmer.
  • What you will turn off: any standard-tuned chord, sample, or pad that would let you stop hearing the microtonal grid.
  • What you will prove: the music still reads as gently out of tune yet consonant after you change the patch and the step order, because the tuning table is locked.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not a switch back to equal temperament.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to one clear section of Aleksi Perälä — “Colundi Sequence”. Perälä built a huge body of work tuned not to the 12 notes of equal temperament but to a bespoke set of frequencies — the so-called Colundi frequencies, chosen for their claimed psychoacoustic effect. Every note in a piece is locked to that table, and a melody is generated by walking the grid step by step. To your ear, trained on standard tuning, the result sits subtly off-pitch yet hangs together: queasy and consonant at once. The timing rule here is commitment to a grid: you fix the tuning table first, then the sequence only ever lands on those frequencies. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is the smallest repeated behavior you can point to?
    Answer

    A short sequence that re-triggers, but every pitch it lands on is drawn from a fixed non-standard tuning table rather than the chromatic scale. Name the behavior — stepping through a microtonal grid — not the mood.

  2. Which device or path could create that behavior in your studio?
    Answer

    The MicroFreak loaded with an alternative tuning table (its microtuning / Scala-style scale support), sequenced from the Digitakt II so the step order is yours. The alien feel is authored in the tuning table, not in the patch.

  3. What should stay boring so the mechanism remains audible?
    Answer

    The timbre and the rhythm. Keep a plain voice and a steady pulse. If you add filter drama or busy drums, you will not be able to tell whether the tuning grid is carrying the strangeness.

  4. What would prove you copied the system rather than the style?
    Answer

    Swap the voice and change the step order: if it still reads as gently out of tune yet consonant, you copied the tuning grid. If it only works with one specific patch or one melody, you copied the style.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II sound and timing: press [SRC] for sample/source settings, [FLTR] for filter, [AMP] for envelope/level, [LFO] for modulation, [FX] for sends, and [TEMPO] for tempo and swing.
  • Digitakt II sync: press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync. Use [YES] and the arrow keys to turn clock and transport receive on or off.
  • MicroFreak: use the Preset encoder to choose a plain voice, Type to choose the oscillator, the Utility/microtuning menu to load or build an alternative scale, Matrix for modulation routing, and Save only after the study behaves correctly.
  • MOTU M4: MicroFreak usually enters input 1. Digitakt II main L/R enter inputs 3/4. Inputs 3/4 have no front-panel gain knobs, so set their record level from the Digitakt II Main Volume.
  • Sound target: one plain synth voice locked to a fixed microtonal table and nothing else — no equal-tempered parts in the room.
  • Recording target: peaks around -12 dBFS in Live before any processing.
  • Copy target: a solo flip between REF and your print makes the shared off-grid-yet-consonant behavior obvious.
  • Variation rule: change one variable per pass — step order, octave register, or which grid notes are used — and write it down. Do not change the tuning table once it is set.
Your rig
Phase 1 · ~20 min

Put the reference in Live and write the hypothesis

Do this
  1. Drop the reference onto a track named REF.
  2. Loop the clearest 30-60 seconds — pick a stretch where the microtonal melody is the only thing playing.
  3. Add Utility to REF and lower it until it is comfortable.
  4. Write: “The mechanism is ___.” Use the listening tag if you need a first guess: a sequence walking a fixed microtonal tuning grid.
  5. Write the one thing you will not copy: the exact Colundi frequencies, the exact step order, the specific patch, or the mix polish.
  6. Create tracks named SOURCE, SYSTEM, and PRINT.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Aleksi Perälä — “Colundi Sequence” and use a software synth that accepts a custom tuning (Scala/.tun import or built-in microtuning), MIDI clips, and clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — Put the reference in Live and write the hypothesis — as the smallest Live set that proves a fixed microtonal grid governs every note while the sequence steps through it.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects whether the tuning grid alone makes it sound alien yet consonant.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: translate Put the reference in Live and write the hypothesis into an FM lead with a loaded microtuning table, sound locks, trig conditions, LFOs, macros, and Overbridge or main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern so it demonstrates a fixed microtonal grid governing every pitch, not just the surface of Aleksi Perälä — “Colundi Sequence”.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase — which grid notes the sequence uses — and name it before recording.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

You have a looped reference, three working tracks, and one written hypothesis before any programming starts.

Phase 2 · ~35 min

Load the tuning grid and generate the pulse on the Digitakt II

Build the smallest system that can prove the hypothesis. One synth voice, a fixed tuning table loaded before any notes, a steady pulse. The point is to commit to the grid first and let the sequence move only within it, not to finish a track.

Do this
  1. On the MicroFreak, open the microtuning / Utility menu and load or build an alternative scale (a Scala-style tuning table). Set it before you write a single note. This is the grid.
  2. Pick a small set of grid notes — say six or eight steps of the table — and write them down. These are the only pitches the piece may use.
  3. Press [PTN], choose an unused pattern slot on the Digitakt II, and press [PLAY]. If you hear anything, stop and choose another slot.
  4. [TRK] + [TRIG 1] selects a MIDI track routed to the MicroFreak. Set it to send notes to the synth, not to trigger a sample.
  5. Press [RECORD] for grid recording and place a steady 16-step pulse. Enter pitches so the sequence walks the grid — step up and down through your chosen table notes. Because the MicroFreak is microtuned, each MIDI note resolves to a Colundi-style frequency, not an equal-tempered one.
  6. Keep the rhythm plain and the patch plain so the tuning is exposed. Do not add an equal-tuned pad or sample.
  7. Listen: the line should sit subtly off against your inner sense of pitch yet hang together. That queasy consonance is the grid working.
  8. Record one pass with the microtuning loaded, then one pass with standard tuning restored — hear how the alien shimmer vanishes the instant the grid is gone.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Aleksi Perälä — “Colundi Sequence” and use a software synth with a custom tuning loaded, MIDI clips, and clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — Load the tuning grid and generate the pulse on the Digitakt II — as the smallest Live set that proves a fixed microtonal grid governing every note.
  3. Import or build a tuning table first; then write a MIDI sequence that only walks the notes of that table.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects the off-grid-yet-consonant feel.

  2. Program the pattern so it demonstrates a sequence stepping through a fixed microtonal grid, not just the surface of Aleksi Perälä — “Colundi Sequence”.
  3. Keep which grid notes the sequence uses on a named macro for the main change in this phase.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.

Phase 3 · ~15 min

Route the studio so the print is clean

Make the signal path boring and dependable. If the recording path is confusing, you will misdiagnose the mechanism.

MicroFreak · input 1 + Digitakt II · inputs 3/4 → MOTU M4 → USB → Live 12 · Monitor Off
Do this
  1. Plug MicroFreak into input 1 — it carries the whole study here. Set the input gain while watching Live.
  2. Plug Digitakt II left/right into inputs 3/4 in case you resample, but expect it near silent.
  3. Set hardware tracks to Monitor Off. This prevents doubled monitoring.
  4. Arm one record track at a time and check peaks. Aim for -12 dBFS, not “as loud as possible.”
  5. If the MicroFreak is too quiet or too hot, adjust its level or input 1 gain and write the position down.
  6. Record 60-90 seconds of the system. Confirm the microtuning is still loaded before you commit the take.
  7. Press ⌘ J to consolidate the best section into a clean clip named PRINT-A.

Phase 4 · ~20 min

Make the system map and the A/B proof

Draw the mechanism, then test the print against the reference. The map is not decoration; it is how you find what failed.

Do this
  1. Put REF and PRINT-A on adjacent tracks.
  2. Use Utility to match loudness before judging.
  3. Listen to 10 seconds of REF, then 10 seconds of PRINT-A.
  4. Write one sentence: “The first thing that gives mine away is ___.”
  5. Fix only that one thing and record PRINT-B.
  6. Keep both prints. The difference between A and B is the lesson.

Final check

Play the reference and your best print at matched loudness, then answer these in the logbook:

  • What mechanism did you copy?
    Answer

    Name the mechanism, not the genre: a fixed alternative tuning grid that governs every pitch, with the melody generated by stepping through it.

  • Where does that mechanism live in your setup?
    Answer

    Point to it: the microtuning / alternative scale loaded on the MicroFreak (the grid), with the Digitakt II MIDI pattern walking that grid — not a standard chromatic scale.

  • What did you deliberately not copy?
    Answer

    The answer should be material: the exact Colundi frequencies, the specific step order, the patch, or the mix polish. The workshop copies systems, not identity.

  • What rule carries into the related lab?
    Answer

    Write it as a usable instruction. Example: “Commit to a tuning table before writing notes; the melody only walks the grid I fixed first.”

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • System diff sentence: “I thought the system worked like ___; after building it, I discovered it worked like ___.”
  • Carry into the lab: open listening-l3.html and reuse the corrected rule, not the reference sound.
