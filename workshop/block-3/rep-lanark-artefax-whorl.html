Replicate: Lanark Artefax — “Whorl”

Copy the reference's working mechanism, not its style. Build a short original study with Live 12, Digitakt II, MicroFreak, and the MOTU M4, then A/B it against the reference at matched loudness.

The point

This drill teaches one thing: a timing rule. Here the rule is that you can build rhythm by subdividing it on itself — take a plain hit, retrigger it into a roll, retrigger the roll into a finer ratchet, and keep varying the count so the detail itself becomes the pattern. The reference gives you the clue; the build proves whether you understood it.

Before you start
  • Live 12 set saved as REP-LANARK-WHORL.
  • Digitakt II connected by USB for sync and by main outs to MOTU M4 inputs 3/4 — this lesson lives on the sequencer's retrigger engine, so the Digitakt II is your main tool.
  • MicroFreak output connected to MOTU M4 input 1 — use it as the source tone you will subdivide.
  • MOTU M4 headphones at the Lab 001 mark; Live tracks set to Monitor Off while recording hardware.
  • Reference on a Live audio track named REF, with Utility inserted for loudness matching.
  • What you will make: a 60-90 second original study where a plain rhythmic spine is fractured by retriggers into nested subdivisions, so detail appears at several time scales at once.
  • What you will turn off: any extra layers, pads, or melody that would let you stop listening to how the hits subdivide.
  • What you will prove: the subdivision engine still reads as motion — still feels like the grid is folding into itself — after you change the source tone and the spine.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not a borrowed loop or a one-shot fill you pasted in.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to one clear section of Lanark Artefax — “Whorl”. Lanark Artefax is the Glasgow producer Calum MacRae, known for highly detailed, micro-edited rhythmic programming. In “Whorl” he builds rhythm by repeatedly subdividing: a single hit becomes a roll, the roll becomes a finer ratchet, so the ear hears motion at several nested time scales at once. The timing rule is recursive — take a hit and apply retrigger or ratchet so it subdivides, then vary the subdivision count and rate so the detail itself becomes the pattern, not an ornament on top of one. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is the smallest repeated behavior you can point to?
    Answer

    A single hit that splinters into a faster group — a retrigger or ratchet whose count and rate change from one occurrence to the next. Name the behavior, not the mood.

  2. Which device or path could create that behavior in your studio?
    Answer

    The Digitakt II RETRIG, set per-trig with its own rate and length, fed by a MicroFreak source tone. The mechanism is authored in the retrig parameters, not in a pre-rolled sample.

  3. What should stay boring so the mechanism remains audible?
    Answer

    The underlying spine and the timbre. Keep a plain, even pulse and a flat tone. If you add melody or filter drama, you will not be able to tell whether the subdivision is carrying the piece.

  4. What would prove you copied the system rather than the style?
    Answer

    Swap the source tone for a plain click and change the spine: if the nested retriggers still read as fractal detail, you copied the subdivision engine. If it only works with one glossy sample, you copied the style.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II retrigger: hold a [TRIG] and use the RETRIG menu (or [FUNC] + the trig) to set retrigger on, then dial its Rate, Length, and velocity curve per step. Use [TRC] / trig conditions to vary which steps subdivide.
  • Digitakt II sound and timing: press [SRC] for sample/source settings, [FLTR] for filter, [AMP] for envelope/level, [LFO] for modulation, [FX] for sends, and [TEMPO] for tempo and swing.
  • Digitakt II sync: press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync. Use [YES] and the arrow keys to turn clock and transport receive on or off.
  • MicroFreak: use the Preset encoder to choose a short, transient-rich source voice, Type to choose the oscillator, Cutoff and Resonance for the filter, Matrix for modulation routing, and Save only after the study behaves correctly.
  • MOTU M4: MicroFreak usually enters input 1. Digitakt II main L/R enter inputs 3/4. Inputs 3/4 have no front-panel gain knobs, so set their record level from the Digitakt II Main Volume.
  • Sound target: one plain percussive source and nothing else — let the retriggers, not extra layers, create the detail.
  • Recording target: peaks around -12 dBFS in Live before any processing.
  • Copy target: a solo flip between REF and your print makes the shared fractal-subdivision behavior obvious.
  • Variation rule: change one variable per pass — retrig rate, retrig count, or which steps subdivide — and write it down.

Phase 2 · ~35 min

Program the subdivision engine on the Digitakt II (RETRIG)

Build the smallest system that can prove the hypothesis. One plain source tone, an even spine, and retriggers doing all the rhythmic work. The point is to make the subdivision the musical event, not to finish a track.

Do this
  1. Press [PTN], choose an unused pattern slot, and press [PLAY]. If you hear anything, stop and choose another slot.
  2. [TRK] + [TRIG 1] selects a track. Route it to a short MicroFreak source tone (or a plain percussive sample) so each trig fires one clean hit.
  3. Program a plain spine first: place even hits — say on steps 1, 5, 9, 13 — and press [PLAY]. This dull pulse is your baseline; do not make it interesting yet.
  4. Now apply RETRIG to specific steps. Hold step 4 (or any chosen trig) and turn its retrigger on, setting a Rate of 1/16 and a count of 2-3. That single hit now subdivides into a roll.
  5. Take a step that already retriggers and push its Rate to 1/32 with a longer length — the roll becomes a finer ratchet. This is the second level of subdivision.
  6. Vary the count and rate across steps so no two subdivisions are identical — some split in 2, some in 3, some in 5. The changing subdivision is the pattern, not a single fixed roll.
  7. Use a trig condition (e.g. 1:2 or a probability) on one retrig step so the fractal detail shifts every other bar instead of repeating.
  8. Record one pass with the retriggers obvious, then one pass with all retriggers off — hear how the spine collapses into a dull pulse when the subdivision is removed.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Lanark Artefax — “Whorl” and use a software source, MIDI clips, the Beat Repeat effect, and clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — Program the subdivision engine on the Digitakt II (RETRIG) — as the smallest Live set that proves single hits subdividing into nested retriggers.
  3. Use Beat Repeat with varied Grid and Interval, plus per-note repeat groups, as your subdivision engine; leave any melodic layer empty.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects the felt fractal detail.

  3. Keep retrig rate on a named macro for the main change in this phase.

Phase 3 · ~15 min

Route the studio so the print is clean

Make the signal path boring and dependable. If the recording path is confusing, you will misdiagnose the mechanism.

MicroFreak · input 1 + Digitakt II · inputs 3/4 → MOTU M4 → USB → Live 12 · Monitor Off
Do this
  1. Plug Digitakt II left/right into inputs 3/4 — the retrigger engine carries the whole study here. Set the record level from its Main Volume while watching Live.
  2. Plug MicroFreak into input 1 in case you record the raw source tone separately, but expect the printed groove to come from the Digitakt II.
  3. Set hardware tracks to Monitor Off. This prevents doubled monitoring.
  4. Arm one record track at a time and check peaks. Aim for -12 dBFS, not “as loud as possible.”
  5. If the dense ratchet bursts spike the meter, lower the Digitakt II Main Volume and write the position down.
  6. Record 60-90 seconds of the system, making sure a clear subdivision burst lands inside the take.
  7. Press ⌘ J to consolidate the best section into a clean clip named PRINT-A.

Final check

Play the reference and your best print at matched loudness, then answer these in the logbook:

  • What mechanism did you copy?
    Answer

    Name the mechanism, not the genre: single hits retriggered into nested subdivisions, where varying rate and count make detail appear at several time scales at once.

  • Where does that mechanism live in your setup?
    Answer

    Point to it: the Digitakt II per-trig RETRIG (rate, length, and count) plus trig conditions — not a pasted-in fill or a pre-rolled sample.

  • What did you deliberately not copy?
    Answer

    The answer should be material: the exact source samples, the pitch of the hits, the harmony, or the mix polish. The workshop copies systems, not identity.

  • What rule carries into the related lab?
    Answer

    Write it as a usable instruction. Example: “If I need a busier loop to make a part feel detailed, I have not yet made the subdivision itself do the work.”

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • System diff sentence: “I thought the system worked like ___; after building it, I discovered it worked like ___.”
  • Carry into the lab: open listening-l3.html and reuse the corrected rule, not the reference sound.
