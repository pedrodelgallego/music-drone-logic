Block 3 · Replication R3 · Study

Rebuild the Process

Take one phasing or pattern-logic passage you analyzed in L3 and reimplement it as sequencer rules on the Digitakt — pattern lengths, conditions, locks. You are not copying bars; you are copying the machine that writes the bars. Where your rules produce the record’s behavior, your analysis was right; where they don’t, you have found what you can’t hear yet.

Replication lab · S2 · E2 M2 F1 · half-cycle — requires the L3 Samples session · filed as study, never portfolio · scaffold

listen → hypothesis → draw the path → calibrate → build → fault → diagnose → print → delta
Start here
  • Put the L3 Samples-session worksheet beside the Digitakt; no worksheet, no R3.
  • Save a new Live set as R3-study and start from a blank Digitakt pattern bank.
  • Program the rules per the worksheet’s rows — lengths first, grammar second, sounds last.
  • Stop at the timebox. The corrected worksheet is the deliverable; the replica is evidence.
The point

A process piece can be transcribed two ways: copy what it played, or copy the rule that played it. Only the second tests your analysis — a copied bar proves you can hear a bar; a reimplemented rule that produces the right next 64 bars proves you heard the system. This lab only accepts the second kind.

What “close” means here

In R1 you matched sounds and levels; in R2, the gesture. Here closeness is behavior over time: the pattern lengths (counted right, so the voices realign where the record’s do), the variation grammar (the same kind of change at the same kind of rate), and the phase relationships (what drifts, what holds, what resets). Your replica should make the record’s prediction true: run it for 64 bars and the same kind of events should happen in the same places — even though no single bar is a copy.

How this lab works

Transcribe the rules

  • One worksheet row = one voice = one rule to implement
  • Pattern lengths counted, not estimated — the realignment proves the count
  • A row too vague to program is itself a finding

Implement as sequencer logic

  • Per-track lengths, conditional trigs, p-locks — the Digitakt’s grammar
  • Your own sounds; the record’s rules — never its audio
  • Declare the master clock before the first trig

Name every miss

  • A/B the behavior, not the bars
  • File each miss under the layer it lives in
  • Correct the worksheet — that is what leaves the room

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
Rule vs renderingThe rendering is what the record played; the rule is what decided it. Two renderings of the same rule can differ in every bar and still be the same system — this lab judges you on the rule, which is why your replica may sound “wrong” bar-by-bar and still pass.
Pattern length vs loop lengthA track’s pattern length is how many steps it runs before restarting; the loop length is how long the whole system takes to return to its starting alignment. Three tracks of 16, 12, and 9 steps make a loop far longer than any of them. Confusing the two is the most common analysis error in this block.
Realignment pointThe moment all the unequal patterns land on step one together again. It is computable before you press play — and predicting it is this lab’s version of the “what happens after 64 bars?” question. If the record realigns where your replica doesn’t, a length was counted wrong.
Conditional trigA sequencer step that fires only when a condition is met — a percentage chance, every Nth pass, only after another step fired. It is how a 16-step pattern produces more than 16 steps of behavior: the grammar lives in the conditions, not the grid.

1 · Target & analysis

The target is the process passage you charted in L3’s Samples session. Your pattern-length-and-grammar worksheet is the blueprint: each voice, its counted length, what varies, and the rule you believe decides the variation. If a row is too vague to program — “the hats change sometimes” — sharpen it into a programmable claim before you start. Finding out a row was unprogrammable already counts — that is the lab working.

  • The firewall. Open a new project and name it R3-study. The replica is study evidence, never portfolio output: it is not published, never “finished,” and nothing from this session — no pattern, no kit, no resample — moves into portfolio work. The corrected worksheet is what leaves this room; the audio does not.
  • Worksheet only. No tutorials, no breakdown videos, no interviews about how the work was made. Someone else’s analysis would test someone else’s hearing. If you get stuck, go back to the record with the six questions — not to a search bar.
  • Your sounds, their rules. You may not sample the record. Every voice in the rebuild is your own material — self-sampled hits and the lab 009–012 kit-building moves — chosen to sit in the reference’s register. The sounds are stand-ins; the rules are the replication.
Before you start
  • The Samples-session worksheet from L3 on the desk, lengths counted and rules named
  • The reference passage available as a local audio file — a stream cannot be dragged onto a track
  • The master clock declared in writing: who is the clock — Live or the Digitakt — and who follows; this is the block’s required declaration, and it applies to study sessions too
  • A 64-bar prediction written down: where will the voices realign, and what should the passage be doing at bar 64?
  • A new Live set saved as R3-study, a blank Digitakt pattern bank — the firewall starts at the project name
  • Phones at the lab-001 mark
  • Timebox: two sessions, three at most; write the end date down
Before building

Pre-commit the predictions

In the logbook, before you program a single trig: which rules do you expect to nail — the lengths, the grammar, or the phase behavior — which do you expect to miss, and why? And separately: the realignment prediction, in bars. Writing the prediction down first is the only honest way to find out what you actually believed. The misses you failed to predict are worth double.

2 · Rebuild & verdict

Setup

The A/B rig

Same rig as R1: the reference on a track named REF, Warp off, gain-matched with a Utility; the Digitakt’s rebuild coming in through the M4 on tracks grouped as REBUILD; one solo press flips between them, serial, never layered. The comparison loop covers the passage the worksheet describes — long enough to contain at least one full realignment. The full step-by-step setup recipe for this block — including how to A/B against a passage whose tempo breathes — is forthcoming; R1’s setup procedure carries you until then.

Expected result

You can solo REF, then solo REBUILD, at the same perceived loudness, over the same number of bars, hands-free — with both running from the declared master clock.

REF (Utility, gain-matched) → solo → master → phones Digitakt (rules running) → M4 in → REBUILD group · solo → master → phones

Only one is ever audible at a time — the comparison is serial, never layered, because layering hides timing and phase misses, which are exactly what this lab is testing.

One worksheet row = one voice = one Digitakt track = one rule the build will prove or break.

  1. Timebox first. Two sessions; three at the absolute most. The replica is finished when the timebox ends, not when it is perfect. The end date is already in the logbook: when it arrives, render and stop, mid-miss if necessary.
  2. Lengths before trigs. Set every track’s pattern length from the worksheet’s counted values, place the plainest version of each voice, and let the system run: do the voices realign where you predicted? Fix the counts before touching the grammar — grammar on wrong lengths is decoration on a wrong skeleton.
  3. Implement the grammar. Turn each named rule into conditions and locks, one voice at a time. Every condition you program is the worksheet’s claim made executable — resist adding rules the worksheet never made, even when they would sound better.
  4. Run the 64-bar test. Let the rebuild run unattended for 64 bars and record it. Compare against your written prediction: did the realignments, fills, and drift land where you said? This is the block’s engineering ritual, applied to someone else’s system.
  5. A/B at matched loudness. Thirty seconds REF solo, thirty seconds REBUILD solo, back and forth — never both at once. Listen for one dimension per pass: lengths and realignment only, then the variation grammar, then groove and micro-timing, then levels.
  6. Write the miss list. Every behavioral difference, each assigned to the layer where it lives — use the table below. The layer matters more than the miss: it tells you which worksheet question you answered wrong.
  7. Correct the worksheet, then quarantine. Strike the wrong counts and rules, write the corrected ones beside them. Render the 64-bar pass into the logbook as study evidence, close the project, and don’t reopen it for portfolio work. Carry the miss list forward: it is C2’s sharpened hypothesis about what time-rules actually sound like.

The detailed per-step recipes — length-counting method, condition-programming moves, the drift-vs-structure test — are forthcoming with Block 3 authoring. The rules above are final; only their expansion is pending.

The difference is…LayerExample
A different kind of sound entirelyMaterialTheir voice is a skin drum with pitch; my stand-in is a clicky sample — a wrong stand-in, not a wrong rule; note it and move on
Same behavior, different weightSignalTheir realignment lands like an event; mine passes unnoticed — the levels around the meeting point were part of the system and I didn’t chart them
Same grid, wrong grammarControlTheir hats vary by probability; mine rotate — the rule was misnamed, and 64 bars exposed it
Same grammar, wrong skeletonTimeTheir voices realign every 48 beats; mine every 64 — one pattern length was counted wrong, and everything downstream drifted from the claim
Decision check — rule miss or hearing miss?

For each item on the miss list: did the behavior fail because the Digitakt can’t express that rule — no per-step probability on that parameter, no smooth phase push — or because you didn’t notice the rule until you tried to program it? A rig limit is a finding about the rig, and the compromise you chose is worth writing down. A hearing miss is the gold — it is the precise edge of your current ear, located.

Pass gate — R3 complete when
  • The replica render — the 64-bar unattended pass, filed in the logbook as study
  • The master-clock declaration and the 64-bar prediction, written before building, filed beside the result
  • The Samples-session worksheet it was rebuilt from, with corrections marked — counts and rules
  • A/B notes at matched loudness
  • The miss list — each miss assigned to its layer, rule or hearing named
  • One sentence: what can you hear now that you couldn’t before?

No fault ritual, no full bundle — this is a half-cycle. If the replica’s bars sound nothing like the record’s bars, the lab can still pass: the process, not the rendering, is what is being judged.

Replication principle

Copy the machine, not its output — a rule that produces the right next bar is closer than a bar that merely matches the last one.
