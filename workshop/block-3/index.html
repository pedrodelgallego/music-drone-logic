Block 3 — Time

Rhythm is system time under constraint

Groove, probability, polymeter, and euclidean logic — connected to clock, latency, trigger accuracy, loop boundaries, and reset behavior. Block 2 taught you where sound comes from; this block decides when anything happens, and makes the deciding itself a system you can draw, predict, break, and reset.

Engineering focus

Musical time is system time under constraint. Every lab in this block requires five artifacts: a timing diagram, a master-clock declaration (who is the clock, and who follows), a “what happens after 64 bars?” prediction written before pressing play, one recorded drift, flam, or latency test, and a reset strategy — what returns the system to state zero? The block’s fault territory is clock, drift, and reset.

Working order

Do these in this order
  1. Programme L3 — run the three listening sessions and name one stolen principle; the programme threads through the whole block
  2. Lab 009 — micro-variation and groove: parameter-lock grammar, swing, micro-timing
  3. Lab 010 — probability grammar: design odds, not notes
  4. Lab 011 — polymeter and phase: unequal lengths as the entire arrangement
  5. Lab 012 — euclidean logic: rule-generated rhythm
  6. R3 — rebuild one process passage as sequencer rules and verify the L3 analysis
  7. Capstone C2 — sources × time as one system; the Year 1 capstone
  • C2 combines captured sources from Block 2 with the probabilistic and polymetric time of Block 3, built as one system, plus a reprise of the C1 piece and the year synthesis.

Listening programme L3 — process & pattern

Session 1 · Records

Four process philosophies

Four answers to the same question — where does the rhythm come from when nobody is writing beats? Analyze each with the six-question frame, then compare the philosophies.

Steve Reich — “Drumming Part I”the process is the score
Jlin — “Erotic Heat”variation instead of a loop
Autechre — “Flutter”the anti-quantization statement
Burnt Friedman & Jaki Liebezeit — Secret Rhythmspolymeter played by hand
Listen for

What generates the next bar in each work — a rule, a hand, a grid, or a refusal? And who, or what, is the clock?

Session 2 · Samples

Deconstruction — pattern length and variation grammar

Pick one process piece and chart it voice by voice: how long is each pattern, what varies, and what rule decides the variation. The worksheet becomes the blueprint for replication lab R3.

Session 3 · Video

Reich ensemble footage — who is the clock?

Watch the hands, not the faces: who carries the pulse, who is allowed to drift, how a phase move is executed without the whole ensemble falling apart, and what happens at the moment of realignment.

  • Deliverable: written system analyses (six questions per reference) + one stolen principle — a structural idea, never material, named in advance and evaluated at C2. Full briefs and worksheets: programme L3
