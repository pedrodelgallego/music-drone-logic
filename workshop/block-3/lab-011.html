Polymeter & Phase

Give two loops unequal lengths and press play: the arrangement writes itself, one bar at a time, as the loops slide against each other toward a distant reunion. Phasing techno on the MicroFreak and the Digitakt’s per-track lengths — the drift is not a flaw to fix; the drift is the entire piece.

The point

Two loops of unequal length need no arrangement — the arrangement is already inside them, unpacking itself one bar at a time until the loops meet again. Your job is to choose the lengths, declare the clock, and know to the bar when the reunion comes.

How this lab works

Choose lengths like intervals

  • Two to four loops with deliberately unequal step counts — 16 against 15 drifts slowly; 16 against 12 churns.
  • Compute every pairing’s reunion point before sequencing; the cycle of coincidence is the piece’s true bar.
  • Test: you can say, on paper, what bar 64 sounds like before you have ever heard it.

Make the drift audible

  • Each loop must be simple enough that the ear can track its slide — phasing dies in clutter.
  • MicroFreak carries the pitched cycle; Digitakt tracks at odd lengths carry the percussion cycles.
  • Test: a listener can point at the moment two loops pass through alignment.

Prove the clock, then print

  • One declared master clock; the drift you keep must be the composed kind, not the accidental kind — this lab teaches you to tell them apart.
  • Record the full cycle of coincidence in one take; the reunion is the form’s only cadence.
  • 6–8 minutes, fully original — the process is borrowed from the canon, the loops are yours.

Minimum passAudible original piece, routing + timing diagrams, fault entry, print map, and system diff exist.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
PolymeterLoops of different lengths sharing one pulse: every layer’s steps tick at the same speed, but one loop is 16 steps long and another 15, so their downbeats part company and travel. Same metronome, different lap lengths.
PhaseWhere one loop currently stands relative to another — together, a step apart, half a cycle apart. In this lab phase is not a mixing concern but the melody: the changing offset is what you are composing.
Cycle of coincidenceThe number of steps before two unequal loops line up again — their lowest common multiple. 16 and 15 reunite after 240 steps; that reunion is the piece’s real bar line, and everything between is one long phrase.
Per-track lengthThe Digitakt feature this lab leans on: each track in a pattern can have its own step count. One pattern, four tracks, four different lap lengths — the machine holds the polymeter for you.
Composed vs accidental driftTwo things slide in this lab: loops against loops (designed, exact, in steps) and clock against clock (a fault, in milliseconds). They can sound alike at first. The first is the piece; the second is the enemy wearing the piece’s uniform — the drift test exists to tell them apart.

The path — one clock, two voices, one tape

Pre-flight — before the first take
  • Gain ledger applied at M4 in 1 and in 3/4 — set by the number, then re-checked at a reunion bar
  • Recording plan: clock drift test take (64 bars) · one full cycle-of-coincidence take per candidate length set · keeper take — in that order
  • Timing diagram drawn with every loop’s lap length on it; the reunion table (every pairing, every meeting bar) computed and pinned
  • MicroFreak clock source confirmed as external and receiving — watch its tempo display agree with the Digitakt before trusting it
  • Live warp OFF on record tracks; Live’s metronome OFF — its grid is not this piece’s grid
  • Phones at the mark · a break planned every fifteen minutes

3 · Build

Build the lab in five passes. Each pass produces an artifact, not just a better-sounding session: a written rule, a printed comparison, a fault entry, and a corrected system sentence.

Build recipe · Polymeter &amp; Phase

Make the timing rule audible

Do this in order
  1. Write the time card. List every voice, its step length or rule, its reset behavior, and what should happen at bar 64 before programming.
  2. Program the neutral state. On Digitakt II choose an empty pattern. Use [TRK] + [TRIG 1] for the anchor voice, [TRK] + [TRIG 2] for the contrast voice, and [RECORD] to enter grid recording.
  3. Add the rule. Apply the lab rule only: p-lock variation, probability, per-track length, euclidean distribution, or rotation. Do not add fills until the rule is audible.
  4. Record the proof. Record a neutral pass and a rule pass into Live through the MOTU M4. Label them PRINT-0 and PRINT-RULE.
  5. Run the fault. Create the block fault: drift, flam, or reset failure. Predict the symptom, create it, diagnose it, fix it, and write the prevention rule.

The fault ritual — clock, on purpose

Predict what 64 bars of follower clock will do — written in milliseconds: jitter per hit, accumulated drift, audible or not → create the fault: degrade the clock path on purpose (route MIDI clock through the laptop and a busy USB hub instead of direct) → observe the printed waveforms of MicroFreak vs Digitakt reference at bar 1 and bar 64, zoomed → diagnose where the slippage enters (transport jitter vs steady rate offset — they look different on the waveform) → fix it: restore the direct clock path and re-measure, or commit the MicroFreak to early printed audio → write the rule learned in the logbook as one sentence, including the millisecond threshold at which you could first hear it.

The full report bundle — ten items, filed in the logbook
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, master peaking −3 dBFS, 6–8 minutes spanning ≥1 full cycle of coincidence
  • Routing diagram and timing diagram (lap lengths + reunion table) — drawn before, corrected after
  • Recording plan — drift test, prediction takes, keeper; what was printed, when
  • Patch notes — every loop’s length and content, the clock topology, the measured jitter and drift numbers
  • Session notes — including where the prediction and the recording disagreed, to the bar
  • Reflection answers, including the listening comparison — which reference contributed what to the hypothesis
  • One removal decision — which loop or layer was cut and what its absence did to the phase texture
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule (faultType: clock)
  • Print map — what exists as audio, sequence data, patch state; which loop lengths are recoverable from the print alone
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the system worked like X; after building it, I discovered it worked like Y.”
  • Minimum pass gate: audible output · routing diagram · fault entry · print map · system diff. Without these five, the lab is incomplete regardless of audio quality — a musically strong piece cannot bypass the engineering spine.

Listening — the hypothesis source

Ten works where loops of unequal length — or players a hair apart — do the composing. The span runs from two pianos in 1967 to a Berlin basement: the same physics, different rooms. Each gets a brief: what to listen for before you press play, and how to check yourself after.

01 Steve Reich — “Piano Phase” the phasing primer

Two pianists play the same twelve-note figure; one creeps ahead until they are a note apart, then two — the entire piece is the journey around that circle.

Listen for
  • The blur during transitions — the queasy stretch while the two pianos are between lock points.
  • The arrival at each new lock: suddenly a fresh melody appears that neither pianist is playing alone.
  • How long each stable plateau is held before the next creep begins.
After — verify

Where did the new melodies come from? From the offset — at each lock point the two copies interleave into a pattern that exists only between them; the composer wrote one bar and the phase wrote the rest. If you only heard repetition, relisten to one transition and name the exact moment the blur snaps into the new pattern — that snap is the piece’s engine, and your lab’s.

02 Steve Reich — “Clapping Music” phase without drift

The same idea with the drift removed: one clapper jumps a whole eighth note at a time — twelve discrete offsets of one pattern against itself, no in-between.

Listen for
  • The jump moments — clean, instant, no blur; compare this to “Piano Phase”’s creep.
  • Which offsets groove hard and which feel like stumbling — the same two patterns, radically different feels.
  • The return home at the end: how unmistakable alignment is after its absence.
After — verify

What does removing the glide change? The piece becomes a catalogue of offsets instead of a journey between them — discrete phase steps, which is exactly what a sequencer does naturally. Your Digitakt cannot creep like a pianist, but it can jump like a clapper; this piece is the proof that jumping is enough. Relisten and rank your three favorite offsets — those rankings are length-design decisions waiting to happen.

03 Terry Riley — “A Rainbow in Curved Air” loops as a garden

Overdubbed organs and tape loops cycling at their own lengths — phasing not as a rigorous process but as a lush, overgrown ecology of unequal cycles.

Listen for
  • How many simultaneous cycles you can count before losing track — then which one you instinctively hold onto.
  • Patterns that seem improvised but recur — long laps coming back around.
  • The difference in temperature from Reich: the same physics, played as joy instead of rigor.
After — verify

Was it process or improvisation? Both — fixed cycles of unequal length underneath, with playing layered over; the lesson is that polymeter is a bed, not a cage. Which cycle did your ear elect as “home”? There is usually one mid-length loop doing that work. If everything washed together, relisten and follow only the lowest layer; the garden has paths.

04 Philip Glass — “Two Pages” length as the moving part

One relentless unison line whose loop grows and shrinks by addition and subtraction — not two loops sliding, but one loop whose length is the variable. The control case for this lab.

Listen for
  • The moments a cell gains or loses a note — the loop lurches longer or shorter mid-stream.
  • How your sense of the downbeat survives, or doesn’t, each length change.
  • No phase, no offset — everything in unison; what does that remove, compared to Reich?
After — verify

What is the variable here, if nothing slides? The lap length itself — additive process changes the loop’s size in real time, where your lab fixes sizes and lets position drift. Hearing both makes the design space legible: length-change vs phase-change are the two axes of loop time. If the lurches floored you, relisten counting the cell’s notes out loud until a count fails — that failure is the event.

05 Manuel Göttsching — E2-E4 one hour, two cells

A 1981 hour of two alternating chords and a sequencer cycle, with guitar arriving thirty minutes in — the missing link between Riley’s loops and techno’s grid, and the patience benchmark for this lab.

Listen for
  • What changes in the first ten minutes — list it; the list is short and every item is small.
  • The sequencer figure’s relationship to the chord cycle — how the two lap each other.
  • When the guitar enters: how an hour-long form makes one entrance feel like an earthquake.
After — verify

How does one hour stay listenable with two chords? Because the form is built on cycles lapping each other plus a strict ration of events — scarcity converts tiny changes into drama. The guitar entrance lands like it does because nothing else had entered for half an hour; your 6–8 minute piece gets the same physics at smaller scale. If you skipped ahead while listening, note where — those skip points are where your own piece will need its reunions.

06 King Crimson — “Discipline” polymeter by hand

Two guitarists interlock repeating figures in different meters — unequal laps held by human players at rock volume; the lab’s system, executed on nerve instead of silicon.

Listen for
  • The two guitar figures — count each one’s lap if you can (they differ), and hear the pattern of their meetings.
  • The drummer’s role: where he plants flags so the listeners (and players) don’t drown.
  • Tiny human timing strain at the lap boundaries — the cost of doing this without a sequencer.
After — verify

What does the rhythm section do that your Digitakt won’t need to? It spends effort just staying aligned — and that audible effort is part of the music’s tension. A sequencer holds unequal laps for free, which means your version must source its tension elsewhere: from the lengths themselves. If you couldn’t separate the guitars, relisten with one ear panned — the interlock is mixed wide on purpose.

07 Voices from the Lake — “Hoba” phasing techno, the destination

Donato Dozzy and Neel’s project: techno where layers slide against each other so gradually the track seems to breathe rather than arrange — the genre this lab’s title names, at its purest.

Listen for
  • Percussive cycles that go in and out of step with the kick — pick one and time its lap.
  • The absence of sections: no breakdown, no drop, yet constant motion — where is the development stored?
  • How quiet the layers are individually; the mix is built so the relationships, not the sounds, are foreground.
After — verify

Where is the arrangement? In the phase relationships — the track is a set of unequal cycles left to interact, with mixing as gardening. That is this lab’s exact brief. Did you find a layer whose lap you could time? If not, relisten to four minutes following one hat figure only; its slow journey against the kick is the form, hiding in plain sight.

08 Mathew Jonson — “Marionette” sequences that lap the bar

A live-sequenced melody longer than the bar it rides on — the lead keeps overshooting the loop boundary and re-entering at new angles, which is why it never wears out across nine minutes.

Listen for
  • The lead line’s true length — longer than you first assume; wait for its actual return.
  • Where its accents land against the kick on successive laps — the same notes, new meanings.
  • What is performed (filter, level swells) versus what the lap structure provides for free.
After — verify

Why doesn’t a nine-minute track with one lead melody get old? Because the melody’s length and the bar’s length disagree — each lap re-harmonizes the same notes against the drums. One good length decision did the work of a dozen variations. If the lead felt repetitive, relisten and mark where its high note lands relative to the kick across four laps; it never lands the same place twice until the cycle closes.

09 Shackleton — “Blood on My Hands” ritual cycles, no four-four

Percussion loops of stubbornly individual lengths circling a vocal — polymeter used not for minimalist clarity but for ritual unease; nothing resolves on a bar line because the bar line never quite exists.

Listen for
  • The percussion layers’ disagreement about where “one” is — and that the disagreement is stable, not sloppy.
  • The bass drones holding the floor so the cycles above can stay unmoored.
  • How dread is manufactured: cycles that approach alignment and veer off.
After — verify

Why does it feel ritual rather than mathematical? Because the unequal cycles never display their arithmetic — no clean reunion is celebrated; alignment is approached and denied, and the denial reads as menace. Same machinery as Reich, opposite emotional sign. Your length choices set this dial: celebrate the reunion or withhold it. If you felt nothing, relisten louder in a dark room; this one is about the body.

10 Beatrice Dillon — “Workaround One” contemporary polymetric precision

From the 2020 album built on one strict tempo: dry, exact percussion cycles of unlike lengths, arranged with surgical clarity — polymeter as contemporary design language rather than hypnosis.

Listen for
  • The dryness: no reverb to glue the cycles, so every relationship is exposed and checkable.
  • Layers entering and leaving at structural points — curated polymeter, with a hand on the faders.
  • The single fixed tempo across the album’s logic — constraint as the enabler of all the play above it.
After — verify

What replaces hypnosis here? Legibility — the cycles are mixed so dry and sparse that you can audit each one, and the pleasure is in the audit. This is the modern restatement of the lab: unequal lengths plus radical clarity, no fog. Did you catch a layer leaving exactly when its cycle stopped contributing? Relisten for one exit; the curation is the human role your spec asks you to declare.

Replication lesson — lesson

Before touching gear, write a one-sentence hypothesis in the logbook naming what you stole from across these ten works — the snap into a new pattern at each lock, discrete jumps instead of glide, the ecology of many laps, length itself as the moving part, scarcity converting small changes into drama, tension sourced from human effort or from the lengths alone, relationships as the foreground, one lead lapping the bar, reunion withheld as menace, or polymeter made fully legible. The target is something like: choose two numbers well and the arrangement composes itself — the drift is the piece. Your sentence, in your words; the 64-bar prediction table is what will test it.

Track-lab principle

Phase is form: unequal lengths are a complete arrangement, unpacking at a speed you chose. Declare the clock, predict the reunion to the bar, and let the loops do the composing.
