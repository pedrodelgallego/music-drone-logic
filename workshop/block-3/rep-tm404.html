Replicate: TM404 — “TM404”

Copy the reference's working mechanism, not its style. Build a short original study with Live 12, Digitakt II, MicroFreak, and the MOTU M4, then A/B it against the reference at matched loudness.

The point

This drill teaches one thing: a timing rule. Here the rule is that long-form music can emerge from several short machine loops left to free-run against each other, rather than being arranged on a timeline. The reference gives you the clue; the build proves whether you understood it.

Before you start
  • Live 12 set saved as REP-TM404.
  • Digitakt II connected by USB for sync and by main outs to MOTU M4 inputs 3/4 — here it hosts several free-running acid and drum loops.
  • MicroFreak output connected to MOTU M4 input 1 — it is the acid/303-style line breathing against the drum boxes.
  • MOTU M4 headphones at the Lab 001 mark; Live tracks set to Monitor Off while recording hardware.
  • Reference on a Live audio track named REF, with Utility inserted for loudness matching.
  • What you will make: a 60-90 second original study where several short machine loops free-run against each other and the long-form shape emerges from their interaction, not from a timeline.
  • What you will turn off: the DAW arrangement view as a writing tool — no cut points, no automation lanes drawn in advance. You record a live pass instead.
  • What you will prove: the jam keeps mutating — never quite repeating — because the loop lengths differ and you ride the knobs by hand.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not a sequence you secretly arranged after the fact.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to one clear section of TM404 — “TM404”. TM404 is the alias of Andreas Tilliander, and the record is made from a stack of Roland-style bassline and drum machines — several 303-style acid lines, plus 606/808-style drums — clocked together and left to run. There is no DAW arrangement: the tracks are recorded as live jams, and the structure emerges from overlapping loop lengths and slow knob movements rather than from editing. The timing rule here is interaction: define short looping patterns on several machines, sync them, then perform variation by hand so the long-form shape emerges instead of being arranged. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is the smallest repeated behavior you can point to?
    Answer

    Several short patterns — an acid line, a drum loop, a percussion loop — each looping at its own length and re-triggering, so they drift in and out of alignment. Name the behavior, not the mood.

  2. Which device or path could create that behavior in your studio?
    Answer

    The Digitakt II hosting several short loops of different lengths plus the MicroFreak running a 303-style acid line, all clocked together and left to free-run. The mechanism lives in the interlocking loops and hand-ridden knobs, not in a drawn arrangement.

  3. What should stay boring so the mechanism remains audible?
    Answer

    The harmony and the patches. Keep one key and plain timbres. If you add chord changes or dramatic sound design, you will not be able to tell whether the interaction of free-running loops is carrying the piece.

  4. What would prove you copied the system rather than the style?
    Answer

    Record one long unedited take and never touch the arrangement view: if the music keeps evolving on its own from the interlocking loops, you copied the emergent jam. If you had to cut and arrange it to make it move, you copied the style.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II loop length: press [FUNC] + [PAGE] to open the per-track length / scale page, where you set each track's step count so loops differ and drift against each other.
  • Digitakt II sync: press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync. Use [YES] and the arrow keys to turn clock and transport send/receive on or off.
  • MicroFreak: use the Preset encoder to choose a 303-style acid voice, Type to choose the oscillator, Cutoff and Resonance for the filter you will ride, Matrix for modulation routing, and Save only after the jam behaves correctly.
  • MOTU M4: MicroFreak usually enters input 1. Digitakt II main L/R enter inputs 3/4. Inputs 3/4 have no front-panel gain knobs, so set their record level from the Digitakt II Main Volume.
  • Sound target: a few short acid and drum loops free-running together — no DAW arrangement in the room.
  • Recording target: peaks around -12 dBFS in Live before any processing.
  • Copy target: a solo flip between REF and your print makes the shared never-quite-repeating jam behavior obvious.
  • Variation rule: change one variable per pass — a loop length, a filter sweep, or an accent — and write it down.

Phase 1 · ~20 min

Put the reference in Live and write the hypothesis

Do this
  1. Drop the reference onto a track named REF.
  2. Loop the clearest 30-60 seconds — pick a stretch where you can hear several loops mutating against each other.
  3. Add Utility to REF and lower it until it is comfortable.
  4. Write: “The mechanism is ___.” Use the listening tag if you need a first guess: several free-running machine loops of different lengths interlocking into an emergent jam.
  5. Write the one thing you will not copy: the exact 303 timbre, the precise notes, the key, or the mix polish.
  6. Create tracks named SOURCE, SYSTEM, and PRINT.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from TM404 — “TM404” and use software synths, looping MIDI clips of different lengths, and clip envelopes.
  2. Build the phase goal — Put the reference in Live and write the hypothesis — as the smallest Live set that proves several free-running loops of different lengths interlocking into an emergent jam.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects whether the loops keep mutating on their own.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: translate Put the reference in Live and write the hypothesis into several tracks of differing length, sound locks, trig conditions, LFOs, macros, and Overbridge or main-out recording.
  2. Program the patterns so they demonstrate an emergent jam from interlocking free-running loops, not just the surface of TM404 — “TM404”.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase — the acid filter cutoff on the macro encoder — and name it before recording.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

You have a looped reference, three working tracks, and one written hypothesis before any programming starts.

Phase 2 · ~35 min

Sync the boxes and let the jam run

Build the smallest system that can prove the hypothesis. A few short loops of different lengths, synced, then left to run while you ride the knobs. The point is to make the interaction do the long-form work, not to finish a track.

Do this
  1. Press [PTN], choose an unused pattern slot, and press [PLAY]. If you hear anything, stop and choose another slot.
  2. Set sync first: [SETTINGS] → MIDI Config → Sync so the Digitakt II is the clock master and the MicroFreak follows. Confirm both start together.
  3. On the Digitakt II, program a short drum loop on one track and a short percussion loop on another. Use [FUNC] + [PAGE] to give them different step lengths — say 16 and 12 — so they drift.
  4. [TRK] + [TRIG 3] selects a MIDI track routed to the MicroFreak; place a short 303-style acid line at yet another length — say 15 steps.
  5. Do not open the arrangement view. Press [PLAY] and let all three loops free-run. Listen for the point where they fall out of phase and the jam stops repeating.
  6. Now ride the knobs: sweep MicroFreak Cutoff and Resonance, nudge an accent on the drums, raise and drop the acid line. This hand performance is the arrangement.
  7. Change one variable until the jam keeps evolving on its own — different loop lengths make it never quite repeat. Do not solve a movement problem by drawing automation.
  8. Record one continuous pass with the knob rides obvious, then one pass with all loops equal length and hands off — hear how the jam locks and dies when the mechanism is flattened.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from TM404 — “TM404” and use software synths and looping MIDI clips of different lengths.
  2. Build the phase goal — Sync the boxes and let the jam run — as the smallest Live set that proves interlocking free-running loops carrying the jam.
  3. Give each MIDI clip a different loop length so they phase against each other, and ride a filter or send by hand instead of drawing automation — the live performance is the engine.
  4. Record or resample one continuous pass to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not arrange it on the timeline.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects the felt mutation.

Phase 3 · ~15 min

Route the studio so the print is clean

Make the signal path boring and dependable. If the recording path is confusing, you will misdiagnose the mechanism.

MicroFreak · input 1 + Digitakt II · inputs 3/4 → MOTU M4 → USB → Live 12 · Monitor Off
Do this
  1. Plug MicroFreak into input 1 — it carries the acid line. Set the input gain while watching Live.
  2. Plug Digitakt II left/right into inputs 3/4 — it carries the drum and percussion loops, so expect a full signal here.
  3. Set hardware tracks to Monitor Off. This prevents doubled monitoring.
  4. Arm both record tracks and check peaks together. Aim for -12 dBFS, not “as loud as possible.”
  5. If either source is too quiet or too hot, adjust its level or input 1 gain and write the position down.
  6. Record one continuous 60-90 second pass of the free-running jam — no stopping to edit.
  7. Press ⌘ J to consolidate the best section into a clean clip named PRINT-A.

Phase 4 · ~20 min

Make the system map and the A/B proof

Draw the mechanism, then test the print against the reference. The map is not decoration; it is how you find what failed.

Do this
  1. Put REF and PRINT-A on adjacent tracks.
  2. Use Utility to match loudness before judging.
  3. Listen to 10 seconds of REF, then 10 seconds of PRINT-A.
  4. Write one sentence: “The first thing that gives mine away is ___.”
  5. Fix only that one thing and record PRINT-B.
  6. Keep both prints. The difference between A and B is the lesson.

Final check

Play the reference and your best print at matched loudness, then answer these in the logbook:

  • What mechanism did you copy?
    Answer

    Name the mechanism, not the genre: several free-running machine loops of different lengths interlocking, hand-tweaked into an emergent jam with no DAW arrangement.

  • Where does that mechanism live in your setup?
    Answer

    Point to it: the synced Digitakt II loops of differing lengths plus the MicroFreak acid line, recorded as one continuous live pass — not a sequence drawn in the arrangement view.

  • What did you deliberately not copy?
    Answer

    The answer should be material: the exact 303 timbre, the precise notes, the key, or the mix polish. The workshop copies systems, not identity.

  • What rule carries into the related lab?
    Answer

    Write it as a usable instruction. Example: “If I need the arrangement view to make a part move, I have not yet let the free-running loops do the work.”

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • System diff sentence: “I thought the system worked like ___; after building it, I discovered it worked like ___.”
