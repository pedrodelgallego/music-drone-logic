Block 3 · Lab 009 · Track lab

Micro-Variation & Groove

One bar of your own sampled hits, kept alive for six to eight minutes by decisions smaller than a sixteenth note: parameter locks, swing, micro-timing shifts, and velocity that behaves like a hand. The loop is the canvas; the micrometer is the music.

Track lab · S2 · D3 E2 M2 F2 · minimal · full cycle · new concepts: 5

listen → hypothesis → draw the path → calibrate → build → fault → diagnose → print → delta
Start here
  • Work the ten listening references at the bottom of this page first; the hypothesis they produce is the start of the build.
  • Sample your own hit kit — MicroFreak percussion patches or struck objects through the M4 — into the Digitakt before any sequencing; this is an M2 lab.
  • Save a new set as LAB-009-groove; keep the lab-001 gain ledger and the lab-002 loopback number on the desk.
  • Complete the gaps in both specs — this is an S2 lab: most fields are provided, the blanks are yours.
  • Expected time: one full cycle — two to three weeks of part-time sessions.
The point

A loop does not groove because of what repeats — it grooves because of what refuses to repeat exactly. Swing, velocity, and parameter locks are differences of milliseconds and decibels, and those differences are the entire composition.

How this lab works

Build a kit you own

  • Six to eight one-shot hits, self-sampled: MicroFreak percussion, struck objects, anything through the M4.
  • Trim, normalize per the ledger, name, and file — the kit is a deliverable.
  • Test: each hit readable at a glance on the Digitakt screen and by ear in solo.

Write one bar, then vary it microscopically

  • One pattern, ≤4 Digitakt tracks sounding at once; the variation budget is p-locks, swing, micro-timing, and velocity — never new notes.
  • Every variation is a written decision: which step, which parameter, by how much, why.
  • Test: mute the variations and the pattern goes dead — that deadness is your measurement.

Prove the time, then print

  • Declare the master clock, predict bar 64, record one drift test, write the reset strategy.
  • Record long takes into Live with mutes and p-lock changes performed by hand.
  • 6–8 minutes, fully original — the hits are yours, the grid is interrogated, nothing is copied.
Pass standard
Minimum passAudible original piece, routing + timing diagrams, fault entry, print map, and system diff exist.
Solid passA listener can feel the difference between the flat bar and the worked bar in a blind A/B — and you can name which micro-decision did it.
Advanced transferYou can take an unfamiliar dead loop and bring it to life in ten minutes using only swing, velocity, and p-locks — and predict which move will matter most before touching it.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
Parameter lock (p-lock)A setting change that belongs to one step instead of the whole track — hold the step, turn the knob, and only that step remembers. Like writing a margin note on one word of a sentence instead of re-writing the sentence.
SwingDelaying every second sixteenth note by a chosen amount so the grid leans instead of marching. A limp built on purpose: 50% is a robot, 70% is a strut.
Micro-timingNudging a single hit a few milliseconds ahead of or behind its grid line. Too small to hear as "early" or "late," big enough to feel as push or drag — the drummer's accent, measured in thousandths of a second.
Velocity humanizationMaking repeated hits land at slightly different strengths, the way no hand ever strikes twice identically. On a sampler, velocity only matters if it is wired to something — level, filter, decay — so humanizing is a routing decision, not a checkbox.
Clock driftTwo devices that each keep their own time slowly disagreeing, like two watches set together this morning reading differently tonight. Inaudible per bar, unmistakable by bar 64 — which is why this block makes you measure it instead of trusting it.
parameter lock swing micro-timing velocity humanization clock drift

2 · Bench preflight

Sampling pass — build the kit

MicroFreak / object → M4 in 1 → USB-C → Live (trim, name) → transfer → Digitakt tracks 1–8

Performance pass — Digitakt is the clock, Live is the tape

Digitakt main out L/R → 2 × TRS → M4 in 3/4 → USB-C → Live (record) → M4 out → phones
Pre-flight — before the first take
  • Gain ledger applied at M4 in 1 (sampling) and in 3/4 (performance) — set by the number, not by ear
  • Recording plan written: flat-bar reference take · drift test take (64 bars) · performed long takes — in that order
  • Timing diagram drawn: Digitakt (master) · Live (free) · MicroFreak (sampled, no clock role) — arrows and their absence both labeled
  • Warp OFF on Live's record tracks — Live must not "fix" the micro-timing this lab is made of
  • Swing value and every micro-timing nudge written in the logbook as numbers — "feels better" is not a measurement
  • Phones at the mark · a break planned every fifteen minutes

4 · Diagnosis & report

Symptom → cause → fix
SymptomLikely causeFix
The groove dies the moment it is recorded into LiveWarp engaged on the record track — Live quantized your micro-timing back onto its own gridDisable warp on record tracks; re-record; compare waveforms at a swung step to confirm the nudge survived
Hits flam against Live's grid by bar 64Two clocks, no sync — Digitakt and Live each keep their own time (this lab's injected fault)Measure the offset at bar 1 and bar 64, compute drift per bar, then either declare Live a free tape (edit by ear) or sync and re-test
Swing audible on hats but the pattern still marchesSwing only moves off-grid sixteenths — kick and snare on downbeats are untouchedGive the swing something to act on: ghost hits on swung steps, or micro-timing nudges on the snare
Velocity variation inaudibleVelocity is not wired to anything — the sample plays at full level regardlessMap velocity to level, filter, or decay in the track's setup; re-test with two extreme velocities before trusting subtle ones
The fault ritual — drift, on purpose

Predict the flam in milliseconds at bar 64 with Live unsynced → create the fault: record 64 bars with both transports started by hand → observe the waveform against Live's grid at bar 1 and bar 64 → diagnose which device ran fast and by how much per bar → fix it: declare one master (sync over USB MIDI clock, or accept the free-tape model and say why) → write the rule learned in the logbook as one sentence.

The full report bundle — ten items, filed in the logbook
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, master peaking −3 dBFS, 6–8 minutes
  • Routing diagram and timing diagram — drawn before patching, corrected after; the diff is the lesson
  • Recording plan — flat bar, drift test, performed takes; what was printed, when, at what stage
  • Patch notes — the kit list, the swing value, every micro-timing nudge and p-lock that mattered, as numbers
  • Session notes — including the flat-bar vs worked-bar A/B verdict
  • Reflection answers, including the listening comparison — which reference contributed what to the hypothesis
  • One removal decision — what was cut from the finished piece and what cutting it did
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule (faultType: drift)
  • Print map — what exists as audio, pattern data, or device memory; what is reproducible and what is gone
  • System diff sentence — "I thought the system worked like X; after building it, I discovered it worked like Y."
  • Minimum pass gate: audible output · routing diagram · fault entry · print map · system diff. Without these five, the lab is incomplete regardless of audio quality — a musically strong piece cannot bypass the engineering spine.

Listening — the hypothesis source

Ten grooves, from Detroit machines to a Lagos drum chair, each answering the same question a different way: when almost nothing changes, what makes it move? Each gets a brief — what to listen for before you press play, and how to check yourself after.

01 Ricardo Villalobos — “Dexter” micro-variation as the lead

Almost nothing happens for nine minutes, and every bar is different — the micro-detail is not decoration on the track, it is the track.

Listen for
  • Pick the hi-hat layer and follow it for two minutes — count how many bars pass before it repeats exactly. You may not find one.
  • The bass figure’s timing against the kick: it sits slightly off, and the offset itself breathes.
  • How sections are marked — no riser, no drop, just a slightly different density of small decisions.
After — verify

Did the track have an arrangement? Yes — but it is built from variation density, not from new elements: the “chorus” is where the micro-decisions cluster. If you heard it as static, relisten to any 16 bars and write a tally mark for every change you catch; most people run out of paper before bar 8.

02 Studio 1 — “Rot” the grid stripped to decisions

Wolfgang Voigt’s untitled color series: minimal techno reduced to a handful of elements per track, so every tiny filter move and level shift is fully exposed — nowhere to hide a lazy decision.

Listen for
  • Count the elements — most tracks run on three to five sounds, total.
  • What carries the six minutes: not new sounds but one parameter moving on one element at a time.
  • The off-beat chord stab’s placement — exactly how late it sits, and that it never moves from there.
After — verify

With so few elements, what kept you listening? The single moving parameter — your ear locks onto whichever sound is changing and treats it as the soloist. If you got bored, relisten and consciously assign your attention to the changing element instead of waiting for a new one; the track assumes you will.

03 Robert Hood — Internal Empire minimal as funk discipline

The blueprint for minimal techno that still moves like funk — patterns cut to the bone, with the swing and accent doing the work a bassline would do elsewhere.

Listen for
  • The accents inside the hat patterns — which sixteenths are loud, and how that choice creates the funk.
  • How short each sound is: nothing rings; the groove lives in the gaps.
  • One track held against another: the same vocabulary, a different accent map — the accent map is the composition.
After — verify

Where does the funk live, given that the sounds are almost neutral? In the accent pattern and the spaces — move the loud sixteenth one slot and you would have a different track. If you could not isolate the accents, relisten to one track and tap only the loud hits; the shape your finger learns is the composition.

04 Theo Parrish — “Solitary Flight” hand-swing at machine tempo

House built on a drum machine but swung like a hand — the timing leans so far it is almost falling over, and that lean is the signature.

Listen for
  • How far behind the grid the hats and chords sit — drag your own finger taps to match.
  • The repeats that are not quite identical: levels and lengths breathing across bars.
  • Whether the drag stays constant or deepens as the track settles in.
After — verify

Could a quantized session reproduce this feel? Not at 50% — the lean is the identity, and it is a chosen, extreme swing value plus level breathing, not randomness. If you felt it as “sloppy,” relisten and notice the drag never wavers: sloppiness is inconsistent, swing is a discipline.

05 Moodymann — “I Can’t Kick This Feeling When It Hits” the loop with a pulse rate

A sampled loop ridden like an instrument — filter, level, and mutes performed by hand, so a fixed loop acquires a pulse rate it never had on the source record.

Listen for
  • The moments the loop ducks, opens, or loses a layer — those are hands, not edits.
  • What stays bit-identical underneath (the sample) versus what never repeats (the riding).
  • How long he dares to let the loop run untouched before the next move.
After — verify

What was varied, given that the sample itself never changes? The treatment: filter, level, mutes — variation applied around a fixed object. That is exactly this lab’s grammar: the pattern is fixed, the micrometer moves. If you missed the hands, relisten and mark every event you are sure is not in the source loop.

06 Model 500 — “No UFO’s” machine funk, the origin

Juan Atkins proving in 1985 that a drum machine and a sequencer could be funky on their own terms — not by imitating a drummer, but by making the machine’s precision swing.

Listen for
  • The bassline’s syncopation against the four-on-the-floor — the funk is in the note placement, not the timing looseness.
  • Accent patterns on the machine drums: which hits are hard, which are ghosts.
  • How mechanical precision reads as intent rather than stiffness here.
After — verify

Is this track swung? Barely — the groove comes from syncopated placement and accents on a tight grid, the opposite strategy to Theo Parrish’s drag. Two opposite answers to the same problem; your lab must choose its position between them. If you heard them as the same kind of groove, relisten to both back to back at matched volume.

07 Drexciya — “Andreaen Sand Dunes” touch programmed into electro

Detroit electro where the machines sound played — stabs and percussion placed with a performer’s asymmetry, then locked and repeated by the sequencer.

Listen for
  • The percussion hits that land off the expected slot — and then land off it identically every bar.
  • Dynamics inside the synth stabs: repeated notes at unequal strengths.
  • The difference between this and a quantized preset beat — name one concrete placement that a default grid would never produce.
After — verify

Played or programmed? Both — the asymmetry was performed or deliberately placed once, then the machine repeats it exactly, which is precisely what a p-lock is. The lesson: humanization is not randomness, it is a specific irregularity, committed to. If everything sounded grid-perfect, relisten to the percussion alone and find the hit that always comes early.

08 Tony Allen — Jealousy the human pole of micro-timing

Afrobeat’s drummer with Africa 70 — one human kit doing everything this lab does with p-locks: accent, displacement, and velocity as a continuous living grammar. The calibration reference for what your machine is imitating.

Listen for
  • The hi-hat: never two strokes at the same strength — listen to one bar as eight separate decisions.
  • The snare ghost notes between the accents — felt as texture until you listen for them, then suddenly everywhere.
  • What stays fixed across minutes (the pattern’s skeleton) versus what never fixes (every stroke’s weight).
After — verify

What does Allen vary and what does he hold? The pattern holds; the weights never do — a fixed skeleton with continuously varying velocity, which is the exact target of this lab’s velocity work. If the kit sounded “even” to you, relisten to thirty seconds of hi-hat only; the unevenness is the instrument.

09 Isolée — “Beau Mot Plage” micro-edits as groove

Microhouse where the groove is assembled from tiny cuts — guitar slivers and clicks placed at edit resolution, so the editing itself swings.

Listen for
  • The guitar fragments: how short they are, and how their start points stagger against the beat.
  • Sounds that function as percussion but are clearly not drums — clicks, pops, sliced vowels.
  • How the track builds momentum with no element longer than a syllable.
After — verify

Where is the groove stored in this track? In the placement of the cuts — the material is almost incidental; move the slice points and the funk evaporates. That is p-lock thinking applied with a razor. If you heard a “guitar riff,” relisten: there is no riff, only fragments arranged to imply one.

10 Konduku — “Kıran” contemporary minimal percussion

Contemporary minimal from Ruben Üvez: spacious percussion patterns that drift and shimmer at low intensity — proof the micro-variation tradition is alive and does not require a four-on-the-floor.

Listen for
  • Where the pulse actually is — the kick is not the metronome here; something quieter is.
  • Percussion hits whose timbre shifts slightly on each recurrence — p-lock-style variation on tuned drums.
  • The dub space around each hit: how emptiness is used as a groove element.
After — verify

What kept the time once you noticed the kick was not doing it? A quieter recurring element — the track delegates the clock to the background, which frees the foreground to vary. If you lost the pulse entirely, relisten and tap along to whatever survives every bar; that survivor is the real clock, and finding it is a Block 3 skill.

Before touching gear, write a one-sentence hypothesis in the logbook naming what you stole from across these ten records — variation density as arrangement, the exposed single parameter, the accent map as composition, drag committed to as identity, riding a fixed loop, syncopation on a tight grid, irregularity locked and repeated, the skeleton fixed while every weight moves, groove stored in cut placement, or the clock delegated to the background. The target is something like: groove is a set of small decisions, repeated exactly — design the decisions, not the notes. Your sentence, in your words; the flat-bar vs worked-bar A/B is what will test it.

Track-lab principle

The grid is free; the groove is engineered. Every millisecond of push, every decibel of accent is a decision the listener feels and never hears — make them on purpose, write them down.
