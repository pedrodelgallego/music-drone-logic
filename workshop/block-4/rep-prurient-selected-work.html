Replicate: song structure on noise

Copy one passage of Prurient. Take harsh noise material and gate it into song-like sections — a synth-pop frame holding power electronics — then compare to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: power-electronics noise is married to a song's architecture — verse/chorus-style sections, clear arrivals and drops — so noise material is held inside a pop structure instead of just running freely. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordPrurient — Frozen Niagara Falls / Bermuda Drain
The effectBrutal noise that nonetheless has verses, choruses, hooks and arrivals — it feels like songs, not just walls
The mechanismPower-electronics noise gated into sectional, song-like structure; the harsh material is shaped by pop architecture — sections, builds, drops

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • MicroFreak on Live's input for both the noise and a synth element, Digitakt II synced for a pulse if the section has one
  • Pen and paper to map the section order: intro / verse / chorus / drop — the song frame you will pour noise into
  • What you will make: a short replica of a Prurient passage. Focus on this effect: harsh material that nonetheless has song sections.
  • What you will turn off: the temptation to let the noise run continuously — the structure is the point, so the noise must obey sections.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a sectional/verse structure imposed onto noise material.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are the noise sources or synth patch, not a formless wall with no sections.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Prurient. Choose the clearest passage with sections. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Can you hear sections — places where the music clearly changes "part"?
    Answer

    Yes. Despite the harshness there are clear blocks — a synth verse, a noise chorus, a drop — that arrive and depart like song sections. The form is borrowed from pop; only the material is extreme. That sectioning is the move you are stealing.

  2. What happens to the noise at a section boundary?
    Answer

    It gets gated — cut on or off, or swapped — at the boundary, instead of bleeding through. The noise obeys the structure: it slams in at a chorus and drops out for a verse. The clean edits at the seams are what make it read as a song.

  3. Is there anything melodic or pop-like holding it together?
    Answer

    Usually — a synth line, a beat, or a sung/spoken vocal that carries the song frame while the noise is the texture. The pop element is the skeleton; the power electronics is the skin. You need both.

  4. How do the dynamics map to the sections?
    Answer

    Choruses are the loud, saturated arrivals; verses pull back to synth or quiet. The loudness follows song logic — build, arrive, drop — so the noise's intensity is sequenced by the structure rather than constant.

Now build the passage you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target: noise material gated into clear song sections, with a synth/pop element carrying the frame.
  • Elements: the section map (intro/verse/chorus/drop) and what plays in each, charted from the REF.
  • Gating: the noise obeys the section boundaries, like theirs.
  • Arrival: the chorus lands as a loud arrival where the record's does.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • MicroFreak: make two patches — a harsh noise/overdriven patch for the choruses and a cleaner synth line for the verses. The contrast between them is what gives the sections their identity.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates audio tracks; arrange sections as clips on a timeline so boundaries are exact. Use a Gate or hard clip edits to cut the noise on and off at section lines, and clip volume/Utility for the build-arrive-drop dynamics. ⌘ J consolidates a finished section.
  • Digitakt II: if a pulse carries the song, sequence it and use pattern changes or mutes to mark the verse/chorus boundaries so the structure is locked to the grid.
  • Recorder or phone: set gain before the take; capture the whole passage so all sections and their transitions are in one continuous take.

Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Build the noise material and the pop frame

Phase focus: prepare the two ingredients — harsh material and a song skeleton. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track, loop the passage, and gain-match with a Utility.
  2. Build a harsh, saturated noise patch for the loud sections.
  3. Build the pop frame — a synth line, a beat, or a vocal scrap — that will carry the song through the quieter sections.
  4. Check both against the REF: the noise as harsh as theirs, the frame as song-like as theirs.

  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects Brutal noise that nonetheless has verses, choruses, hooks and arrivals — it feels like songs, not just walls.

Expected result

A short written diff naming where your structure blurred and where the noise refused to obey a section.

Verify against the record

A/B your passage against the same stretch of Prurient — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Can a listener point to your sections?

Answer

If they can say "that's the verse, that's the chorus" the structure is doing its job and the mechanism worked. If it all blurs into one wall, the gating is too soft — the whole point is that song architecture is legible even through harsh material, so sharpen the seams until the form is unmistakable.

Does the chorus land as an arrival?

Answer

It should — the noise chorus is the payoff the verse sets up. The common miss is keeping the verse as loud as the chorus, so the arrival has nowhere to arrive from. Prurient pulls the verse back to make the chorus hit; if yours feels flat, deepen the contrast before the drop.
