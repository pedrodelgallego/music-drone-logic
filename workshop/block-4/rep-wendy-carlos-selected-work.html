Replicate: polyphony from mono voices

Copy one short contrapuntal passage from Wendy Carlos. Build each line as a separate monophonic take with its own timbre, record a short multitrack, and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: polyphony is assembled from one-note-at-a-time voices, and each voice is given a distinct timbre so the ear can track it through the texture. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordWendy Carlos — Switched-On Bach (1968) / Sonic Seasonings
The effectSeveral independent lines moving at once, each one clear and singable even when they overlap
The mechanismA monophonic synth plays one line at a time, multitracked into counterpoint; the timbre of each voice is chosen to clarify which line is which

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • MicroFreak on Live's input, Digitakt II synced (lab 002's clock setup) in case you want a bass voice from it
  • Pen and paper to write down the two or three lines you will track, and which timbre goes with which
  • What you will make: a short replica of a Carlos contrapuntal passage. Focus on this effect: several independent lines moving at once, each one clear and singable even when they overlap.
  • What you will turn off: any polyphonic patch, any chord pad, any reverb that smears the lines together before you have proven they are separable.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: polyphony built from layered mono voices, each with a distinct timbre and role.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are voice-leading choices or rig limits, not a single fat chord standing in for real counterpoint.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of a Carlos two- or three-voice passage. Choose the clearest short section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How many independent lines can you actually follow at once?
    Answer

    Usually two or three, and you can follow them because each one has its own colour. Try to hum the lowest line while a higher one moves — if you can hold one in your head against the other, they are genuinely independent, not a chord.

  2. What is different about the timbre of the top line versus the bass line?
    Answer

    The bass tends to be rounder and darker, the upper voices brighter or reedier. The contrast is not decoration — it is the thing that lets your ear assign each note to a line instead of hearing a blur. Carlos picks a patch per voice the way an orchestrator picks an instrument.

  3. When two lines cross or land on the same beat, can you still tell them apart?
    Answer

    Yes, and that is the test. At the moment of a crossing, pitch alone would fuse them; the timbre difference keeps them legible. If you lose one at the crossing, listen again — the colour you stopped tracking is the one whose patch is closest to its neighbour's.

  4. Does any single voice ever play a chord, or is each one strictly one note at a time?
    Answer

    Each voice is one note at a time — that is the constraint the original Moog imposed and Carlos turned into a method. The richness comes from stacking mono takes, not from a polyphonic patch. That constraint is exactly what you are stealing.

Now build the passage you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target: each line is a separate mono take with its own patch — no polyphonic shortcut, no shared timbre across voices.
  • Elements: two or three lines transcribed from the section by ear, lowest to highest.
  • Clarity: the timbre contrast that keeps their lines legible keeps yours legible too.
  • Crossing: wherever two lines cross in the record, both are still trackable in your take.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip. Use a separate track per voice so each take stays independent.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs. Keep the voice setting monophonic — one note per take is the point. Change the engine or filter between voices so each line has its own colour.
  • Digitakt II: if you use it for the bass voice, press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample, and play one note at a time so the line stays monophonic.
  • Recorder or phone: set gain before each take and do not touch it during the take, so all voices sit at honest relative levels.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~15 minutes

Transcribe the lines and assign a timbre to each

Phase focus: transcribe each line and decide its colour before recording anything. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track and loop the clearest 4–8 bars of the passage. Gain-match it with a Utility so comparisons are honest.
  2. Transcribe each line separately — lowest first — note for note: pitches and rhythm.
  3. Beside each line, write the colour you will give it: which is round and dark, which is bright, which is reedy. Each must be distinct.
  4. Audition one patch per voice and check it against the REF voice it represents — does it sit in the same register and have the same general brightness?
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Wendy Carlos — Switched-On Bach (1968) / Sonic Seasonings and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — Transcribe the lines and assign a timbre to each — as the smallest Live set that proves A monophonic synth plays one line at a time, multitracked into counterpoint; the timbre of each voice is chosen to clarify which line is which.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects Several independent lines moving at once, each one clear and singable even when they overlap.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: translate Transcribe the lines and assign a timbre to each into FM tracks, sound locks, trig conditions, LFOs, macros, and Overbridge or main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates A monophonic synth plays one line at a time, multitracked into counterpoint; the timbre of each voice is chosen to clarify which line is which, not just the surface of Wendy Carlos — Switched-On Bach (1968) / Sonic Seasonings.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.

Two or three written lines, each with a named, distinct timbre, ready to track one at a time.

Phase 2 — ~30 minutes

Track each voice mono, one at a time

Phase focus: build the polyphony by stacking mono takes. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

MicroFreak (mono, one patch per voice) → one audio track per line → master
Do this

Expected result

A short written diff naming exactly where your counterpoint reads as a chord and theirs reads as separate lines.

Verify against the record

A/B your passage against the same bars of the Carlos recording — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Can you follow each line in your take as clearly as in theirs?

Answer

Test it by humming one line over your own playback. If you can hold the bass against the moving top voice, the timbres are doing their job. The usual failure is the middle voice — it gets crowded from both sides, so if any line disappears it is normally that one, and the fix is to give it a sharper, more distinct colour.

At the crossing point, do both voices survive in yours the way they do in theirs?

Answer

They should — that crossing is the whole reason the mechanism exists. If your two lines fuse exactly where theirs stay split, the patches are too close in brightness; Carlos avoids this by making one voice unmistakably darker. Widen the contrast and re-track the brighter of the two.
