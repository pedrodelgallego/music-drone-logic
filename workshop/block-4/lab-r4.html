Rebuild the Decay

Take one degradation passage you analyzed in L4 and re-run it as a generation-loss process on your own material. Your material is necessarily different — your loop, your chords — so closeness here means the process: what each pass takes, in what order, at what rate. Where the decay curves match, your analysis was right; where they don’t, you have found what you can’t hear yet.

Replication lab · S3 · E3 M4 F1 · half-cycle — requires the L4 Samples session · filed as study, never portfolio · complete

Start here
  • Put the L4 Samples-session worksheet beside the machine; no worksheet, no R4.
  • Save a new set as R4-study.
  • Make or choose one short piece of your own material, then run it through the worksheet’s process — generation by generation, every pass printed and numbered.
  • Stop at the timebox. The corrected worksheet is the deliverable; the replica is evidence.
The point

You cannot re-decay Basinski’s tape — and that is exactly why this lab works. With the material removed from the comparison, everything left to match is the process: which stage does the damage, what each generation costs, whether the loss is even or accelerating. The decay is the analysis, made audible.

What “close” means here

In R2 you matched a gesture; here you match a process. Three things must land: the loss profile (what goes first — highs, transients, dynamics — and what survives, in the same order as the reference), the rate (your generation three should sit where their early passes sit, your generation ten where their late ones do), and the character of the damage (erosion or error — smooth wearing-down or clean fragments with violent edges, per your worksheet’s call). A stranger hearing both should recognize the same kind of dying, in different material.

How this lab works

Author the process spec

  • S3 — you write the process from your own worksheet, from prompts, not from a recipe
  • Name the path, the entry level per pass, and the predicted loss per generation
  • A row too vague to run is itself a finding

Run the generations

  • One pass = one printed, numbered render — never destructive, never skipped
  • The worksheet’s order and levels are the claim under test
  • Stop only at the timebox or the planned generation count

Name every miss

  • A/B the decay curve, not the material
  • File each miss under the layer it lives in
  • Correct the worksheet — that is what leaves the room

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
The processEverything about a decay that isn’t the material: the path each pass travels, the level it enters at, what each generation takes and adds, and the rate it all happens at. Two different loops run through the same process die the same way — that sameness is what you are rebuilding.
Generation mapYour printed passes, numbered: G0 is the source, G1 the first copy, and so on. The map is the experiment’s data — without numbered prints you cannot compare your generation three to their early passes, and the lab collapses into “it got worse.”
Loss profileThe order in which qualities die: highs before lows? transients before sustain? dynamics before pitch? Every degradation path has a signature order, and matching it — not the sound — is the closeness test.
Gear miss vs hearing missA gear miss is “my path can’t degrade that way” — a cassette deck erodes differently from thirty-year-old reel tape; a note for the rig. A hearing miss is “I didn’t notice that loss until I tried to produce it” — the real finding: the exact edge of your current ear.

1 · Target & analysis

The target is the degradation passage you traced in L4’s Samples session. Your source-trace worksheet is the blueprint: the source’s condition, the chain in order, each stage marked linear or nonlinear, the per-pass losses, the erosion-or-error call. This is an S3 lab: turn that worksheet into a written process spec before touching anything — which path, what enters at what level, how many generations, what you predict each one costs. If a row is too vague to run, sharpen it before you start. Finding out a row was unrunnable already counts — that is the lab working.

  • The firewall. Open a new project and name it R4-study. The replica is study evidence, never portfolio output: it is not published, never “finished,” and nothing from this session — no generation, no resample, no decayed loop — moves into portfolio work. The corrected worksheet is what leaves this room; the audio does not.
  • Worksheet only. No tutorials, no breakdown videos, no interviews about how the work was made. Someone else’s analysis would test someone else’s hearing. If you get stuck, go back to the record with the six questions — not to a search bar.
  • Your material, their process. The material restriction runs the other way here: you may not lift audio from the reference at all. The G0 source is yours — a phrase you record or synthesize on the rig, ideally with the reference’s general character (sustained, melodic, voiced) but never its content.
Before you start
  • The Samples-session worksheet from L4 on the desk, chain and per-pass columns filled
  • The reference passage available as a local audio file — a stream cannot be dragged onto a track
  • Your written process spec: path, entry level per pass, planned generation count, predicted loss per generation
  • A degradation path chosen and calibrated: the lab 017 tape loop, the lab 007 analog resample loop, or a digital damage chain — matching your worksheet’s erosion-or-error call
  • A new Live set saved as R4-study — the firewall starts at the project name
  • Phones at the lab-001 mark
  • Timebox: two sessions, three at most — write the end date down
Before building

Pre-commit the predictions

In the logbook, before you run any pass: what do you expect generation 3 to sound like, and generation 10? Which quality dies first, which survives longest, and is the loss even or accelerating? Writing the prediction down first is the only honest way to find out what you actually believed. The misses you failed to predict are worth double.

2 · Rebuild & verdict

Setup

The A/B rig

Same rig as R1 and R2: the reference on a track named REF, Warp off, gain-matched with a Utility; your numbered generations on tracks grouped as REBUILD; one solo press flips between them, serial, never layered. Because the target is a process, the comparison is staged: REF early passage against your early generations, REF late passage against your late ones. The setup recipe below covers two decay timelines of different lengths; use the same serial A/B discipline as R1.

Expected result

You can solo REF at any point in its decay, then solo the generation of yours that should match it, at the same perceived loudness, hands-free.

G0 (your source) → degradation path → print G1 → G1 re-enters the path → print G2 … Gn REF (Utility, gain-matched) → solo A/B against G1…Gn → master → phones

Each generation is printed, numbered, and kept — the row of prints is the experiment. Only one track is ever audible at a time; layering hides exactly the losses you are listening for.

  1. Timebox first. Two sessions; three at the absolute most. The replica is finished when the timebox ends, not when it is perfect. The end date is already in the logbook: when it arrives, render and stop, mid-generation if necessary.
  2. Print G0. Record or synthesize your source phrase — yours alone — and print it as the numbered zero generation, with its level written down. Every later claim is measured against this print.
  3. Run the generations. Each pass follows the process spec: same path, same entry level, printed and numbered before the next pass begins. No pass is destructive, none is skipped, and nothing is “touched up” between generations — the process must do all the work.
  4. Log per pass. One line per generation: what died this pass, what survived, anything the prediction didn’t expect. Three words per line is enough; the pattern across lines is the data.
  5. A/B at matched loudness. Thirty seconds REF, thirty seconds of the matching generation, back and forth — never both at once. Listen for one dimension per pass: loss profile only, then rate, then the damage character, then levels.
  6. Write the miss list. Every audible difference in the process, each assigned to the layer where it lives — use the table below. The layer matters more than the miss: it tells you which worksheet question you answered wrong.
  7. Correct the worksheet. Re-answer the rows the rebuild proved wrong — strike the old claim, write the corrected one beside it, including every loss that turned out to have a different cause, order, or rate. The corrected analysis is the deliverable that outlives the replica.
  8. Close and quarantine. Render the generation map into the logbook as study evidence, close the project, and don’t reopen it for portfolio work. Carry the miss list forward: it is C3’s sharpened hypothesis about what transformation actually costs.

The detailed per-step recipes are included above: tape-loop handling, resample-loop calibration, decay-curve comparison method. Use them as the test sequence for the rebuild.

The difference is…LayerExample
A different kind of damage entirelyMaterialTheirs erodes smoothly; mine glitches at the edges — the erosion-or-error call was wrong, or the path doesn’t match the call
Same damage, wrong order of lossesSignalTheir highs die first and lows survive; my dynamics collapsed before anything dulled — a chain stage is missing or misordered
Same losses, wrong rateControlTheir decay creeps over forty minutes; mine is unlistenable by generation four — the entry level per pass is too hot, the per-pass claim was wrong
Same decay, lands differently in timeTimeTheir loop’s dropouts become a rhythm; my gaps fall anywhere — the loop length and the damage aren’t locked the way the worksheet claimed
Decision check — gear miss or hearing miss?

For each item on the miss list: did the process fail because the rig can’t degrade that way — fresh cassette stock will not flake like thirty-year-old reel tape, a clean converter will not wow — or because you didn’t notice that loss until you tried to produce it? A gear miss is a note for the gear roadmap. A hearing miss is the gold — it is the precise edge of your current ear, located.

Pass gate — R4 complete when
  • The generation map — G0 through the final pass, printed, numbered, filed in the logbook as study
  • The written process spec it was run from, with entry levels per pass
  • The Samples-session worksheet it was rebuilt from, with corrections marked — cause, order, and rate
  • A/B notes at matched loudness, staged early-to-early and late-to-late
  • The miss list — each miss assigned to its layer, gear or hearing named
  • One sentence: what can you hear now that you couldn’t before?

No fault ritual, no full bundle — this is a half-cycle. If your material sounds nothing like the record’s, the lab can still pass: the process, not the material, is what is being judged.

Replication principle

The material was never yours to copy; the process always was. Rebuild the dying, not the thing that died.
