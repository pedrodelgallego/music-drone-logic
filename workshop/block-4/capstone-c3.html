Block 4 · Capstone C3 · Gate

Score for an Imagined Scene

Brief-driven sound design, transformation-heavy. You have labs 013–017, R4, and programme L4 behind you; this gate hands you one paragraph describing a scene that does not exist, and asks for the sound of it — built from your own material, shaped almost entirely by Block 4 transformations. The gate is not the score — it is whether you can explain the system that made it.

Capstone · Gate tier · constraints-only brief · full cycle · requires labs 013–017 + R4 + programme L4 · closes Block 4

What a gate is

A block does not end when its labs are done; it ends when you can run the operating loop without scaffolding. C3 is deliberately not a track: the scene removes the genre conventions you could have leaned on, leaving only material, transformation, and the four questions this block asks of every processor.

1 — Brief & constraints

Write — or have someone hand you — a one-paragraph scene brief: a place, a time, an event, and a change. Not a mood board, not a film clip — a paragraph. Then score it: 4–8 minutes of original sound that a stranger, given the paragraph, would recognize. Every sound starts as self-sourced material — your recordings, your synthesis, your captures from Blocks 1–3 — and almost everything the listener hears is that material transformed: resonated, saturated, granulated, sliced, degraded. The stolen principle you named during L4 is now due: it must appear structurally in the score, and you must point to it in the defense.

  • The scene brief. Four sentences are enough: where, when, what happens, what changes. Example shape — “An automated lighthouse, decades after the last keeper left. Mid-winter, before dawn. The rotation mechanism is wearing out. By the end, the lamp turns for the last time.” Author your own or take one from someone else; either way, freeze it in the logbook before any sound exists, because scoring a moving target proves nothing.
  • Transformation-heavy means: the interest of the score lives in what the chains do to the material, not in what the material is. If a sound appears untransformed, that must be a decision you can defend — the one clean thing in a degraded world is a compositional statement; ten clean things are a Block 2 relapse.
  • Scaffolding: C3 is S3 — author both specs from prompts. Write your own filled System Spec and Engineering Spec before patching anything; the four Block 4 questions (entry and exit level, linear or nonlinear, order, reversibility) must be answered in the Engineering Spec for every chain in the score.
  • Originality: the score is fully original portfolio work. Steal systems, never material — nothing from the R4 quarantine, nothing lifted from any reference.

Pick four constraints from the menu and write them at the top of your spec before anything else. The constraints are the brief’s other half; everything not forbidden is permitted.

  1. One source session. All material is captured or synthesized in a single recording session before any transformation begins; after that session, nothing new enters the system.
  2. Three sources maximum. Everything the listener hears descends from at most three original sounds — the family tree of every element must be drawable.
  3. One chain per scene element. Each element of the scene (place, event, change) owns exactly one transformation chain, and no element passes through another’s chain.
  4. At least one real generational process. One element must pass through the lab 017 tape path (or another genuinely generational loop) at least three printed generations — emulation does not count.
  5. The change in the scene is a change in the processing, not in the material. Whatever the brief says “changes,” it is rendered by moving a chain’s parameters or generation count — never by introducing a new sound.
  6. No reverb and no delay. Space and distance, if the scene needs them, must come from Block 4 tools: filtering, saturation, degradation, dynamics. (Block 5 will give you the real thing; this constraint proves you do not need it yet.)
  7. One processor order, flipped once. Somewhere in the score, the same chain appears in both orders (A→B, then B→A), and the flip is placed where the scene turns.
  8. Entry levels are composed. At least one nonlinear stage is driven by performed input-level moves — gain riding into the processor as the lead gesture, with the compensation documented.
  9. Nothing is reversible. Every transformation is printed destructive-forward: each stage is committed as audio before the next begins, and the session contains no live chains at the end.
  10. Silence is structural. The score contains at least two passages of full silence, each at least ten seconds, placed as scene events — not as fades.
Decision check

Do your four chosen constraints contradict each other anywhere? Three sources plus one chain per element plus no reverb is a hard corner — where will depth come from? Finding the contradiction before patching is part of the spec work; finding it during the build means the spec was incomplete.

2 — Bench delta

Gate capstones renew the bench certification. The bench has changed since C2 — a tape path entered the room at lab 017, and resampling loops have multiplied. The gate checks whether the map in your head still matches the room.

  1. Redraw the Studio Signal Map from memory — no peeking at the current version, no opening sessions. Every source, every gain stage, every monitoring path, and every degradation loop, as you believe it is. The tape 4-track and its record/playback levels must appear.
  2. Compare against the current filed version. Mark every discrepancy — a missing gain stage, a wrong monitoring assumption, a resample path you forgot exists. The diff is the lesson.
  3. File the redrawn map as the next version. The map re-versions at every capstone and every gear arrival; the current version is whichever one survives a redraw.
  4. Re-run the certification list below. Out loud or on paper, but completely.
Bench certification — renewed at C3
  • Draw the full studio path, including the tape record/playback loop and every resampling path
  • Identify every gain stage — and name which ones are nonlinear
  • State the entry and exit level of every processor chain in the score, from the gain ledger
  • Distinguish intended distortion from accidental clipping at any stage, by ear and by meter
  • For one chain, predict the result of a generation pass before running it — then run it and compare
  • If any item fails: return to lab 014 or 017 before continuing. The gate waits — there is no penalty for the detour, only for skipping it.

3 — Report & synthesis

C3 files the full report bundle — the same ten items as a track lab, with the capstone additions below. Capstones run one fault like any track lab: choose a gain-into-processing or cumulative-degradation fault — Block 4’s territory — and run the full ritual: predict the symptom, create the fault, observe, diagnose, fix, write the rule.

The full bundle — ten items
  • Rendered score — 24-bit WAV, peaking around −3 dBFS — filed with the frozen scene brief
  • Routing diagram — drawn before patching, corrected after; the diff is the lesson
  • Recording plan — what gets printed, when, at what stage; in this block, the generation map of every degraded element
  • Patch notes — the settings that mattered, including entry levels into every nonlinear stage
  • Session notes
  • Reflection answers, including the listening comparison — which L4 reference contributed what to the hypothesis
  • One removal decision — what was cut from the finished score and why
  • Fault entry — a gain-into-processing or cumulative-degradation fault: symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule
  • Print map — what now exists as audio, MIDI, automation, patch state, tape, or device memory; which generations are reproducible and which are gone forever
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the system worked like X; after building it, I discovered it worked like Y.”
  • Minimum pass gate: audible output · routing diagram · fault entry · print map · system diff. Missing any one of these means C3 is incomplete regardless of how the score sounds — a musically strong piece cannot bypass the engineering spine.

The seven-layer worksheet

Capstones only — one page, seven answers, written about this score. Each layer is a question you answer in a few honest lines.

Seven layers — answered for this score

The defense

Answered in the logbook — but out loud first, as if explaining the score to a colleague who will check your drawings against the session. Gate-scaled: short answers, no essays.

  1. Draw the final audio path from memory.
  2. Draw the control path separately.
  3. Identify the most fragile part of the system.
  4. Explain one failure and its fix.
  5. Explain what was printed, what remains live, and what cannot be recovered.
  6. Name the stolen principle and point to where it appears structurally in this score.
  7. Name one thing you removed and why.

Block synthesis

One paragraph in the logbook: what does Block 4 claim, in your own words — and where did your build disagree with it? The disagreement is not a failure of the block or of you; it is the most useful sentence you will write this cycle.

  • Passing the gate: bundle complete + certification renewed + defense answered. Then Block 5 — Space & Mix opens.
Gate principle

The gate is not the score; it is whether you can explain the system that made it — chain by chain, generation by generation.
