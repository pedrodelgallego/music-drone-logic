Block 4 — Transformation

How sound is altered

Resonance, saturation, granular reconstruction, slicing, and tape degradation — taught as level into transformation: what enters the processor matters as much as the processor. Year 2 opens here, and so does its hidden curriculum — every transformation is path-dependent.

Year 2 · Domain C · Labs 013–017 · Replication R4 · Programme L4 · closes at C3 (gate)

Engineering focus

Level into transformation. Every lab asks the same four questions of every processor: what level enters and what level leaves? Is the stage linear or nonlinear — does doubling the input double the output, or change its character? Does processor order matter — is A→B the same sound as B→A? And is the degradation reversible, cumulative, or generational — what remains after one pass, after three, after ten?

Working order

Do these in this order
  1. Programme L4 — run the three listening sessions first and name one stolen principle
  2. Lab 013 — ride one filter until its resonance is the lead voice
  3. Lab 013b — predict the spectrum from the FM ratio before you hear it; harmonic vs inharmonic by one number
  4. Lab 014 — stage saturation deliberately: same chain at three input levels, both processor orders
  5. Lab 015 — reconstruct material from grains until texture becomes harmony
  6. Lab 016 — slice your own recorded break and reassemble it by grammar, not grid
  7. Lab 017 — run degradation loops on real tape and make the path's history audible
  8. R4 — rebuild one degradation passage and verify the L4 analysis
  9. Capstone C3 — score an imagined scene; the gate out of the block
  • Gate capstone here. Block 4 closes at C3 — Score for an Imagined Scene: brief-driven sound design, transformation-heavy, constraints only. The gate is passed by explanation, not by the piece alone.
  • Gear at this block: a real magnetic tape record/playback path — a used Porta-class cassette 4-track, or any cassette recorder with a record head. Lab 017 needs generational degradation, not emulation; its arrival triggers a Studio Signal Map re-version.
  • Gear honesty — the tape strand. Be plain about which path you are on. The used cassette 4-track is the hardware path, and it is the intended one: real generation loss is physical and one-way, and lab 017 is written for it. When no tape machine is on the bench, the stated fallback is software tape emulation — Live's saturation and degradation chain (Saturator, Redux/Vinyl Distortion, a re-record loop) — used honestly as a stand-in, not pretended to be the real thing. Name in your logbook which path each degraded element actually took; an emulated pass and a printed tape generation are not interchangeable, and the difference is part of what you are learning to hear.

Listening programme L4 — material under stress

Session 1 · Records

Four ways material breaks

Four works in which the transformation, not the material, is the composition. Analyze each with the six-question frame, then name the contrast the worksheets are built around: erosion vs error.

William Basinski — “Disintegration Loop 1.1”erosion — analog generation loss
Aphex Twin — “Rhubarb”degradation held at the threshold of warmth
Steve Reich — “It’s Gonna Rain”the medium itself as the transform
Oval — “Do While”error — digital damage, curated
Listen for

What enters each system, and what does the path do to it per pass? Which works erode — losing a little of everything continuously — and which work by error: perfect, then suddenly wrong?

Session 2 · Samples

Deconstruction — trace one sample-based work to its sources

Pick one sample-based work and trace it backwards: what was the source before the chain, and what did each stage of the chain take from it or add to it? The worksheet becomes the blueprint for replication lab R4.

Session 3 · Video

Tape-music interviews and footage

Watched for the hands on the machines: how loops are cut and spliced, where levels are set going to tape, what is monitored during a pass, and what each generation makes unrecoverable.

  • Deliverable: written system analyses (six questions per reference) + one stolen principle — a structural idea, never material, named in advance and evaluated at C3. Full briefs and worksheets: programme L4
Voice — what are you refusing?

A recurring reflection seed, introduced here and carried forward in every block from this one on. Year 2 is where a voice starts to form, and a voice is defined as much by its refusals as by its moves. So end each cycle with one written line: what are you refusing? Which transformation, which gesture, which easy effect did you decline — and not because the lab forbade it, but because it is not yours? Keep the answers in one running list across blocks; the pattern that emerges is the first sketch of your voice.
