Replicate: montage shaped into gesture

Copy one dramatic passage from Tod Dockstader. Take a few raw recordings, shape each with an envelope, and montage them into a single directed gesture, then compare to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: found sound becomes drama through montage and envelope-shaping — material is placed, layered, and given an attack and decay so a heap of recordings reads as one rising-and-falling gesture. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordTod Dockstader — Apocalypse / Quatermass
The effectA dense, cinematic swell built from unrecognisable sounds, surging toward a peak and releasing
The mechanismTape-concrète montage of "found" sound, each layer envelope-shaped (attack/swell/release) and timed so the assembly reads as one dramatic gesture

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Three or four raw recordings on hand (field recordings, your own found sounds), loaded into Live or the Digitakt II
  • Pen and paper to sketch the gesture's shape — where it starts, where it peaks, where it releases
  • What you will make: a short replica of a Dockstader passage. Focus on this effect: a dense swell from raw sound that surges to a peak and releases.
  • What you will turn off: any musical pitch, beat, or recognisable instrument — the material is found sound, the form is gesture.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: montage plus envelope-shaping of raw recordings into a directed gesture.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are which recordings you used, not a flat collage that never builds to anything.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Dockstader. Choose the clearest dramatic passage. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Can you tell what any of the source sounds originally were?
    Answer

    Rarely — and that is intentional. Sounds are transposed, layered, and reshaped until their origin is lost and only their texture and energy remain. You are stealing the shaping, not the sources, so it does not matter what they were.

  2. Does the passage have a clear shape — a beginning, a peak, an end?
    Answer

    Yes. Despite the abstract material there is a dramatic arc: tension gathers, density and loudness build to a climax, then release. The arc is the composition; the found sounds are just what it is built from.

  3. How does a single layer enter and leave — abruptly, or shaped?
    Answer

    Shaped. Layers swell in and fade out with deliberate attacks and releases, like crossfades on tape. Even sudden hits are placed and contoured. The envelope on each layer is what turns raw recordings into musical gesture.

  4. What makes the peak feel like a peak?
    Answer

    Layers stacking up and loudness rising together — the density and amplitude both reach maximum at the same moment, then drop. The peak is engineered by timing the envelopes so several layers crest at once. That coincidence is what you will reproduce.

Now build the passage you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target: three or four raw recordings, each envelope-shaped, montaged so the whole reads as one rising-and-falling gesture.
  • Elements: the gesture's shape (start / peak / release) and roughly how many layers crest at the peak, sketched from the REF.
  • Shaping: every layer has a deliberate attack and release, like theirs.
  • Peak: your layers crest together where the record's do.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track per layer. Use clip volume automation or a Utility per track to draw each layer's attack/swell/release. ⌘ J consolidates the montage once it works. Transposition and reversing live in the clip's sample box for disguising sources.
  • Digitakt II: press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to load a recording, then use the amp envelope (attack/hold/decay) to shape how each one-shot enters and leaves. Sample start and pitch help disguise the source.
  • MicroFreak: optional — use it to add a synthetic layer (a swell, a rumble) that thickens the peak; envelope it the same way as the recordings.
  • Recorder or phone: set gain before the take and capture the whole gesture in one pass so the build and release stay continuous.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Gather and disguise the material

Phase focus: turn recognisable recordings into raw texture. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track, loop the dramatic passage, and gain-match with a Utility.
  2. Load three or four raw recordings — your own, not the record's. Transpose, reverse, or filter each until its origin is no longer obvious.
  3. Sort them by role: which is the bed, which is the surge, which is the hit at the peak.
  4. Audition each against the REF — does it sit in a similar textural register?
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Tod Dockstader — Apocalypse / Quatermass and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — Gather and disguise the material — as the smallest Live set that proves Tape-concrète montage of "found" sound, each layer envelope-shaped (attack/swell/release) and timed so the assembly reads as one dramatic gesture.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects A dense, cinematic swell built from unrecognisable sounds, surging toward a peak and releasing.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate Gather and disguise the material into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.

Phase 2 — ~30 minutes

Envelope each layer and montage the gesture

Phase focus: shape each layer and time them into one arc. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

raw layers → per-layer envelope (attack / swell / release) → montage timed to one peak
Do this

  2. Place the layers in time so their swells overlap and several crest together at the peak you sketched.
  3. Time the release so the gesture falls away after the peak rather than just stopping.
  4. Record the montage in one pass. A/B against the REF immediately.

Expected result

A short written diff naming where your gesture stayed flat and where the record's drama outpaced yours.

Verify against the record

A/B your gesture against the same passage of Dockstader — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Does yours read as one gesture, or as a pile of sounds?

Answer

If you cannot trace a single arc through it, the envelopes are not doing their job and it is a collage, not a gesture. The mechanism is proven when a listener feels the build and release as one shape even though the material is unrelated raw sounds — that unity comes entirely from the shaping and timing.

Does your peak feel engineered the way theirs does?

Answer

It should — the peak is where the most layers crest at the loudest point at the same instant. The common miss is layers that swell at slightly different times, so the climax smears and softens. If yours feels weaker than theirs, align the swells so they all reach maximum together.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: "Matched: …" · "Missed: … (best guess why)" · "Change next: …"
  • These three lines feed the full miss list in replication lab R4.
  • And one more line — which layer's envelope did the most to create the peak.
