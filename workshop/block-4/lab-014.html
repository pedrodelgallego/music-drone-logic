Saturation Staging

The canonical "gain is composition" lab. One chain of nonlinear processors, fed the same material at three input levels — and three different instruments come out. Then the chain is reordered, and a fourth appears. In linear land, level is housekeeping; the moment a saturator enters the path, level is writing. This lab makes that audible, measurable, and then musical: an industrial piece whose arrangement is drive amounts.

The point

Into a linear processor, level is just loudness. Into a nonlinear one, level is tone — the same saturator is a warmer, a thickener, or a destroyer depending only on what you feed it. Once you hear that, the gain knob stops being utility and becomes an instrument.

How this lab works

One chain, three levels

  • Build one fixed chain — e.g. EQ → Saturator → compressor — and never touch its knobs again.
  • Feed the same loop in at low (−20 dBFS), nominal (−12), and hot (−3); print all three.
  • Compare at matched loudness: the differences that remain are what the input level composed.

Order is a parameter

  • Swap the chain: filter → drive vs drive → filter, EQ before vs after the saturator.
  • Predict the difference in writing before each swap; print and verify.
  • Linear stages commute, nonlinear ones do not — hear where the rule lives.

A piece written in drive

  • An industrial track, 6–8 minutes, whose sections are defined by how hard each element hits the chain.
  • Pre-gain pushes, post-gain compensates — loudness stays level while intensity climbs.
  • One deliberate accident kept: the difference between intended distortion and a clip you chose to forgive.
Pass standard
Minimum passAudible original piece, routing diagram with levels at every stage, fault entry, print map, and system diff exist.
Solid passIn a blind A/B of your own renders at matched loudness, you can name which input level and which processor order produced each.
Advanced transferYou can hear a distorted record and say where in its chain the dirt most likely lives — before the filter or after, intended or accidental — and defend the guess in path terms.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
Linear vs nonlinearA linear processor treats quiet and loud signals identically — turn the input up 6 dB, the output goes up exactly 6 dB, same shape. A nonlinear one changes its behavior with level, like a polite conversation that becomes a different conversation when you shout.
SaturationGentle, progressive overload: the loudest parts of the wave get squashed and new harmonics appear, related to the input like overtones to a note. Tape, tubes, and the Saturator device all do versions of it — it is distortion with table manners.
Hard clippingThe cliff edge: a stage that runs out of numbers or volts and simply slices the wave flat. Converter clipping is the accidental kind — it sounds like splatter, carries no music, and lives at whatever stage hit its ceiling, which is rarely the stage you were looking at.
Pre / post gain compensationThe two knobs around every nonlinear stage: pre-gain decides how hard you hit it (tone), post-gain brings the result back to the planned level (housekeeping). Push one up, pull the other down — intensity changes, loudness does not. This pairing is the lab's core move.
Processor orderThe sequence of the chain. EQ into a saturator changes what gets distorted; a saturator into EQ changes what you keep of the distortion. With linear stages order is cosmetic; with one nonlinear stage in the path, order is composition.

Listening — the hypothesis source

Ten records that compose with overload. None of them is "badly recorded" — every one chose its distortion, staged it, and used level into a nonlinear path as a writing tool. Each gets a brief: what to listen for before you press play, and how to check yourself after.

01 Regis — “Gymnastics” drive as groove

Birmingham techno where the saturation is not on the drums — it is the drums: the overload glues kick, hat, and noise into one moving block.

Listen for
  • Where one element ends and the next begins — you mostly cannot tell, and that smear is the saturation summing them.
  • The hats: dirty but never harsh — the overload is staged before something that rounds it.
  • What "louder" means inside the track: sections intensify without the meter feeling like it moved.
After — verify

Was the distortion per-element or on the whole bus? Mostly the bus — the elements share one dirt, which is why they move as a block; per-track distortion would leave each with its own texture. That shared dirt is a print decision, not an effect choice. If you heard separate clean drums, relisten to the hats against the kick: their grit pulses with the kick, which means the kick is driving the stage they share.

02 Throbbing Gristle — “Discipline” the system run past its ratings

Live industrial at the origin of the word: every stage — mics, mixer, amps, the room — driven past its comfort zone at once, and the overload of the whole path becomes the music's authority.

Listen for
  • How many different distortions coexist: the vocal's, the synth's, the recording's — each stage clips with its own accent.
  • What stays intelligible anyway — the chant and the pulse survive every overload above them.
  • The crowd and room arriving through the same overdriven path as the band.
After — verify

Could you separate the performance's distortion from the recording's? Partly — the vocal distortion pumps with the shouting (performance), while a constant grain coats everything evenly (path). Hearing two layers of overload at once is the skill this record trains. If it read as one undifferentiated roar, relisten following only the vocal: notice its dirt moves while the backdrop's dirt holds still.

03 Cabaret Voltaire — “Nag Nag Nag” fuzz as the front door

Punk-era electronics passed through cheap fuzz until voice, synth, and rhythm box share one snarling surface — distortion as the band's unifying instrument.

Listen for
  • The vocal and the synth wearing the same fuzz — evidence they passed through the same box, or boxes set to agree.
  • What the distortion does to the drum machine's timing feel: edges blur, the grid stays.
  • Where the chorus lifts: more drive, not more elements.
After — verify

How did the track get bigger at the chorus without adding parts? Input level into the fuzz — harder in, more harmonics out, reads as "more band." That is pre-gain as arrangement, this lab's exact move. If you counted new instruments at the chorus, relisten and try to name one; you will mostly find the same parts, hotter.

04 SPK — “Slogun” overload as threat

Early industrial at its most extreme: screamed vocals and metal percussion driven so hard the path itself sounds dangerous — the emotional reading of distortion is calibrated by how far past nominal it sits.

Listen for
  • The difference between the percussion's distortion (struck metal, naturally rich) and the vocal's (electrically clipped).
  • Moments where the level momentarily drops and the path "recovers" — the cleanest seconds are the scariest by contrast.
  • Whether anything in the track is actually clean — find one element, if you can.
After — verify

What made this feel dangerous rather than just loud? The path audibly losing control — clipping that responds to the performance, so the system's distress mirrors the performer's. Calibration note for your build: this is the far end of the drive axis, and it is a choice, reproducible at safe monitoring levels. If it read as noise, relisten once at low volume and notice how composed the structure underneath is.

05 Einstürzende Neubauten — “Yü-Gung (Fütter Mein Ego)” metal into metal

Struck steel and power tools as the drum kit, recorded hot — the source material is already overtone-rich before any electronics, so every later stage multiplies what the metal began.

Listen for
  • Which clangs are acoustic overload (the metal itself ringing chaotically) versus electric overload (the chain clipping the recording).
  • The groove's precision underneath the chaos — the hits land machine-tight.
  • How the vocal sits: dry and forward, a clean reference that makes the dirt around it legible.
After — verify

Why keep the vocal comparatively clean? Contrast calibration — one clean element gives the ear a ruler to measure the surrounding distortion against; dirt on everything reads as mud, dirt against clean reads as power. Note that for your arrangement. If everything sounded equally dirty, relisten to verse one and rank vocal, percussion, and bass by grit — they are distinctly tiered.

06 Pan Sonic — Vakio distortion under a microscope

Finnish minimalism built from test-tone-like materials and precisely chosen overloads — saturation studied one stage at a time, the laboratory version of what industrial does in a riot.

Listen for
  • How clean the silence around each sound is — the distortion lives inside the elements, never on the bus.
  • Single sine-like tones that clip into buzz and back — you can hear the threshold being crossed and uncrossed.
  • The kick: a thump whose click is pure overload, placed like a drum tuned by drive amount.
After — verify

Where does the distortion live on this record — per element or on the whole? Per element, surgically: each sound carries its own exactly-dosed overload and the master stays clean, the opposite staging from Regis. Together they are your two poles. If you could not hear thresholds being crossed, relisten to any track's bass tone and wait for the buzz to switch on — that switch is the nonlinearity, naked.

07 Ancient Methods — The Jericho Records staged aggression

Modern industrial techno where the aggression is meticulously tiered — drums, chords, and voices each distorted by different amounts, the hierarchy of drive doing the work of a mix.

Listen for
  • The tiering: rank three elements in one track by how distorted they are — the ranking is stable and deliberate.
  • Transients surviving the wall: kicks keep their attack inside heavy saturation, which means the order was drive-then-shape, not shape-then-drive.
  • How sections change: drive amounts shift on existing elements more often than elements enter or leave.
After — verify

Does the heaviest element change from section to section? Listen again to one full track: the answer is usually yes — the drive hierarchy is re-ranked at each section, which is exactly "arrangement by gain." If sections seemed to differ by instrumentation, relisten and check whether the "new" sound is an old one pushed into a new tier.

08 Paula Temple — “Deathvox” contemporary wall engineering

Industrial techno from the 2010s: a distortion wall that would have been mud twenty years earlier, kept legible by modern staging — proof the craft advanced even where the aesthetic stayed brutal.

Listen for
  • Low end that stays defined under maximum aggression — the sub is protected from the distortion somewhere in the chain.
  • The wall breathing with the kick: sidechain or bus compression interacting with the saturation.
  • Hi-hats and air on top of the wall — something up there escaped the crush, by routing, not by luck.
After — verify

How is the sub still clean inside this much distortion? It is almost certainly split out — distorted mids and highs over a protected low band, a routing decision (band-split drive) rather than a knob setting. Your rig can do this with a crossover or parallel chain. If the whole spectrum seemed equally crushed, relisten on phones and focus below the kick's thump: the fundamental holds steady while everything above it seethes.

09 Merzbow — “Woodpecker No. 1” past the end of the dial

Japanese noise at total saturation: every stage clipping into every other until no source survives — the limit case that defines the axis your piece will sit somewhere along.

Listen for
  • Whether you can identify any source at all — what, if anything, survives total overload (rhythmic ghosts, register, gesture).
  • Variation inside the wall: it is not static; the texture churns, which means hands are moving gains somewhere.
  • Your own adaptation: after two minutes, the ear starts hearing detail in what first read as undifferentiated.
After — verify

Is there composition here? Yes — at full saturation the only remaining parameters are which stage clips and how the gains move, and those are audibly being played. This is the proof that gain riding is performance even when nothing else survives. If you heard only chaos, relisten to the first ninety seconds and track one register (just the lows, say) — it has an arc.

10 British Murder Boys — “Learn Your Lesson” restraint inside the wall

Regis and Surgeon together: industrial techno where the distortion is constant but the pressure is performed — the track teaches how much dynamic range still exists inside an apparently saturated sound.

Listen for
  • Intensity changes that happen with no new elements and no level jump — drive, EQ into the drive, or both.
  • The voice sample: how its intelligibility is rationed by how hard it is hit.
  • The end of the track versus the start: same loop family, different temperature — name what actually changed.
After — verify

Where does this track keep its dynamics if everything is already loud? Inside the nonlinearity: small pre-gain moves produce large harmonic changes, so the track breathes in density rather than in level. That is the headroom-free dynamics trick your piece should steal. If start and end sounded the same, relisten to the first and last minute only, back to back — the difference you hear was made almost entirely before the distortion stage.

Requirement

Your written hypothesis names how this piece will stage its distortion — bus dirt that glues, the whole path past its ratings, drive as the chorus, overload as threat, a clean ruler against the wall, surgical per-element dosing, a re-ranked drive hierarchy, the protected sub under the crush, gain riding as the last surviving performance, or density as dynamics — and what was stolen from the comparison. The target idea across all ten: where the level meets the nonlinearity is where this music is written. What you steal is a structure, never a sound.

2 · Bench preflight

The block rule, at full strength: a level written at the input and output of every processor in the chain — three times, once per input level. This grid of numbers is the lab's bench artifact; the piece is composed on top of it.

parent loop → −20/−12/−3 → pre-gain → level in → saturator → level out → post stage → comp. gain → bus → master −3 dBFS
Pre-flight — before any drive is musical
  • Parents recorded clean at ledger levels — the experiment is worthless if the input is already accidentally dirty
  • The chain built and its knobs frozen; a screenshot or photo of the settings filed
  • The level grid ruled out in the logbook: one row per stage, one column per input level — fill it during calibration
  • Loudness-matching method chosen (post-gain by meter, or a loudness meter on the bus) and noted
  • Order-swap plan written: which two stages swap, and your predicted difference, in writing, before printing
  • Phones at the lab 001 mark; matched-loudness A/B discipline agreed with yourself in advance

3 · Build

Build the lab in five passes. Each pass produces an artifact, not just a better-sounding session: a written rule, a printed comparison, a fault entry, and a corrected system sentence.

Build recipe · Saturation Staging

Make the transformation chain audible

Do this in order
  1. Print the source. Record one clean MicroFreak or self-sourced audio phrase into Live at about -12 dBFS. This is SOURCE and never gets overwritten.
  2. Build the chain. Duplicate SOURCE to CHAIN. Add only the processor family the lab studies: filter, saturation, grain, slice, or tape/generation loss.
  3. Level-match the chain. Put Utility before and after the chain. Pre level drives the processor; post level returns the result to the same apparent loudness.
  4. Print comparison passes. Print low, nominal, and hot versions, or first-generation and later-generation versions, as the lab requires.
  5. Run the fault. Overdrive the wrong stage or accumulate one bad pass on purpose. Diagnose by walking backward through the chain one stage at a time.

4 · Diagnosis & report

The full report bundle — ten items, filed in the logbook
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, master peaking −3 dBFS, 6–8 minutes
  • Routing diagram — the chain with the full level grid: in and out of every processor, at all three input levels
  • Recording plan — the comparison renders (three levels, both orders) plus the section stems
  • Patch notes — the frozen chain settings, the post-gain compensation values, and the one forgiven accident
  • Session notes — your tone descriptions per input level, in your own words
  • Reflection answers, including the listening comparison — which record contributed what to the hypothesis
  • One removal decision — what was cut from the finished piece and what cutting it did
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule (faultType: gain into processing)
  • Print map — which drive states exist as audio, which only as automation, and what is no longer reproducible
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the system worked like X; after building it, I discovered it worked like Y.”
  • The fault ritual: predict the symptom in writing, then create the fault — insert a +6 dB utility after the saturator where the plan said before, and separately push one parent until the converter (not the saturator) clips. Observe both, describe how intended distortion and accidental clipping differ in sound and on the meters, locate each at its stage, fix by moving the right gain, and write the rule learned.
  • Minimum pass gate: audible output · routing diagram · fault entry · print map · system diff. Without these five, the lab is incomplete regardless of audio quality.
Track-lab principle

Gain is composition: into a nonlinear chain, the level knob writes the timbre, the order writes the sentence, and the compensated loudness is what lets anyone hear it as music instead of as volume.
