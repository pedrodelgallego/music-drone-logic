Replicate: density as the only form

Copy one stretch of Hijokaidan's harsh noise. Build a saturated wall and shape it using density and dynamic surges alone — no pitch, no rhythm — then compare to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: total saturation is the material, and the only form-bearing parameters are density and dynamics. There is no melody and no beat to lean on — the shape is made entirely of how thick and how loud the wall is, moment to moment. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordHijokaidan — Romance
The effectAn overwhelming wall of noise that, despite having no notes or beat, clearly moves — surging, thickening, receding
The mechanismTotal saturation as a single mass; structure comes only from density (how many layers) and dynamic surges, never from pitch or rhythm

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • MicroFreak on Live's input, distortion/saturation devices ready; monitor at a safe level — the material is loud by nature
  • Pen and paper to chart the density/dynamics curve: where it thickens, where it surges, where it recedes
  • What you will make: a short replica of a Hijokaidan stretch. Focus on this effect: a wall that moves through density and dynamics alone.
  • What you will turn off: any pitched note, any rhythm, any musical hook — those are not the material and will read as cheating.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: density and saturation envelopes as the only form-bearing parameter.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are which noise sources you used, not a flat unchanging drone or a smuggled-in beat.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Hijokaidan. Choose the clearest stretch. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Is there any pitch or beat you can hold onto?
    Answer

    No — and that absence is the point. There is no note to hum, no pulse to tap. Everything your ear normally uses to track music is gone, which forces you to hear density and loudness as the structure instead. That is what you are stealing.

  2. If it is "just noise," how do you know it is changing?
    Answer

    By thickness and loudness. More layers pile in and it gets denser; the level surges and recedes. You feel the wall move even with no notes because density and dynamics are doing all the work melody and rhythm usually do.

  3. Does it ever drop out, and what does that do?
    Answer

    Occasionally it thins or dips, and the contrast is huge — after total saturation, even a small recession feels like a vast event. The dynamic surges only register because there are troughs to surge from; the wall is shaped, not constant.

  4. What gives the saturation its character — is it one source or many?
    Answer

    Many, piled on top of each other and overdriven together. The "wall" is several noise sources saturating into one mass, and the density is literally how many are active. Adding and removing layers is how you sculpt it.

Now build the stretch you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target: a saturated wall of several noise sources, shaped only by adding/removing layers and by dynamic surges.
  • Elements: the density/dynamics curve — where it thickens, surges, and recedes — charted from the REF.
  • No music: no pitch, no rhythm anywhere in the wall.
  • Contrast: the wall has troughs so the surges register, like theirs.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • MicroFreak: use the noise-capable engines and push resonance and modulation into self-oscillation; drive the output hard. Each setting is one noise layer of the wall.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates audio tracks — one per noise layer. Stack saturation/overdrive/distortion (Saturator, Overdrive, Amp) to fully overdrive each source. Use track volume and mutes to add/remove layers, and a master Utility to drive the surges. Density = how many layers are unmuted at once.
  • Digitakt II: optional — sample harsh noise and layer it, but do not let it impose a rhythm; the one-shots should overlap into continuous mass, not a beat.
  • Recorder or phone: set gain before the take and protect your ears and the input; the dynamic surges must survive, so avoid a limiter that flattens them.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Build the saturated wall

Phase focus: stack noise sources into one overdriven mass. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track, loop the stretch, and gain-match with a Utility.
  2. Build three or four noise layers — each a different texture (hiss, scream, low rumble) — and overdrive each into saturation.
  3. Unmute all layers and confirm they fuse into one mass, not separate sounds.
  4. A/B the full wall against the REF — same thickness, same character — before shaping it.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Hijokaidan — Romance and use Drum Rack, MIDI clips, Groove Pool, and clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — Build the saturated wall — as the smallest Live set that proves Total saturation as a single mass; structure comes only from density (how many layers) and dynamic surges, never from pitch or rhythm.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects An overwhelming wall of noise that, despite having no notes or beat, clearly moves — surging, thickening, receding.
  1. Use Analog Rytm MKII for this phase: translate Build the saturated wall into drum voices or samples, trig conditions, parameter locks, scenes, performance macros, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates Total saturation as a single mass; structure comes only from density (how many layers) and dynamic surges, never from pitch or rhythm, not just the surface of Hijokaidan — Romance.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A saturated wall of several layers that A/Bs against the record's density as the same mass, with no pitch or rhythm in it.

Phase 3 — ~15 minutes

A/B at matched loudness, then write the diff

Phase focus: verify against the record at honest level. Follow the steps in order.

Do this
  1. Match loudness so 10 seconds of yours and 10 of theirs feel equally loud, then switch between them. Loudness-matching is essential here — raw noise tricks the ear into thinking louder is better.
  2. Write the diff: whether your wall moves as much as theirs, and whether any pitch or rhythm crept in.

Expected result

A short written diff naming where your wall stayed static and whether anything musical sneaked in.

Verify against the record

A/B your stretch against the same stretch of Hijokaidan — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Does yours move on density and dynamics alone?

Answer

If a listener can tell your wall is changing without any pitch or beat to follow, the mechanism worked — they are tracking thickness and level, exactly as in the record. If yours only feels like it moves because you slipped in a tone or a rhythm, you cheated the drill; strip it out and make density carry the shape.

Did you trust the troughs?

Answer

You should have. Beginners keep the wall at maximum the whole time, which makes it exhausting and oddly static. Hijokaidan thins and dips so the next surge feels enormous. If your version never lets up, find the troughs in theirs and build them into yours.
