Replicate: micro-edit recombination

Copy one passage of John Oswald's plunderphonics. Micro-edit a recognisable recording into fragments and recombine them so the source is both present and transformed, then compare to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a known recording is cut into tiny pieces and reassembled so densely that the source is simultaneously recognisable and remade — its identity survives the transformation. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordJohn Oswald — Plunderphonics / Plexure
The effectYou hear a familiar recording and a brand-new one at the same time — recognisable yet impossible
The mechanismRecognisable recordings micro-edited into fragments and recombined at high density so the source's identity is present and transformed at once

Before you start
  • One recognisable source recording loaded into Live (for the logbook only — the replica stays quarantined as study evidence)
  • Pen and paper to note which fragments stay intact enough to be recognised and which get reordered
  • What you will make: a short replica of an Oswald passage. Focus on this effect: a recording that is recognisable and transformed at the same time.
  • What you will turn off: the urge to disguise the source completely — if you cannot recognise it, the mechanism is gone.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: dense micro-edit recombination where identity survives transformation.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are which recording you cut, not a result that is either unrecognisable mush or an untouched copy. The replica is study evidence and stays in the logbook.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Oswald. Choose the clearest passage. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Can you recognise the source even as it is being torn apart?
    Answer

    Yes — and that recognition is the whole point. Oswald keeps just enough of each fragment intact (a vocal syllable, a hook, a timbre) that your brain identifies the source while simultaneously hearing it remade. Lose the recognition and you have abstract collage, not plunderphonics.

  2. How small are the edits?
    Answer

    Often tiny — fractions of a second, single syllables or beats — spliced end to end at high density. The micro scale is what lets the source be both present and unrecognisably reordered; longer chunks would just sound like a remix.

  3. Is the original's rhythm and pitch kept, or remade?
    Answer

    Remade. Fragments are reordered, repeated, and re-pitched so the new rhythm and melody are Oswald's, not the source's, even though the timbre and identity are clearly borrowed. Identity survives; structure is rebuilt.

  4. What holds the recombination together as music?
    Answer

    A new pulse or phrase that the edits land on — the fragments are placed to a fresh grid so the chaos has a beat. Without that grid the micro-edits would be noise; with it they become a new piece made of an old one.

Now build the passage you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target: a known recording cut into tiny fragments and recombined to a new grid, recognisable and transformed at once.
  • Elements: which fragments stay intact for recognition, and the new pulse/phrase the edits land on, noted from the REF.
  • Density: the edits are micro-scale and dense, like theirs.
  • Survival: the source is identifiable in yours the way it is in the record.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Digitakt II: sample the source, then press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to move sample start; place many short [TRIG] hits, each playing a different start point, so one sample becomes a chopped instrument. Vary pitch per trig and use micro-timing to lock the chops to a grid.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates audio tracks. Slice the source clip to a grid or by transient, then re-sequence the slices into a new order — short fragments, dense. ⌘ J consolidates the recombination once it works. Warp/repitch slices to remake melody.
  • MicroFreak: optional — re-pitch or resynthesise a fragment so a borrowed timbre is bent further while still recognisable.
  • Recorder or phone: set gain before the take; capture enough of the recombination that the new pulse and the source recognition both register.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Cut the source into micro-fragments

Phase focus: slice the recording small enough to reorder but legible enough to recognise. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track, loop the passage, and gain-match with a Utility.
  2. Sample your own source and slice it into tiny fragments — syllables, single beats, hook scraps.
  3. Mark the two or three fragments that must stay intact enough to be recognised; the rest can be cut harder.
  4. Audition fragments against the REF — are yours at the same micro scale as theirs?
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from John Oswald — Plunderphonics / Plexure and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.

  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects You hear a familiar recording and a brand-new one at the same time — recognisable yet impossible.

Verify against the record

A/B your passage against the same stretch of Oswald — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Is the source both present and transformed, or only one?

Answer

Both is the only pass. If a listener cannot name the source, you destroyed it; if they think it is just the original, you did not transform it. The mechanism is proven when the same listener says "that's clearly X — but it's also clearly not X," which is the double-hearing Oswald engineers.

Does a new pulse hold your recombination together?

Answer

It should — the grid is what makes micro-edits into music instead of noise. The common miss is fragments scattered with no fresh pulse, so it reads as glitchy chaos rather than a new piece. If yours feels formless next to theirs, lock every edit to a clear new beat.

  • And one more line — which intact fragment did the most to keep the source recognisable. The replica stays in the logbook as study evidence.
