Resonance as Melody

One bass pattern, never edited, for the whole piece — and a filter whose resonant peak does all the singing. The notes are not the melody; the cutoff is. This is the block's first lesson in level into transformation: the same filter is a polite tone control at low resonance and a screaming second oscillator at high, and what you feed it decides which one you get.

The point

A resonant filter is an instrument hiding inside a tone control. Turn resonance up far enough and the filter stops shaping a sound and starts being one — the melody of acid lives in the cutoff knob, not the keyboard.

How this lab works

Freeze the notes

  • One 16-step bass pattern on the MicroFreak, written in the first hour and never edited again.
  • Accent and slide are the pattern's only vocabulary — the 303 grammar.
  • Test: the pattern alone, filter wide open, should be almost boring. If it is interesting unfiltered, it will fight the filter.

Make the filter sing

  • Resonance high enough that the peak has its own pitch; cutoff ridden by hand and recorded as automation.
  • Every musical event for 6–8 minutes is a cutoff move, a resonance move, or an accent landing on both.
  • Test: hum the "melody" back after a take — if you hummed the notes, the filter is not yet the voice.

Measure what resonance costs

  • The resonant peak adds level the meter must absorb: measure the same accented step at low and high resonance.
  • Level into the filter changes the filter's character — drive it and the peak compresses and growls.
  • Both numbers go in the gain plan; "it got louder" is not a measurement.
Pass standard
Minimum passAudible original piece, routing diagram, fault entry, print map, and system diff exist.
Solid passThe pattern never changes after hour one, and a listener can still describe a verse, a peak, and a release — all of it done with cutoff, resonance, and accent.
Advanced transferYou can state, with measured numbers, how much level your resonance setting adds at the peak, and set the input gain into the filter on purpose for the growl you want.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
ResonanceA boost the filter adds right at its cutoff point — a peak that emphasizes whatever frequency the cutoff is parked on. Like running a wet finger around a wine glass: push the emphasis hard enough and the glass starts to sing on its own.
Self-oscillationWhat happens when resonance goes all the way: the filter's peak becomes a pure tone with no input needed. The tone control has become an oscillator — and its pitch is the cutoff knob.
AccentThe 303's signature move: one step hits both louder and brighter, because velocity pushes level and cutoff together. One control, two destinations — an accent is a gain event that the filter turns into a timbre event.
SlideTwo notes tied so the pitch glides between them instead of stepping. On the grid everything is even; the slide is the one place the bass line moves like a voice instead of a machine.
Drive into the filterThe level of the signal entering the filter. Filters with character are nonlinear: feed them gently and they sweep cleanly; feed them hot and the resonant peak compresses, thickens, and growls. Same knob positions, different sound — the input level is a hidden parameter.
resonance self-oscillation accent slide drive into the filter

Listening — the hypothesis source

Ten records where a resonant peak — of a filter or of a room — is the lead voice. Eight rode a 303 or its descendants; two prove the principle without one. Each gets a brief: what to listen for before you press play, and how to check yourself after.

01 Plastikman — “Plasticity” one knob, eleven minutes

A 303 line that barely changes its notes for eleven minutes while the filter writes the entire dramatic arc — the patient end of acid, where the sweep is the story.

Listen for
  • How rarely the actual notes change versus how constantly the timbre does — try to catch one new note entering.
  • The speed of the cutoff moves: minutes-long openings, not bar-long wobbles.
  • Where resonance, not cutoff, is the thing moving — the peak narrowing and sharpening over the same brightness.
After — verify

What changed across the whole piece: the pattern or the filter? Almost entirely the filter — the notes are a fixed conveyor belt and the cutoff is the hand sculpting what rides it. If you heard “new parts” arriving, relisten: most are the same line crossing a resonance threshold where new harmonics suddenly speak.

02 Hardfloor — “Acperience 1” stacked resonances

Not one 303 but several, layered and ridden against each other — the maximalist proof that a resonant peak is a voice, because here it is a choir.

Listen for
  • How many distinct acid lines are running at once — count them at the peak; it is more than two.
  • Each line's register: the layers stay out of each other's way by cutoff range, like singers by tessitura.
  • The long build: which layer's filter opens first, and which is saved for last.
After — verify

How were the layers kept separate — by notes or by filter range? By filter range: the patterns overlap, but each peak lives in its own band, which is arrangement done with cutoff. If the layers blurred into one for you, relisten to the breakdown and follow a single line back out into the full texture.

03 Armando — “Land of Confusion” the grammar, raw

Chicago 1988: a 303, a drum machine, and almost nothing else — the accent-and-slide grammar at its most exposed, before anyone polished it.

Listen for
  • The accents: which steps jump out louder and brighter at once — that pairing is the 303's signature.
  • The slides: pitches that pour into each other instead of stepping.
  • How few elements there are in total — count the tracks; it is fewer than five.
After — verify

Could you separate the accent's loudness from its brightness? You should not be able to — they are one gesture, level driving timbre, which is this block's whole thesis in a single step. If accents and slides blurred together, relisten to eight bars and mark each on paper: accents punch, slides smear.

04 Charanjit Singh — “Raga Bhairav” acid before acid

Bombay, 1982: a 303 and an 808 playing raga scales, years before Chicago — proof that the machine's grammar was discovered, not invented, and that it carries melody traditions far older than house music.

Listen for
  • The 303 playing an actual mode — long melodic phrases, not riff loops.
  • How little the filter moves compared to Chicago acid — here the notes lead and the resonance colors.
  • The ornaments: where slides stand in for the vocal glides of the raga.
After — verify

Same machine as “Land of Confusion” — what flipped? The hierarchy: here pitch leads and resonance decorates; in Chicago, resonance leads and pitch is a conveyor. Hearing both tells you the machine has no genre — the grammar is a choice. If they sounded alike, relisten to thirty seconds of each back to back and ask which knob you would have to touch to turn one into the other.

05 Josh Wink — “Higher State of Consciousness” resonance pushed to the pain line

The famous sweep that takes resonance to the edge of self-oscillation and holds it there — a study in how much level a peak can add and how long an ear will tolerate it.

Listen for
  • The moment the filter peak gets louder than everything else in the track — it is a gain event, not just a timbre event.
  • How the peak's pitch tracks the sweep — at maximum resonance the cutoff is audibly a note.
  • What the production does to survive it: what ducks or disappears while the peak screams.
After — verify

At the climax, was the filter shaping the sound or producing it? Producing — the peak is effectively a second oscillator riding the bass line, and the engineer had to budget level for it. If you only heard “a sweep,” relisten on low volume and watch for the peak crossing from color to pitch — there is a findable moment where it becomes a note.

06 Emmanuel Top — “Acid Phase” the slow European ride

One line, enormous patience, and a filter ride stretched across nine minutes — acid rebuilt as hypnosis rather than frenzy, the closest relative to what this lab asks you to make.

Listen for
  • The rate of change: how many bars pass between audibly different filter states.
  • The dynamic arc drawn with resonance alone — tension is a Q setting here.
  • What the track uses instead of a breakdown — find the moment of maximum closure and what it releases into.
After — verify

Where was the track's emotional peak, and which knob made it? Almost certainly a point of maximum resonance and near-closed cutoff — tension is the peak sharpening, release is it opening. If you located the peak at a drum change instead, relisten with the drums deliberately ignored: the filter was telling the story underneath the whole time.

07 Tin Man — “Nonneo” acid as melancholy

Contemporary Vienna acid: the same machine grammar slowed and saddened, the resonant peak singing an actual mournful melody — proof the vocabulary still has unexplored emotional registers.

Listen for
  • The 303 as a lead singer with a feeling, not an energy machine — name the mood in one word.
  • How low the resonance sits compared to rave acid — the peak rounds the notes instead of slicing them.
  • The space around the line: reverb and patience doing what extra layers would in older acid.
After — verify

What made this acid sad where “Acperience” is euphoric — notes or treatment? Mostly treatment: moderate resonance, slower tempo, space. The same grammar at a different operating point is a different emotion, which is exactly the transfer this lab wants from you. If the mood read as “generic acid,” relisten following only the longest slides — they droop, and the drooping is the melancholy.

08 Luke Vibert — “I Love Acid” the grammar, self-aware

Acid about acid: every classic move — accent, slide, sweep, scream — deployed knowingly, like a writer quoting a language they love. Useful here because the moves are unusually legible.

Listen for
  • Each named device arriving almost on schedule: find one clean accent run, one long slide, one full sweep.
  • The interplay of two lines with different resonance settings — one rounded, one sharp.
  • How the mix leaves the midrange empty for the peaks to live in.
After — verify

Could you point to one accent, one slide, and one sweep with confidence? You should — this track is the vocabulary list read aloud. If any of the three still escapes you, relisten with that single device as your only assignment; do not return to your own pattern until all three are findable on demand.

09 Alvin Lucier — “I Am Sitting in a Room” a room is a filter

A voice re-recorded into the same room again and again until only the room's resonant frequencies remain — resonance as melody with no synthesizer at all, and a preview of lab 017's generational method.

Listen for
  • Which frequencies grow with each generation and which vanish — the room is choosing.
  • The moment speech stops being speech — somewhere the words lose to the tones.
  • The final state: a chord. Whose chord is it? Not the speaker's.
After — verify

What was the filter in this piece, and what was the resonance? The room was the filter; its standing waves were the resonant peaks, and repetition turned those peaks into the melody. Each pass is the same trick as turning a resonance knob up one notch. If the endpoint sounded like electronics to you, relisten to the first three generations in a row — you can hear the room's pitches already lurking under the words.

10 Rrose — “Vanishing Pools” resonance as texture-melody

Modern hypnotic techno where narrow resonant bands drift through a dense surface — the acid idea abstracted: no 303, no riff, just peaks moving like slow melodies inside texture.

Listen for
  • Narrow bands of emphasis sliding through the spectrum — follow one as far as you can.
  • Whether the moving peaks have pitch — hum along with one; you can.
  • How the drifting resonances replace chord changes — the harmony is where the peaks currently sit.
After — verify

Was there a melody in this track? Yes — but it was played on emphasis, not on notes: the resonant bands trace singable contours through a static texture. That is the lab's idea freed from its genre. If you heard only texture, relisten and hum whatever your ear tracks; the line you produce is the resonance path, and that you could hum it is the proof.

2 · Bench preflight

The block rule starts here: a level written down at the input and output of every processor. The filter is the lab's only transformation, so it gets both numbers — twice, once polite and once at performance resonance.

MicroFreak osc → level in → analog filter · res high → level out → MicroFreak out → TS → M4 in 1 → USB-C → Live track 1 → master
Pre-flight — before the first ride
  • Gain ledger applied at M4 in 1 — set against the worst case: maximum resonance, accented step, cutoff at the peak's loudest point
  • Two measurements in the logbook: track peak with resonance at zero, and at your performance setting, same step — the delta is your resonance tax
  • Accent depth chosen and written down: how much velocity adds to level and to cutoff
  • Monitoring mode declared from lab 002's table; the ride must feel immediate
  • Thirty seconds of armed silence recorded at performance settings — know the floor resonance exposes
  • Phones at the lab 001 mark; first high-resonance sweep done at low volume, opened upward

3 · Build

Build the lab in five passes. Each pass produces an artifact, not just a better-sounding session: a written rule, a printed comparison, a fault entry, and a corrected system sentence.

Build recipe · Resonance as Melody

Make the transformation chain audible

Do this in order
  1. Print the source. Record one clean MicroFreak or self-sourced audio phrase into Live at about -12 dBFS. This is SOURCE and never gets overwritten.
  2. Build the chain. Duplicate SOURCE to CHAIN. Add only the processor family the lab studies: filter, saturation, grain, slice, or tape/generation loss.
  3. Level-match the chain. Put Utility before and after the chain. Pre level drives the processor; post level returns the result to the same apparent loudness.
  4. Print comparison passes. Print low, nominal, and hot versions, or first-generation and later-generation versions, as the lab requires.
  5. Run the fault. Overdrive the wrong stage or accumulate one bad pass on purpose. Diagnose by walking backward through the chain one stage at a time.

4 · Diagnosis & report

The full report bundle — ten items, filed in the logbook
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, master peaking −3 dBFS, 6–8 minutes
  • Routing diagram — drawn before patching, corrected after, with level in and level out written at the filter
  • Recording plan — which rides were printed, when, at what stage
  • Patch notes — oscillator type, resonance setting, accent depth, glide time, and the measured resonance tax in dB
  • Session notes
  • Reflection answers, including the listening comparison — which record contributed what to the hypothesis
  • One removal decision — what was cut from the finished piece and what cutting it did
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule (faultType: gain into processing)
  • Print map — which rides exist as audio, which as automation, what patch state is reproducible and what is gone
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the system worked like X; after building it, I discovered it worked like Y.”
  • The fault ritual: predict the symptom in writing, then create the fault on purpose — push resonance to maximum and drive an accented step into the filter hot. Predict where it clips first: inside the MicroFreak, at the M4 input, or in Live. Observe, locate the actual stage, fix it by lowering the right level (not the nearest fader), and write the rule learned.
  • Minimum pass gate: audible output · routing diagram · fault entry · print map · system diff. Without these five, the lab is incomplete regardless of audio quality.
Track-lab principle

A resonant filter is a voice, and a voice has a level — write the melody with the cutoff, and budget for the peak like you would for a singer.
