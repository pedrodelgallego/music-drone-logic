Material Under Stress — four ways a sound is changed

Three listening sessions about what a path does to the material that travels it: what each pass takes, what it adds, and when damage becomes the music. Every transformation you stage in this block starts from what you hear here.

Listening programme · 3 sessions · one full cycle · feeds labs 013–017 and capstone C3 · complete

The point

A processor is never just an effect — it is a path with an entry level, a character, and a memory. These sessions teach you to hear the path inside the sound: how many passes the material survived, what each one cost, and whether the damage was erosion or error. In Block 4 you become the person deciding how much damage, and where.

Listening is the loop

These sessions are not homework beside the labs — they are the first step of the operating loop. Every transformation chain you stage in this block is stolen from here, and the questions below are the same five System Spec questions the labs use, plus a sixth.

  • Programme: complete — the briefs, references, and method below are final in shape; the worksheets and replication lessons are included below.
Start here

  • Answer the same six questions for every reference, even when the answer is a guess.

  • Finish by naming one stolen principle before any build lab uses it.

New concepts — in plain words

Four terms carry this page. Here is what each one means, before any of them is used.

ConceptIn plain words
Generation loss Copy a copy of a copy and each pass takes a small toll — like photocopying a photocopy. The loss is cumulative and one-directional: what generation three lost, generation ten cannot get back. In this block the toll is not a problem to avoid; it is a compositional tool with a direction built in.
Linear vs nonlinear A linear stage treats loud and quiet the same way — turn the input up 6 dB and the output goes up exactly 6 dB, character unchanged (a fader, a clean EQ). A nonlinear stage changes its behavior with level — push it harder and new harmonics appear (saturation, tape, clipping). With nonlinear stages, the entry level is part of the sound.
Erosion vs error Two opposite ways material breaks. Erosion is analog: every pass takes a little of everything, gradually, smoothly — tape hiss rises, highs dull, edges soften. Error is digital: the sound is perfect until it is suddenly, completely wrong — a skip, a glitch, a dropped buffer — at full amplitude with no warning. The worksheets in this programme name which one each record runs on.
Path history Everything that has happened to a sound stays audible in it: every preamp, every tape pass, every resample leaves a fingerprint. “History is audible in the path” means you can hear not just what a sound is, but what has been done to it, in what order, how many times.

How to listen — the six-question frame

Every work in this programme gets the same six questions, answered in writing in your logbook. A few lines per question is enough; vagueness is the only wrong answer.

  1. Inputs — what raw material enters the system, and what condition was it in before the transformation began?
  2. Transforms — what changes it, in what order — and which stages are linear, which nonlinear?
  3. Feedback / variation — what keeps it alive over time? In this block the answer is often the degradation itself.
  4. Constraints — what prevents clutter?
  5. Human role — what is performed, decided, curated — and what does the process do unattended?
  6. Likely audio path — what had to be routed, recorded, copied, or re-recorded for this to exist — and how many generations did the material survive between source and record?

The method is three passes per work. First pass: just listen. No notes, no pausing. Second pass: answer questions 1–5. Third pass: question 6 only. The sixth is the engineering pass and the hardest in this block — you are counting tape generations, guessing entry levels into machines you cannot see, and deciding whether damage was performed or left to run. Guessing is the exercise.

Add a contemplative pass

Before the three analytic passes, take one contemplative listening pass — and protect it from the analysis. Play the whole work once with the logbook closed, eyes shut, no phone, nothing to do but be in the room with the sound. Do not name the transform, do not count generations, do not decide erosion or error. Just notice what the piece does to you: where attention drifts, where the body responds, what feeling the degradation carries before you explain it. Some of these works are made of damage and silence; they reward presence over dissection. Write nothing during this pass; afterward, one sentence on what it felt like is allowed, but the point is to have listened without a worksheet at all. Analysis is the rest of the page — this pass is the reminder that you are listening to music.

Social & historical context prompt

For at least one work per session, write a few lines on the context it came from: when and where it was made, on what equipment, by whom, and under what conditions. Why did this person reach for degradation, plunder, or noise — was it the only gear they could afford, a reaction against the polish of their moment, a tradition they were inside, a scene that made it legible? A transform is never neutral: tape decay meant one thing in a 1965 studio and another to a 2001 New Yorker watching loops crumble; harsh noise meant something specific in the rooms it was first played in. Naming that context keeps your stolen principle honest — you are borrowing a structural idea, but you should know what world it answered, so your use of it is a choice rather than an accident of fashion.

Session 1 — Records: four ways material breaks

Four answers to the same question — what does the path do to the material? Listen to each with the six-question frame, then compare. Two of these systems erode and two contain almost no erosion at all; finding which is which is the session’s real work.

01 William Basinski — “Disintegration Loop 1.1” erosion — analog generation loss

A short pastoral loop on decades-old tape, digitized in 2001 while the magnetic coating flaked off against the playback head — the act of playing the music destroyed it, and the destruction was recorded. Over an hour, one loop dies in public. This is the block’s anchor: a piece whose only transform is the medium wearing out, pass by pass.

Listen for
  • What disappears first and what survives longest — which parts of the spectrum the tape gives up early, and what is still standing near the end
  • The rate of decay — is the loss even, or does it accelerate? Compare minute 5 to minute 25 to minute 50, not adjacent passes
  • The gaps: the moment the dropouts stop being damage on the music and become the rhythm of the piece
After — verify

Which element survived longest, and can you say why physically? The low, sustained spine outlasts the bright transient detail — high frequencies live in the thinnest layer of the magnetic coating and go first. If you only heard “it fades out slowly,” relisten by sampling one minute at the 10, 30, and 50-minute marks and listing what is present in each.

02 Aphex Twin — “Rhubarb” degradation held at the threshold of warmth

From Selected Ambient Works Volume II (1994): a few soft chords looping under a film of hiss and pitch wobble. Nothing here is being destroyed — the degradation is held exactly at the threshold where it reads as warmth and distance instead of damage. It sits in this programme as the control case: the same forces Basinski lets run to completion, stopped at the first degree and kept there.

Listen for
  • The pitch instability on the sustained tones — the slow wobble that says tape (or a machine behaving like tape), and how constant it stays across the piece
  • The hiss bed: where it sits in level against the chords, and whether it ever moves — noise floor used as glue, not as accident
  • The loop point — can you locate where the phrase joins back onto itself, or has the softness of every edge hidden the seam entirely?
After — verify

Is the degradation here cumulative? No — and that is the finding. The wobble and hiss are constant from the first minute to the last: one fixed degree of damage, chosen and held, not a process left running. If you heard it as “just a warm pad,” relisten to the first thirty seconds on headphones and follow only the hiss and the pitch of one held note.

03 Steve Reich — “It’s Gonna Rain” the medium itself as the transform

1965: one recorded phrase of a street preacher, copied onto two tape loops, played on two machines that drift slightly out of speed. No filter, no distortion, no edit touches the audio — the only transform is the imperfection of the machines, and it is enough to take one voice from unison through echo and canon into pure texture. The cheapest fault in the room, promoted to the entire composition.

Listen for
  • The thresholds: the moment unison becomes a slap echo, the echo becomes a canon, the canon becomes a texture where the words dissolve
  • What the phrase becomes when meaning falls away — the melody and rhythm that were always inside the preacher’s delivery
  • What is not degrading: the loops themselves stay intact; everything that changes is the relationship between them
After — verify

What transformed the sound? Nothing touched the audio — only time, applied unevenly by two imperfect machines. This is the block’s boundary case: a transformation with no processor in it. If you couldn’t place the first threshold, relisten to the opening two minutes and raise a finger at the instant you first hear two voices instead of one.

04 Oval — “Do While” error — digital damage, curated

From 94diskont (1995): music built from deliberately damaged CDs — discs marked so they skip, the skips recorded, the most musical fragments looped into a long shimmering piece. This is erosion’s opposite pole: digital damage does not wear material down, it errors — each fragment is pristine inside its boundaries and ruptured at them, at full amplitude, with no warning and no hiss.

Listen for
  • The texture of the cuts: every fragment is perfectly clean inside, brutally clipped at its edges — damage that lives entirely at the boundaries
  • The absence of a noise floor — between and behind the fragments is digital black, not tape hiss; compare this silence directly with Basinski’s
  • The curation: out of what must have been hours of skipping, why these loops — what makes a CD error musical enough to keep?
After — verify

Could this piece exist on tape? No — and saying precisely why is the verify: tape damage is gradual and global (everything erodes a little), CD damage is instant and local (one read errors completely, the rest is untouched). If the fragments sounded “lo-fi” to you, relisten to any thirty seconds and notice that every fragment is in fact hi-fi — only the joins are violent.

Decision check

Which relationship to damage is yours? Not which record you liked most — whether you want to run a process and keep what survives, or hold a process at one chosen degree. Lab 017’s tape decisions should name it, and name which record it was stolen from.

Session 2 — Samples: trace one work to its sources

Session 2 · Deconstruction

One sample-based work, traced backwards through its chain

Pick one sample-based work — one of the Session 1 records, or another work whose material was clearly something else first. Loop a short passage and trace it backwards: for every element, guess what the source was before the chain, then reconstruct the chain stage by stage — what each pass took from the material, what it added, and in what order the stages must have run. Where a source is identifiable, say what condition it arrived in; where it isn’t, describe what kind of sound could have been the input. Finish with the sixth question for the whole passage: how many generations stand between the source and the record?

Worksheet — one row per audible element
Element (e.g. brass loop, voice, hiss bed)—
Source: what it was before the chain, in what condition—
Chain: likely stages, in order, each marked linear / nonlinear—
Per pass: what each generation took, what it added—
Erosion or error — and is it reversible, cumulative, or generational?—
Confidencelow / mid / high
Note

You will be wrong, and being wrong in writing is the point. The hardest call in this block is direction: is that dullness the source aging, or a filter? Is that wobble in the material, or in the machine that played it? A guess on paper can be corrected; an impression cannot.

This worksheet is a blueprint

In replication lab R4 you will re-run this passage’s degradation as a generation-loss process on your own material — and find out, empirically, which stages you placed in the wrong order and which losses you attributed to the wrong cause. Write each row as if someone will have to build the process from it. Someone will: you.

After — verify

Every row should have a source guess, an ordered chain, and a per-pass claim — a chain with no order, or a loss with no cause, is half a row. If a row resists the trace entirely, mark it low confidence; R4 will test exactly that row first. The worksheet template is the table above; the rows are enough to run the session now.

Session 3 — Video: the hands on the machines

Session 3 · Video

Tape-music interviews and studio footage, watched twice

Find interview or studio footage of tape-music practitioners working — any era, any region; the canon-breadth rule applies, so consider a practitioner outside the obvious canon for this slot. Watch it twice: once as an audience member, then again watching only the hands, the reels, and the meters. The suggestions below are searches, not curated picks — choose footage where you can actually see the machines being operated.

Search: William Basinski interview tape loopswatch for how loops are stored, handled, and played until they fail
Search: Steve Reich tape phase interviewwatch for two machines as one instrument, and where the drift comes from
Search: BBC Radiophonic Workshop tape techniqueswatch for splicing, speed changes, and levels set going to tape
Search: cassette 4-track tape loop techniquerig-relatable: the Porta-class path you will run in lab 017
  1. The hands — what do they touch between passes: heads, reels, gain, splice blocks? What is adjusted and what is left alone?
  2. Levels to tape — where is the level set going in, and how do they decide? Watch for the glance at the meter before record is pressed.
  3. Monitoring — during a pass, what are they listening to: the source, the return off tape, or both? When do they stop a pass early?
  4. The unrecoverable — what does each generation destroy? Once a loop has worn or a splice is cut, what can never be taken again?
Watch for the one-way door

Digital processing forgives almost everything; tape forgives almost nothing. Count the moments where a pass, once printed, closed a door — a generation that cannot be un-copied, a splice that cannot be un-cut. That count is why lab 017 runs on real tape and not a plugin.

After — verify

Can you describe where the record level was set, and how the practitioner decided, without rewatching? What was monitored during a pass — source or return? If you can’t answer either, rewatch ten minutes — watching only the hands and the meters.

Deliverable — the stolen principle

File all worksheets in the logbook. Then write down one stolen principle — a structural idea, never material — before you build anything with it. It will be applied and judged at capstone C3. Naming it in advance makes it a commitment instead of a story you tell afterwards.

Pass standard
Minimum passThree sessions filed, six-question notes exist, the source-trace worksheet exists, every record is named erosion or error, and one stolen principle is named.
Solid passThe stolen principle is structural enough that another person could build from it without copying any sound.
Advanced transferYou can hear the same principle operating in a clean, undamaged work — transformation logic audible where nothing is breaking.
  • Valid: “run the process past the point of taste and keep what survives” — degradation as a one-way form generator
  • Valid: “hold the damage at one fixed degree and never move it” — the threshold as identity, stolen from Rhubarb’s move, not its chords
  • Invalid: “crackly tape loops like Basinski’s” — that is material, not structure
