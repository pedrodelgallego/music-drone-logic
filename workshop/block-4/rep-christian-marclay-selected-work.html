Replicate: media artifacts as structure

Copy one section of Christian Marclay's damaged-record work. Use a skip, a locked groove, and surface noise as the actual structure of the piece, then compare to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: the damage to the medium — dust, locked grooves, skips — is the composition, and the playback object itself is the instrument. You do not hide the artifacts; you build with them. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordChristian Marclay — Record Without a Cover / More Encores
The effectMusic made of the sounds a record is "not supposed" to make — clicks, skips, loops, surface hiss — felt as rhythm and form
The mechanismThe medium's damage is the compositional material; locked grooves and skips become loops and beats, surface noise becomes texture, and the playback object is the instrument

Before you start
  • A source recording to "damage" — any clip loaded into Live or sampled into the Digitakt II
  • Pen and paper to note which artifact does which job: which skip is the beat, which loop is the bed, which noise is the texture
  • What you will make: a short replica of a Marclay damaged-record section. Focus on this effect: artifacts felt as rhythm and form.
  • What you will turn off: the instinct to clean up — no denoise, no de-click, no "fixing" the loop point.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: media artifacts (skip, loop, surface noise) foregrounded as structure.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are which source you damaged, not a clean track with artifacts sprinkled on top as decoration.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Marclay. Choose the clearest section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is making the rhythm — a drum, or a defect?
    Answer

    A defect. A skip or a locked groove repeats at a fixed interval and that interval becomes the pulse. There is no drum machine; the needle jumping the same way every revolution is the beat. That is the move you are stealing.

  2. Is the surface noise hidden in the background or part of the music?
    Answer

    Part of the music. Hiss, crackle, and dust are foregrounded as texture, not buried — the "flaw" is promoted to a layer. If you cleaned it up you would be removing the composition, not improving it.

  3. When something loops, is it a clean musical loop or a stuck one?
    Answer

    A stuck one — a locked groove repeating a fragment that was never meant to loop, often mid-word or mid-phrase. The "wrongness" of the loop point is exactly what makes it interesting; a clean loop would lose the effect.

  4. Where is the source material recognisable, and where is it just noise?
    Answer

    The source flickers in and out — a scrap of music or speech surfaces, then the damage swallows it. The tension between recognisable source and pure artifact is structural; you want both present, not one or the other.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target: artifacts doing structural jobs — a skip as the pulse, a stuck loop as the bed, surface noise as the texture.
  • Elements: which artifact carries the rhythm, which the loop, and how loud the surface noise sits, noted from the REF.
  • No cleanup: the defects stay foregrounded, like theirs.
  • Flicker: the source surfaces and is swallowed, where the record's does.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Digitakt II: sample your source, then press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D for sample start; set a very short loop region to fake a "locked groove," and use trig conditions or retrig to create a stuck skip. The amp envelope can chop the source into a click.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates audio tracks. Use a Beat Repeat or a tightly looped clip to manufacture a skip; layer a vinyl-noise or hiss recording as its own track so the surface noise is a real layer, not a plugin afterthought. ⌘ J consolidates once the artifact loop works.
  • MicroFreak: optional — a tiny click or static burst from it can stand in for needle noise; envelope it to a transient and trigger it on the skip's pulse.
  • Recorder or phone: set gain before the take; do not engage any noise reduction — the noise is the point.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Manufacture the artifacts

Phase focus: turn a clean source into defects you can build with. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track, loop the section, and gain-match with a Utility.
  2. Sample a source clip and make a "skip": loop a tiny fragment and let it stutter at a fixed interval so the repeat becomes a pulse.
  3. Make a "locked groove": set a deliberately wrong, mid-phrase loop point on a longer fragment so it sticks unmusically.
  4. Add a surface-noise layer — hiss or crackle on its own track — at the level the REF sits it.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Christian Marclay — Record Without a Cover / More Encores and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — Manufacture the artifacts — as the smallest Live set that proves The medium's damage is the compositional material; locked grooves and skips become loops and beats, surface noise becomes texture, and the playback object is the instrument.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects Music made of the sounds a record is "not supposed" to make — clicks, skips, loops, surface hiss — felt as rhythm and form.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate Manufacture the artifacts into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates The medium's damage is the compositional material; locked grooves and skips become loops and beats, surface noise becomes texture, and the playback object is the instrument, not just the surface of Christian Marclay — Record Without a Cover / More Encores.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.

Three usable artifacts — a pulsing skip, a stuck loop, and a noise bed — each ready to carry a structural role.

Expected result

A short written diff naming where you cleaned something up that the record left damaged.

Verify against the record

A/B your section against the same stretch of Marclay — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Is the defect doing the structural work, or is it just an effect?

Answer

Mute everything except the artifacts: if the rhythm and bed survive, the defects are the structure and the mechanism worked. If muting them leaves a clean track standing, you only decorated, and the fix is to delete the clean elements until the damage is load-bearing.

Did you resist cleaning it up?

Answer

You should have. The strongest pull in this drill is to "improve" the loop point or tame the noise, and every such fix erases the composition. If your version sounds tidier than theirs, you cleaned too much — put the grit back and trust that the damage is the music.
