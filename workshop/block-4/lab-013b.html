FM & Inharmonic Spectra

Subtractive synthesis carves a spectrum away; FM builds one up — and the single number that decides whether you get a warm harmonic tone or a clanging bell is the ratio between two oscillators. This lab teaches you to predict the spectrum from the ratio before you ever hear it, using Ableton Operator.

The point

FM's reputation as "unpredictable" is a myth that comes from twiddling without a model. The model is simple: a modulator oscillator bends a carrier oscillator's pitch thousands of times a second, adding sidebands. Where those sidebands land is set entirely by the carrier-to-modulator ratio; how loud they are is set by the modulation index. Two numbers, fully predictable.

1 · Listen first — harmonic versus inharmonic

Listen to four pieces of classic FM. The first two are harmonic (electric pianos, warm basses); the last two are inharmonic (bells, mallets, metal). The whole lab lives in the difference your ear is about to learn to predict.

01 Any DX7 electric-piano record (the "DX Rhodes") harmonic — integer ratio

The most famous FM sound on earth: a bell-tinged electric piano. The body is harmonic — a clear pitch you can sing — with a brief metallic attack on top.

Listen for
  • A definite, singable pitch — the sustained tone is harmonic, built from integer-related partials.
  • A short inharmonic "tine" at the very attack that fades as the note settles — that is a modulation index that drops over time.
After — verify

Hum the note, then hum the attack. The body has a pitch; the attack barely does. That split — harmonic body, inharmonic transient — is an envelope on the index, which you will build in step 3.

Listen: YouTube

02 Any FM bass (DX7 / Operator) record harmonic — bright but pitched

FM bass is harmonic and aggressive — a clear fundamental with bright integer harmonics stacked on top, the brightness controlled by index.

Listen for
  • A solid pitched fundamental — it works as a bassline, so it must be harmonic.
  • How the brightness (the upper harmonics) can be turned up without changing the pitch — that is index moving, not ratio.
After — verify

If you can play a melody with it and it stays in tune, the ratio is integer. Brightness without detuning = harmonic FM.

Listen: YouTube

03 Any FM bell / tubular-bell patch inharmonic — non-integer ratio

A bell's partials are not whole-number multiples of the fundamental — that is why a bell sounds like a bell and not like a flute. FM makes bells trivially: pick a non-integer ratio.

Listen for
  • A pitch that is present but "smeared" — you can roughly name it, but the tone has a metallic shimmer around it.
  • Partials that beat and clash rather than fuse — the signature of inharmonic spectra.
After — verify

Try to sing the exact pitch. You will get close but the tone fights you — that ambiguity is inharmonicity, and it comes straight from a non-integer ratio like 1 : 1.41.

Listen: YouTube

04 Aphex Twin / Autechre — any FM-mallet or metallic-percussion track inharmonic — percussion from ratio

IDM lives on inharmonic FM percussion — wood blocks, metal, glass. The pitch is almost gone; what remains is the spectrum's clash.

Listen for
  • Percussive hits with a metallic or wooden colour but no clear melody — extreme inharmonicity plus short envelopes.
  • How the same patch at a different ratio would change the material (wood → glass → metal) without changing the rhythm.
After — verify

Ask: could you play a tune on this sound? If no, the ratio is far from integer. Material, not melody, is what inharmonic FM is for.

Listen: YouTube

Write a one-sentence hypothesis predicting the rule. The target is something like — integer carrier:modulator ratios give harmonic (singable) spectra; non-integer ratios give inharmonic (bell/metal) spectra; the modulation index sets how many sidebands and how loud, and enveloping the index makes the timbre evolve. The build will prove or break this sentence.

3 · Concepts — the three numbers of FM

ConceptIn plain words
Carrier & modulatorThe carrier is the operator you hear. The modulator is an operator you do not hear directly — instead its output is wired to bend the carrier's pitch up and down very fast. Those tiny fast bends add new frequencies (sidebands) to the carrier's tone.
Ratio (C:M)The frequency relationship between carrier and modulator, set by their coarse/fine tuning. Integer ratios (1:1, 1:2, 2:3) place every sideband on a whole-number multiple of the fundamental → harmonic, singable. Non-integer ratios (1:1.41, 1:3.5) scatter sidebands onto non-harmonic frequencies → inharmonic, bell/metal. This one choice decides the character.
Modulation indexHow hard the modulator bends the carrier — set by the modulator's output level. Low index = few sidebands = nearly a pure sine. High index = many strong sidebands = bright/complex. Index controls brightness and richness, never pitch.
Operator feedbackAn operator fed back into itself. Small amounts add edge; large amounts push the sine toward a sawtooth and then into noise. Use it as a seasoning, not the main course.

4 · Build

Step A — the 1:1 harmonic tone

modulator B
ratio 1 → modulates → carrier A
ratio 1 → harmonic tone
(singable)
Step 01

Two operators, integer ratio, watch the harmonics appear

Do this
  1. Add Operator to a track named HARMONIC PAD. Choose the algorithm where operator B sits on top of A (B modulates A; A is the only carrier reaching output). Turn C and D off.
  2. Set both A and B Coarse = 1 (ratio 1:1). Carrier A level full; modulator B level at zero to start.
  3. Hold a note and slowly raise B's level (the modulation index). Watch the Spectrum: at zero you see one spike (a sine); as B rises, evenly spaced harmonics appear above the fundamental — 2×, 3×, 4×. The pitch never moves.
  4. Set a pad-like amp envelope on A (Attack ~300 ms, long sustain). Note the B level that gives a pleasing brightness without harshness.

  • Other harmonic ratios to try: 1:2 (hollow, clarinet-like odd emphasis), 2:1, 1:3, 2:3 — all integer-related, all stay singable. Note in the logbook how the colour changes while the pitch stays put.

Step B — move to a non-integer ratio

modulator B

ratio 1 → inharmonic tone
(bell/metal)
Step 02

Detune the modulator off integer — watch harmony break

Do this
  1. Duplicate the patch onto a track INHARMONIC BELL. Keep carrier A Coarse 1.
  2. Move modulator B to a non-integer ratio: use Coarse and Fine to reach ≈ 1.41 (or try 3.5 for a clangier result). Now the sidebands land on non-harmonic frequencies.
  3. Hold a note and watch Spectrum: the spikes are now unevenly spaced — no longer integer multiples. Try to sing the pitch; it resists.
  4. Give it a percussive amp envelope (Attack 0, Decay ~600 ms, Sustain 0) — inharmonic spectra read most clearly as struck bells/mallets.

Step C — envelope the modulation index

Step 03

Make the timbre evolve across the note

Do this
  1. On either voice, give the modulator (B) its own amp envelope with a fast decay — Attack 0, Decay ~150 ms, Sustain low. Because B's level is the index, this makes the spectrum bright at the attack and mellow as it settles.
  2. Hold a note and watch Spectrum animate: many sidebands at the start, collapsing toward a near-sine. This is exactly the DX-Rhodes "tine fades to body" you heard in listening 01.
  3. Compare to a static index (full sustain on B): the tone is the same brightness start to finish — and sounds dead. Note the difference; you will turn it into a fault in step 5.
Why this is the whole game

A static FM tone is a fixed spectrum — interesting for one second, then inert. An enveloped index means the spectrum changes shape over the note, which is what makes FM read as a living instrument rather than a buzzer. The index envelope is the difference between "a patch" and "a sound."

Step D — two contrasting voices and a phrase

Step 04

Write a short phrase contrasting harmonic and inharmonic

  1. Finalize voice 1 as a harmonic pad (1:1, slow attack, gentle index envelope) and voice 2 as an inharmonic mallet/bell (1:1.41 or 3.5, struck envelope, fast index decay). Add a touch of operator feedback to the bell for edge if it sounds too clean.
  2. Write a 2–4 bar phrase: the pad holds a chord; the bell plays a sparse melodic figure over it. The point is to hear harmonic fuse and inharmonic clash side by side.
  3. Document, for each voice: the ratio, the index (B level), and the index-envelope shape. These three lines must let someone else predict the spectrum without listening.

5 · The prescribed faults — induce and diagnose

Required

Manufacture both faults, hear them, document the diagnosis. These are the two ways FM goes wrong in practice — over-driving a high carrier, and forgetting to move the index.

Fault A

Aliasing from too-high index on a high carrier

  1. On the bell, play a high note (so the carrier is already high) and push modulator B's level (index) to maximum.
  2. Listen and watch Spectrum: high sidebands exceed Nyquist and fold back as gritty, detuned partials in the wrong places — a harsh, dirty quality that gets worse the higher you play.
  3. Diagnose: aliasing. Fixes — lower the index, lower the carrier ratio, play lower, or accept it as deliberate dirt. Note that index that sounds fine on a low note can alias on a high one.
Fault B

The dead tone from a static index

  1. Remove the modulator's envelope (B at full sustain, no decay). The index is now constant for the whole note.
  2. Hold a long note: the spectrum never moves; the tone is fixed and lifeless — a buzzer, not an instrument.
  3. Restore the index envelope; A/B and write the rule that FM aliveness comes from the index moving.

6 · The Ratio Prediction Test — certified here

Certification

Predict the spectrum before you hear it

  1. A partner (or your future self) names a ratio — e.g. "1:2" or "1:1.78". Before playing it, you state: harmonic or inharmonic, and roughly bright or dark for a given index.
  2. Play it and confirm on the Spectrum analyzer. Score yourself across at least five ratios.
  3. File one sentence stating the rule in your own words and the evidence (the Spectrum screenshots).
Pass condition

You correctly call harmonic vs inharmonic from the ratio every time, and you can predict that raising the index adds and strengthens sidebands without moving the pitch. If you are still surprised by the spectrum, the model has not landed yet.

7 · Pass gate

Gate

Two voices, one harmonic and one inharmonic, fully documented

  1. A harmonic pad (singable, integer ratio) and an inharmonic mallet/bell (not singable, non-integer ratio) in one short phrase.
  2. For each: ratio, index, and index-envelope shape written down — enough that the spectrum is predictable from the page alone.
  3. Spectrum screenshots proving harmonic (even spacing) vs inharmonic (uneven spacing).
Pass condition

The two voices are demonstrably one harmonic and one inharmonic; the documentation proves you can predict harmonic vs inharmonic from the ratio, and the index envelope makes each voice evolve rather than sit still.

Track-lab principle

In FM the ratio decides harmonic or inharmonic, the index decides how bright, and the index envelope decides whether the sound is alive — three numbers, fully predictable.
