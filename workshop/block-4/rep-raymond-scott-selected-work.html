Replicate: a self-running circuit as engine

Copy one section of Raymond Scott's sequenced electronic music. Build a self-running repeating pattern, vary it only by slow parameter drift, and compare to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: a circuit runs itself, repeating an ostinato indefinitely, and the only changes are slow drifts of one or two parameters. You are not performing the part — you set a system going and let it run. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordRaymond Scott — Soothing Sounds for Baby / Manhattan Research
The effectA hypnotic, tireless repetition that feels generated rather than played, drifting almost imperceptibly
The mechanismAn early sequenced ostinato from a self-running circuit; repetition is the engine, varied only by slow drift of a single parameter

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Digitakt II synced to Live (lab 002's clock setup), MicroFreak on its input or sequenced by the Digitakt II
  • Pen and paper to write the one or two parameters you will let drift — and how slowly
  • What you will make: a short replica of a Scott sequenced section. Focus on this effect: a tireless repetition that feels generated, drifting almost imperceptibly.
  • What you will turn off: the urge to play a melody by hand, add fills, or "perform" — the circuit performs.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a self-running repeating circuit as the engine, varied only by slow parameter drift.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are the choice of pattern or sound, not added human performance covering for the system.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of a Scott sequenced piece. Choose the clearest repeating section. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How long is the loop before it repeats exactly?
    Answer

    Usually short — a one- or two-bar cell that comes back unchanged. The piece is built on that cell repeating far past the point a human player would vary it, which is the tell that a circuit, not a person, is running it.

  2. Does anything change over the minute, and if so, what exactly?
    Answer

    Very little, and slowly — often a filter opening, a tone brightening, or one element fading in or out across many bars. The notes themselves stay fixed; only a parameter or two creeps. That creep is the only variation you are allowed to add.

  3. Could a person play this, or does it sound machine-locked?
    Answer

    It sounds machine-locked. The timing is too even and the repetition too patient to be hand-played for that long — the inhuman steadiness is part of the hypnosis, and is exactly what your sequencer should reproduce.

  4. What carries the hypnosis — the pattern, the timbre, or the drift?
    Answer

    The repetition itself does the heavy lifting; the slow drift keeps it from going dead. Take the drift away and it would still hypnotise but feel static; take the repetition away and there is nothing to hypnotise with. The engine is repetition, the life is drift.

Now build the section you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target: a fixed repeating cell that runs without intervention, with one or two parameters drifting slowly across many bars.
  • Elements: the ostinato's notes and rhythm, plus the identity of the drifting parameter(s), transcribed by ear.
  • Steadiness: the repetition is machine-even, like theirs.
  • Drift: the change is slow enough that you cannot point to the moment it happens.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Digitakt II: press [RECORD] for grid recording, the numbered [TRIG] keys to place hits, and [PLAY] to run the pattern. Press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose a sample. Use the LFO page to set a very slow LFO onto filter cutoff or sample start for the drift — the LFO is your "self-running" variation source.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates the audio track to record the loop. If you drift a parameter from Live instead, draw one long, slow automation line across the whole section, not a busy curve.
  • MicroFreak: if it carries the ostinato, set it monophonic and let the Digitakt II sequence it so the part runs itself. Assign the LFO at a slow rate to cutoff for drift; do not turn knobs by hand during the take.
  • Recorder or phone: set gain before the take and leave it; capture enough repetitions that the slow drift is audible end to end.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Set the circuit running

Phase focus: build a cell that repeats itself with no input from you. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Import the record on a REF track, loop the clearest repeating section, and gain-match with a Utility.
  2. Transcribe the ostinato cell — its notes and rhythm — onto one track and let it loop. Match the length of their cell exactly.
  3. Chase the timbre of the cell, then leave it. Confirm the loop runs with your hands off the gear.
  4. A/B the looping cell against the REF — same notes, same steadiness — before adding any drift.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Raymond Scott — Soothing Sounds for Baby / Manhattan Research and use Live audio/MIDI tracks, Utility, automation, and resampling.

  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects A hypnotic, tireless repetition that feels generated rather than played, drifting almost imperceptibly.

Phase 2 — ~20 minutes

Phase focus: vary only by slow drift, hands off. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

self-running cell (Digitakt II) → slow drift on one parameter → master
Do this

  3. Record the section running for enough repetitions that the drift completes, hands off the gear the whole time.

Expected result

A short written diff naming where you performed instead of letting the circuit run, and how your drift rate compares to theirs.

Verify against the record

A/B your section against the same stretch of the Scott piece — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Does yours feel generated, or do you hear a hand in it?

Answer

If you hear yourself making decisions — a fill, an accent, a tweak — the circuit is not running the piece, you are, and the drill failed. The mechanism is proven when nothing happens that you did not set going in advance, and the section sustains itself with your hands off.

Is your drift as patient as theirs?

Answer

Almost certainly it is too fast — beginners rush the change because the loop feels like it needs help. Scott trusts the repetition and lets the parameter creep over far more bars than feels safe. If your drift completes in a few bars and theirs takes the whole minute, slow yours down.
