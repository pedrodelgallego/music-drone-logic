Replicate: phrasing a continuous pitch

Copy one short phrase from Clara Rockmore. Drive a zero-step instrument with continuous pitch and shape it into discrete notes using amplitude alone, then compare to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: the instrument has no keys and no steps — pitch is one unbroken glide, and the notes you hear are carved out of that glide by amplitude shaping. Do only the steps that make that mechanism audible.

The system you are replicating
Source recordClara Rockmore — The Art of the Theremin
The effectA singing, vocal melody where notes are clearly articulated even though the instrument never stops sliding
The mechanismContinuous pitch and continuous amplitude with no discrete steps; portamento plus amplitude shaping ("aerial fingering") cut a glide into separate notes

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • MicroFreak on Live's input; if you have an expression pedal or aftertouch, route it to amplitude
  • Pen and paper to mark where each note "begins" inside the glide — the articulation points, not the pitches alone
  • What you will make: a short replica of a Rockmore phrase. Focus on this effect: a singing melody whose notes are articulated even though the pitch never truly stops moving.
  • What you will turn off: quantised MIDI, the keyboard's discrete note-on retriggers, and any patch where pitch jumps in steps.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a continuous instrument articulated into phrasing by amplitude shaping.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are intonation or vibrato, not notes that step instead of glide.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to about one minute of Rockmore. Choose the clearest short phrase. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Does the pitch ever jump instantly between two notes, or does it always travel?
    Answer

    It always travels — there is a tiny slide into almost every note, because the hand moves through the air and the pitch follows it continuously. Even fast passages are a connected glide whose stopping points become the notes.

  2. If the pitch is continuous, how does she make one note end and the next begin?
    Answer

    With the volume hand. She dips the amplitude between notes — a quick duck and rise — so the slide is masked at the moment of change and you hear two separate notes. The "articulation" is amplitude, not pitch. That dip is the move you are stealing.

  3. What does the vibrato do to your sense of pitch?
    Answer

    It centres it. A small, fast pitch wobble around the target makes the held note read as in-tune and vocal, and it hides how unstable a continuous pitch really is. It is part of how the glide is tamed into a melody.

  4. Where are the phrase's breaths — the points where the line lets go?
    Answer

    At the ends of phrases the amplitude falls to silence and the pitch can drift unheard, exactly like a singer breathing. These breath points are where the volume hand fully closes — mark them, because they shape the phrasing as much as the notes do.

Now build the phrase you described. Do not build from memory.

  • Sound target: one continuous voice, pitch always gliding, notes defined by amplitude dips — never by retriggered note-ons.
  • Elements: the phrase's pitch contour and the location of each amplitude dip, transcribed by ear.
  • Vibrato: a small, fast wobble that centres the held notes the way hers does.
  • Breath: the amplitude falls to silence at phrase ends, where hers does.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • MicroFreak: set the voice to Mono with Glide turned up high so any move between notes is a slide, not a jump. Use the LFO at a small depth and a fast rate into pitch for vibrato. The pressure-sensitive keybed and the assignable LFO/envelope give you a continuous controller; do not let the keybed retrigger hard envelopes between notes.
  • Live 12: if you draw the part instead of playing it, automate amplitude (a Utility gain or the synth volume) as a continuous curve, and draw pitch as one unbroken line — no stepped MIDI notes. ⌘ T creates the audio track for recording the take.
  • Expression source: map a pedal or aftertouch to amplitude so the volume hand is independent from the pitch hand, mirroring how the theremin separates the two.
  • Recorder or phone: set gain before the take and leave it; the dynamics are the instrument, so do not let any input gain or limiter flatten them.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Build a zero-step glide voice

Phase focus: make an instrument that physically cannot step. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

Do this
  1. Set Mono and turn Glide high. Play two distant notes and confirm the pitch travels between them with no instant jump.
  2. Add a small, fast LFO to pitch for vibrato — enough to centre a held note, not so much it warbles.
  3. Put amplitude on a continuous controller (pedal, aftertouch, or drawn automation) so you can open and close the note independent of pitch.
  4. Import the record on a REF track, loop the phrase, and gain-match with a Utility.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Clara Rockmore — The Art of the Theremin and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — Build a zero-step glide voice — as the smallest Live set that proves Continuous pitch and continuous amplitude with no discrete steps; portamento plus amplitude shaping ("aerial fingering") cut a glide into separate notes.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects A singing, vocal melody where notes are clearly articulated even though the instrument never stops sliding.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate Build a zero-step glide voice into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates Continuous pitch and continuous amplitude with no discrete steps; portamento plus amplitude shaping ("aerial fingering") cut a glide into separate notes, not just the surface of Clara Rockmore — The Art of the Theremin.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A single mono voice that glides between any two pitches and whose loudness you can shape by hand, with a gentle centring vibrato.

If it fails

Phase 2 — ~25 minutes

Articulate the phrase with amplitude

Phase focus: cut the glide into notes using the volume hand. Follow the steps in order, then compare against REF before moving on.

continuous pitch (glide voice) → amplitude dips at note boundaries → master
Do this

Expected result

A short written diff naming exactly where your articulation came from pitch steps instead of amplitude.

Verify against the record

A/B your phrase against the same passage of Rockmore — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, matched loudness. Then answer two questions in writing.

Are your notes carved by amplitude, or are you secretly stepping the pitch?

Answer

Mute the amplitude automation and listen to the raw pitch line: if it still sounds like discrete notes, you stepped it and the drill failed. The proof is that with pitch alone you hear a glide, and only the amplitude shaping turns it into a melody — that is the mechanism working.

Does your phrase breathe where theirs does?

Answer

It should — the breaths are the phrasing. The common miss is holding through a point where Rockmore lets go, which makes your line sound mechanical and tireless. If your phrase never quite rests, find her silences and let the amplitude fall fully to zero there.
