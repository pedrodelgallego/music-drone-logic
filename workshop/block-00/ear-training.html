Ear Training

The workshop's listening programmes teach you to analyse a track — what the system is doing and why. This primer teaches something different: hearing a sound and being able to do something about it. That means naming intervals, identifying processes, and — most importantly — rebuilding a sound from scratch just by listening. These are atomic drills. You repeat them until the connection between ear and hand is automatic.

Primer · recurring drills · ends in a five-sound recreate-by-ear gate

Your anchor instrument

You'll use the whole rig for this primer — MicroFreak, Digitakt II, and Live — because the goal is to hear something and then build it on whatever tool is in front of you. The primary structured practice app is Syntorial (paid, the standard for synthesis ear training). Free alternatives are listed below. The workshop's own replication labs are ear training in disguise — do them and you are doing this.

Two different skills — don't confuse them

The workshop has strong listening programmes throughout each block. Those programmes teach system analysis: you hear a track and answer six questions about its architecture — what's making the groove, where the space comes from, how the tension is built. That is a top-down skill. Ear training is bottom-up: you hear a single note, interval, chord, or sound and identify its atomic properties. Both matter. Neither replaces the other. This primer is the bottom-up half.

Drill 1

Interval recognition — sing and identify

An interval is the distance between two notes. Knowing intervals by ear means you can identify the emotional weight of a melody or bassline without a score. Start with the most common ones.

The drill
  1. Play a root note on the MicroFreak. Then play a second note a perfect fifth above it (seven semitones). Listen to the two together. The fifth is stable, open, powerful — it's the interval under most drones and power chords. Sing it back.
  2. Now try a major third (four semitones). Brighter, more resolved. Then a minor third (three semitones). Darker, more tense. These three intervals — fifth, major third, minor third — build every basic chord.
  3. Use a drone: hold one note on the Digitakt II (or a pad in Live) and play the second note on the MicroFreak. Hearing an interval against a drone makes it stick faster.
  4. Repeat this the other direction: play a two-note figure and name the interval before checking. Use teoria.com or musictheory.net to test yourself with randomised intervals.
Prove it

Have someone play (or use a tool to generate) a random two-note interval. Name it — "fifth," "major third," "minor third" — without counting semitones. Get five in a row correct.

Drill 2

Chord quality — major vs minor by ear

A chord quality is whether a chord sounds bright (major) or dark (minor). The difference is one semitone — the third. Hearing it immediately is the first step toward understanding any harmonic movement.

The drill
  1. Play a major chord in Live (three notes: root, major third, fifth). Listen. Then move the middle note down one semitone — now it's minor. The colour shift is dramatic despite the tiny change.
  2. Do this on several root notes so you're hearing the quality, not memorising a specific chord shape.
  3. Now listen to reference recordings — a track you know well. Identify: is this section major or minor? Where does it shift?
  4. Expand once you're confident: try diminished (both thirds are minor — tense, unstable) and suspended chords (the third is replaced by a second or fourth — ambiguous, neither major nor minor).
Prove it

Generate ten random chords (major or minor, any root) using a tool. Identify each as major or minor before looking. Get eight out of ten correct.

Drill 3

Name the move — filter or LFO?

In synthesis, many controls can create similar-sounding results. A sweep can be a filter opening, a pitch rising, or an LFO speeding up. Training your ear to tell them apart makes you faster at building and fixing sounds.

The drill
  1. Build a patch on the MicroFreak. Record yourself making four separate moves: (a) cutoff sweeping up, (b) LFO rate speeding up on the filter, (c) pitch bending up, (d) LFO depth increasing on pitch. Do not label which is which.
  2. Play the recordings back the next day. Try to name each one. The differences are: a filter sweep sounds like brightness increasing; an LFO rate increase sounds like a wobble getting faster; a pitch bend sounds like the whole note rising; an LFO depth increase on pitch sounds like the vibrato widening.
  3. Repeat with a partner: one person makes a move, the other names it. Switch.
Prove it

Listen to a mystery move (generated by a partner or a preset being edited blind). Name whether it's (a) a filter move, (b) an LFO rate change, (c) a pitch move, or (d) an LFO depth change. Get four out of five correct.

Drill 4

Spot the process — compression, reverb, or EQ?

Effects and processors leave fingerprints in the sound. Learning to hear them means you can identify what a mix needs — or what's wrong — without a spectrum analyser.

The drill
  1. Compression pumping. Load a drum loop in Live. Apply heavy compression (ratio 10:1, attack 5 ms, release 100 ms). Listen to how the whole mix ducks after each kick — the room breathes in and out. That rhythmic volume pulse is the "pump." Now set a very slow release (500+ ms) for a more exaggerated pump. Now set a very fast release (10 ms) for a subtle one.
  2. Reverb tail. Apply a reverb with 3-second decay to a single drum hit and play it in isolation. Listen past the attack — the smear of sound after the hit is the reverb tail. Now reduce decay to 0.5 s and hear the tail shrink. Now increase predelay to 60 ms and hear the tail detach slightly from the hit.
  3. EQ cut vs boost. Apply Live's EQ Eight to a pad. Make a narrow notch cut at 500 Hz — it gets slightly hollow. Boost the same frequency — it gets boxy or nasal. Make a broad shelf boost at 8 kHz — it gets airier. These are the sounds of EQ moves.
  4. Now mix: take a full short loop (kick + bass + pad) and apply one process at a time while it plays. Listen to just that one change. The goal is to hear a process in a mix context, not in isolation.
Prove it

Listen to two versions of the same loop — one dry, one processed with a single effect (compression, reverb, or EQ). Name the process and describe what you hear differently. Get three out of three correct.

Drill 5

Recreate by ear — the keystone drill

This is the drill that all the others point toward. You hear a sound, then you build it from scratch — no looking at the source. The synthesis recipes in the other primers and every replication lab in the workshop use this skill. Until you can do it, you are reading a recipe without being able to taste. When you can do it, every lab becomes fast and every piece of gear becomes learnable by ear.

The drill
  1. Start Syntorial (or pick a preset from any synth). Hear the target sound for 10 seconds. Do not look at the patch.
  2. Ask yourself: what waveform is that? Is the filter open or closed? Is there a lot of attack or does it click in immediately? Is there movement — an LFO, a filter envelope? Is there reverb? Delay?
  3. Start a blank patch on the MicroFreak or in Live's Analog. Make one decision at a time. Build toward the sound. Each decision is a hypothesis: "I think that's a sawtooth — I'll try a sawtooth and check."
  4. Compare your version to the target. Note the biggest remaining difference. Fix it. Repeat until you are satisfied or cannot hear a difference.
  5. The gap between what you made and the target is exactly what Syntorial trains. Run it daily in 10-minute sessions. The skill compounds.
Prove it

Pick three sounds from different categories (a bass, a pad, a moving effect) from Syntorial or from presets you haven't made. Recreate each from a blank patch. You pass when someone who hasn't seen your settings can hear both versions and agree they're in the same ballpark.

  • Syntorial (paid) — the gold standard for synthesis ear training. Hear a sound, build it step by step, then check. Works exactly like this primer's drills, but with guided progression and scoring. syntorial.com
  • Free alternatives: teoria.com and musictheory.net for intervals and chord quality. Functional Ear Trainer (app) for melodic ear training in context. Ableton's Learning Synths for synthesis recreation without needing a synth.
  • The workshop's own replication labs (blocks 2–8) are ear training in disguise. Each one asks you to hear a style and rebuild it. If you do them seriously, you are doing Drill 5 repeatedly on real music.

Proof gate

You pass this primer when you can…
  • Recreate a kick by ear. Hear a synthesised kick drum. Build it from a blank patch — pitch envelope, amp decay, waveform. Match it close enough that both versions thump at the same weight.
  • Recreate a bass by ear. Hear a short synth bass. Build it. Focus on: waveform, filter position, envelope shape.
  • Recreate a pad by ear. Hear a sustained pad. Build it. Focus on: attack, release, filter, any modulation making it move.
  • Recreate a delay throw by ear. Hear a single note with a delay effect. Match the delay time (what note value?), feedback amount (how many repeats?), and filtering character (bright or warm repeats?).
  • Recreate a filter sweep by ear. Hear a filter opening over four bars. Match the start and end cutoff position, the resonance amount, and the speed of the sweep.

This gate is a recurring practice, not a one-time pass. Do it at the start of each workshop session for the first month. It gets faster. When you can complete all five in under 20 minutes, you're where you need to be.

What you carry into the workshop

You can hear a sound and start building it immediately. Every lab in the workshop assumes you can recognise what you're hearing and make deliberate choices in response. The replication labs in Block 2 onward are where you use this skill on full musical systems — whole grooves, whole textures, whole mixes.
