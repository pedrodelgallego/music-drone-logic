Levels, gain & loudness

Everything about how loud a signal is, where its volume gets changed, and what happens when it is too loud. This is the language of Block 1.

Gain

The amount a single point turns the volume up or down. "Gain" is just the audio word for "how much louder or quieter this point makes the signal."

Why it matters Every knob and fader in your studio is a gain control. Knowing which one to reach for is half of clean recording.

See also Gain stage, Unity gain, Nominal level

Gain stage

Any single point in your studio where a signal's volume can be changed: a knob, a fader, a slider, an effect. A typical bench has about ten of them between the synth and your ears.

Why it matters Each stage was either set on purpose or left wherever it landed — and a forgotten knob does not stay neutral. Problems hide at the stage you forgot.

In the workshop Lab 001 has you walk the whole chain and mark every gain stage on your signal map.

See also Gain staging, Gain ledger

Gain staging

Setting the volume at every stage so the signal stays healthy all the way through — never so quiet it picks up hiss, never so loud it distorts.

Why it matters Good gain staging means one clean signal end to end. Bad gain staging means a sound that is too quiet here and clipped there, and you cannot tell why.

See also Headroom, Clipping, Noise floor

Gain ledger

A simple sheet that lists every gain stage in your studio with its agreed setting, one row per control.

Why it matters Written down, your levels become repeatable. You can rebuild the same clean setup tomorrow instead of guessing.

In the workshop You first fill the gain ledger in Lab 001, and update it in Lab 004b for long, held sounds.

Nominal level

target level

The volume you decide to aim for at each stage — chosen once and written down. Like a cruising speed on a road trip: the exact number matters less than everyone agreeing on it.

Why it matters Once you have a target, "too quiet" and "too loud" actually mean something. The workshop's default is peaks around −12 dBFS, never above −6 dBFS.

See also dBFS, Headroom, Unity gain

Unity gain

The "change nothing" setting: the signal leaves at exactly the volume it arrived. A fader at 0 dB is at unity.

Why it matters Start everything at unity, and whenever something is louder or quieter you know a person decided it — nothing changed by accident.

Headroom

The space left between your normal signal level and the absolute ceiling where clipping starts. If you peak at −12 dBFS, you have 12 dB of headroom.

Why it matters That empty space above is not waste. It is room for an effect, a reverb tail, or the mix bus to grow into without distorting.

See also Clipping, Crest factor

dBFS

decibels full scale

The volume scale your DAW's meters use. 0 dBFS is the absolute top of what the system can record digitally, so every real signal reads as a negative number — −12 dBFS means twelve decibels below the top.

Why it matters It is the shared ruler for "how loud." When the workshop says "peaks at −12," it means on this scale.

Peak vs. average (RMS)

Two ways to measure loudness. Peak is the single highest instant of the wave. Average (RMS) is the overall energy over time — closer to how loud something actually feels.

Why it matters A snappy drum can peak high but feel quiet; a steady drone can feel loud while peaking low. You need both numbers to judge a sound.

See also Crest factor, LUFS

Clipping

What happens when a sound is too loud: the top of the wave gets cut flat, like scissors trimming the peaks. It adds a harsh, gritty edge.

Why it matters Clipping can be a fault to avoid or a sound to use on purpose — but only if you know where it is happening. The whole point of Lab 001 is telling the kinds apart.

See also Converter clipping, Float overs, Saturation

Converter clipping

Clipping at the chip that turns electricity into numbers (the analog-to-digital converter, in your audio interface). Feed it a signal that is too loud and the damage is permanent — baked into the recording.

Why it matters This is the clipping you must not let happen, because no fader can undo it later. Watch your interface input meter.

See also Float overs, Preamp

Float overs

32-bit float

Inside the DAW, "too loud" is just a big number, not damage. Because Live works in 32-bit floating point, you can pull a fader down and the sound comes back perfectly — like a calculator, not scissors.

Why it matters Same red light on the meter as converter clipping, completely different problem. One is recoverable inside the box; the other is not.

See also Converter clipping

Crest factor

The gap between a sound's peak and its average level — in other words, how "peaky" it is. A kick drum is spiky (high crest factor); a held drone is flat and steady (low crest factor).

Why it matters Peaky and steady sounds need different level targets. A drone that averages as loud as a kick will peak far lower, which changes how you set its nominal level.

In the workshop Lab 004b has you compare a kick against a held tone and amend the gain ledger for sustained material.

Saturation

A gentle, musical kind of overload that adds warmth, weight, and extra harmonics — distortion used as a flavour rather than as damage.

Why it matters It is how a bare sound gets body and grit without anything being added to the arrangement. Pressure on the signal becomes the texture.

In the workshop The listening list in Lab 001 includes records (Sunn O))), Emptyset) where saturation is the whole sound.

See also Clipping, Limiting

Limiting

An automatic ceiling. A limiter watches the signal and clamps any peak that tries to go above a level you set, so nothing gets through louder than the ceiling.

Why it matters It is a safety net against clipping and a tool for making things feel loud and dense. The workshop puts one on the master before any feedback or loud experiment.

See also Headroom, Feedback

Noise floor

The quiet hiss that is always present under everything — from your gear, cables, and room. The "floor" your wanted sound has to sit above.

Why it matters Record too quietly and you have to turn it up later, dragging the noise up with it. Set a healthy level and the noise stays buried.

In the workshop Pole's "Tanzen 2" (Lab 001 listening) makes the noise floor itself the lead instrument.

See also Signal-to-noise, Self-noise

Signal-to-noise ratio

How much louder your wanted sound is than the background noise. More signal over less noise is better.

Why it matters It is the single best reason to record at a healthy level instead of a timid one.

LUFS

loudness units

A measurement of how loud something feels to a listener over time, not just its peak. Streaming services use it to set playback volume.

Why it matters Two tracks can peak at the same number yet feel very different. LUFS catches the felt loudness your ear responds to.

See also True peak, Peak vs. average

True peak

The real highest point of a wave, including peaks that fall between the digital samples and can slip past an ordinary meter.

Why it matters A signal that looks safe on a normal meter can still clip once it becomes sound again. A true-peak meter catches it.

Monitoring discipline

Keeping your listening volume fixed at one marked position for the whole session.

Why it matters Louder always sounds better, so if your listening level creeps up during a session, your judgment drifts with it and your mix decisions go wrong.

In the workshop Lab 001 has you physically mark the headphone knob and start every session at the mark.

See also Monitoring

The signal path & routing

How sound and control travel from box to box, and the cables and connections that carry them.

Signal path

The route a sound takes from where it is made to where you hear it — synth, cable, interface, computer, headphones, and every stage in between.

Why it matters If you can draw your signal path, you can find any problem fast. If you cannot, every problem looks the same: "it sounds bad somewhere."

In the workshop Drawing and correcting your Studio Signal Map is the first deliverable of Lab 001.

See also Routing, Signal map, Audio vs. control

Audio vs. control (MIDI)

Two different kinds of signal on the bench. Audio is the actual sound you can hear. Control (usually MIDI) is instructions — "play this note," "the tempo is 124" — that carry no sound at all.

Why it matters Keeping them separate in your head stops a whole class of confusion, like wondering why a USB cable carrying only MIDI makes no sound.

See also MIDI clock, Transport

Preamp

A small amplifier behind an interface's input jack, set by its gain knob. It boosts a weak mic or instrument signal up to a usable strength (line level).

Why it matters The preamp is where you set your recording level, and where converter clipping happens if you push it too far.

See also Line vs. mic level, Converter clipping

Line level vs. mic level

Two standard signal strengths. Mic level is very weak (from a microphone) and needs a preamp. Line level is much stronger (from a synth, drum machine, or mixer) and is ready to use.

Why it matters Plug a line-level synth into a mic input and it overloads; plug a mic into a line input and it is too quiet. Matching levels is matching the jack to the source.

Balanced vs. unbalanced

Two kinds of cabling. Balanced connections carry the signal in a way that cancels out hum and interference, good for long runs. Unbalanced connections are simpler but pick up more noise over distance.

Why it matters Using balanced cables where you can keeps a faint whine out of your recordings.

See also TS / TRS, Impedance

TS and TRS cables

Two ¼-inch plug types. TS (one black ring) is unbalanced — the right cable for a guitar or the mono MicroFreak output. TRS (two black rings) can carry a balanced signal that rejects noise.

Why it matters Using TRS on the Digitakt II's outputs rejects hum; TS still works but is noisier. Knowing which is which prevents mystery noise.

See also Balanced vs. unbalanced

Impedance

A kind of electrical "resistance" that a source and the input it feeds need to get along. Roughly, it is about whether two devices are a good electrical match.

Why it matters A mismatch can make a source sound weak or dull. Most modern gear matches automatically, but it is why a "Hi-Z" instrument input exists.

DI box

direct injection

A small box that turns a high-impedance instrument signal into a balanced, mic-level one that plugs cleanly into a mixer or interface.

Why it matters It is the standard way to record an instrument over a long cable without noise or level problems.

Send / return

A way to peel off a copy of a track's signal, pass it through an effect (like reverb), and blend the result back in. You "send" to the effect and it "returns."

Why it matters One reverb on a send can be shared by many tracks, and you can perform a track live just by riding its sends.

In the workshop Basic Channel's "Quadrant Dub I" (Block 1) is a static loop performed almost entirely from filter, sends, and level.

See also Bus / group, Dry / wet

Bus / group

A channel that several tracks are routed into so you can treat them together. A "drum bus," for example, sums all your drums onto one fader.

Why it matters It is a place to glue sounds together — and a place clipping can happen even when each track looks fine on its own.

See also Master, Gain stage

Master

The final stage where everything is summed into one signal before it leaves your DAW — the last fader and meter the whole mix passes through.

Why it matters It is the last place to catch clipping, and where a safety limiter lives. Master clipping sounds different from a single track clipping.

Monitoring

What you are listening through, and how. It also names a track setting in Live (Monitor: In / Auto / Off) that decides whether you hear the live input or only the recording.

Why it matters Much of what "sounds wrong" is really a monitoring choice. Some music barely exists on the wrong playback path at all.

In the workshop Thomas Köner's "Permafrost" (Block 1) lives below ≈200 Hz — huge on the right system, near-silent on a laptop.

See also Monitoring discipline, Direct vs. software monitoring

Direct vs. software monitoring

Two ways to hear a live input. Direct monitoring hears it straight from the interface (no delay). Software monitoring hears it after it has gone through the computer and back (with a small delay).

Why it matters Hearing both at once gives you a hollow, flanged sound. The rule is one listening path only.

See also Comb filtering, Latency

Loopback

Wiring an output of your interface straight back into one of its inputs, so the computer can send a signal out and measure what comes back.

Why it matters It is how you measure your system's real round-trip delay, instead of trusting the number the software claims.

In the workshop Lab 002 uses a loopback to measure latency and set the correct compensation.

See also Latency, Drift

Routing

The choices about how signals are connected — which output goes to which input, which track feeds which bus, where an effect sits in the chain.

Why it matters In experimental music the routing often is the instrument. Change the path and you change the piece.

Timing, clock & groove

How machines stay in time together, and how small timing choices create feel. The language of Block 3.

Latency

The short delay between a signal going into your system and coming back out, caused by the time the computer needs to process it.

Why it matters A constant latency is knowable and fixable; you can compensate for it. It only becomes a problem when it is large or when it grows.

In the workshop Lab 002 measures it and tells constant latency apart from drift.

See also Buffer size, Drift

Buffer size

How big a chunk of audio the computer works on at once, measured in samples (e.g. 256). Small buffers mean less delay but more strain; large buffers mean more delay but smoother running.

Why it matters It is the main trade-off behind your latency. Change the buffer and you change the delay, so any latency compensation has to be re-checked.

See also Latency

Clock / MIDI clock

A steady tempo pulse that one device sends to others so they all run at the same speed. It carries timing, not sound.

Why it matters One clock keeps your synth, drum machine, and DAW locked together. Two clocks fighting is a classic cause of drifting, sloppy timing.

In the workshop The "Spastik" drill (Block 1) makes Live the one clock master and the Digitakt II follow.

See also Sync, Transport, Drift

Transport

The play / stop / position controls. "Transport" messages let one device's play and stop buttons start and stop another.

Why it matters Separate from clock: a follower can know the tempo (clock) but still need transport to start on bar 1 with the master.

See also Sync, MIDI clock

Sync (clock master & follower)

Locking devices to one tempo. The master sets the tempo and sends the clock; the followers receive it and obey.

Why it matters Exactly one master is the rule. The most common timing bug is two devices both trying to lead.

Drift

Timing that slowly grows apart over time, so two parts that started together end up further and further off.

Why it matters Unlike constant latency, drift cannot be fixed by compensation — you have to fix the clock chain. A growing offset is the tell.

In the workshop The "Spastik" drill proves the clock by checking the bar 1 and bar 16 kicks sit the same distance from the grid.

See also Jitter, Latency

Jitter

Tiny, random wobble in timing from one moment to the next — not a steady offset, just small flickers.

Why it matters A little is normal; a lot smears transients and softens a tight groove.

Quantize

Snapping notes or hits onto the nearest grid line so they land exactly on the beat.

Why it matters It is how you get machine-tight timing — and, used too hard, how you flatten the life out of a human performance.

See also Groove, Micro-timing

Swing

Pushing the in-between beats slightly late (or early) by a set amount, giving a rolling, bouncing feel instead of a stiff one.

Why it matters Swing is an exact, measurable timing amount — not just a vibe. On the Digitakt II, 50% means no swing.

In the workshop Mr. Fingers' "Can You Feel It" (Block 1) is the swing-you-can-set reference.

See also Micro-timing, Groove

Micro-timing

Very small nudges of individual hits earlier or later than the grid, measured in milliseconds.

Why it matters It is the fine control behind a custom groove — and something you zero out when you want dead-straight timing.

In the workshop J Dilla's Donuts (Block 1) is groove built from lateness that repeats exactly.

Groove / pocket

The feel that comes from how parts sit in time against each other — slightly ahead, behind, or dead-on. "The pocket" is the sweet spot where it locks.

Why it matters Groove is a timing relationship you can build on purpose, not a mystery. Even perfectly straight timing is a groove choice.

In the workshop D'Angelo's "Playa Playa" (Block 1) layers parts each a fixed amount behind the kick — apart, but locked.

BPM

beats per minute

The tempo: how many beats happen in a minute. 120 BPM is two beats a second.

Why it matters It is the number the clock master sets and the followers run at.

Polymeter / polyrhythm

Layering different counts at once. Polymeter runs patterns of different lengths together (a 4-step over a 5-step). Polyrhythm runs different divisions of the same beat (3 against 4).

Why it matters Both make simple loops keep evolving, because the parts line up differently every cycle before they finally meet again.

See also Phase (rhythmic), Euclidean rhythm

Euclidean rhythm

A way of spreading a number of hits as evenly as possible across a number of steps, using a simple rule (the same maths Euclid used for dividing numbers). 3 hits over 8 steps gives a familiar pattern.

Why it matters It turns rhythm into a couple of numbers you can dial, and it lands on patterns from traditions all over the world.

Probability / chance

Setting a step to fire only some of the time — say, a 60% chance each cycle — so the pattern is never quite the same twice.

Why it matters A little randomness keeps a loop alive and human without you touching it. It is a core tool of Block 6's self-playing systems.

Phase (rhythmic)

Two identical patterns running at slightly different speeds so one slowly slides against the other, constantly changing how they line up.

Why it matters From almost nothing you get endless variation. (Not to be confused with phase in the stereo/mixing sense.)

In the workshop Steve Reich's Music for 18 Musicians (Block 1) and his phase pieces are the model.

Parameter lock (p-lock)

An Elektron feature: a setting saved to one single step of a pattern, so just that one hit has a different pitch, decay, or filter while the rest stay as they are.

Why it matters It lets a fixed pattern develop from the inside — the sequencer becomes the sound design, with no new parts added.

In the workshop DBX's "Losing Control" (Block 1) builds a whole track from accents, p-locks, and dropouts.

Synthesis & sound design

Building a sound from scratch and shaping it — the vocabulary of synths and the labs that make tones from nothing.

Oscillator

The part of a synth that makes the raw tone — a repeating wave at a chosen pitch. It is the starting sound everything else shapes.

Why it matters Almost every synth voice begins here. The waveform you pick (sine, saw, square) sets the raw character.

See also Subtractive synthesis, Fundamental

Subtractive synthesis

The most common way to make a sound: start with a bright, harmonically rich waveform, then use a filter to carve unwanted parts away.

Why it matters It is the method behind classic analog tones, and the whole basis of the one-voice étude in Lab 004.

See also Filter, Cutoff

FM synthesis

Frequency modulation: using one oscillator to rapidly wobble the pitch of another. Done fast enough, this creates new, often metallic or bell-like harmonics.

Why it matters It makes sounds subtractive synthesis struggles to — clangs, bells, sharp digital textures. Live's Operator is an FM instrument.

See also Operator, Modulation

Wavetable synthesis

Building a tone from a stored set of different waveforms and sweeping smoothly from one to the next, so the sound morphs as it plays.

Why it matters It gives evolving, moving timbres from a single note. Live's Wavetable instrument does exactly this.

See also Wavetable (device)

Filter

A tool that removes or reduces part of a sound's frequency range. A low-pass filter keeps the lows and cuts the highs; a high-pass does the opposite.

Why it matters The filter is the single most expressive control on a synth. Open and close it and one note can sound like several instruments.

In the workshop Phuture's "Acid Tracks" (Block 1) is one ridden filter heard as a whole arrangement.

See also Cutoff, Resonance

Cutoff

The frequency where a filter starts working — the point above (or below) which sound gets removed. Moving the cutoff is "opening" or "closing" the filter.

Why it matters It is the knob you ride for movement and energy. Most filter sweeps you hear are the cutoff moving.

Resonance

A boost right at the filter's cutoff point that emphasises it, adding a vocal, whistling, or zingy quality. Pushed far enough, the filter starts to sing on its own.

Why it matters Resonance is where a filter gets its character and bite — and where it can get loud or unstable, so it needs watching.

See also Self-oscillation, Cutoff

Self-oscillation

When a filter's resonance is turned up so high that the filter generates its own pure tone, even with no input — it "sings" at the cutoff pitch.

Why it matters It turns a filter into an oscillator you can play, and is a building block of feedback and drone patches.

In the workshop The rebuild of Alvin Lucier's "Long Thin Wire" (Block 2) uses a filter on the edge of self-oscillation as a stand-in for a wire under tension.

Envelope (ADSR)

The shape of how a sound changes over the life of a note, set by four stages: Attack (how fast it starts), Decay (the initial fall), Sustain (the held level), and Release (how it fades after you let go).

Why it matters The envelope is the difference between a sharp pluck and a slow swell. A long attack and release is how you make a sound with no clear beginning or end.

See also Transient, Sustain

LFO

low-frequency oscillator

A slow, automatic knob-mover. Instead of making sound you hear, an LFO gently moves another control — the filter, the pitch, the volume — up and down on a repeating cycle.

Why it matters It is how a held sound stays alive without your hands. Set it slow and shallow and a drone breathes; set it fast and it becomes wobble or vibrato.

In the workshop Lab 005 keeps a sustained tone interesting with very slow LFOs and long envelopes.

See also Modulation, Mod matrix

Modulation

One control automatically changing another over time — an LFO moving the filter, an envelope moving the pitch. "Modulating" something is having a source drive it instead of your hand.

Why it matters Modulation is what makes a sound move and evolve on its own. It is the whole reason "do less, but make each element evolve" is possible.

Mod matrix

modulation matrix

A small routing table inside a synth where you choose what controls what — for example "LFO → filter cutoff, small amount."

Why it matters It is where you wire up movement. The MicroFreak's mod matrix is how you connect a source to a destination by a chosen amount.

See also Modulation, LFO

Detune

Setting two tones to very slightly different pitches instead of exactly the same one.

Why it matters A small detune fattens and thickens a sound. A tiny one creates a slow throb (see beating); a wider one sounds lush or unstable.

In the workshop Coil's Time Machines (Block 1) is two detuned tones held still, with the throb between them as the only motion.

See also Beating, Overtones

Beating

A slow, pulsing throb you hear when two tones are very close in pitch but not identical. The two waves drift in and out of step, and the volume swells and dips at the rate of the difference.

Why it matters It is movement made from stillness — pure interference, with nothing being modulated. The closer the pitches, the slower the throb.

In the workshop Lab 004b builds a piece where beating is the only thing happening.

See also Detune

Drone

A tone held for a very long time. A good drone is not static — it is slow. The interest lives inside the sound itself rather than in notes or rhythm.

Why it matters When nothing repeats and nothing pulses, the ear starts examining the sound closely. It is the whole world of Block 2's beatless work.

In the workshop Lab 005 and 005b are built around sustaining a drone and keeping it alive.

See also Sustain, Held Tone Test

Sustain

The held part of a sound — the level it stays at while a note is down, after the initial attack and before it is released. As a verb, "to sustain" is simply to hold.

Why it matters Long sustains are what make drones and pads possible. They also behave differently on the meters than short, peaky sounds.

See also Envelope, Crest factor

Overtones / harmonics

The quieter, higher tones that sit on top of a note's main pitch and give it its colour. Every natural sound is its fundamental plus a stack of overtones.

Why it matters Filtering, saturation, and detuning all work by changing which overtones you hear. Light them up one at a time and the overtones become a melody.

In the workshop Folke Rabe's "What??" (Block 2) lights the overtones of one held tone one by one.

See also Fundamental, Timbre

Fundamental

The main, lowest pitch of a note — the one you name when you say "that's an A." Everything above it is overtones.

Why it matters A very low fundamental may only be reproduced by some speakers, which can make a piece's bass disappear on the wrong system.

In the workshop LFO's "LFO" (Block 1) hides a sub hook whose fundamental only some playback paths can play.

Timbre

tone colour

The quality that makes two instruments playing the same note sound different — the "colour" or "texture" of a sound, set mostly by its overtones and how it starts.

Why it matters When pitch and rhythm are fixed, timbre is what you compose with. Much of this course is learning to hear and shape it.

Sampling, recording & capture

Bringing real-world and recorded sound into your system, and reshaping it. The toolkit of Block 2.

Sample

A recorded piece of sound — anything from a single drum hit to a whole phrase — stored so you can play it back.

Why it matters A sample is raw material with conditions already attached: where it was recorded, how loud, with what noise. Owning those conditions is the lesson of Block 2.

See also Sampler, Resampling

Sampler

An instrument that plays back samples, often letting you pitch them, loop them, and shape them like any synth voice.

Why it matters A sampler turns any recording into something you can play across a keyboard. Live's Sampler and Simpler, and the Digitakt II, are all samplers.

In the workshop DJ Shadow's "Building Steam" (Block 2) makes unrelated sources sound like one band by passing them all through one sampler.

Resampling

Recording your own sound back into the system as a new sample, so you can reshape it again — slice it, stretch it, pitch it, or blur it.

Why it matters It is a way to commit to a sound and then treat the result as fresh raw material. Each pass can wear the sound down or transform it further.

In the workshop Lab 007 resamples one source through three different architectures and compares them.

See also Print / commit, Loop point

Field recording

Recording real-world sound on location — a street, a forest, a machine room — rather than making it in the studio.

Why it matters In the field, the gain decision is a one-shot: set it wrong and the moment is gone. That makes capture a skill of its own.

In the workshop Lab 008 certifies the field kit, with a safety and ethics checklist.

See also Contact mic, Hydrophone, Musique concrète

Contact mic

A microphone that picks up vibration travelling through a solid object rather than sound in the air. Stuck to a fence, a window, or a radiator, it hears the object's inner life.

Why it matters It reveals sounds your ears cannot reach, and makes the surface you attach it to part of the instrument.

In the workshop Toshiya Tsunoda's Extract from Field Recording Archive (Block 2) is one contact mic on five surfaces.

See also Induction coil, Hydrophone

Hydrophone

A microphone built to record underwater.

Why it matters A whole world of sound exists only for the right sensor. Underwater, the silence an air mic would hear becomes an orchestra.

In the workshop Jana Winderen's Energy Field (Block 2) is the hydrophone reference.

Induction coil

electromagnetic pickup

A pickup that listens to the electromagnetic fields leaking from electronics — screens, chargers, transit systems — and turns them into sound. It works like a guitar pickup, but for stray fields instead of strings.

Why it matters Every circuit leaks a field with a voice. The coil hears a layer of the world that is completely silent to your ears.

In the workshop Christina Kubisch's Five Electrical Walks (Block 2) is built entirely from these fields.

Musique concrète

concrète

Music made from recordings of real-world sounds — trains, doors, voices — treated as raw material to be edited and transformed, rather than from instruments playing notes.

Why it matters It is the foundation of capture-based experimental music. Repeating a recorded sound exactly strips its label and leaves a pure "sound object."

In the workshop Pierre Schaeffer's "Étude aux chemins de fer" (Block 2) is the founding example.

Granular synthesis

Making sound from tiny fragments ("grains") of a sample, a few milliseconds each, layered into clouds. You can freeze a moment, stretch it forever, or blur it into a texture.

Why it matters It turns any recording into an endless, evolving atmosphere. Live's Granulator does exactly this.

In the workshop Lab 008b granulates your own voice into something unmoored from speech.

See also Granulator, Formant

Formant

The fixed resonances that give a voice its vowel sounds and a body its character. "Formant shifting" moves these to make a voice sound bigger, smaller, or alien — separately from its pitch.

Why it matters It is how you change the identity of a voice without simply pitching it up or down like a chipmunk.

Transient

The short, sharp spike at the very start of a sound — the "click" of a kick, the "pick" of a string. It is what your ear uses to identify the hit.

Why it matters A strong transient cuts through a mix; a softened one sits back. It is also the part most easily smeared by latency or jitter.

In the workshop Lab 006b builds drum sounds from scratch, shaping the transient against the body.

See also Envelope

Loop point

The spot where a sample is set to jump back and repeat. Choosing exactly where the loop starts and ends decides what music a recording becomes.

Why it matters The same two seconds contain many different loops. Even a loop that "stumbles" can become the whole feel of a track.

In the workshop Oneohtrix Point Never's "Replica" (Block 2) treats the loop point itself as the instrument.

Warp / time-stretch

Changing the length of a recording without changing its pitch (or changing pitch without changing length). It is how a sample locks to your tempo, or stretches into a drone.

Why it matters It frees timing and pitch from each other. Pushed far, the stretching artefacts become a sound of their own.

Bit depth & sample rate

The two numbers that set a recording's resolution. Sample rate (e.g. 48 kHz) is how many snapshots per second; bit depth (e.g. 24-bit) is how finely each snapshot's level is measured.

Why it matters They set the quality and the headroom of a recording. The workshop records at 48 kHz as standard.

See also dBFS, Float overs

Self-noise

The faint hiss a microphone or recorder makes on its own, even in perfect silence.

Why it matters On very quiet field recordings, self-noise sets the real floor. It is why a quiet capture wants a low-noise mic and a healthy level.

See also Noise floor

Space, mix & stereo

Placing sounds in a space and balancing them against each other. The territory of Block 5.

Reverb

The sense of a space around a sound — the wash of reflections you hear in a room, a hall, or a cave, recreated as an effect.

Why it matters Reverb sets distance and mood. Drowned deep enough, it can blur a repeating loop into pure atmosphere.

In the workshop GAS's "Pop 1" (Block 1) dissolves its loop into reverb until the edges vanish.

See also Dry / wet, Delay

Delay

An effect that repeats a sound after a set time, creating distinct echoes. Feed some of the output back in and the echoes repeat and fade.

Why it matters Delay creates rhythm, depth, and dub-style space. Set off the grid, it can erase the bar line while keeping the pulse.

In the workshop Lee "Scratch" Perry's Super Ape (Block 1) and Vladislav Delay's Multila (Block 2) are delay references.

See also Feedback, Comb filtering

Feedback

Sending a signal's output back into its own input. A little feedback makes a delay repeat; too much builds into a runaway howl (the screech of a mic too close to a speaker).

Why it matters It is a powerful sound source and a real hazard. The workshop always meets it with a limiter and a hand on the fader.

In the workshop Block 6–7 build no-input feedback systems with a full safety checklist.

See also Limiting, Self-oscillation

Dry / wet

The balance between the untouched signal (dry) and the effected signal (wet). 100% wet is all reverb or delay and none of the original.

Why it matters The dry/wet balance often is the arrangement — it decides whether a sound is in your face or buried in the distance.

Mono / stereo

Mono is a single channel — the same sound in both ears. Stereo is two channels, so sounds can sit left, right, or anywhere between.

Why it matters Many systems (club subs, phone speakers) play in mono. A mix has to survive being summed to mono.

See also Width, Mono compatibility

Width

How wide a sound seems to spread across the stereo field — from a tight point in the centre to a broad wash filling both sides.

Why it matters Width adds size and space, but extreme width often collapses in mono. It is something you create on purpose, not by accident.

Panning

Placing a sound left, right, or centre in the stereo field.

Why it matters Panning separates sounds so they stop fighting for the same space, and is a simple way to add movement.

Mono compatibility / collapse

What survives when a stereo mix is summed to mono. "Collapse" is when wide or out-of-phase parts get quieter or vanish in mono.

Why it matters If a key element disappears in mono, it disappears on a lot of real-world systems. You check for this on purpose.

See also Phase, Correlation meter

Phase

Where two waves are in their cycle relative to each other. Lined up, they reinforce; opposite, they cancel and the sound thins or disappears.

Why it matters Phase problems are a hidden cause of weak bass and sounds that vanish in mono. You often see them before you hear them.

See also Comb filtering, Correlation meter

Comb filtering

A hollow, metallic colouring that happens when a signal is mixed with a slightly delayed copy of itself. Some frequencies cancel and others add, in a pattern that looks like a comb.

Why it matters It is the exact sound of a doubled-monitoring mistake or a phase problem — once you can name it, you can find its cause.

In the workshop Lab 002's fault is the comb filter you get from hearing your input twice.

See also Direct vs. software monitoring, Phase

Correlation meter

A small meter that shows how the left and right channels relate — whether they agree (mono-safe) or work against each other (will collapse in mono).

Why it matters It turns an invisible phase problem into a reading you can act on, before it ruins the mix on a mono system.

In the workshop Lab 003b drills the correlation meter as one of the tools that make invisible problems visible.

EQ

equalisation

A tool for boosting or cutting specific frequency ranges — adding bass, taming harshness, carving room for one sound by lowering another.

Why it matters EQ is how sounds stop overlapping and start fitting together. It is also how you shape tone without changing the performance.

See also Spectral tilt, Masking

Spectral tilt

The overall slope of a sound's frequency balance — whether it leans bright (more highs) or dark (more lows) across the whole spectrum.

Why it matters A small global tilt can shift the entire mood of a track without touching any single element. It is a slow, whole-mix treatment.

See also EQ

Masking

When a louder sound hides a quieter one that lives in the same frequency range, so you stop hearing the quieter one clearly.

Why it matters Masking is why a mix can feel cluttered even when nothing is loud. The fix is usually EQ or arrangement, not volume.

See also EQ, The mute test

Spectrum analyzer

A display that shows the frequency content of a sound — how much energy sits in the lows, mids, and highs, moment to moment.

Why it matters It confirms with your eyes what your ears suspect, turning "sounds bad somewhere" into "too much energy here."

In the workshop Lab 003b drills the spectrum analyzer alongside the correlation meter.

Method & workshop terms

The course's own vocabulary — the working process, the kinds of lab, and the things you produce.

The working loop

The repeating process behind every lesson: listen → say what is happening → wire it up → set the levels → build or measure → record the result → compare with the original → fix the difference → write the rule.

Why it matters It turns listening into a test you can pass or fail, instead of a matter of taste. It is the backbone of the whole course.

Six-question frame

A fixed set of six questions you ask of every listening reference, ending in the big one: "what setup had to exist for this record to be made?"

Why it matters It analyses music as a system rather than a feeling, so two very different tracks can be compared on the same terms.

See also Deconstruction, Reduced listening

Deconstruction

Working a track backwards to its sources: for each sound, what was the raw material, what was done to it, and in what order.

Why it matters Tracing a record back to its path is how you learn to build forward. Your deconstruction worksheet becomes the blueprint for a rebuild lab.

See also Replication / rebuild

Replication / rebuild lab

R1, R2, rep-…

A lesson where you copy a reference track you have analysed yourself, to learn how it works — then use what you learned in your own original piece.

Why it matters Copying to learn is allowed and useful; copying as your finished work is not. The replica is filed as a study, with a list of what you missed.

In the workshop Most rebuild lessons are optional practice; R1 and three named drills are required before Lab 003.

See also Stolen principle, System vs. material

Stolen / borrowed principle

One structural idea or method you take from a listening reference and name in advance — a way of arranging, a kind of movement — never the actual sounds.

Why it matters It is how you learn from the greats while staying original. You steal the structure, never the material.

In the workshop Each listening programme asks for exactly one, evaluated at the block's capstone.

See also System vs. material

System vs. material

The course's core distinction. Material is the actual sounds — a specific kick, a specific sample. System is the method that organises them — the routing, the rules, the structure.

Why it matters You are allowed to borrow systems; you must invent your own material. It is the line between learning and copying.

The mute test

A simple check for whether an element earns its place: mute it. If the track does not get worse, delete it.

Why it matters It enforces the "do less, but make each element matter" philosophy. Every part should be load-bearing.

In the workshop Robert Hood's Minimal Nation (Block 1) is the mute-test reference.

See also Masking

A/B comparison

Switching back and forth between two things — your version and the reference, or two settings — to hear the difference. Crucially, you match their loudness first.

Why it matters Without matched loudness the louder one always seems better, and you fool yourself. A/B done right is how you find what is actually different.

See also Monitoring discipline

Logbook

Your running journal through the course — where you write settings, decisions, faults, and the rules you learn.

Why it matters Written-down knowledge is repeatable knowledge. The logbook is where a lucky result becomes a method you own.

See also Gain ledger, System diff

Deliverable

A named thing a lab asks you to produce — a recording, a diagram, a filled-in table — listed up front so nothing gets invented at the end.

Why it matters Knowing the outputs before you start keeps the work honest. "Done" is a file, not a feeling.

Capstone / gate

C1–C7

A project that closes a block. It gives you a brief made of rules (constraints), not a recipe, and you must work through the whole process on your own.

Why it matters It is where you prove you can do without the scaffolding what the labs taught you to do with it.

In the workshop C1 asks for a second hypnotic piece from a constraint menu, plus renewed bench certification.

Étude

A short, focused exercise piece that drills one specific skill — borrowed from the music word for a study.

Why it matters An étude strips away everything but the one thing you are learning, so the lesson is unmissable.

In the workshop Lab 004 is a 3-minute étude built from a single synth voice.

Track lab / bench lab / étude

The three kinds of build lab. A track lab makes a full original piece around one technique. A bench lab is engineering — calibration, routing, measurement. An étude is a short focused exercise.

Why it matters Knowing the type tells you what success looks like: a finished piece, a certified setup, or a single skill drilled.

Held Tone Test

A recurring check from the drone strand: can you hold a single sustained tone interesting for a set length of time (three minutes at first, longer later)?

Why it matters It certifies that you can make stillness compelling — the hardest and most fundamental skill of beatless music.

In the workshop Introduced in Lab 005b and renewed at later capstones.

See also Drone

Fault ritual

A deliberate, controlled breakage in a lab: predict the symptom, create the fault, observe, find where it lives, fix it, and write a rule to prevent it.

Why it matters You learn a system best by breaking it on purpose, once, safely — so the real failure later is one you have already met.

See also System diff

System diff

A one-sentence note of what surprised you: "I thought it worked like X; it actually worked like Y."

Why it matters The gap between what you expected and what happened is the real lesson. Naming it turns a surprise into understanding.

Print / commit

Recording a sound or a whole part down to plain audio, fixing it in place so it can no longer be tweaked endlessly.

Why it matters Committing is a constraint that frees you to move on, and it is the first step of resampling. A "print map" tracks what is audio, what is still editable state, and what is gone.

See also Resampling

Signal map

A hand-drawn diagram of your studio — every box, every cable, and every point where the level can change — kept as two views: the audio path and the control path.

Why it matters Drawn before you patch and corrected after, the difference between the two is the lesson. It is your master reference for finding faults.

In the workshop The Studio Signal Map is the first deliverable of Lab 001.

See also Signal path, Routing

Reduced listening

Listening to a sound for what it actually is — its shape, texture, and movement — instead of for what made it or what it means.

Why it matters It is the core skill of concrète and analysis: hear the sound object, not the story. It is what the six-question frame trains.

See also Musique concrète, Six-question frame

The core gear

The instruments and tools the labs are written around. The method matters more than the model — but these are the named boxes.

Arturia MicroFreak

A compact mono synthesizer with an unusual range of sound engines and a built-in modulation matrix. The workshop's main "one voice" instrument.

Why it matters Mono and hands-on, it is perfect for learning subtractive synthesis, modulation, and the one-oscillator étude.

See also Oscillator, Mod matrix

Elektron Digitakt II

A hardware drum machine and sampler with a powerful step sequencer and per-step parameter locks.

Why it matters It is the workshop's rhythm and sampling brain, and the home of p-locks — the feature behind much of the course's "fixed pattern, evolving sound" work.

See also Parameter lock, Sampler

MOTU M4

The audio interface — the box that gets sound in and out of the computer, with preamps on its inputs and a headphone output.

Why it matters It is the meeting point of your whole rig, and where input gain and converter clipping live. In Live, the M4 is set as both the input and output device.

See also Preamp, Loopback

Ableton Live 12 (Suite)

The DAW (digital audio workstation) — the software where you record, arrange, process, and mix. "Suite" is the full edition, which includes all the built-in instruments below.

Why it matters It is the hub the whole course runs through. The Suite already contains powerful instruments, so the rule is "build the sound, don't buy it."

See also Operator, Granulator

Operator · Wavetable · Sampler · Granulator · Corpus

Live Suite's main instruments. Operator is FM and subtractive synthesis. Wavetable morphs between waveforms. Sampler plays your recordings as an instrument. Granulator makes clouds of grains from any file. Corpus models the resonance of physical objects.

Why it matters Between them, these cover synthesis, sampling, granular work, and physical-modelled resonance — most of what the course needs, already owned.

See also FM synthesis, Wavetable synthesis, Granular synthesis

DT 990 Pro

The workshop's reference headphones — an open-back, 250-ohm pair used for monitoring.

Why it matters Open-back phones invite turning up "just a little" all session, which is exactly why the monitoring mark and discipline matter.

See also Monitoring, Monitoring discipline

Launchpad Pro MK3

A grid control surface — a pad of buttons used to launch clips, play notes, and map controls in Live. It sends control data, never audio.

Why it matters It is how you perform Live with your hands instead of a mouse, important for the live-set work of Block 7.

See also Audio vs. control
