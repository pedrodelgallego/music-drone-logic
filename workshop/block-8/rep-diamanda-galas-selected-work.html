Replicate: one voice multiplied into a mass by live processing

Copy the core mechanism of Diamanda Galás’ The Litanies of Satan: a single voice routed through live multi-delay, reverb, feedback, and pitch until one performer sounds like a swarming multitude. Build a short take on the rig — multiplied in real time, never overdubbed — and compare its dread to the record.

The point

This drill teaches the record’s core mechanism: one voice is made into a crowd by live processing, not by stacking takes. Multi-tap delay, reverb, feedback, and pitch are the instruments of dread — a single source returns as many overlapping copies, so the terror comes from one person sounding like a possessed multitude. You steal the structure — one source, multiplied live — never Galás’ vocal material. Do only the steps that make that one-into-many effect audible.

The system you are replicating
SourceDiamanda Galás — The Litanies of Satan (1982)

The mechanismOne voice multiplied by live processing — multi-tap delay, reverb, feedback, and pitch shift — so a single source returns as many overlapping voices in real time, with no overdubbed copies

Before you start
  • A microphone into Live with monitoring on, and headphones — feedback chains can spike, so keep a hand on the master and limit the output
  • Digitakt II synced to Live (lab 002’s clock setup), MicroFreak on its input — optional for drones or resampled smears under the voice, but the multiplication must be live processing of the single voice
  • Pen and paper for a processing chart — transcribed from the record before you build: where does one voice read as one, and where does it tip into a crowd?
  • What you will make: a short replica of The Litanies of Satan’s one-into-many effect. Focus on this: a single live voice that processing alone swells into a mass.
  • What you will turn off: any overdubbed copies of your voice, any second singer, any pre-recorded vocal layers — the crowd must be made by processing the one live source, never by stacking takes. Also turn off decoration that hides whether the multiplication is working.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF share the mechanism: one source multiplied live into many.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are timbre, range, or rig limits, not a different method — yours is processing, not overdubs. This replica stays in the logbook as study evidence; it is never released.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to a stretch of The Litanies of Satan where one voice becomes many. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. When it sounds like a crowd, how many people are actually singing?
    Answer

    One. It is a solo performance. The multitude is an illusion built from a single voice returned through delay and reverb — the “others” are echoes and reflections of the same source, not separate performers.

  2. What turns one voice into many — overdubbed takes, or something happening live to the sound?
    Answer

    Live processing. The copies overlap and tumble in a way layered takes do not: tails of one phrase collide with the next, delays repeat and feed back, the reverb smears edges together. It is the room and the effects multiplying one source in real time, not a choir assembled in the mix.

  3. What makes it frightening rather than merely big — what are the effects doing emotionally?
    Answer

    Density and amplitude become threat. As the delays pile up and the level swells, the voice stops sounding human-scaled and starts sounding like a possession — a single throat producing more sound than one body should. Processing and loudness are used as instruments of dread, not as polish.

  4. Does the mass ever thin back to one voice — and what does that contrast do?
    Answer

    Yes. The crowd surges and then drops back toward a bare, exposed single voice, and that swing is the drama. The lone voice is vulnerable; the multiplied one is overwhelming. Pulling the processing back to one source makes the next bloom into many even more terrifying.

Now build the one-into-many you described — a single live voice that processing alone turns into a mass. Do not build from memory.

  • Structural target: one live voice in, a crowd out — the entire mass is processed copies of the single source, with zero overdubbed vocal layers.
  • Source: your own voice and your own utterance — not Galás’ words or pitches — chosen so it survives heavy delay and feedback without turning to mush.
  • Build: density rises only by opening the processing — more delay taps, more feedback, more reverb, more pitch spread — never by adding a second take.
  • Contrast: the take must visit a bare single voice and a full multiplied mass, so the swing between vulnerable and overwhelming is audible.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson tells you to restart.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Build the multiplying chain on one voice

Phase focus: route a single live voice through processing that returns it as many. Follow the steps in order.

Do this
  1. Make one audio track for the mic and arm monitoring. This single track is the only voice in the project — everything downstream is a copy of it.
  2. On that track, place a multi-tap delay (Live’s Delay or Echo) with several offset repeats so one phrase returns as a cluster of slightly displaced voices. Add feedback so the repeats accumulate rather than die quickly.
  3. Send the track to a reverb (a large hall or convolution) so the delayed copies smear into one another and lose their individual edges — the crowd should feel like a space full of voices, not a row of echoes.
  4. Add a pitch shifter (or set Echo’s modulation / a second detuned delay) so some returns sit slightly above and below the source, widening one voice into a chord of voices. Keep a master limiter in place — feedback can spike.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from the reference and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — Build the multiplying chain on one voice — as the smallest Live set that proves One voice multiplied by live processing — multi-tap delay, reverb, feedback, and pitch shift — so a single source returns as many overlapping voices in real time, with no overdubbed copies.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects A single performer, working extreme range and amplitude through live delay and reverb, blooms into a swarming crowd — terrifying because one voice sounds like a possessed multitude closing in.

Expected result

One held or declaimed phrase that, untouched after you sing it, returns as a small overlapping crowd — delayed, detuned, and blurred by reverb — with nothing in the project but the single live source and its processing.

Phase 2 — ~20 minutes

Perform the swing from one voice to a mass

Phase focus: play the processing live so the single voice blooms into a crowd and thins back. Follow the steps in order.

one live voice → multi-tap delay + feedback + pitch → reverb smear → a swarming mass
Do this
  1. Arm recording. Open with one bare voice — feedback and delay sends near zero — so the lone, exposed source is on the take first.
  2. Perform the bloom by hand: ride the delay feedback, send level, and pitch spread up while you keep singing, so the crowd grows out of the same voice in real time — never an added layer.
  3. Push amplitude and density together to the threatening peak — the point where one throat clearly sounds like a multitude — then hold it long enough to feel oppressive.
  4. (Optional) Resample a held vocal phrase or sustain a low drone under the mass for weight — but add no second vocal take; the voices must all be the one live source.
  5. Thin the processing back to a single bare voice to end, so the take ends on the contrast between the lone and the multiplied.

Expected result

A take that opens on one exposed voice, swells into a swarming mass through live processing alone, holds the dread at its peak, and falls back to one — all from a single source, with no overdubbed copies.

Verify against the record

A/B your take against The Litanies of Satan at matched loudness — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, levels gain-matched with a Utility. Then answer two questions in writing.

Is your crowd made from one live voice, with no overdubbed copies?

Answer

Scan the project: if any vocal sound is a separate recorded take rather than a processed return of the single source, you broke the mechanism. Galás’ terror is that one performer produces the multitude in real time. If yours needed a stacked layer to feel full, the chain was too thin — add taps, feedback, and pitch spread, not another take.

Does the multiplication frighten the way theirs does — does density plus loudness read as dread?

Answer

It should feel like one throat overflowing into a possessed mass, not like a pleasant doubling. If yours is lush but calm, you grew density without amplitude, or the reverb softened the threat — re-take pushing send level and feedback up together so the crowd arrives loud, and let the peak sit long enough to become oppressive before you thin it back to one.
