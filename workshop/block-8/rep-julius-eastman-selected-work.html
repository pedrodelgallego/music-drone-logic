Replicate: additive accumulation to breakdown

Copy the structural engine of Julius Eastman’s Stay On It: one short cell, added to itself until it can no longer hold, then allowed to fall apart. Build a short take on the rig and compare its shape to the record.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record’s core mechanism — Eastman’s “organic music”: a single simple cell is accreted layer by layer until the structure overloads and destabilizes, so growth and decay are one continuous process driven by accumulation. You steal the structure, never Eastman’s material. Do only the steps that make the accretion-to-breakdown arc audible.

The system you are replicating
SourceJulius Eastman — Stay On It (1973)
The effectA piece that builds from one giddy little figure into euphoric overload, then frays into noise and silence — the same material carries you up and lets you fall
The mechanism“Organic music”: a single cell is additively layered and gradually destabilized; form grows and decays purely by accumulation, with no new material introduced to rescue it

Before you start
  • Digitakt II synced to Live (lab 002’s clock setup), MicroFreak on its input
  • Pen and paper for an accretion chart — transcribed from the record before you build: when does each new copy of the cell enter, and where does the structure stop coping?
  • What you will make: a short replica of Stay On It’s arc. Focus on this effect: one figure that grows into overload and then decays — never replaced, only accumulated and abandoned.
  • What you will turn off: any new sounds, fills, or rescues that hide whether accumulation alone can build and break the structure.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF share the mechanism: additive accumulation of one cell to the point of breakdown.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are timbre or rig limits, not a different structural logic. This replica stays in the logbook as study evidence; it is never released.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to Stay On It from the opening cell through the first collapse. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is the cell — how long is it, and how much of it never changes?
    Answer

    It is a short, almost pop-like riff — a one- or two-bar figure that is stated plainly at the top and then essentially never changes. Everything that follows is more copies of it, stacked and offset, not new tunes.

  2. How does the piece get bigger — new ideas, or more of the same idea?
    Answer

    More of the same. Players keep adding instances of the cell — doublings, octave layers, slightly displaced repeats — so density rises while the material stays fixed. The growth is purely additive.

  3. Where does the structure stop holding, and what does breakdown sound like?
    Answer

    There is a point where the stacking exceeds what the ensemble can keep locked. The cell smears, ragged repeats and shouts and percussion take over, and the tidy riff dissolves into a churn that is closer to noise than to the tune. The breakdown is the accumulation overflowing, not a separate section.

  4. Does any rescue arrive — a new theme or a clean reset — or does the piece simply decay from where it overloaded?
    Answer

    No rescue. Nothing new is brought in to save the structure; it thins, frays, and lets go from the same material that built it. Decay is the back half of the same single gesture.

Now build the arc you described — one cell, added to itself to failure. Do not build from memory.

  • Structural target: the entire piece is one cell plus copies of it. No second theme, no drop, no fill that is not itself the cell.
  • Cell: a short original figure of your own — not Eastman’s notes — chosen so it is satisfying on the first hearing and oppressive by the twentieth.
  • Build: density rises only by adding instances; the overload point is where the layers stop reading as one figure.
  • Decay: from overload, you only remove and degrade — the piece dies from the same material that grew it.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson tells you to restart.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Write one cell, then clone it into layers

Phase focus: build the single accumulating unit. Follow the steps in order.

Do this
  1. Write one short original figure — one or two bars, melodic and a little jubilant — and record it as a loop into Live.
  2. Duplicate the cell onto a second and third track. Offset each copy slightly — an octave, or a sixteenth late — but change no notes.
  3. Build a percussion cell that is the same figure’s rhythm, so even the drums are copies of the one idea, not a separate groove.
  4. Mute every layer but the first; arm them so you can bring copies in by hand, one at a time.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from the reference and use Live audio/MIDI tracks, Utility, automation, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — Write one cell, then clone it into layers — as the smallest Live set that proves “Organic music”: a single cell is additively layered and gradually destabilized; form grows and decays purely by accumulation, with no new material introduced to rescue it.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.

Expected result

A stack of identical cells you can introduce one by one — the raw material for accumulation, with nothing in the project that is not a copy of the single figure.

Phase 2 — ~20 minutes

Accrete to overload, then decay

Phase focus: perform the grow-and-break arc live. Follow the steps in order.

one cell → add copies until it stops reading as one figure → remove / degrade to silence
Do this
  1. Arm recording. Start with one cell and unmute a new copy every few bars — never anything new, only another instance.
  2. Keep adding past the point of comfort. The overload is reached when the ear can no longer separate the layers into the one figure — let it get ugly.
  3. From overload, do not reset. Begin pulling copies out and pushing the survivors toward noise (drive, detune, filter) so the same material decays.
  4. End on one ragged copy or on silence — the piece dies where it grew, with no rescue introduced.

Expected result

A take whose whole life — rise, overload, fall — is carried by one cell being added and then abandoned, with a clear moment where structure tips into breakdown.

If it fails

Verify against the record

A/B your take against Stay On It at matched loudness — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, levels gain-matched with a Utility. Then answer two questions in writing.

Did your piece grow and break from one cell, with no new material introduced?

Answer

Scan your own track count: if anything in the project is not a copy of the opening figure, you broke the mechanism. Eastman’s discipline is that the single cell is enough to both build the euphoria and cause the collapse. If yours needed a second idea, the cell was not stated densely enough — add more copies, not more music.

Is the breakdown the accumulation overflowing, or a separate section?

Answer

It should be continuous: the overload point and the decay are the back half of the same gesture, not a new passage. If your collapse sounds like a fresh “noise section,” you reset instead of letting the stack fail — re-take and push the layering one step further so it breaks on its own.

  • And one more line — at how many copies did your structure actually overload, and was that sooner or later than the record’s.
