Replicate: a listening machine as duo partner

Copy the structural engine of George Lewis’s Voyager: a machine that listens to a human player and answers, so the music is a negotiation between two improvisers — one of whom is software. Build a small listening responder on the rig and play a duet with it.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches Voyager’s core mechanism: an interactive computer improviser that analyses what the human plays and responds with its own choices, so neither party is accompanist — the piece is human↔machine negotiation. You steal the structure (a listening responder you trade with), never Lewis’s specific algorithm or output. Do only the steps that make the listening-and-answering loop audible.

The system you are replicating
SourceGeorge Lewis — Voyager
The effectA duet in which the electronic voice clearly hears the human — surging when you surge, retreating into its own ideas when you rest — so it feels like a partner with opinions, not a backing track
The mechanismA listening machine: analysis of the human’s input (level, density, register) drives the machine’s response, making the form an ongoing two-way negotiation rather than a fixed part

Before you start
  • Digitakt II synced to Live (lab 002’s clock setup), MicroFreak on its input
  • Pen and paper for a response map — transcribed from the record before you build: what does the machine do when the human gets louder, busier, or stops?
  • What you will make: a small listening responder — an envelope-follower or analysis driving a synth voice — and a short recorded duet with it. Focus on this effect: the machine audibly reacts to you.
  • What you will turn off: any pre-sequenced machine part or fixed clip that would let the “responder” play the same thing regardless of what you do.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF share the mechanism: a listening machine (analysis → response) acting as a duo partner.
  • What counts as success: a listener can tell the machine heard you. The replica stays in the logbook as study evidence; it is never released.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to a few minutes of Voyager, watching the relationship between the human player and the electronic voice. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. How do you know the machine is listening at all?
    Answer

    Its behaviour changes in step with the human’s — it gets denser when the player gets denser, leaves gaps when the player leans in, and changes character at the player’s phrase boundaries. The correlation is the tell; a tape would not track you.

  2. Does the machine mirror the human, or argue with it?
    Answer

    Both, unpredictably. Sometimes it doubles the human’s energy; sometimes it does the opposite — answering a loud surge with sudden sparseness. It is responding, not copying, which is why it reads as a partner with its own taste.

  3. What happens to the machine when the human stops playing?
    Answer

    It does not just stop. It either keeps developing its own line into the silence or thins out — but the human’s rest is clearly an input that produces a response, not a dead patch. The silence is part of the conversation.

  4. Is there a fixed form, or is the shape produced by the exchange itself?
    Answer

    There is no through-composed form; the shape is whatever the negotiation produces on the night. Sections arrive because one party pushed and the other answered, not because a chart said so.

Now build a responder that makes those reactions audible. Do not build from memory.

  • Structural target: the machine’s output is driven by analysis of your live input — not a clip, not a fixed pattern.
  • Inputs: at minimum the human’s amplitude (envelope follower); ideally also density (how busy) so silence is itself a trigger.
  • Responses: map each input to a musical move — louder → filter opens; you stop → the machine takes a solo turn — and let at least one response be a contrary one.
  • Duet: you and the responder trade; neither is the backing.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, and ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode. Use Envelope Follower (right-click a parameter → Modulate, or the Mapping browser) to turn audio into control.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track; [FUNC] + the LFO/parameter pages route an LFO or input to a destination. Use a track’s LFO retrig and conditional trigs to make its behaviour depend on changing state.
  • MicroFreak: the responder voice — select its engine with Type; the front-panel Cutoff, LFO, and envelope are the parameters Live’s follower will drive over MIDI/CV.
  • Recorder or phone: capture the duet in one pass; do not edit the machine’s reactions afterward — its mistakes are the evidence.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Build the listener: analysis → response

Phase focus: wire your live input so it drives the machine voice. Follow the steps in order.

human input (MicroFreak / voice) → Live Envelope Follower (level + density) → machine voice parameters
Do this
  1. Route your live input (play the MicroFreak by hand, or sing) into an audio track and put an Envelope Follower on it.
  2. Map the follower to a second, machine voice — open its filter as you get louder, push its LFO rate as you get busier.
  3. Add one contrary mapping: when your input drops below a threshold (you stop), trigger the machine to start its own phrase — so silence is an input, not an off switch.
  4. Test by playing a loud surge, then stopping dead. Confirm the machine surges, then takes its turn.

Expected result

A machine voice that demonstrably changes with your level and density, and that does something deliberate when you go quiet — a responder, not a clip.

Phase 2 — ~20 minutes

Play the duet

Phase focus: improvise a two-way exchange and record it in one pass. Follow the steps in order.

Do this
  1. Arm recording and play a short statement, then leave room and listen to what the machine answers.
  2. React to the machine in turn — push when it pushes, withdraw when it crowds you — so the shape comes from the trade.
  3. Deliberately stop once, fully, to hear the machine carry the silence and take its own turn.
  4. Do not edit the take. Keep the machine’s wrong-footed responses; they are proof it was reacting, not playing back.

Expected result

A few minutes where the form is visibly produced by the exchange — you can point to a moment the machine “heard” a move of yours and answered it.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The machine sounds the same no matter what you doThe follower range is too narrow, or the mapping is too subtle to hearWiden the mapped range and pick a destination with obvious effect (filter cutoff, voice on/off) before adding subtle ones
When you stop, the machine just stops tooNo contrary mapping — silence reads as “off” not “your turn”Add a low-threshold trigger so dropping below it launches the machine’s own phrase
Verify against the record

A/B your duet against a stretch of Voyager at matched loudness — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, gain-matched with a Utility. Then answer two questions in writing.

Could a listener tell the machine was hearing you?

Answer

The proof is correlation a stranger could spot — your surge and its surge land together, your silence changes its behaviour. If your machine’s output would be identical with the input muted, you built an accompaniment, not a listener. Re-check that the follower is actually driving an audible parameter.

Did the machine ever answer rather than mirror?

Answer

It should at least once do the opposite of you — thin out when you surge, or speak into your silence. That contrariness is what makes Lewis’s system feel like a partner with its own mind rather than a delay line. If yours only mirrors, your mappings are all positive; add an inverse or threshold response.

  • And one more line — name the single moment the machine most convincingly “heard” you, and what mapping produced it.
