Replicate: psychoacoustic byproducts (difference tones) as intended material

Copy the compositional engine of Maryanne Amacher’s ear-tone work: tune two precise sustained tones so close that the listening ear itself produces a third pitch, then move one tone so that emergent pitch becomes the melody. You compose the byproduct, not the source tones. Build a short take on the rig and compare its behaviour to the record — at a safe, moderate volume.

The point

This drill teaches the record’s core mechanism: treat the perceptual byproduct — the difference tone the ear and room generate from two close, precise tones — as the actual material of the piece. The line you write is not in the file; it is what the listener’s hearing manufactures. You steal the structure (compose the byproduct), never Amacher’s tones or frequencies. Do only the steps that make the emergent tone audible and musical.

The system you are replicating
SourceMaryanne Amacher — Sound Characters (Music for Sleeping & Waking Minds) (1999), the otoacoustic-emission pieces
The effectTones tuned so precisely that your own ear emits additional pitches — you clearly hear notes that are not in the recording; the room and the ear become instruments alongside the speakers
The mechanismCompose with two (or more) close, precisely-tuned tones so the resulting difference tone (f2 − f1) carries the melodic and structural content; the byproduct is the intended line, not an accident

Safety — read before you make a sound

Amacher’s otoacoustic-emission work was historically presented at high sound-pressure levels. Do not chase loudness. High SPL can permanently damage hearing, and the effect does not require it: difference tones can be coaxed at moderate, safe levels with tones that are close in frequency and sustained. Set your monitoring to a comfortable, conversational level and leave it there. If a tone makes you wince, wince away, or feel pressure in your ears, it is too loud — turn it down. Take breaks. Protect your hearing; the goal is to make your ear produce a tone, not to overpower it.

Before you start
  • Monitoring set to a moderate, comfortable level before anything plays — and a commitment not to raise it chasing the effect (see Safety above)
  • A tuner or frequency readout you can trust: Live’s Tuner device on the master, or a spectrum/frequency meter, so you can see the exact Hz of each tone and the gap between them
  • Digitakt II synced to Live (lab 002’s clock setup), MicroFreak on its input
  • Pen and paper for a difference-tone chart — transcribed from your own ear before you build: at each tuning, what pitch does the ear hear that the meter does not show?
  • What you will make: a short replica of Amacher’s ear-tone behaviour. Focus on this effect: two precise tones whose difference tone is heard as a third, moving pitch — composed on purpose, at a safe level.
  • What you will turn off: any third oscillator, sample, or melody that actually plays the line — if a real source plays the tune, you have hidden whether the byproduct works.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF share the mechanism: the audible line is a perceptual difference tone (f2 − f1), not a sound present in the file.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are timbre or rig limits, not a different mechanism. This replica stays in the logbook as study evidence; it is never released.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to a stretch of Sound Characters at a moderate level. Sit still, let your ears settle, and notice the pitches that seem to arrive from inside your head rather than from the speakers. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Which pitches feel like they are in front of you, and which feel like they are inside your ear?
    Answer

    The sustained source tones sit out in the room, with the speakers. The extra pitches — the ones that appear, shimmer, and move slightly when you turn your head — seem to come from inside the ear itself. Those interior pitches are the otoacoustic emissions / difference tones; they are the point.

  2. If you move your head or cover one ear, what happens to the extra tones?
    Answer

    They change — they can shift in level, location, or even pitch, because they depend on how the two source tones combine at your ear and in the room. A sound that is truly in the recording would not behave this way. This instability is the tell that you are hearing a perceptual byproduct, not a recorded note.

  3. Is the “melody” you follow made of the source tones, or of something they produce together?
    Answer

    Of something they produce together. The source tones are often nearly static or move only slightly; the line you actually follow is the difference between them, which moves as the gap between the two tones changes. The composition lives in the byproduct.

  4. Does the piece push you with volume, or hold you with precise tuning?
    Answer

    It holds you with precision. The effect depends on the exact frequency relationship between the tones, not on force. At a controlled level, closely-tuned sustained tones already generate the emissions — loudness only adds risk, not music. Keep it safe.

Now build the behaviour you described — two precise tones, a third pitch that is not in the file. Do not build from memory, and do not turn it up.

  • Structural target: the heard line is a difference tone (f2 − f1), never a third oscillator playing the tune. The two source tones carry no melody on their own.
  • Tones: two sustained, near-sine, precisely-tuned voices of your own — not Amacher’s frequencies — close enough that their difference falls in a low, audible range.
  • Motion: the melody comes from moving the gap between the tones; one tone glides while the other holds, so f2 − f1 traces a line.
  • Level: moderate and fixed. The whole effect is achieved by tuning, not by SPL.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson tells you to restart.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Generate two precise tones and find the difference tone

Phase focus: make two close, precisely-tuned tones and hear the pitch they create together. Follow the steps in order. Keep the level moderate throughout.

Do this
  1. Set a near-sine engine and hold one steady tone — say a mid pitch around A4. Use little or no filter movement; you want a clean, sustained voice. Set its level so the monitor is comfortable, then leave the monitor alone.
  2. On a second track, run Operator (or a second oscillator) as a sine and play a second sustained tone a small interval away. Open the Tuner on the master and read both frequencies in Hz so you know the exact gap, f2 − f1.
  3. Slowly close the gap from a few Hz up to a few dozen Hz. Below ~20 Hz you should hear beating (a slow throb); as the gap widens past hearing’s low limit, that throb resolves into a low pitch — the difference tone. Note in your chart the gap in Hz and the pitch you hear.
  4. Confirm the heard tone is the byproduct: cover one ear, or nudge one source by a Hz, and watch the third pitch move while the meter still shows only two tones.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from the reference and use Operator or Wavetable into Auto Filter, with clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — Generate two precise tones and find the difference tone — as the smallest Live set that proves Compose with two (or more) close, precisely-tuned tones so the resulting difference tone (f2 − f1) carries the melodic and structural content; the byproduct is the intended line, not an accident.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects Tones tuned so precisely that your own ear emits additional pitches — you clearly hear notes that are not in the recording; the room and the ear become instruments alongside the speakers.
  1. Use Octatrack MKII for this phase: translate Generate two precise tones and find the difference tone into Flex/Static machines, slices, recorder buffers, scenes, the crossfader, and main-out recording.
  2. Program the pattern, source, or control path so it demonstrates Compose with two (or more) close, precisely-tuned tones so the resulting difference tone (f2 − f1) carries the melodic and structural content; the byproduct is the intended line, not an accident, not just the surface of the reference.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

Two sustained tones on the meter, and a clearly audible third pitch that is not on the meter — whose pitch tracks the gap f2 − f1 — all at a safe, fixed level.

Phase 2 — ~20 minutes

Compose using the byproduct as the line

Phase focus: turn the difference tone into a moving melody by moving the gap. Follow the steps in order. Level stays moderate and fixed.

tone A (f1) + tone B (f2) → ear + room → emergent difference tone (f2 − f1) = the line
Do this
  1. Hold tone A perfectly steady. Automate tone B’s pitch to glide slowly — because the heard line is f2 − f1, moving B alone moves the byproduct melody. Write the line you want the ear to hear, then back-calculate where B must sit for each note.
  2. Use the MicroFreak as the moving voice if you prefer hands-on tuning: glide one tone gently by hand while the other holds, listening to the third pitch rise and fall. Keep both voices near-sine and clean.
  3. Gate the tone pair rhythmically — sequence short sustains that start and stop the two tones together so the difference tone appears in phrases. The Digitakt II punctuates; it must not play the melody itself.
  4. Record a short take. Watch that nothing in the project actually sounds the melody: only A and B play, and the tune lives entirely in their difference. Keep the monitor level where it has been.

Expected result

A take where the followed melody is a difference tone produced by your ear, moving as the gap between two clean source tones changes — with no oscillator anywhere playing that melody, at a safe level.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The melody is clearly “in” the track, stable on headphones for anyoneA third voice is doubling the line, or the source tones drift enough to be heard as the tuneMute everything but A and B; if the melody survives, it is a real voice — remove it. The line must exist only as f2 − f1
The difference tone moves the wrong way or jumpsYou moved the wrong source, or both at once, so the gap changed unpredictablyHold A fixed and move only B; chart f2 − f1 for each target note and tune B to it precisely
Verify against the record

A/B your take against Sound Characters at matched loudness — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, levels gain-matched with a Utility — and keep that matched level moderate and safe; do not raise it for the comparison. Then answer two questions in writing.

Is the line you follow a perceptual byproduct, or a sound in the file?

Answer

Test it the way you tested the record: cover one ear, move your head, or nudge one source tone by a Hz. If the melody shifts, dims, or relocates while the meter still shows only two tones, you have replicated the mechanism — the tune is the difference tone (f2 − f1) made by your hearing. If the line is rock-solid and identical to any listener, a real voice is carrying it; remove that voice and re-take.

Did you hold a safe level, and does the effect still work?

Answer

It should. The emissions come from precise, close tuning, not from SPL — if you needed loudness to hear the byproduct, your tones were not clean or close enough. Re-tune toward pure sines and a tighter, more controlled gap, and the third pitch returns at a comfortable level. If anything caused ear pressure or discomfort, stop, lower the level, and rest your hearing before continuing.
