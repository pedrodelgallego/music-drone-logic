Replicate: Kelly Moran — Ultraviolet

Copy the reference's working mechanism, not its style. Build a short original study with Live 12, Digitakt II, MicroFreak, and the MOTU M4, then A/B it against the reference at matched loudness. Here the mechanism is fusion: prepared-piano timbres fused with synth into one hybrid voice — the acoustic preparation and the synthesizer are blended so tightly they read as a single instrument, not piano-plus-pad.

The point

This drill teaches one thing: that two sources — a struck, prepared piano and a sustained synthesizer — can be bonded so tightly they stop sounding like two things and become one hybrid instrument. The reference gives you the clue; the build proves whether you understood it.

Before you start
  • Live 12 set saved as REP-KELLY-MORAN-ULTRAVIOLET.
  • Digitakt II connected by USB for sync and by main outs to MOTU M4 inputs 3/4 — here it carries the prepared-piano source.
  • MicroFreak output connected to MOTU M4 input 1 — here it is your sustained synth partner, bonded to the piano, not a colour beside it.
  • MOTU M4 headphones at the Lab 001 mark; Live tracks set to Monitor Off while recording hardware.
  • Reference on a Live audio track named REF, with Utility inserted for loudness matching.
  • What you will make: a 2-4 minute original study built from one struck/prepared piano-like source and one sustained synth, fused into a single voice — plus a sentence naming how you bonded them.
  • What you will turn off: drums, anything that keeps the piano and the synth audibly separate, and any move that makes them read as two instruments.
  • What you will prove: the piece holds attention as one hybrid timbre — the listener cannot point to where the acoustic ends and the electronic begins.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not a missing mechanism.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to one clear section of Kelly Moran — Ultraviolet. A prepared piano is struck — buzzing, muted, metallic strings — and a synthesizer sustains underneath it, but they do not sit as two layers. The attack of the piano and the body of the synth share one envelope and one space, so a single bell-like hybrid voice rings out. Try to find the seam between acoustic and electronic. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What makes the piano and the synth read as one voice rather than two?
    Answer

    Do not name the mood first. Name the bond: the synth fades in under the piano's attack, the two share the same decay tail, the same reverb, and a matched pitch — so the ear hears one strike, not a note plus a pad.

  2. Which device or path could create that hybrid in your studio?
    Answer

    Pick a pair: a struck/muted-string sample on the Digitakt II for the attack, plus a MicroFreak pad tuned to the same pitch for the body. The bond should come from tuning, envelope, EQ, and shared reverb — not from playing them loud together.

  3. What should stay boring so the fusion stays audible?
    Answer

    Keep the part plain — one repeated figure or a fixed dyad. With no busy rhythm or melody to hide behind, whether the two sources truly bond is exposed, which is exactly the point.

  4. What would prove you copied the system rather than the style?
    Answer

    Your study should sound nothing like Moran yet still read as one fused hybrid timbre. If a listener can instantly say "that's a piano and that's a pad," the mechanism is still missing.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II sound and timing: press [SRC] for sample/source settings, [FLTR] for filter, [AMP] for envelope/level, [LFO] for modulation, [FX] for sends, and [TEMPO] for tempo and swing.
  • Digitakt II sync: press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync. Use [YES] and the arrow keys to turn clock and transport receive on or off.
  • MicroFreak: use the Preset encoder to choose a plain sustained sound, Type to choose the oscillator, Cutoff and Resonance for the filter, the amp envelope to match the piano's decay, and Save only after the fusion behaves correctly. Here you tune it to bond with the piano, not to sit beside it.
  • MOTU M4: MicroFreak usually enters input 1. Digitakt II main L/R enter inputs 3/4. Inputs 3/4 have no front-panel gain knobs, so set their record level from the Digitakt II Main Volume.
  • Sound target: one struck piano-like source and one sustained synth tuned and shaped so they ring as a single hybrid voice.
  • Recording target: peaks around -12 dBFS in Live before any processing.
  • Copy target: a solo flip between REF and your print should reveal the same kind of fusion, not the same notes.
  • Variation rule: change one variable per pass and write it down.

Phase 1 · ~20 min

Put the reference in Live and write the hypothesis

Do this
  1. Drop the reference onto a track named REF.
  2. Loop the clearest 30-60 seconds — one passage where piano and synth clearly fuse into one voice.
  3. Add Utility to REF and lower it until it is comfortable.
  4. Write: “The mechanism is ___.” Use the listening tag if you need a first guess: piano and synth as one voice.
  5. Write the one thing you will not copy: sound, melody, sample, room, mix polish, or performance style.
  6. Create tracks named PIANO, SYNTH, and PRINT.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Kelly Moran — Ultraviolet and use a struck-string Sampler patch plus Operator or Wavetable, bonded through one Auto Filter and one Reverb send.
  2. Build the phase goal — Put the reference in Live and write the hypothesis — as the smallest Live set that proves prepared-piano timbres fused with synth into one hybrid voice.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects how tightly the two sources bond.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: translate Put the reference in Live and write the hypothesis into one FM voice with a sharp percussive attack and one sustaining voice tuned to it, so the two operators read as a single hybrid strike.
  2. Program the sources and tuning so they demonstrate piano and synth as one voice, not just the surface of Ultraviolet.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same fusion stays audible.
Expected result

You have a looped reference, three working tracks, and one written hypothesis before any patching starts.

Phase 2 · ~35 min

Bond one piano source and one synth into a single voice

Build the smallest pair that can prove the hypothesis. Keep the part plain. The point is to make a struck source and a sustained source ring as one instrument, then keep them bonded.

Do this
  1. Write one system sentence: “This piece works by bonding ___ and ___ into one voice, while forbidding any move that splits them.”
  2. On the Digitakt II, load a muted or struck-string sample for the attack. [TRK] + [TRIG 1] selects track 1.
  3. On the MicroFreak, choose one sustained oscillator Type, tune it to the same pitch as the piano hits, and shape its amp envelope to share the piano's decay tail.
  4. Send both sources to one shared Reverb so they sit in the same space — this is most of the bond.
  5. Add only devices that fuse them: EQ Eight to overlap their bands, Saturator for a shared edge, Echo, and a Glue or bus compressor so they breathe as one.
  6. Record a short proof piece. If you can name "piano" and "pad" separately on first listen, the fusion is not there yet.
  7. Rewrite the system sentence after the take. The corrected sentence is the real result.
  8. Export the proof and keep the system sentence beside it.

Phase 3 · ~15 min

Route the studio so the print is clean

Make the signal path boring and dependable. If the recording path is confusing, you will misdiagnose the mechanism.

MicroFreak · input 1 + Digitakt II · inputs 3/4 → MOTU M4 → USB → Live 12 · Monitor Off
Do this
  1. Plug MicroFreak into input 1 — it is the sustained half of the voice, so set its gain carefully while watching Live.
  2. Plug Digitakt II left/right into inputs 3/4 — it carries the struck piano source.
  3. Set hardware tracks to Monitor Off. This prevents doubled monitoring.
  4. Arm one record track at a time and check peaks. Aim for -12 dBFS, not “as loud as possible.”
  5. If inputs 3/4 are too quiet or too hot, adjust Digitakt II Main Volume and write the position down.
  6. Record 60-90 seconds of the fused voice in motion.
  7. Press ⌘ J to consolidate the best section into a clean clip named PRINT-A.

Phase 4 · ~20 min

Make the system map and the A/B proof

Draw the mechanism, then test the print against the reference. The map is not decoration; it is how you find what failed.

Do this
  1. Put REF and PRINT-A on adjacent tracks.
  2. Use Utility to match loudness before judging.
  3. Listen to 10 seconds of REF, then 10 seconds of PRINT-A.
  4. Write one sentence: “The first thing that gives mine away is ___.”
  5. Fix only that one thing and record PRINT-B.
  6. Keep both prints. The difference between A and B is the lesson.

Final check

Play the reference and your best print at matched loudness, then answer these in the logbook:

  • What mechanism did you copy?
    Answer

    Name the mechanism, not the genre: a struck acoustic source and a sustained synth bonded by pitch, envelope, and shared space into one hybrid voice with no audible seam.

  • Where does that mechanism live in your setup?
    Answer

    Point to the path: a struck/muted-string sample on the Digitakt II, a tuned MicroFreak pad, and the shared reverb and bus compressor that fuse them — or a Sampler-plus-Operator pair in Live.

  • What did you deliberately not copy?
    Answer

    The answer should be material: Moran's exact preparations, her harmony, or her phrasing. The workshop copies systems, not identity.

  • What rule carries into the related lab?
    Answer

    Write it as a usable instruction. Example: “If a listener can name the piano and the synth separately, the two sources have not fused into one voice yet.”

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • System diff sentence: “I thought the system worked like ___; after building it, I discovered it worked like ___.”
