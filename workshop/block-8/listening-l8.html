Block 8 · Programme L8 · Listening

The Student’s Own Archive — three years, heard as a stranger

Three listening sessions where the canon is replaced by your own filed work. You have spent three years analyzing other people’s systems; this programme points the same instruments at the archive with your name on it, and ends with one sentence: the thesis of your voice.

Listening programme · 3 sessions · one full cycle · feeds labs 032–035, R8, and capstone C7 · scaffold

The point

A voice is not what you intend; it is what keeps happening when you think you are doing something else. Three years of output contain a pattern you have never been far enough away to see. This programme manufactures that distance — you listen to your own work as a stranger would, through the seven layers, and write down the system that was there all along.

Listening is the loop

These sessions are the first step of the block’s operating loop, not homework beside it. The thesis sentence this programme produces is the hypothesis that lab 034 builds and capstone C7 defends. The frame is no longer the six questions — at this altitude it is the seven layers, the same worksheet the final defense uses.

  • Finish by writing the thesis sentence before lab 034 begins.

New concepts — in plain words

Four terms carry this page. Here is what each one means, before any of them is used.

ConceptIn plain words
Hearing as a stranger Listening to your own piece with no access to your memories of making it — only what is actually in the audio. The render is all a stranger gets; the intentions, the struggles, and the abandoned versions are inadmissible. If a quality is not audible, it does not exist for this exercise.
The recurring system The structural habit that shows up across pieces that were supposed to be different: the same relationship to repetition, the same place the energy lives, the same thing your hands always end up riding. Voice is this recurrence, located — not the genre, not the gear.
The thesis sentence One written sentence naming the recurring system: “my pieces are X done to Y under constraint Z.” It must be structural enough that a stranger could check it against the audio, and falsifiable enough that some of your pieces fail it. A sentence every piece passes is a horoscope, not a thesis.
Retrospective listening Following one artist across decades and watching the voice evolve: what they kept, what they shed, what only became visible late. The model matters because your thesis sentence is a snapshot — the retrospective shows what a voice looks like as a moving object.

How to listen — the seven-layer frame

Every piece in this programme gets the same seven questions — the graduate frame, the same worksheet capstones file and the final defense runs on. A few honest lines per layer; vagueness is the only wrong answer.

The seven layers — asked of each of your own pieces
What is the sound: oscillator, sample, field recording, tape, feedback?Material
Where does audio travel, change level, clip, degrade, widen, sum?Signal
What is MIDI, CV, automation, modulation, probability, gesture?Control
What is clocked, what drifts, what loops, what resets?Time
Which choices were rules, chance, generative behavior — and which were taste?Decision
What did the maker’s hands actually do, and when?Human
What was printed, named, versioned, documented — and what is unrecoverable?Archive

The method is three passes per piece. First pass: just listen, no notes — let the piece be three years younger than you. Second pass: layers one through six. Third pass: the archive layer only, answered first from memory and then checked against the piece’s filed documentation. The gap between the two answers is data for R8.

Session 1 — Records: the archive, in chronological order

Select six to ten pieces spanning all three years — at minimum: the lab 003 piece, C1, C2, one Block 4 or 5 piece, C5, and C6’s set recording. Play them in the order they were made, as a stranger auditioning an unknown artist’s back catalogue. Run the seven layers on each, then read the worksheets side by side and hunt the recurrence.

Session 1 · Worksheet

One row per piece, then one column read top to bottom

File one seven-layer worksheet per piece. Then make the summary grid below — pieces as rows, layers as columns — and read it vertically: one layer at a time, across three years. The voice lives in the columns that barely change.

Summary grid — one row per piece
Piece, year, block—
One line per layer: material · signal · control · time · decision · human · archive—
What a stranger would say this artist always does—
What the maker thought the piece was about (memory — admissible only in this row)—
Listen for

Not your favorite moments — your repetitions. Which layer is most consistent across three years? Which piece is the outlier, and is the outlier a failed experiment or the voice trying to get out? Where does the energy always live: in the material, the time layer, the riding hand?

After — verify

Can you name, in writing, one structural habit that appears in at least four of the pieces — and one piece where it is absent? If every habit you found is a gear fact (“always the Digitakt”) rather than a system fact (“always one element eroding under repetition”), re-read the grid vertically again: the gear is the alibi, the system is the voice.

Session 2 — Samples: one of your own pieces, deconstructed

Session 2 · Deconstruction

Trace one piece to its sources and prints — by ear first

Pick one of your own Year 1 pieces — ideally the one R8 will rebuild. Loop a short passage and split everything you hear into sources (what the raw material was: which engine, which capture, which resample generation) and prints (what was committed to audio, when, and what state it froze). Work entirely by ear, as if the piece were someone else’s. Only when the worksheet is full do you open the piece’s print map and lab report — never the project file — and mark every disagreement.

Worksheet — one row per audible element
Element (e.g. kick, drone bed, ridden filter voice)—
Source, as heard: engine / capture / resample, and its chain—
Print, as heard: committed audio or live device? at what stage?—
What the print map actually says—
Verdict: ear wrong · documentation wrong · both silent—
Note

Three years ago you wrote that print map believing it was complete. Every row where the documentation cannot answer the question — not wrong, just silent — is an archive/print ambiguity, this block’s named fault class, found in the wild. Count them.

This worksheet is a blueprint

In replication lab R8 you rebuild this piece from its documentation alone. Every row marked “documentation wrong” or “both silent” is a place the rebuild will fail — predicted in advance, which is exactly what a hypothesis is.

After — verify

Does every row have a verdict? A row you could not check because the documentation never addressed it gets “both silent” — that is a finding, not a blank. Full worksheet template forthcoming; the columns above are enough to run the session now.

Session 3 — Video: one retrospective, a voice evolving

One career-spanning retrospective, chosen by you and watched twice — once as an audience member, once for the mechanics of evolution: what the artist kept, what they abandoned, and what they only understood about their own work decades later. Candidates: any of the Sisters with Transistors subjects (Oram, Derbyshire, Ciani, Spiegel — the film itself works as the retrospective), Tony Conrad’s Completely in the Present, or a career-spanning Autechre or Eno interview. Pick the artist whose problem feels most like yours.

01 Your chosen retrospective — student-selected a voice evolving

This slot is deliberately yours to fill. Choose one artist retrospective — a documentary, a filmed career-spanning interview, an archival portrait — covering at least two decades of one artist’s work. The subject does not need to share your genre; they need to have a voice that demonstrably changed while remaining theirs. Write the choice and one sentence on why into the logbook before watching.

Listen for
  • The invariant — the thing present in the earliest and latest work, however differently dressed
  • The sheddings — techniques, gear, and even whole aesthetics abandoned without the voice dying
  • The late self-understanding — moments where the artist names something about their early work they clearly could not have named at the time
After — verify

Write the artist’s thesis sentence — the same one-sentence form you will write for yourself. If you cannot, the retrospective was watched as a fan, not as a colleague: rewatch the sections covering the earliest and latest work back to back, and name only what survived the gap.

Find it: Sisters with Transistors · Tony Conrad — Completely in the Present · Autechre career interview · Eno career interview

Replication lesson — forthcoming (lands with Block 8 authoring; in this programme the replica is the thesis sentence itself)

  1. The invariant — what is recognizably the same artist across the whole span, named structurally?
  2. The sheddings — what was abandoned, and did the voice survive the loss? (It did. Note what that implies about gear.)
  3. The pivots — where did the voice change direction, and what forced it: technology, failure, boredom, a room?
  4. The archive — how does this artist’s old work survive: prints, scores, documentation, myth? What survived because it was written down — and what is gone?

Deliverable — the archive analysis and the thesis sentence

File all worksheets in the logbook. Then write the thesis sentence of your voice — one sentence, structural, falsifiable, naming the system that recurs across three years. It is carried into lab 034 as the brief for the signature piece, and defended at C7 against the EP itself. Naming it in advance makes it a commitment instead of a story you tell afterwards.

Pass standard
Minimum passThree sessions filed: the chronological archive grid, the sources-and-prints deconstruction with verdicts, the retrospective worksheet — and one thesis sentence, written before 034 begins.
Solid passThe thesis sentence is structural and falsifiable: a stranger could check it against your renders, and at least one of your own pieces fails it.
Advanced transferYou can say how the thesis sentence will have to change in the next three years — the voice heard as a moving object, the retrospective’s lesson applied to yourself.
  • Valid: “one element erodes under repetition while everything else holds still” — structural, checkable, and two of the pieces fail it
  • Valid: “the human role is always a single ridden parameter; everything else is sequenced” — a system fact about the human layer
  • Invalid: “dark hypnotic textures with deep bass” — a mood board, not a system; no piece could fail it
Programme L8 — pass gate
  • Six to ten seven-layer worksheets — your own pieces, chronological, three passes each
  • The summary grid, read vertically, with one named recurrence and one named outlier
  • One deconstruction worksheet — sources vs prints, every row carrying a verdict, ambiguities counted

  • Your thesis sentence — structural, falsifiable, filed before lab 034 opens
