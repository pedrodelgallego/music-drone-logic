Replicate: splice-rhythm and gesture as the concrète form-bearer

Copy the structural engine of Beatriz Ferreyra’s Médisances (1968): the rhythm and shape of the piece come from where and how recorded sounds are cut and joined, not from a beat or a synth pattern. Record a handful of physical sound gestures, then edit them so the splices themselves carry the motion. Compare your montage’s sense of movement to the record.

The point

This drill teaches the record’s core mechanism: in classic musique concrète, the edit is choreography. Sounds are hurled, swept, and cut so that the splices create rhythm and bodily momentum — the form is built in the cutting and joining, not laid over a grid. You steal the structure — splice-rhythm and gesture as the form-bearer — never Ferreyra’s material. Do only the steps that make the montage gestures audible as the thing driving the piece.

The system you are replicating

The effectA piece with a strongly physical, gestural sense of motion — sounds thrown, swept, and abruptly cut so you feel bodily momentum and impact, as if the sounds were being handled and hurled across the space

Before you start
  • Digitakt II synced to Live (lab 002’s clock setup), MicroFreak on its input, a recorder or phone for capturing objects and voice
  • Pen and paper for a gesture map — transcribed from the record before you build: list the recurring motions you hear (a sweep, a hurl-and-stop, a tumble, a sudden cut) and roughly how long each lasts
  • What you will make: a short replica of Médisances’ sense of motion. Focus on this effect: recorded sounds cut and joined so the splices themselves create rhythm and physical momentum.
  • What you will turn off: any metronomic beat and any synth groove. There is no grid-locked pulse and no looping pattern — if a steady beat or a repeating sequencer line is carrying the time, the mechanism is hidden. The rhythm must come from the splices.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF share the mechanism: form built from the placement and shape of cuts, not from a beat.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are timbre, source choice, or rig limits, not a different structural logic. This replica stays in the logbook as study evidence; it is never released.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to a clear minute of Médisances — pick a stretch with obvious motion. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is carrying the time — a beat, or the edits themselves?
    Answer

    The edits. There is no metronomic pulse and no synth pattern underneath. Your sense of when things happen comes entirely from where Ferreyra places her cuts — the timing of entries, the length she lets a sound run before she stops it, the gap she leaves before the next gesture. The splices are the rhythm section.

  2. Pick one moment that feels physical — what gesture is it, and how was it made with edits?
    Answer

    Usually a sound that is swept up and then hard-cut, or hurled in and abruptly stopped, or tumbled through a fast string of fragments. The physicality comes from the shape of the edit: a quick fade-in that accelerates, a clean cut with no tail so the sound seems to hit a wall, a reversal that makes a sound rush toward its own attack. The motion is in the handling, not the source.

  3. How do consecutive sounds relate — do they crossfade smoothly, or butt against each other?
    Answer

    Often they butt hard. A clean, abrupt join makes the second sound feel like an impact or a recoil from the first. Where she does fade, it is shaped to throw the ear forward, not to smooth things over. The articulation between sounds — hard cut versus glide — is itself a rhythmic and gestural choice.

  4. Does any steady loop or pattern emerge to organize the piece — or is each gesture placed by hand?
    Answer

    No loop. Material recurs and is varied, but nothing settles into a repeating grid. Each gesture is placed individually, by ear, for what it does to the one before and after it. The form is a sequence of decisions about cutting and joining, not a pattern set running.

Now build the motion you described — rhythm made of splices, not of a beat. Do not build from memory.

  • Structural target: the whole piece is recorded gestures, cut and joined by hand. No metronome, no looping pattern — pacing comes only from the placement and shape of the edits.
  • Material: your own physical sound gestures — swept MicroFreak tones, struck and scraped objects, voice — not Ferreyra’s sounds.
  • Gesture: each edit has a shape — a sweep, a hurl-and-stop, a tumble, a hard cut, a reversal — chosen for the bodily motion it produces.
  • Rhythm: the felt pulse of the piece lives in where cuts land and how long sounds run, exactly as on the record.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson tells you to restart.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Collect and record source gestures

Phase focus: capture a small set of physical sound gestures to cut up later. Follow the steps in order.

Do this
  1. Record three or four swept gestures — open the filter fast under a held tone, ride pitch up and let it snap off, run an LFO so the sound writhes. Capture each as a separate clip into Live.
  2. Record three or four physical objects — a struck pan, a scraped surface, a dropped book, paper tearing — anything with a clear attack and a sense of being handled.
  3. Record a few voice gestures with no words you can parse — a sharp breath, a swooping vowel, a whispered hiss — so the voice reads as material, not speech.
  4. Import everything onto separate audio tracks in the Arrangement and label each clip by its motion (“sweep-up,” “hit,” “tear,” “breath”), not by its source.

Phase 2 — ~25 minutes

Cut and join into splice-rhythm

Phase focus: build the piece by editing — the rhythm emerges from where and how you cut and join. Follow the steps in order.

recorded gestures → cuts & joins (placement, length, fades, reversals) → rhythmic montage
Do this
  1. Turn the grid off — disable snapping so cuts land by ear, not on bars. There is no metronome in this take.
  2. Drag gestures into a single timeline and cut them by hand. Decide each edit by what motion it makes: a hard butt-join for impact, a fast fade-in for a sweep, a reversed clip so a sound rushes toward its attack.
  3. Build the rhythm from placement and length — vary the gaps and how long each sound runs before you cut it. Let a fast string of short fragments tumble, then hold one gesture and stop it dead.
  4. Optionally load one or two of your sharpest gestures as one-shots and trigger them by hand for percussive splice accents — placed by feel against the montage, still with no looping pattern running.
  5. Consolidate the finished edit (⌘ J) so the splice-rhythm is committed as one continuous gesture, then listen end to end.

A short montage whose pulse and momentum you can feel — and which lives entirely in the cuts. Mute nothing, add no beat: the timing reads as deliberate because the edits place it there.

Verify against the record

A/B your montage against the same stretch of Médisances at matched loudness — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, levels gain-matched with a Utility. Then answer two questions in writing.

Is the rhythm of your piece carried by the splices, with no beat or pattern underneath?

Answer

Solo your timeline and ask what would happen if you removed every recurring pulse — there should be none to remove, because the timing lives in the individual cuts. If your sense of motion collapses the moment a Digitakt loop is muted, the loop was carrying the time and the mechanism is hidden. Ferreyra’s discipline is that the edit alone makes the rhythm — re-edit until the cuts, not a pattern, are placing the pulse.

Does a gesture in your version feel as physical as one in theirs — thrown, swept, or hard-stopped?

Answer

Pick your most physical moment and their most physical moment and compare the motion, not the timbre. The bodily feeling comes from the shape of the edit — an accelerating fade, a clean cut with no tail, a reversal that rushes toward an impact. If yours feels softer, your joins are probably too smooth; sharpen the cut so the sound hits a wall the way theirs does.
