Replicate: one extended vocal technique explored exhaustively

Copy the structural engine of Joan La Barbara’s Voice Is the Original Instrument: take a single extended vocal technique and make a whole piece from nothing but its variations. Build a short take on the rig and compare its discipline to the record.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record’s core mechanism — the voice treated as a sound source to be investigated, where one extended technique is the entire material and the piece is the exhaustive survey of its variations. You steal the structure — pick a technique, exhaust it, let nothing else in — never La Barbara’s actual sounds. Do only the steps that make the single-technique discipline audible.

The system you are replicating
SourceJoan La Barbara — Voice Is the Original Instrument (1976)
The effectA catalog of extended vocal techniques — multiphonics, circular singing, vocal trills — presented as raw sonic material in their own right, the voice investigated rather than sung through
The mechanismOne extended vocal technique is taken as the sole material and exhausted: every variation of that single technique becomes the whole piece, with no melody, no lyric, and no second sound source introduced to carry it

Before you start
  • Digitakt II synced to Live (lab 002’s clock setup), MicroFreak on its input, a microphone into Live or the Digitakt II input
  • Pen and paper for a variation chart — transcribed from the record before you build: which single technique is in play, and how many distinct variations of it you can count before anything else enters
  • What you will make: a short replica of Voice Is the Original Instrument’s discipline. Focus on this effect: one extended vocal technique, exhausted across its variations, as the entire piece.
  • What you will turn off: any second technique, any instrument that carries melody, any lyric or pitch line that would let you stop investigating the one sound.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF share the mechanism: a single vocal technique investigated exhaustively, treated as raw material rather than as a means to a tune.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are timbre, your voice, or rig limits, not a different structural logic. This replica stays in the logbook as study evidence; it is never released.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to one piece from Voice Is the Original Instrument — pick one built on a single technique (a circular-breathing drone or a sustained multiphonic). Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is the one technique — and does anything else ever join it?
    Answer

    It is a single extended vocal behaviour — a circular-breathing drone, a multiphonic, a trill — stated and then held as the only thing happening. Nothing else joins it: no instrument, no second voice with a different job. The technique is the piece.

  2. How does it stay interesting if the material never changes — what is actually moving?
    Answer

    The variations move. Within the one technique she shifts the formant, the partials she favours, the breath pressure, the vowel, the micro-pitch — small investigations of the same sound, so attention is held by detail rather than by new material.

  3. Is the voice being sung through to a melody, or investigated as a sound object?
    Answer

    Investigated. There is no tune to follow and no words to parse — the voice is presented as a raw sound source, and you listen to its texture and behaviour the way you would listen to an instrument being examined, not to a song.

  4. How does the piece end — by arriving somewhere, or by exhausting the technique?
    Answer

    By exhaustion, not arrival. It ends when the survey of that one sound is complete — the variations have been run through — rather than at a melodic resolution. The form is “I have shown you this sound fully,” not “the song is over.”

Now build the survey you described — one technique, its variations, nothing else. Do not build from memory.

  • Structural target: the entire piece is one vocal technique plus its variations. No second technique, no instrument carrying a tune, no lyric.
  • Technique: pick one you can actually produce — a circular-breathing drone, a sustained multiphonic, or a vocal trill — chosen because it has variations in it to find.
  • Material: every layer in the project must be a recording of that one technique; the MicroFreak may reinforce or process it but never replace it with a synth voice.
  • End: the piece closes when the variations are exhausted, not when a melody resolves.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson tells you to restart.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Pick one technique and record its variations

Phase focus: gather the single technique’s variations as raw material. Follow the steps in order.

Do this
  1. Choose one technique you can sustain — a circular-breathing drone, a held multiphonic, or a vocal trill — and write it at the top of the chart. That choice is locked for the whole session.
  2. Record many short takes of only that technique — vary one parameter per take: vowel, breath pressure, the partial you lean on, micro-pitch, brightness. Aim for ten or more distinct variations.
  3. Load your best variations across separate Digitakt II tracks (or into Live Simpler) so each track holds one variation of the same technique and nothing else.
  4. Audition the variations solo and discard any that are really a different technique sneaking in — the chart should only list versions of the one sound.

Phase 2 — ~20 minutes

Build the whole piece from the variations alone

Phase focus: sequence the variations into a survey, reinforcing — never replacing — the voice. Follow the steps in order.

one technique’s variations → MicroFreak reinforces / processes the voice → survey ends when variations are exhausted
Do this
  1. Arm recording. Move through the variations one at a time — overlap, cross-fade, or layer them — so the piece is a continuous investigation of the single sound, never a new idea.
  2. Where you want reinforcement, run a voice clip into the MicroFreak or use it to track the voice’s pitch — it thickens or processes the technique, but introduces no melody of its own.
  3. Let detail carry the time: automate filter, formant, or pitch on the variations so attention stays on the texture, not on any tune.
  4. End when the variations are used up — close on the barest version of the technique, with no melodic resolution added to wrap it.

Verify against the record

A/B your take against the chosen piece from Voice Is the Original Instrument at matched loudness — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, levels gain-matched with a Utility. Then answer two questions in writing.

Is your whole piece built from variations of a single technique, with nothing else introduced?

Answer

Scan your track list: if any layer is a second technique, a melody instrument, or a lyric, you broke the mechanism. La Barbara’s discipline is that one sound, fully investigated, is enough to be a whole piece. If yours needed a second idea, you stopped investigating too early — find more variations of the one technique, not more material.

Is the voice investigated as a sound object, or sung through to a tune?

Answer

It should sit as raw material: the listener attends to texture and behaviour, not to a melody. If your take has a hummable line, the voice slipped back into being a means to a tune. Re-take focusing the ear on the sound itself — let the detail of the technique, not a pitch contour, be the thing that moves.
