Replicate: mapping an external structure onto electronic material

Copy the structural method of Else Marie Pade’s Syv Cirkler: take a non-musical plan — a sequence of events, a diagram, a shape — and let it govern the order, length, and density of the sound. Build a short take on the rig and check that the form follows the map, not your taste.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record’s core mechanism: the architecture of the piece comes from outside the music. Pade mapped an extramusical structure onto electronic material, so the form was decided by a plan before a single sound was chosen. You steal the method — translate a structure into sound — never Pade’s tones or her diagram. Do only the steps that make the external structure audible as form.

The system you are replicating
SourceElse Marie Pade — Syv Cirkler (Seven Circles) (1958)
The effectAn early electronic tape work whose shape feels deliberate and non-developmental — sections arrive in an order that the ear cannot explain by musical logic alone, because the logic is borrowed from somewhere else
The mechanismMap an external structure onto electronic material as the form: choose a non-musical structure and let it dictate the order, duration, and density of each section — the plan decides the music

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Digitakt II synced to Live (lab 002’s clock setup), MicroFreak on its input
  • Pen and paper — choose and sketch the external structure on paper before building: draw the diagram, list the events, or trace the shape, then number its parts
  • One firm decision made on paper before the gear is touched: which part of the structure becomes which kind of sound
  • What you will make: a short replica of Syv Cirkler’s method. Focus on this effect: a form whose order, durations, and densities are fixed by a non-musical structure you sketched first.
  • What you will turn off: improvising the form. You do not arrange by feel, you do not extend a section because it sounds good, and you do not reorder for flow — the structure decides it, and you execute.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF share the mechanism: an external plan, translated by explicit rules, becomes the musical form.
  • What counts as success: a listener with your map in hand can point to which part of the structure each section is. Remaining differences are timbre or rig limits, not a different way of deciding form. This replica stays in the logbook as study evidence; it is never released.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to a few minutes of Syv Cirkler. Do not try to enjoy it as a tune — listen for how it is cut into parts. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Do the sections develop into one another, or do they simply succeed one another like items on a list?
    Answer

    They succeed one another. Sections begin and end as discrete blocks rather than growing organically out of what came before; the transitions feel placed, not earned. That blockiness is the giveaway that an external order is being followed.

  2. When a section changes, can you predict the next from the music alone — or does it feel decided elsewhere?
    Answer

    You usually cannot predict it from the music. The next section’s register, density, or duration does not follow from a musical argument, because the sequence is governed by a plan outside the sound. The order is consistent but not musically motivated.

  3. Are section lengths even and arbitrary-feeling, or shaped by phrasing?
    Answer

    They tend to feel measured rather than phrased — durations that seem assigned to a section rather than arrived at by the music breathing. A length set by a plan reads differently from a length set by an ear.

  4. If you imagine a diagram behind the piece, what would the diagram have to specify?
    Answer

    Order, duration, and density at minimum: which section comes when, how long each lasts, and how thick or sparse each is. That triple — sequence, length, weight — is exactly what an external structure can supply and what the method asks you to map.

Now build a piece whose form is decided by a structure of your own — not Pade’s. Do not build from memory.

  • Structural target: every section boundary in your take corresponds to a part of the external structure. There are no sections that exist only because they sounded good.
  • The structure: a concrete non-musical thing of your own — a fireworks sequence, a building’s floor plan, a train timetable, a route on a map. It is chosen and sketched before any sound exists.
  • Mapping rules: written down, explicit, and the same for the whole piece — e.g. each event → one section; size of event → duration; height/position → register; crowding → density.
  • Discipline: when the map and your taste disagree, the map wins. The lesson is the translation, not the tune.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson tells you to restart.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Choose the external structure and write the map

Phase focus: turn a non-musical thing into a score before any sound exists. Follow the steps in order.

Do this
  1. Pick one concrete external structure and sketch it on paper — e.g. a fireworks display read left to right, a floor plan walked room by room, or a station timetable. Number its parts in their natural order.
  2. Write the mapping rules, the same for every part: each event/room/entry → one section; size or weight of the part → section duration; height or position → register; crowding or overlap → density. Decide the rules now and do not change them later.
  3. Run the structure through the rules to produce a score: a numbered list of sections with a length in bars, a register, and a density for each. This is your map.
  4. Lay the map onto the Arrangement timeline as locators — one marker per section at its bar position — so the empty timeline already carries the whole form.

Expected result

A written score derived purely from the external structure, and a Live timeline whose section boundaries are placed before a single note exists — the form decided in advance.

Phase 2 — ~25 minutes

Realize the map as electronic material

Phase focus: fill each marked section with sound that obeys the register and density the map assigned it. Follow the steps in order.

external structure (sketch) → mapping rules (order · duration · register · density) → electronic material laid out as the form
Do this
  1. For each section in turn, dial a tone in the register the map assigned it — sparse, isolated tones for low-density sections, layered or fast material for high-density ones. Let the rule, not the sound, set how busy it is.
  2. Where a section’s density calls for rhythm or texture, program a pattern whose activity matches the assigned density — fewer trigs for sparse, packed trigs for dense — and record it into that section’s span only.
  3. Place each take inside its locators and trim it to the exact bar length on the map. Do not let a section spill past its boundary because it feels unfinished — the duration is fixed.
  4. Render the whole arrangement in one pass and listen end to end with the map in front of you, confirming each section is in its mapped place, length, register, and density.

Expected result

A take in which every section sits where the structure put it, lasts as long as the rule said, and is as thick or thin as the map demanded — a form you can read off the diagram.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The piece flows smoothly and develops — it sounds “composed”You smoothed the joins and let sections grow into each otherRe-render honoring the hard boundaries; the method produces succession, not development — let the seams show
A section was extended or repeated because it was workingImprovising the form crept back inCut it back to its mapped length; if you want more of it, the structure would have had to contain more of it
Two sections sound identical despite mapping to different partsRegister or density rules were applied looselyRe-check the rule for each — a clearly higher register or a visibly denser pattern must distinguish them
Verify against the record

A/B your take against Syv Cirkler at matched loudness — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, levels gain-matched with a Utility. You are not matching timbre; you are matching the kind of form. Then answer two questions in writing.

Could a stranger hold your map and point to which part of the structure each section is?

Answer

That is the whole test. Hand the sketch and the score to someone and play the take; if they can follow along and say “that’s firework three, that’s the corridor,” the external structure became the form. If they cannot, your sections drifted from the map — the durations slipped or the register and density stopped tracking the rule. Re-render against the score, not against your ear.

Does your form feel decided-in-advance the way the record’s does — succession rather than development?

Answer

Like Syv Cirkler, your piece should read as blocks placed in an order from outside, not phrases growing into each other. If yours sounds like a normal arrangement with intros and builds, taste recaptured the form. The proof of the mechanism is that the order is consistent but not musically motivated — it is motivated by the plan. If it feels too musical, you obeyed the ear instead of the map.
