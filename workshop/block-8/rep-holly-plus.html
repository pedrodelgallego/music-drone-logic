Replicate: Holly Herndon & Mat Dryhurst — Holly+

Copy the reference's working mechanism, not its style. Build a short original study with Live 12, Digitakt II, MicroFreak, and the MOTU M4, then A/B it against the reference at matched loudness. Here the mechanism is a model of the artist's own voice — an AI/ML instrument trained on one person — used as a playable synth that re-voices any input in that timbre.

The point

This drill teaches one thing: that an identity can be made into an instrument — a model trained on one voice becomes a synth that anyone can play, so the timbre is fixed while the performance is open. You will fake the principle with a sampled/resynthesised personal voice, since the trained model is not in your rig. The reference gives you the clue; the build proves whether you understood it.

Before you start
  • Live 12 set saved as REP-HOLLY-PLUS.
  • Digitakt II connected by USB for sync and by main outs to MOTU M4 inputs 3/4.
  • MicroFreak output connected to MOTU M4 input 1 when the lesson needs a synth source.
  • A short recording of your own voice (one sustained vowel and one phrase) imported into Live — this is your stand-in for the trained model.
  • MOTU M4 headphones at the Lab 001 mark; Live tracks set to Monitor Off while recording hardware. Reference on a track named REF with Utility for loudness matching.
  • What you will make: a 2-4 minute original study where your own voice, captured once, becomes a playable instrument re-voicing new material — plus a sentence on who authored it.
  • What you will turn off: singing the part live each time; the voice timbre must be fixed material, played, not re-performed.
  • What you will prove: a fixed personal timbre can be driven by MIDI or sequencing to “sing” lines the original take never sang.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not a missing mechanism.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to one clear demonstration of Holly+. Notice the split: the timbre is unmistakably one person, yet the melody, the words, even the player can be anyone. The voice has become an instrument that holds its identity no matter what is played through it. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is the smallest repeated behavior you can point to?
    Answer

    Do not name the mood first. Name the behavior: an input pitch arriving and coming back out in a fixed personal timbre, regardless of what was sung in.

  2. Which device or path could create that behavior in your studio?
    Answer

    Pick one place: a sampled vowel of your own voice loaded into the Digitakt II and played chromatically, or a granular/Simpler instrument in Live built from your voice and triggered by MIDI.

  3. What should stay boring so the mechanism remains audible?
    Answer

    Keep the line plain — a few long notes. If the melody is busy, you cannot hear whether the timbre stayed fixed across pitches.

  4. What would prove you copied the system rather than the style?
    Answer

    Your study should let your fixed voice instrument sing a line you never actually sang. If it only works by recording a new vocal take each time, the “instrument” idea is missing.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Load your voice sample, press [RECORD] for grid recording, place hits on numbered [TRIG] keys, and [PLAY] to hear it. Use [FUNC] + a TRIG for chromatic/keyboard mode to play the sample at different pitches.
  • Digitakt II sound and timing: press [SRC] for sample/source settings, [FLTR] for filter, [AMP] for envelope/level, [LFO] for modulation, [FX] for sends, and [TEMPO] for tempo and swing.
  • Digitakt II sync: press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync. Use [YES] and the arrow keys to turn clock and transport receive on or off.
  • MicroFreak: use the Preset encoder to choose a plain starting sound, Type to choose the oscillator, Cutoff and Resonance for the filter, Matrix for modulation routing, and Save only after the study behaves correctly.
  • MOTU M4: MicroFreak usually enters input 1. Digitakt II main L/R enter inputs 3/4. Inputs 3/4 have no front-panel gain knobs, so set their record level from the Digitakt II Main Volume.
  • Sound target: one captured personal-voice timbre, played as an instrument.
  • Recording target: peaks around -12 dBFS in Live before any processing.
  • Copy target: a solo flip between REF and your print shows the same idea — a fixed voice singing open material.
  • Variation rule: change one variable per pass and write it down.

Phase 1 · ~20 min

Put the reference in Live and write the hypothesis

Do this
  1. Drop the reference onto a track named REF.
  2. Loop the clearest 30-60 seconds — one passage where the fixed voice sings a varying line.
  3. Add Utility to REF and lower it until it is comfortable.
  4. Write: “The mechanism is ___.” Use the listening tag if you need a first guess: a voice model as a playable instrument.
  5. Write the one thing you will not copy: sound, melody, sample, room, mix polish, or performance style.
  6. Record your own voice — one long vowel and one phrase — onto a track named SOURCE.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from Holly+ and build a Simpler or granular instrument from your voice sample, played by MIDI.
  2. Build the phase goal — Put the reference in Live and write the hypothesis — as the smallest Live set that proves a voice model as a playable instrument.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects how fixed the timbre stays across pitches.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: feed a sampled vowel-like FM voice and play it chromatically as a fixed-timbre instrument.
  2. Program the source so it demonstrates a voice model as a playable instrument, not just the surface of Holly+.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

You have a looped reference, a captured personal voice on SOURCE, and one written hypothesis before any playing starts.

Phase 2 · ~35 min

Turn your voice into a playable instrument

Build the smallest instrument that can prove the hypothesis: a fixed personal timbre that sings whatever you sequence. Keep the line plain. The point is to play your voice, not re-sing it.

Do this
  1. Write one system sentence: “This piece works by playing a fixed voice timbre, while forbidding ___.”
  2. Load your vowel sample into a Digitakt II audio track and set it to play chromatically. [TRK] + [TRIG 1] selects track 1.
  3. Sequence a short line — a few long notes the original take never sang.
  4. Use [FLTR] and [AMP] gently so the timbre stays recognisable across pitches.
  5. Add only devices that keep the voice coherent: Utility, EQ Eight, a light Saturator, a touch of Reverb.
  6. Record a short proof piece. If it sounds like a sample pitched up and down, push for legato and formant stability so it reads as one voice.
  7. Rewrite the system sentence after the take. The corrected sentence is the real result.
  8. Export the proof and keep the system sentence beside it.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: build a Simpler/granular instrument from your voice and drive it with a MIDI clip.

Phase 3 · ~15 min

Route the studio so the print is clean

Make the signal path boring and dependable. If the recording path is confusing, you will misdiagnose the mechanism.

MicroFreak · input 1 + Digitakt II · inputs 3/4 → MOTU M4 → USB → Live 12 · Monitor Off
Do this
  1. Plug MicroFreak into input 1 if the study uses it for a counter-layer.
  2. Plug Digitakt II left/right into inputs 3/4 — your voice instrument prints from here.
  3. Set hardware tracks to Monitor Off. This prevents doubled monitoring.
  4. Arm one record track at a time and check peaks. Aim for -12 dBFS, not “as loud as possible.”
  5. If inputs 3/4 are too quiet or too hot, adjust Digitakt II Main Volume and write the position down.
  6. Record 60-90 seconds of the voice instrument.
  7. Press ⌘ J to consolidate the best section into a clean clip named PRINT-A.

Phase 4 · ~20 min

Make the system map and the A/B proof

Draw the mechanism, then test the print against the reference. The map is not decoration; it is how you find what failed.

Do this
  1. Put REF and PRINT-A on adjacent tracks.
  2. Use Utility to match loudness before judging.
  3. Listen to 10 seconds of REF, then 10 seconds of PRINT-A.
  4. Write one sentence: “The first thing that gives mine away is ___.”
  5. Fix only that one thing and record PRINT-B.
  6. Keep both prints. The difference between A and B is the lesson.

Final check

Play the reference and your best print at matched loudness, then answer these in the logbook:

  • What mechanism did you copy?
    Answer

    Name the mechanism, not the genre: a fixed personal timbre captured once, then played as an instrument to sing open material.

  • Where does that mechanism live in your setup?
    Answer

    Point to the device or path: a voice sample played chromatically on the Digitakt II, or a Simpler/granular instrument in Live driven by MIDI.

  • What did you deliberately not copy?
    Answer

    The answer should be material: Herndon's exact voice, her melodies, or her processing. The workshop copies systems, not identity.

  • What rule carries into the related lab?
    Answer

    Write it as a usable instruction. Example: “If I have to re-sing every line, my voice is still a take, not an instrument.”

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • System diff sentence: “I thought the system worked like ___; after building it, I discovered it worked like ___.”
  • Carry into the lab: open lab-032.html and reuse the corrected rule, not the reference sound.
