Rebuild Your Own

Seven replication labs taught you to rebuild other people’s records from your own analysis. The eighth turns inward: one Year 1 piece, reconstructed from its print map and documentation alone — the old project file never opened. This is the archive’s reproducibility test. Where the rebuild fails, the documentation was the fault: the miss list indicts the archive, not the ears.

Start here
  • Pick the Year 1 piece — ideally the one L8’s deconstruction session already worksheeted.
  • Pull its complete documentation bundle from the logbook: print map, routing diagram, recording plan, patch notes, lab report. Paper on the desk.
  • Do not open the old project file. Not to check one thing, not once. The render and the paperwork are all you get.
  • Save a new set as R8-study.
  • Stop at the timebox. The verdict on the archive — not the replica — is the deliverable.
The point

For three years, every lab report promised that the documentation could resurrect the work. Nobody has ever checked. R8 is the check: if the print map, the diagram, and the patch notes are what they claim to be, a Year 3 version of you can rebuild a Year 1 piece from paper. Every place the rebuild stalls or misses is a hole in the archive — found now, while you can still fix the practice, instead of in ten years when the piece is wanted and gone.

What “close” means here — and who is on trial

In R1–R7 a miss meant you could not hear something. Here your ears are the best they have ever been and the system was yours to begin with — so a miss means the documentation could not say something. Closeness is judged against the filed render, A/B at matched loudness as always; but every audible difference is assigned to the document that should have prevented it. The miss list indicts the archive, not the ears. An archive that survives the rebuild is certified; one that doesn’t has just written your documentation rules for the next decade.

How this lab works

Gather the paper

  • The full Year 1 bundle: print map, diagram, patch notes, plans
  • The filed render — the reference track, and the only audio allowed in
  • The old project file is sealed for the duration

Rebuild from documentation alone

  • Every routing move justified by a line in a document
  • Where the paper is silent, log the gap before improvising
  • Improvised guesses are marked as guesses in the session notes

Indict the archive

  • A/B against the filed render at matched loudness
  • Every miss assigned to the document that failed
  • The verdict feeds the C7 archive-layer defense

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
ReproducibilityA piece is reproducible if its documentation alone can bring it back — the audio path, the settings, the prints, the gestures. Science holds itself to this standard; R8 holds your logbook to it. “I remember how I did it” is not reproducibility; memory is the thing the archive exists to replace.
Print mapThe lab report item that says what exists as committed audio, what as MIDI, what as automation, what as patch state or device memory — and what is gone. It is R8’s primary blueprint, which is exactly why R8 exists: the print map has never had to carry weight before.
Archive missA difference between rebuild and render caused by documentation that was wrong, vague, or silent: a level with no number, a chain with no order, a print with no stage named. The defining miss of this lab — each one becomes a documentation rule.
Drift missA difference no document could have prevented: a device’s analog character, a firmware change, a sample lost with old hardware. Logged honestly and separated from archive misses — drift indicts entropy, not the logbook. Most misses claimed as drift turn out to be archive misses on a second look.

1 · Target & documentation audit

The target is one Year 1 piece — the lab 003 piece, the C1 capstone, or another filed Block 1–3 work. Prefer the piece L8’s Samples session deconstructed: its sources-and-prints worksheet already predicts where the documentation is silent, and R8 tests those predictions empirically. Before any audio, audit the bundle itself.

  • The seal. The old project file stays closed for the entire lab — no “quick check,” no peeking at a device chain, no opening it read-only. The moment the session view is seen, the experiment is contaminated and the lab restarts with another piece. The filed render is admissible; the project is not.
  • Documentation only. The blueprint is the bundle you filed in Year 1: print map, routing diagram, recording plan, patch notes, session notes, lab report. Your L8 deconstruction worksheet may sit beside them — it is analysis, and this is still a replication lab built on your own analysis.
  • The firewall, as always. New project, named R8-study. The replica is study evidence, never portfolio output. The corrected documentation and the verdict are what leave this room; the audio does not.
Before you start
  • The complete Year 1 bundle printed or on a second screen — never inside the rebuild project
  • The filed render available as a local audio file, on a track named REF
  • The L8 archive session complete; its deconstruction worksheet on the desk if it covered this piece
  • A new Live set saved as R8-study
  • Phones at the lab-001 mark
  • Timebox: two sessions, three at most; write the end date down
Before building

Pre-commit the predictions

In the logbook, before touching gear: read the bundle end to end and mark every line you already suspect cannot be executed — a level without a number, a “usual delay settings,” a print with no stage named. Predict which parts of the rebuild will land and which will fail, and which document will be to blame. The archive holes you fail to predict are worth double: they are the ones your current documentation habits would still produce.

2 · Rebuild & verdict

Setup

The A/B rig

Same rig as every replication lab: the filed render on a track named REF, Warp off, gain-matched with a Utility; your rebuild tracks grouped as REBUILD; one solo press flips between them, serial, never layered. The comparison loop covers the rebuilt passage — at F1 scale, rebuild the piece’s core system and one representative passage, not all eight minutes.

Expected result

You can solo REF, then solo REBUILD, at the same perceived loudness, over the same passage, hands-free — your Year 1 self against your Year 3 self, separated only by paper.

REF (the filed Year 1 render, gain-matched) → solo → master → phones REBUILD group (built from documentation alone) → solo → master → phones
  1. Timebox first. Two sessions, three at the absolute most. An archive that needs five sessions to execute has already returned a verdict. When the end date arrives, render and stop, mid-hole if necessary.
  2. Rebuild from the paper, in the paper’s order. Follow the recording plan and routing diagram as written: sources first, then chains, then prints, then rides. Every move you make should be traceable to a line in a document — work with a pencil and tick the lines as you execute them.
  3. Log every gap at the moment you hit it. When a document is silent or ambiguous, stop, write the gap down — which document, which question it could not answer — and only then improvise a bridge. Mark every improvised value as a guess in the session notes. Unlogged improvisation is the one way to fail this lab.
  4. A/B at matched loudness. Thirty seconds REF, thirty seconds REBUILD, never both. One dimension per pass: material first, then levels and signal placement, then movement, then time-feel.
  5. Write the miss list — against the archive. Every audible difference is assigned to the document that should have prevented it (table below), or honestly classed as drift. The seven layers still organize the listening; the indictment column names the failing document.
  6. Correct the documentation. Re-write the failing entries the way they should have been written: the missing number, the missing order, the missing stage. Strike nothing — the Year 1 entry stays legible beside its correction, because the diff is the lesson and C7 evidence.
  7. File the verdict and quarantine the audio. Render the rebuilt passage into the logbook as study evidence, close the project, and write the verdict: reproducible / reproducible with named gaps / not reproducible — plus the documentation rules the gaps imply. The verdict and the rules walk straight into C7’s archive-layer defense.

The detailed per-step recipes are included above: bundle audit procedure, gap log template, the verdict form. Use them as the test sequence for the rebuild.

The difference is…Indicted documentExample
Wrong sound at the source — engine, sample, or generationPrint map / patch notesThe print map says “bass: printed audio” but never named the resample generation; the rebuild’s bass is cleaner than the render’s
Right sounds, wrong levels or placementGain plan / routing diagramThe diagram shows the send but no return level; the rebuild’s space is a guess
Right sounds, wrong movementPatch notes / recording plan“Filter ridden during break” — ridden how far, from where, over how many bars? The notes describe an event, not a gesture
Right system, wrong time-feelLab report / session notesSwing, clock source, or a latency offset that lived in the session and was never written anywhere
Unpreventable by any documentNone — driftA hardware voice’s drift, a lost sample, a firmware change; logged, dated, and kept out of the indictment
Decision check — archive miss or drift?

For each item on the miss list: could any sentence, number, or diagram you might plausibly have written in Year 1 have prevented it? If yes, it is an archive miss and produces a documentation rule. If genuinely no, it is drift — and note what would have contained it (a printed stem, a sampled patch, a bounced send). Most claimed drift dissolves under that second question; the honest residue is small.

Pass gate — R8 complete when
  • The replica render — the rebuilt passage, filed in the logbook as study
  • The gap log — every documentation silence, recorded at the moment it was hit, with the improvised bridge marked as a guess
  • A/B notes at matched loudness, one dimension per pass
  • The miss list — every audible difference assigned to its indicted document, or honestly classed as drift
  • The corrected documentation — Year 1 entries legible beside their Year 3 corrections
  • The verdict: reproducible / reproducible with named gaps / not reproducible — plus the documentation rules it implies, ready for C7

No fault ritual, no full bundle — this is a half-cycle, and the lab is the fault ritual: archive/print ambiguity, injected three years ago by an earlier you, diagnosed now. A rebuild that misses badly can still pass — the verdict on the archive, not the closeness of the replica, is what is being judged.

Replication principle

The archive is the only collaborator who outlives your memory — R8 is where you find out whether it can be trusted.
