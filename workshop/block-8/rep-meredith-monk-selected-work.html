Replicate: the voice as non-verbal instrument in interlocking ostinati

Copy the structural engine of Meredith Monk’s Dolmen Music: short wordless vocal cells that lock together like hocketed parts into a repeating, ritual fabric. Record your own non-verbal cells, sample them into the rig, and build a short interlocking loop you can compare to the record.

The point

This drill teaches the record’s core mechanism: the human voice used as a non-verbal instrument inside interlocking ostinati — short, wordless vocal cells that interlock (hocket-like) into a single repeating fabric, so melody and rhythm are carried entirely by vocal sound and not by words. You steal the structure, never Monk’s actual phrases. Do only the steps that make the interlocking-cell fabric audible.

Before you start
  • Digitakt II synced to Live (lab 002’s clock setup), MicroFreak on its input, and a way to record your own voice — the input you tracked the rig through is fine
  • Pen and paper for a cell chart — transcribed from the record before you build: how many distinct wordless cells are interlocking, and which cell sounds on which beat?
  • What you will make: a short replica of Dolmen Music’s vocal fabric. Focus on this effect: two or three wordless cells that lock into one repeating, ritual-feeling pattern with no words.
  • What you will turn off: lyrics, sung words, instrumental melodies, and any pad that does the work the voices should do — the cells must carry the music alone.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF share the mechanism: short non-verbal vocal cells interlocking into a single ostinato fabric.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are voice timbre or rig limits, not a different structural logic. This replica stays in the logbook as study evidence; it is never released.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to a vocal-heavy stretch of Dolmen Music. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. Are there any words — and if not, what is the voice doing instead of speaking?
    Answer

    There are no words. The voices use extended technique — pure vowels, hums, glottal clicks, slides, breathy attacks, ululation — so each voice is functioning like an instrument. Pitch and articulation carry the music; language carries none of it.

  2. How many distinct vocal cells can you separate, and how long is each one?
    Answer

    A small number — often two or three short cells of one or two beats each, repeating. Each cell is simple on its own; the richness comes from several of them sounding at once, not from any one cell being elaborate.

  3. How do the cells fit together — do they sing the same rhythm, or fill each other’s gaps?
    Answer

    They interlock. Where one voice rests, another sounds, so the parts dovetail into a continuous, hocket-like fabric. No single voice plays the whole pattern; the pattern only exists when the cells are stacked and offset against each other.

  4. Why does it feel ancient or ritual rather than like a pop vocal?
    Answer

    Because it is built from repeating, looping cells with no verse-chorus and no lyric to date it — the same wordless figures cycle the way chant or work-song does. The repetition plus the raw, pre-linguistic vocal sound reads as ceremony, not performance of a text.

Now build the fabric you described — your own wordless cells, interlocked. Do not build from memory.

  • Structural target: the whole loop is made of short wordless vocal cells that interlock. No words, no lead vocal line, no instrument carrying the tune.
  • Cells: two or three short original figures of your own — your vowels and clicks, not Monk’s phrases — each simple enough to sing in one breath.
  • Interlock: the cells are offset so they fill each other’s gaps; the pattern exists only when they are stacked.
  • Repetition: the cells loop unchanged long enough to feel like ritual, not like a passing phrase.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press the numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern. Press [SRC] and turn DATA ENTRY knob D to choose the sampled cell on a track.
  • MicroFreak: select the oscillator engine with the Type control, then set cutoff, resonance, envelope, LFO, and volume with the front-panel knobs before recording. When the page gives a value, move that knob slowly while A/Bing against REF.
  • Recorder or phone: press record once to arm or meter, set gain before the take, press record again to capture, and do not touch the gain during the take unless the lesson tells you to restart.
Your rig
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Record wordless cells and sample them in

Phase focus: capture the raw vocal material as playable cells. Follow the steps in order.

Do this
  1. Sing two or three short wordless figures — one or two beats each, pure vowels, hums, or clicks, no words. Keep each one simple enough to repeat exactly.
  2. Record each cell as a clean isolated take into Live, one cell per audio track, named so you know which is which.
  3. Sample each cell into the Digitakt II (or load it into a Simpler in Live) so every cell is one playable, pitchable hit you can trigger from a trig.
  4. Trim each sample tight to its cell so silence does not bleed into the gaps the other voices need to fill.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from the reference and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2. Build the phase goal — Record wordless cells and sample them in — as the smallest Live set that proves The voice as a non-verbal instrument inside interlocking ostinati: short wordless vocal cells that lock together (hocket-like) into a repeating fabric.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects Wordless human voices, using extended technique, woven into repeating cells that feel ancient and ritual — the music is melodic and rhythmic with no language in it at all.

Phase 2 — ~25 minutes

Interlock the cells into one repeating fabric

Phase focus: offset the cells so they dovetail into a single ostinato. Follow the steps in order.

wordless cells (your voice → Digitakt II) → offset so each fills the others’ gaps → repeating fabric (+ optional MicroFreak drone)
Do this
  1. Put each cell on its own Digitakt II track and sequence it so the cells land in different slots of the bar — where one cell sounds, leave a gap for the next.
  2. Tune the cells against each other so the stacked result is a single agreeable mode, not three keys at once; nudge sample pitch, not the singing.
  3. Optional: add one sustained MicroFreak drone or a low doubling of one cell to seat the fabric — keep it under the voices so they still carry the music.
  4. Loop the whole thing long enough to feel like ritual, arm recording, and capture one continuous pass with no changes after it locks.

Expected result

A repeating loop in which no single voice plays the whole pattern — the pattern only exists because the wordless cells interlock — with melody and rhythm carried entirely by vocal sound.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
It sounds like one voice with backing, not an interlockThe cells sit on the same beats and stack vertically instead of dovetailingShift each cell to a different subdivision so they fill each other’s rests — remove one cell and the gap should be obvious
The MicroFreak drone is carrying the tuneThe synth got louder than the voices and took over the melodyPull the drone down until muting it changes color but not the melody — the cells must hold the music alone
Verify against the record

A/B your loop against Dolmen Music at matched loudness — 10 seconds of yours, 10 of theirs, levels gain-matched with a Utility. Then answer two questions in writing.

Is the music carried by interlocking wordless cells, or by one voice and some words?

Answer

Mute any one cell and listen: if the pattern survives mostly intact, your voices are stacked, not interlocked, and one of them is really the lead. In Monk’s fabric each cell is load-bearing — pull one and a gap opens — and none of them say a word. If yours collapses to a single tune or a lyric surfaces, the mechanism is not there yet; re-offset the cells and re-strip the language.

Does the loop read as ancient or ritual, or as a modern song?

Answer

The ritual quality comes from looping wordless cells with no verse-chorus and no datable lyric — the same figures cycle like chant. If yours feels like a pop hook, you probably let a cell evolve or added a clear arrival; hold the cells unchanged longer and let the repetition itself do the work, the way the record does.
