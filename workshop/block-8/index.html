Block 8 — Synthesis & Voice

Design, operate, explain, teach

“Voice” means you can design, operate, troubleshoot, perform, record, explain, and teach a system that sounds like you. Three years of labs end here — not with a new technique, but with the proof that the techniques have become one practice with your name on it.

Year 3 · Domain G · Labs 032–035 · Replication R8 · Programme L8 · Final capstone C7

What “voice” means here

Not a sound, a preset, or a genre allegiance. Voice is operational: you can design a system from a blank page, operate it, troubleshoot it when it fails, perform it, record it, explain it to a colleague, and teach it to a stranger — and the system that survives all seven verbs sounds like you and nobody else. Every page in this block tests one or more of the verbs.

Engineering focus

Both specs are authored from constraints alone — S4, no prompts, no templates beyond the ones you have internalized. The fault territory is this block’s own: integration failure (subsystems that each work but not together) and archive/print ambiguity (the moment you discover your own documentation cannot answer what was printed, what remains live, and what is gone). R8 hunts the second fault class on purpose.

Working order

Do these in this order
  1. Programme L8 — three years of your own output, heard as a stranger; the archive session comes first because everything else in the block builds on its verdict
  2. Lab 032 — author your own constraint system and both specs, from nothing
  3. Lab 033 — build a piece from that spec, crossing two domains
  4. Lab 034 — the signature piece, the strongest single statement of the practice
  5. Lab 035 — write the lab note that teaches 034 to a stranger
  6. R8 — rebuild one Year 1 piece from its documentation alone; run it any time after L8’s archive session
  7. Capstone C7 — the release, and the final defense
  • Sequence inside the block: 032 → 033 is a hard prerequisite — the hybrid piece is built from the spec the étude authors. 034 → 035 is the same dependency in teaching form: the lab note documents the signature piece. R8 floats: schedule it anywhere after the L8 archive session, ideally before C7 so its verdict can feed the defense.
  • No new gear. Block 8 introduces nothing. The rig you have — and the three years of documentation behind it — is the entire instrument.

Listening programme L8 — the student’s own archive

Session 1 · Records

Three years of your own output, heard as a stranger

The records session turns the analytical ear inward: a curated pass through your own filed work, C1 to C6, analyzed through the seven layers exactly as if a stranger made it. The question is not “is it good?” but “what system keeps reappearing?”

Session 2 · Samples

Deconstruction — one of your own pieces, traced to its prints

Pick one of your own filed pieces and split everything you hear into sources and prints — by ear first, then checked against its print map. The gap between what you hear and what you documented is the blueprint for replication lab R8.

Session 3 · Video

One artist retrospective — a voice evolving

One career-spanning retrospective, chosen by you — the Sisters with Transistors subjects, Tony Conrad’s Completely in the Present, an Autechre or Eno career-spanning interview — watched as a model of how a voice changes while remaining recognizably one voice.

  • Deliverable: the archive analysis + the thesis sentence of your voice — one sentence naming the system that recurs across three years, carried into lab 034 and defended at C7. Full briefs and worksheets: programme L8 scaffold
