Replicate: claire rousay — a softer focus

Copy the reference's working mechanism, not its style. Build a short original study with Live 12, Digitakt II, MicroFreak, and the MOTU M4, then A/B it against the reference at matched loudness. Here the mechanism is the extreme case of authorship: personal field recordings and spoken voice arranged as confessional "emo-ambient" — the voice and the lived recordings are both the material and the authorship, and intimacy itself becomes the compositional system.

The point

This drill teaches one thing: that a piece can be authored entirely from personal documentary material — your own field recordings, room tone, and spoken phrases — with sparse synth pads only as a bed underneath. The recordings and the voice are the composition. The reference gives you the clue; the build proves whether you understood it.

Before you start
  • Live 12 set saved as REP-CLAIRE-ROUSAY-SOFTER-FOCUS.
  • Phone or recorder ready to capture room tone, field recordings, and a few spoken phrases of your own.
  • Digitakt II connected by USB for sync and by main outs to MOTU M4 inputs 3/4 — you will load your own recordings onto it.
  • MicroFreak output connected to MOTU M4 input 1 — here it is only a sparse pad under the recordings, not the subject.
  • MOTU M4 headphones at the Lab 001 mark; Live tracks set to Monitor Off while recording hardware.
  • Reference on a Live audio track named REF, with Utility inserted for loudness matching.
  • What you will make: a 2-4 minute original study built mostly from your own field recordings and spoken voice, with a thin synth pad underneath — plus a sentence naming whose life it documents.
  • What you will turn off: drums, hooks, anything polished, and any urge to make the recordings sound "produced" rather than overheard.
  • What you will prove: the piece holds attention through lived material — voice, room tone, texture — without leaning on a beat or a melody.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are source choices or rig limits, not a missing mechanism.
Reference pass · 10 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to one clear section of claire rousay — a softer focus. There is barely a beat, often no key, no riff. What carries it is the lived material: a half-heard voice memo, room tone, a phone notification, a friend talking, a pad drifting under it. Nothing is being performed for you — you are overhearing a diary. Write one short answer for each question before you touch the gear.

  1. What is the smallest piece of lived material you can point to?
    Answer

    Do not name the mood first. Name the material: a spoken phrase, a notification chime, footsteps, room tone, a conversation fragment, or breath. The recorded moment, not the feeling.

  2. Which device or path could capture that material in your studio?
    Answer

    Pick your capture: your phone for field recordings and voice memos, dropped into Live, and onto the Digitakt II as samples. The pad can come from the MicroFreak — but the authorship is the recording, not the synth.

  3. What should stay sparse so the diary remains audible?
    Answer

    Keep the synth bed plain — one slow pad, or a single sustained chord. With no rhythm and no hook to hide behind, the personal material is exposed, which is exactly the point.

  4. What would prove you copied the system rather than the style?
    Answer

    Your study should sound nothing like rousay yet still be authored from your own recordings and voice. If it only works once you add a beat or a sample-pack loop, the mechanism is still missing.

Steps

  • Button map for the steps below: use these controls whenever the lesson names the device.
  • Live 12: ⌘ T creates an audio track, ⇧⌘ T creates a MIDI track, ⌘ R renames the selected track, ⌘ , opens Settings, ⌘ M enters MIDI Map mode, and ⌘ J consolidates the selected clip.
  • Digitakt II: [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] selects the active track. Press [RECORD] for grid recording, press numbered [TRIG] keys to place or remove hits, and press [PLAY] to hear the pattern.
  • Digitakt II sound and timing: press [SRC] for sample/source settings, [FLTR] for filter, [AMP] for envelope/level, [LFO] for modulation, [FX] for sends, and [TEMPO] for tempo and swing. Load your own field recordings here as samples.
  • Digitakt II sync: press [SETTINGS] for MIDI Config → Sync. Use [YES] and the arrow keys to turn clock and transport receive on or off.
  • MicroFreak: use the Preset encoder to choose a plain pad sound, Type to choose the oscillator, Cutoff and Resonance for the filter, Matrix for slow modulation, and Save only after the study behaves correctly. Here the pad is a bed, not the subject — the recordings are the subject.
  • MOTU M4: MicroFreak usually enters input 1. Digitakt II main L/R enter inputs 3/4. Inputs 3/4 have no front-panel gain knobs, so set their record level from the Digitakt II Main Volume.
  • Sound target: your own recorded material — voice, room tone, field recordings — leading, with a thin synth pad underneath.
  • Recording target: peaks around -12 dBFS in Live before any processing.
  • Copy target: a solo flip between REF and your print should reveal the same kind of overheard, diaristic feel, not the same notes.
  • Variation rule: change one variable per pass and write it down.

Phase 1 · ~20 min

Put the reference in Live and write the hypothesis

Do this
  1. Drop the reference onto a track named REF.
  2. Loop the clearest 30-60 seconds — one passage where a spoken phrase or field recording clearly leads.
  3. Add Utility to REF and lower it until it is comfortable.
  4. Write: “The mechanism is ___.” Use the listening tag if you need a first guess: the diary as the composition.
  5. Write the one thing you will not copy: rousay's exact recordings, her voice, her city's room tone, her mix, or her structure.
  6. Create tracks named FIELD, VOICE, and PAD.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from claire rousay — a softer focus and import your own recorded field audio and voice memos onto named tracks, with a soft pad from a stock instrument underneath.
  2. Build the phase goal — Put the reference in Live and write the hypothesis — as the smallest Live set that proves the diary as the composition.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects the intimacy.
  1. Use Digitone II for this phase: translate Put the reference in Live and write the hypothesis into a sparse pad underneath, while your own recordings carry the piece — treat the synth as a bed, not the subject.
  2. Program the pad and control path so it demonstrates the diary as the composition, not just the surface of a softer focus.
  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

You have a looped reference, three working tracks, and one written hypothesis before any recording starts.

Phase 2 · ~35 min

Capture your own diary and let it lead

Capture the smallest set of lived material that can prove the hypothesis. Keep the pad sparse. The point is to author the piece from your own recordings and voice, then let them — not the synth — carry the attention.

Do this
  1. Write one system sentence: “This piece works by documenting ___, while keeping the synth only as a bed.”
  2. On your phone, record three things now: a minute of room tone where you are, one short spoken phrase, and one field recording (a street, a kitchen, a notification).
  3. Import all three into Live and onto the Digitakt II as samples, then arrange them as the primary material — voice and field first, pad last.
  4. On the MicroFreak, hold one slow pad chord underneath. [TRK] + [TRIG 1] selects track 1 if you trigger field recordings on the Digitakt II.
  5. Add only devices that keep it intimate and raw: Utility, EQ Eight, a touch of Reverb or Echo. Do not over-polish or quantise the voice.
  6. Record a short proof piece. If it needs a beat to be interesting, the diary is not carrying the voice yet.
  7. Rewrite the system sentence after the take. The corrected sentence is the real result.
  8. Export the proof and keep the system sentence beside it.

Phase 3 · ~15 min

Route the studio so the print is clean

Make the signal path boring and dependable. If the recording path is confusing, you will misdiagnose the mechanism.

MicroFreak · input 1 + Digitakt II · inputs 3/4 → MOTU M4 → USB → Live 12 · Monitor Off
Do this
  1. Plug MicroFreak into input 1 — it is only the pad bed, so set its gain low while watching Live.
  2. Plug Digitakt II left/right into inputs 3/4 since it is playing back your field recordings and voice samples.
  3. Set hardware tracks to Monitor Off. This prevents doubled monitoring.
  4. Arm one record track at a time and check peaks. Aim for -12 dBFS, not “as loud as possible.”
  5. If inputs 3/4 are too quiet or too hot, adjust Digitakt II Main Volume and write the position down.
  6. Record 60-90 seconds of the diary playing through.
  7. Press ⌘ J to consolidate the best section into a clean clip named PRINT-A.

Phase 4 · ~20 min

Make the system map and the A/B proof

Draw the mechanism, then test the print against the reference. The map is not decoration; it is how you find what failed.

Do this
  1. Put REF and PRINT-A on adjacent tracks.
  2. Use Utility to match loudness before judging.
  3. Listen to 10 seconds of REF, then 10 seconds of PRINT-A.
  4. Write one sentence: “The first thing that gives mine away is ___.”
  5. Fix only that one thing and record PRINT-B.
  6. Keep both prints. The difference between A and B is the lesson.

Final check

Play the reference and your best print at matched loudness, then answer these in the logbook:

  • What mechanism did you copy?
    Answer

    Name the mechanism, not the genre: personal documentary material — your own field recordings and spoken voice — arranged as a diary, with the synth only as a bed.

  • Where does that mechanism live in your setup?
    Answer

    Point to the path: your phone recordings imported into Live and onto the Digitakt II as samples, with a sparse MicroFreak pad underneath.

  • What did you deliberately not copy?
    Answer

    The answer should be material: rousay's exact recordings, her voice, her city, or her arrangement. The workshop copies systems, not identity — your diary must be yours.

  • What rule carries into the related lab?
    Answer

    Write it as a usable instruction. Example: “If the piece needs a beat to be interesting, the diary is not yet the voice.”

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: Matched: ___ · Missed: ___ (best guess why) · Change next: ___
  • System diff sentence: “I thought the system worked like ___; after building it, I discovered it worked like ___.”
