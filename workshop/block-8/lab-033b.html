Near-Silence

Compose with silence and threshold-level material as designed states, not as absence waiting to be filled. A near-silent bed, a few very quiet events, long gaps between them — and a dynamic range you decide on purpose and then protect through every gain stage. Quiet music is the most demanding gain-staging exercise in the workshop: the noise floor becomes audible, the listener's room joins the mix, and a careless normalize can destroy the whole idea.

Start here
  • Save a new set as LAB-033B-nearsilence. Metronome off, ruler in m:ss.
  • You need: the DT 990 Pro at the mark (your reference monitor), a phone speaker, and a laptop speaker — three playback worlds the piece will be judged on.
  • Run the 30-second silence test first: record nothing and look at the meter — that reading is your noise floor, the canvas you are composing on.
  • This lab is unforgiving of sloppy gain staging. If your Lab 001 gain ledger is not current, refresh it before you start.
  • Expected time: one cycle — a calibration session, a composing session, a translation-and-print session.
The point

Loud music hides its own engineering; quiet music exposes all of it. When the music sits near the threshold of hearing, the silence frames the event that follows, the noise floor stops being background and becomes content, and every gain decision is audible. The skill is to choose a dynamic range from quietest to loudest, protect it from anything that would flatten it, and decide — on purpose — which playback systems the piece survives on and which it intentionally abandons.

1 · Listen first — music at the threshold

Three works that compose with quiet as material. Listen at the mark, then listen quieter, and notice that the music asks something of you and your room — attention, and a noise floor low enough to let it exist.

01 Éliane Radigue — “Trilogie de la Mort” (excerpt) change below the threshold of noticing

Radigue's tones sit low and quiet and change so slowly that you notice the change only after it has happened. The silence around and within the sound is part of the structure, not the gaps between it.

Listen for
  • How quiet the loudest moment is — and how complete the piece feels anyway.
  • How the near-stillness makes the smallest movement into an event.
After — verify

Try it at two volumes. At the mark it is a world; far below the mark it vanishes into your room. The piece's existence depends on your monitoring — that dependency is this lab's subject.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

02 Alvin Lucier — “I Am Sitting in a Room” the room as the instrument

Speech re-recorded in a room until the room's resonances replace the words. It makes audible the thing this lab forces you to confront: the playback space is part of the signal chain, always.

Listen for
  • The point where the room takes over from the voice — content emerging from the space itself.
  • How the noise and resonance of a room is not a flaw but the material.
After — verify

Listen on phones, then on a speaker in your room. The room you played it in changed it again — proof that you never control the final stage of playback, only your decision about it.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

03 Radu Malfatti / Wandelweiser-adjacent quiet (e.g. “Nariyamu”) silence as the main material

Long silences punctuated by single quiet events. The silence is not empty — it is charged by the event that just ended and the one you are waiting for. Composing the gap is the work.

Listen for
  • How a single quiet tone, after a long silence, lands with enormous weight.
  • That the silence has shape — duration is composed, not accidental.
After — verify

During a silence, notice what your room adds — a fridge hum, traffic, the hiss of your own playback. In this music that is not interruption; it is what the silence frames. Decide whether you embrace or exclude it.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
Noise floor The quietest sound your system makes when "nothing" is playing — hiss, hum, the converter's own floor. In loud music it is buried; in quiet music it can become the loudest thing in the room. You either drive it below your quietest event on purpose, or you treat it as material on purpose. What you cannot do is ignore it.
Perceived dynamic range The distance, in dB, between your quietest intended sound and your loudest. Not the file's peak — the span the listener experiences. In near-silent music a 30 dB span feels vast; flatten it and the whole point collapses.
Reference level The fixed listening volume you compose at — the mark on the phones knob from Lab 001. Without it, "quiet" and "loud" are meaningless, because you can always just turn it up. The reference level is the contract that makes your dynamic decisions real.
Translation What survives when the piece leaves your studio for a phone, a laptop, earbuds, a car. Quiet detail can simply disappear on small speakers. Translation is not a problem to fix away — it is a decision: where does this piece live, and where does it intentionally not?
noise floor perceived dynamic range reference level translation

Write a one-sentence hypothesis before composing. Target shape: silence frames the event — my piece works at the mark on headphones and intentionally disappears on a phone, and that disappearance is the point. Your words, your decision about where the piece lives; the translation test will hold you to it.

2 · The two specs

3 · Calibrate before you compose

Step 01

Set the reference level and read the floor

Do this
  1. Set the phones to the marked reference position. Do not move it again this session.
  2. Record 30 s with everything armed but silent. Read the noise floor on the meter and write the number down — this is the bottom of your usable range.
  3. If the floor shows hum (50/60 Hz) rather than just hiss, fix it now: cable routing, ground, power. You cannot compose near a floor you have not minimized.
  4. Write the intended dynamic range as two dBFS numbers, both comfortably above the floor.
Decision checkpoint

Is your quietest intended event at least ~10 dB above the measured noise floor? If not, either raise the quietest event or lower the floor — composing in the gap between them is composing on quicksand.

Logbook

“Floor ≈ −__ dBFS. Intended range: quietest −__, loudest −__. Reference: phones mark.”

4 · Build

Step 02

Lay the near-silent bed

Do this
  1. Establish one continuous bed: a captured room tone, or a very low MicroFreak drone printed at low level, or a contact-mic recording of a near-still surface (a wall, a window).
  2. Sit it just above the floor and well below the events to come — present but barely. It is the ground the silence is measured against.
  3. Long fade in over 20–40 s. Listen for three minutes at the mark; the bed should reward attention without ever asking for it.
Step 03

Place sparse threshold-level events

Do this
  1. Place only a few events across the piece — single quiet tones, tiny ticks, a breath of texture. Each should sit at or near the threshold of comfortable audibility at the reference level.
  2. Compose the silences between them deliberately: long enough that each event lands fresh, short enough to keep tension. Time them in m:ss; the gap is the structure.
  3. Let each event's tail decay into the bed — the silence after an event is charged by it. That framing is the composition.
  4. Resist filling. If you want to add a fourth event, first ask whether removing the third would make the second stronger.
  • Silence is not empty. After a loud-music habit it will feel like you have done nothing. Trust the meter and the clock: a 25-second silence between two quiet events is a composed object, not a mistake.
Step 04

Fix the dynamic range and protect it

Do this
  1. Confirm the quietest event and the loudest event hit the dBFS numbers you wrote in Step 01. Adjust by level, not by compression.
  2. Remove anything that flattens the span: no master limiter, no bus compressor lifting the floor, no normalize-on-export.
  3. Master peaks can sit far below 0 dBFS — that is correct here. The span, not the peak, is the deliverable.
  4. One full listen at the mark, eyes closed, no adjustments. If you reached for the volume knob, note when — that moment is a dynamic-range decision, not a monitoring one.
Decision checkpoint

Can you state the intended range in two numbers and confirm nothing in the chain is silently compressing it toward those numbers? A 30 dB span that a limiter has quietly shrunk to 8 dB is the failure this lab exists to prevent.

5 · The prescribed fault

Step 06

Induce both quiet-music faults, then diagnose

Goal: make both ways quiet music fails so you can tell a designed disappearance from an accidental one, and a material floor from a fault floor.

Induce fault A — the vanishing piece (accidental)
  1. Play the piece on a phone speaker at a normal volume. Note which events and how much of the bed simply do not reproduce.
  2. Decide: is each disappearance a choice you can defend, or a sound you assumed everyone would hear? Mark the accidental ones.
Induce fault B — the audible floor
  1. Solo a long silence and turn the monitor up (then return to the mark). The hiss/hum that surfaces is your noise floor becoming audible because the music is so quiet.
  2. If you did not choose that floor as material, it is a fault — trace it (converter, gain staging, ground hum) and lower it.

6 · Pass gate — the translation test

Certification

Stated range, played on three systems, with a translation note

  1. A short piece (3–5 min) with a near-silent bed, sparse threshold-level events, and composed silences between them.
  2. A documented intended dynamic range — two dBFS numbers — with nothing in the chain secretly compressing it.
  3. Play it on the DT 990s at the mark, a phone speaker, and a laptop speaker. Log what survives on each.
  4. Write a translation note: where the piece works, and where it intentionally does not — a stated decision, not an apology.
Pass condition

Every disappearance is accounted for — either reproduced where you intended or declared an intentional loss — and no unchosen noise floor competes with the music. If something vanished that you wanted heard, the fix is in the level or the range, not the monitor knob. The deliverable is a piece and a clear statement of where it lives.

Report bundle — filed in the logbook
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, normalize off, 3–5 minutes
  • Noise-floor reading and the intended dynamic range as two dBFS numbers
  • Reference-level statement — the phones mark held for the whole session
  • Translation note — what survives on phones / laptop / reference, and which losses are intentional
  • Fault entry — the vanishing piece and the audible floor, with fixes or declared decisions
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the events were the piece; the silence between them was.”
Pass standard
Minimum passA quiet piece with a documented intended dynamic range and a translation note stating where it works and where it intentionally does not.
Solid passThe silences are clearly composed (timed, defended), the floor is either inaudible or deliberately used, and the span survives the render untouched.
Advanced transferYou can design two versions of the same piece for two destinations (reference vs. phone) without betraying the idea — translation as authorship.
  1. Which silence carries the most weight, and what event charged it?
  2. Where, exactly, did your room enter the piece — and did you keep it or fight it?
  3. What disappeared on the phone, and was that the right thing to lose?
  4. Did holding the reference level change a decision you would otherwise have made by turning it up?
Worked system diff — the expected granularity

“I thought I was composing quiet events; I was composing the silences — the events only existed to give the silence a shape, and the noise floor I tried to hide turned out to be the one thing every system reproduced.”

Étude principle

Silence is material — do less, almost nothing, but protect the dynamic range that makes the little you do an event.
