Replicate: a drum machine played as a live voice

Copy one mechanism from Ikue Mori — Garden: build a bank of small rhythmic cells in advance, then perform them — triggering, layering, muting and mutating them by hand in real time so the machine answers itself instead of looping. Build a short take on the rig and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: the composition is done in advance, but only as a vocabulary of cells — the music is the live recombination of them. You decide which cell next, when to cut, when to layer, when to mutate, in the moment. You are stealing the structure (pre-built cells, performed live), never Mori's exact rhythms.

The system you are replicating
Source workIkue Mori — Garden (1996), her drum-machine improvisation work
The effectA drum machine sounds like a live, responsive voice — intricate non-grooving cells that start, stop, mutate and collide in real time, answering each other rather than running on loop
The mechanismA bank of short rhythmic and sonic cells is authored offline; the piece is performed by triggering, layering, muting, retriggering and recombining those cells live as an improvised line

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Digitakt II as the centerpiece — empty project ready for several short patterns/cells, with pattern chaining, mutes, retrig, conditional trigs and parameter locks all reachable by hand while playing
  • Live open to capture one continuous take (one armed audio track) and to host light processing; MicroFreak ready for a pitched cell accent
  • Free VCV Rack if you want an extra generative cell source; pen and paper to name each cell as a distinct gesture
  • What you will make: a short live take. Focus on this effect: a machine that recombines pre-built cells in real time, like a voice answering itself.
  • What you will turn off: a steady groove, a single looping pattern, and any post-take editing of the performance.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: cells composed in advance, the music made by live recombination decisions.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are cell-content choices or rig limits, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 12 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to three to four minutes of Garden. Write one short answer per question before you touch the gear.

  1. Does the material loop, or are patterns being switched and recombined live?
    Answer

    Recombined live. A figure appears, then is dropped, retriggered, or stacked with another — you never hear a bar repeat unchanged for long. The give-away is that returns are never identical; a loop would be.

  2. Is there a steady groove you could nod to, or do cells start and stop unpredictably?
    Answer

    No steady groove. Cells enter and cut off on their own logic, not on a downbeat you can anticipate. The pulse, where it exists at all, belongs to a single cell, not to the whole piece.

  3. Roughly how many distinct cells can you count?
    Answer

    A handful — usually five to eight recognizable gestures that keep returning in new orders and combinations. The richness comes from recombining a small vocabulary, not from endless new material.

  4. Does it feel pre-sequenced or decided in the moment — and what tells you?
    Answer

    Decided in the moment. The timing of cuts, overlaps and collisions has the slight unevenness of a hand making choices; a programmed track would place transitions on grid every time. The asymmetry is the tell.

Now build the mechanism you described. Do not try to reproduce her actual rhythms.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Author a bank of small cells offline

Phase focus: write the vocabulary — distinct gestures, none of them a finished beat.

Do this
  1. Build five to eight short patterns, one cell each — e.g. a stuttered hat figure, a single low knock on odd steps, a metallic ping cluster, a short noise burst. Each is one idea, not a full kit playing.
  2. Keep them deliberately incomplete: a cell should sound unfinished alone, so it only becomes music when combined with another.
  3. Add one pitched cell — a short melodic or tonal gesture — sampled into the Digitakt II or kept live for an accent.
  4. Name each cell on paper as a gesture ("stutter", "knock", "ping", "noise", "ping-pitched"). This named bank is your composition-in-advance.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from the reference and use Drum Rack, MIDI clips, Groove Pool, and clip automation.
  2. Build the phase goal — Author a bank of small cells offline — as the smallest Live set that proves A bank of short rhythmic and sonic cells is authored offline; the piece is performed by triggering, layering, muting, retriggering and recombining those cells live as an improvised line.
  3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.
  5. A/B ten seconds of PRINT against REF and change only the setting that most affects A drum machine sounds like a live, responsive voice — intricate non-grooving cells that start, stop, mutate and collide in real time, answering each other rather than running on loop.
  1. Use Analog Rytm MKII for this phase: translate Author a bank of small cells offline into drum voices or samples, trig conditions, parameter locks, scenes, performance macros, and main-out recording.

  3. Keep one clear performance control for the main change in this phase; name it before recording so the move is repeatable.
  4. Record the device into Live or a recorder as a full pass, then compare it with REF at matched level.
  5. If the target box suggests an easier shortcut, use it only when the same mechanism stays audible.
Expected result

A bank of five to eight named cells, each a distinct gesture, none of them a self-sufficient loop.

Phase 2 — ~30 minutes

Set up live performance controls

Phase focus: make every recombination move doable by hand, in real time, without stopping.

cell bank (Digitakt II patterns) → live controls: switch · mute · retrig · p-lock → captured take (Live)
Do this
  1. Set up performance-style pattern switching so you can jump from any cell to any other on the fly, and chain a couple where useful — but leave the order open, not fixed.
  2. Map mutes per track so you can drop and add cell layers instantly, and learn the retrig hold so a cell can stutter on demand.
  3. Pick one or two parameters per cell (filter, pitch, sample start) you will p-lock or twist live, so a cell can mutate as you play it.
  4. Arm one audio track to capture the whole performance; run a rehearsal pass to confirm every move is reachable without looking down for long.

Phase 3 — ~30 minutes

Perform one continuous take, recombining live

Phase focus: improvise the recombination — switch, layer, cut, mutate — responding to what just happened, no editing after.

Do this
  1. Hit record and play one cell. Then make every next move a reaction to the last: answer a stutter with silence, a knock with a ping, a build with a cut.
  2. Force yourself to recombine, not settle: never leave one cell looping more than a few bars before switching, muting, layering or mutating it.
  3. Let cells collide on purpose — overlap two for a moment, then drop one — and twist a p-lock so a returning cell comes back changed.
  4. Run the take to a natural end and keep it whole. Do not edit, quantize or comp afterward — the live decisions are the music.

One unedited take where a small bank of cells is recombined in real time — no bar repeats unchanged, and the transitions carry the unevenness of a hand deciding.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
It settles into a loop and groovesYou stopped deciding and let a cell runSet a hard rule — change something every few bars — and treat any steady groove as a failure to recombine
It sounds busy but not responsiveMoves are random, not answers to the last gestureListen, then react: each move should comment on the previous cell, not just add more on top
Verify against the work

A/B your take against a few minutes of Garden — matched loudness, 15 seconds of yours, 15 of theirs. Then answer two questions in writing.

Does yours recombine live the way theirs does, or does it fall into a loop?

Answer

If any stretch of yours repeats unchanged while theirs keeps reshaping a small vocabulary, you stopped performing and let the machine run. Live recombination is the proof you played the cells rather than sequenced them.

Do your transitions have the hand-made unevenness theirs has?

Answer

Theirs cuts and overlaps land slightly off-grid because a person decided them; if yours all snap to the downbeat, the take reads as programmed. The asymmetry is the signature of a real-time decision.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: "Matched: …" · "Missed: … (best guess why)" · "Change next: …" — e.g. "Missed: fell into a loop instead of recombining."
  • Note which single cell carried the most weight in the take — that is the one to build the next bank around.
