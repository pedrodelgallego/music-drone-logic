The Long Form

Curation at scale. Fifteen minutes and more, held together not by events but by pacing, returns, and the listener’s memory — the skills of a piece applied to the size of a set.

The point

Past fifteen minutes, arrangement stops being a sequence of events and becomes a budget of attention. The composer’s job turns into a curator’s: deciding what is allowed in, how often it may return, and how long the listener can be trusted with nothing happening.

How this lab works

Design the macro-form

  • One page, drawn before any audio: states, arcs, and returns across the full span — the form map.
  • Memory points planned: what must the listener recognize at minute 14 from minute 2, and what changed in between.
  • At most three families of material; the form is their schedule, not their sum.

Pace the spend

  • A pacing budget written per section: events per minute, declared like a gain ledger.
  • The long gap treated as material — silence and stasis cost budget and buy weight.
  • Escalation must be earned: every peak names the minutes that paid for it.

Curate, then commit

  • Material is auditioned at scale — a loop that charms for two minutes must prove it survives ten.
  • Removal decisions happen at form level: whole sections die, not bars.
  • The block’s operational artifacts apply here too: this length will be performed at C6, so it is engineered like a performance now.

Beyond curation — the large-scale form

This lab’s craft is curation — scheduling a small set of families so a quarter hour survives. There is a second, larger skill it points toward but does not require here: composing a long arc as a single statement — a 20–40 minute piece, or an album heard as one shape, where the form is authored rather than scheduled.

Sections as movements

  • The arc is divided into named sections that each do one job — exposition, development, climax, return — not just “more of the same, paced.”
  • Transitions between sections are composed events, not crossfades: the seam is where the statement is made.
  • An album-as-statement treats track boundaries the way this lab treats section boundaries — the silence between tracks is part of the form.

Development, not repetition

  • Material is transformed across the span — inverted, fragmented, re-orchestrated — so the listener hears growth, not only return.
  • The climax is the consequence of everything before it; you can trace the line of cause back through the sections that paid for it.
  • The return is changed by the journey: the opening material, heard again at minute 30, means something it could not have meant at minute 2.

The statement

  • One sentence the whole arc is trying to say — written first, the way a thesis precedes an essay.
  • Every section is defended against that sentence: if a section does not advance the statement, it is curated out at form level.
  • This is the skill C6’s 30-minute set, and Block 8’s authored work, will ask for in full — here it is named, not yet graded.
Distinct from this lab

Curation asks “does this belong here, at this size?” Large-scale form asks “what is the whole arc saying, and does each section move it forward?” You can pass this lab on curation alone. But if your three families want to become movements with development and a hard-won return, write the arc as a statement first — that is the more ambitious form this duration can hold.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
Macro-formForm above the phrase: the shape made of whole states and their order, visible only from a distance — like a coastline that no single wave reveals. Drawn on one page, or it does not exist.
Pacing budgetEvents per minute treated as spendable currency. A long form goes broke two ways: spending everything early (nothing left for minute 12) or hoarding (nothing happens at all). The budget is written down so overspending is visible.
Memory pointA recognizable return — a sound, a figure, a register — that tells the listener where they are. In a 3-minute track the chorus does this for free; at 20 minutes you must plant and tend it deliberately.
CurationChoosing among existing material rather than making more — the long form is selected at least as much as it is composed. The curator’s question is never “is this good?” but “does this belong here, now, at this size?”
Fatigue curveThe honest graph of a listener’s attention across the span: where it sags, what kind of change buys it back, and how the cost of a surprise rises each time one is used. Long-form pacing is fatigue-curve management.

Listening — the hypothesis source

Ten works that hold a quarter hour or far more — from a 37-minute brass loop to a five-hour piano. Listen with a clock visible: mark when your attention sags, what brings it back, and how each work schedules its returns. Each gets a brief: what to listen for before you press play, and how to check yourself after.

01 Ricardo Villalobos — “Fizheuer Zieheuer” 37 minutes, one loop

A single Balkan brass loop sustained for thirty-seven minutes of minimal house — the most radical pacing budget in club music: one family of material, scheduled with watchmaker patience.

Listen for
  • How long the brass is withheld each time before returning — absence as the main expressive device.
  • What actually changes across ten minutes: percussion micro-events, never the premise.
  • The moment you stop waiting for development and settle in — your own fatigue curve flattening.
After — verify

Where did your attention sag, by the clock? Write the minute marks down — then check what the track did shortly after each one. The returns are scheduled close to where boredom statistically lands, which is curation at scale performed on the listener. If you never sagged, relisten to the middle third only and try again honestly.

02 Jeff Mills — Exhibitionist curation at mixer speed · video

The 2004 DVD of Mills mixing on three decks and a 909, filmed from above — an hour-plus form built from dozens of records, each allowed only the minutes it earns. Where Block 1 studied “The Bells” as a track, this is the same mind curating at set scale.

Listen for
  • How short each record’s stay is — material is dismissed the moment its work is done.
  • Density managed across the hour: where he thins to almost nothing and why it lands there.
  • The 909 as a continuity device — one voice that belongs to the set, not to any record.
After — verify

Pick any fifteen-minute span: could you draw its form map? It has one — a rise, a clearing, a return — built from other people’s tracks, which proves macro-form is independent of who made the material. That is curation as authorship. If the span felt like just mixing, rewatch it counting how many records serve one arc.

03 Donato Dozzy — Plays Bee Mask one source, one hour

Seven reworkings of a single Bee Mask piece, sequenced into an album-length arc — the curation problem in its purest form: the material is fixed, so every minute of variety must come from treatment and order. Block 3 studied Dozzy’s Aquaplano work; this is his form-scale discipline.

Listen for
  • What survives from track to track — the memory point that makes seven versions one piece.
  • The sequencing logic: which version opens, which is the peak, which is allowed to dissolve.
  • How treatment substitutes for new material — filter, time, and space doing composition’s job.
After — verify

Could the tracks be reordered? Try mentally swapping two — the arc objects: each version spends a different region of the fatigue curve, and the sequence is the composition. Your lab’s three families will need the same scheduling logic. If the versions blurred, relisten to the first two and list what one withholds that the other spends.

04 Éliane Radigue — Naldjorlak an hour inside one resonance

Radigue’s first fully acoustic work: a solo cello (Charles Curtis) tuned so its whole body converges on one wolf tone, explored for around an hour — long form as a single location examined, not a journey taken. Block 2’s “Kyema” lesson is this composer at synthesizer scale; this is her method handed to a performer.

Listen for
  • How much variety lives inside what is nominally one pitch — beating, breath, bow position as events.
  • Changes arriving over minutes, below the threshold of “now” — you notice they happened, never them happening.
  • Your own recalibration: after ten minutes, a quarter-tone feels like a key change.
After — verify

What was the form? Not stasis — a slow traversal of one resonance’s neighborhood, with departures and homecomings you can place by the clock once you listen for them. The lesson for your pacing budget: at low event rates, perception re-scales, and tiny moves become structural. If it felt formless, relisten to the first and last ten minutes and describe the difference — there will be one.

05 Tony Conrad — “Four Violins” (1964) duration as confrontation

Four overdubbed violins sustaining just-intoned intervals for over half an hour — early minimalism’s hardest line: no events at all, only the slowly opening interior of a chord. The long form with the pacing budget set to zero.

Listen for
  • The combination tones — pitches no violin is playing, generated in your ear by the tuning.
  • Texture as the only narrative: bow changes and beating patterns become headline events.
  • What “nothing happening” actually contains once you stop waiting.
After — verify

Did the piece change, or did you? Mostly you — the material is nearly constant, and the form is the listener’s adaptation. That is the extreme this lab borrows from, not the standard it sets: your brief requires returns and arcs, but Conrad proves how little is survivable when commitment is total. If you bailed early, relisten for ten minutes with the volume modest and attention soft.

06 The Necks — Sex one hour, one groove, three humans

An Australian piano trio’s 1989 debut: a single 56-minute piece built from one gently looping figure, developed by addition so gradual it feels geological — long form sustained by players, in real time, with no electronics at all.

Listen for
  • The entry schedule: how many minutes each new layer waits, and what its arrival re-weights.
  • Human micro-variation doing the work your LFOs usually do — no two bars identical, no bar surprising.
  • The single arc: one rise across an hour, with no second act — and why it does not need one.
After — verify

When did the piece peak? Most listeners place it somewhere past the 40-minute mark — an escalation paid for by half an hour of patience, the purest example of earning a peak in this set. Map their entry schedule against your own planned form: are your layers cheaper? If you cannot place the peak, relisten to minutes 30–50 and feel for the tilt.

07 Autechre — AE_LIVE (2015 series) an hour generated nightly

Documented hour-long live sets from a touring generative system — each night the same patches, never the same hour. Block 4 studied Autechre at track scale; this is their answer to curation at scale: build the system, then curate its states in real time, in the dark.

Listen for
  • State changes versus transitions — the set moves by mutation, almost never by crossfade.
  • How long a state is mined before being abandoned — the pacing budget of an improvising system.
  • Across two different sets (pick any two): which structures recur — that skeleton is the composed part.
After — verify

Compare ten minutes from two sets of the series. The materials differ; the behavior — state lengths, density ceilings, when relief arrives — rhymes. The macro-form lives in the system’s constraints, not in a timeline, which is one honest way to fill your form map: as rules rather than minutes. If both excerpts felt arbitrary, relisten to one and log every moment the whole texture changes at once.

08 Klara Lewis — “Ingrid” (2020) one loop, twenty minutes, to destruction

A single cello loop sustained for twenty minutes and pushed — by accumulating distortion and pressure — from elegy to violence: the lab’s own duration class (15+), built from one family of material whose only development is degradation.

Listen for
  • The loop itself memorized in the first two minutes — you will need it as the reference for everything after.
  • The degradation schedule: when each new layer of grit arrives, and how evenly the violence is rationed.
  • The point of no return — the minute after which the opening material could not come back clean.
After — verify

Draw its form map from memory: it is close to a single straight line from intact to destroyed, which makes it the cleanest possible homework — a 20-minute form with one process as its entire schedule. Where on that line did you sag? If nowhere, you have found a pacing model your own piece can borrow; if somewhere, note what the track lacked there. Relisten to verify the line really never turns back.

09 Morton Feldman — “For Philip Guston” (1984) four hours of quiet

Around four hours for flute, percussion, and piano, almost all of it quiet — the concert tradition’s deepest study of memory at scale: patterns recur slightly wrong, so the listener is never sure whether the music changed or they misremembered it. Sample it in 30-minute sittings if a whole performance is impractical; album-level listening is the assignment.

Listen for
  • Near-repetition: figures that return a semitone or a beat askew — wrongness as the development device.
  • How the extreme length disarms anticipation — form stops being suspense and becomes weather.
  • Your memory failing on purpose: was that chord new? (Feldman is counting on you not knowing.)
After — verify

After one sitting: write down, from memory, two figures you believe recurred. Then spot-check by relistening — at least one will have recurred changed, and your memory will have smoothed it. That gap between what returns and what you remember returning is a compositional material your 15-minute piece can use at smaller scale: memory points planted slightly wrong are stickier than exact ones.

10 La Monte Young — The Well-Tuned Piano five hours, one tuning

A five-hour solo improvisation-composition in just intonation, grown since 1964 — the maximal case of curation at scale: themes are visited, left for an hour, and returned to like rooms in a house. The 1987 recording is the canonical document; sample it by sections, but sample sections whole.

Listen for
  • The named themes returning across vast distances — memory points at the hour scale.
  • “Clouds”: passages where fast playing fuses into sustained harmony — texture replacing melody as the unit of form.
  • How the tuning makes certain returns feel like arriving home — consonance as a structural landmark, not a pleasantness.
After — verify

Take one hour of it: how many distinct regions did it visit, and did any return? Even one hour shows the method — a map of places rather than a line of events, traversed at walking pace. Your 15 minutes is a single neighborhood of such a map, which is why your form map draws states, not bars. If the regions blurred, relisten to twenty minutes and name each texture change as a room.

Requirement

Your written hypothesis names how this piece will survive its length — absence scheduled against boredom, records dismissed when their work is done, one source re-treated seven ways, an hour inside one resonance, a budget set to zero, a peak bought with forty minutes, form as rules instead of minutes, one process as the whole schedule, memory planted slightly wrong, or themes as rooms in a house — and what was stolen from the comparison. The target idea across all ten: a long form is curated, not composed — the material matters less than its schedule of returns. What you steal is a structure, never a sound.

2 · Bench preflight

Pre-flight — before the long session
  • Macro-form map drawn on one page and taped in sight — if it does not fit one page, the form is not yet designed
  • Pacing budget written per section, next to the map
  • The three material families printed/committed before the long session — curation works on fixed objects, not on maybes
  • Long-print engineering checked: disk space for 25 minutes at 24-bit, CPU headroom verified with a full-density test minute, computer sleep and notifications off — long prints fail differently from short ones
  • Recording strategy armed and tested with a 60-second dry run, including how a failed print at minute 22 will be detected and handled
  • Panic path written; gain ledger applied; monitoring mode declared; phones at the lab-001 mark

3 · Build

Build the lab in five passes. Each pass produces an artifact, not just a better-sounding session: a written rule, a printed comparison, a fault entry, and a corrected system sentence.

Build recipe · The Long Form

Make the performance system audible

Do this in order
  1. Map the hands. Limit the rig to six reachable controls. Name every control, its safe range, and what it changes in the form.
  2. Write the panic path. Before sound, write the one-gesture mute, bypass, stop, monitor-lower, and save procedure. Rehearse it silently.
  3. Rehearse without repair. Run the first minute twice without recording. Fix the map, not the take.
  4. Print the take. Record one full take in Live. Do not edit automation after the fact; failed takes are performed again.
  5. Run the fault. Trigger the performance fault safely: wrong mute, too-hot feedback, lost loop, or missed cue. Recover using the written panic path.

4 · Diagnosis & report

The full report bundle — ten items, filed in the logbook
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, master peaking −3 dBFS, 15–25 minutes, one continuous span
  • Form map — the planned page and the corrected page; the diff is the lesson
  • Pacing budget — planned versus actual, with the overdrafts marked
  • Routing diagram — live signal-flow, drawn before, corrected after
  • Recording plan — what was printed, when, at what stage, including the long-print precautions
  • Session notes — including where the build deviated from the map and why
  • Reflection answers, including the listening comparison — which document contributed what to the hypothesis
  • One removal decision — a whole section cut at form level, and what its absence bought
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule (faultType: performance failure)
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the system worked like X; after building it, I discovered it worked like Y.”
  • Minimum pass gate: audible output · form map with diff · routing diagram · fault entry · system diff. Without these five, the lab is incomplete regardless of audio quality — a musically strong piece cannot bypass the engineering spine.
Track-lab principle

Length is not duration — it is trust. A long form survives when every minute knows why the listener is still there, and the map of those reasons is drawn before the music.
