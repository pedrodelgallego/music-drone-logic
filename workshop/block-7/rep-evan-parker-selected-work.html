Replicate: a permutation engine that never stops

Copy one mechanism from Evan Parker — Conic Sections: take a tiny cell of three to five notes and run a permutation engine that endlessly reorders it without pausing and without literally looping. Build a short continuous version on the rig and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill treats improvisation as a real-time decision system. Parker's solo soprano is not a tune played from memory — it is a small set of figures fed through continuous permutation, so the stream self-generates, never breathes, and never repeats literally. You are stealing the engine (small cell → endless reorder/octave/articulation, no gaps), not Parker's notes or his circular breathing.

The system you are replicating
Source workEvan Parker — Conic Sections (1989), solo soprano saxophone improvisations
The effectOne monophonic horn sounds like several interlocking voices — an unbroken stream of fast notes that never stops to breathe
The mechanismA small cell of figures is continuously permuted — reordered, octave-shifted, re-articulated — and circular breathing keeps the stream literally gapless, so the line is endless and self-similar but never a literal loop

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • A permutation engine ready: free VCV Rack with a Turing-machine / shift-register patch is ideal, or the MicroFreak arpeggiator/sequencer, or the Digitakt II sequencer with conditional/retrig trigs
  • MicroFreak set to a fast monophonic voice that can articulate quickly with no envelope gaps between notes
  • Live recording one continuous take; a way to sustain/overlap so the stream never fully stops (continuity stands in for circular breathing)
  • Pen and paper to write your cell down first — the whole improv must come from that one small set
  • What you will make: one continuous take of a self-permuting stream. Focus on this effect: a single line, never stopping, that starts to imply more than one voice.
  • What you will turn off: chords, harmony, a written melody, song form, and any pause for "phrasing."
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a small cell run through real-time permutation, endless and gapless, never a literal loop.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are timbre or cell choices, not missing steps.
Reference pass · 12 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to three to four minutes of a Conic Sections solo. Write one short answer per question before you touch the gear.

  1. Does the line ever stop or take an audible breath?
    Answer

    No. There is no gap. Circular breathing keeps the stream continuous, so the sound never fully stops — that unbroken continuity is the spine of the whole mechanism, not a flourish.

  2. How many distinct notes are actually in play?
    Answer

    Surprisingly few. Underneath the blur is a small cell — a handful of pitches and figures — recycled at high speed. The density comes from how the cell is reordered, not from a large note vocabulary.

  3. Is the material repeating literally, or being reordered?
    Answer

    Reordered. You hear the same cells return, but the order, octave and articulation keep changing, so it is self-similar without ever being a literal loop. That is the permutation engine at work.

  4. Does one monophonic horn start to sound like more than one voice — and why?
    Answer

    Yes. The speed and the octave leaps split the single line into implied streams: low notes group into one voice, high notes into another, and the ear hears polyphony from one monophonic instrument. Implied polyphony is an emergent result of fast permutation across registers.

Now build the engine you described. Do not build from memory of the notes.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Define a small cell and commit to it

Phase focus: prove the whole improv will come from one tiny set.

Do this
  1. Write a cell of three to five pitches — no key, no melody, just a small ordered set you can play fast. Pick intervals that survive octave leaps (a few seconds and a wide jump work well).
  2. Play the cell by hand at speed, in one octave, with no gaps between notes — prove it is comfortable as a continuous loop before you add any variation.
  3. Write the rule out loud: "every note in this take is one of these pitches, in some octave." This is the constraint that makes the engine, not the taste, do the composing.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from the reference and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.

Expected result

A written cell of three to five pitches you can already play as a gapless fast loop, and a one-line rule that bans any note outside it.

Phase 2 — ~30 minutes

Build the permutation engine

Phase focus: make the cell reorder itself endlessly, gapless, never literally looping.

small cell → shift-register / sequencer permutation (VCV Rack · Digitakt II · MicroFreak) → endless gapless monophonic stream
Do this
  1. Patch a Turing-machine / shift-register engine feeding pitch from your cell — set the lock/randomize control so the order keeps changing slowly, returning to known cells without ever freezing into one fixed loop.
  2. If you prefer hardware, hold the cell in the sequencer and use conditional trigs / retrig and the arpeggiator to reorder and re-articulate it; let probability or fill conditions vary the order each pass.
  3. Add an octave operation: route a second slow source to push some notes up or down an octave, so the single line spreads across registers and starts to imply two voices.
  4. Set the voice so notes overlap or glue together — short release, high speed, no silence between trigs. The stream must never fully stop; this continuity stands in for circular breathing.

A gapless monophonic stream built only from your cell, reordering and octave-shifting on its own, self-similar but never repeating a fixed pattern — and already hinting at more than one voice.

Phase 3 — ~25 minutes

Perform one continuous take, steering live

Phase focus: drive the parameters in real time without ever stopping the stream.

Do this
  1. Record one continuous take from the first note. There is no edit point and no restart — the take is the performance.
  2. Steer two parameters live as the decision system: density (how fast / how busy the permutation runs) and register spread (how far the octave operation throws notes). Move them by hand to build and release tension.
  3. Keep the one rule sacred while you steer: never stop the stream, and never let it settle into a literal loop. If you feel a loop forming, nudge the permutation, not the pitches.
  4. Aim for three to four minutes unbroken. Listen for the implied polyphony to emerge from your own line as the register spread widens.

One unbroken take, built entirely from your cell, that thickens and thins under your hands but never stops and never loops literally — with at least one stretch where it implies more than one voice.

Verify against the work

A/B your take against a few minutes of Conic Sections — matched loudness, 15 seconds of yours, 15 of theirs. Then answer two questions in writing.

Does your stream stay gapless and non-literal the way theirs does?

Answer

If you can hear a breath, a stop, or a bar that repeats exactly, your engine is not running the way theirs is. The proof is continuity plus constant reordering — gapless but never the same order twice. A nameable seam or a fixed loop means the engine stalled.

Does one line of yours imply more than one voice the way theirs does?

Answer

Theirs splits into implied streams because of speed plus octave leaps. If yours reads as a single flat line, your register spread or density is too low — implied polyphony is the emergent payoff of permuting fast across registers, and its absence means the engine is running too tame.

  • Logbook — the miss list, two to three lines: "Matched: …" · "Missed: … (best guess why — e.g. stream stalled, fell into a literal loop, no implied second voice)" · "Change next: …"
  • Note which permutation operation did the most work — reorder, octave, or articulation — that is the one to lean on next attempt.
