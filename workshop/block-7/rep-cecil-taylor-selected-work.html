Replicate: dense cells, implied pulse

Copy one mechanism from Cecil Taylor — Unit Structures: develop short percussive "energy units" at high density and let momentum carry the pulse, with no steady beat ever stated. Build a short version on the rig and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism: improvisation as a real-time decision system. You take a few short cells — a cluster, a fast run, an interval leap — and drive the form by developing them (fragment, transpose, intensify, collide), so the pulse is felt and internal, never laid down as a click or a kick. You are stealing the structure (cells developed at density, pulse implied), never Taylor's notes.

The system you are replicating
Source workCecil Taylor — Unit Structures (1966)
The effectPiano played as percussion — dense, high-energy clusters and runs with a strong felt momentum but no stated metronomic beat
The mechanismShort motivic "energy units" (cells) are stated, varied and intensified at high density; cell-transformation and energy drive the form, and the pulse stays implied and internal rather than gridded

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Live open with the metronome OFF and record quantize OFF — you will play, not program; the piano-roll/keys are treated as percussion
  • MicroFreak set up as the "percussive piano": fast clustered notes, hard/short envelopes, easy register leaps; no long pads
  • Digitakt II ready with percussive sampled accents and clusters — but NOT armed as a steady kick pattern; free VCV Rack on hand if you want extra clustered voices
  • Pen and paper to name your two or three cells before you record — the units are defined, the development is improvised
  • What you will make: a short, continuous improvisation. Focus on this effect: high-density cell development with momentum and no stated beat.
  • What you will turn off: the metronome, quantize, any steady kick or click, and the habit of playing "lines" instead of developing material.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: small cells driven by repetition-with-variation, pulse implied, energy shaping the form.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are touch and material choices or rig limits, not a missing mechanism.
Reference pass · 12 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to a few minutes of Unit Structures. Write one short answer per question before you touch the gear.

  1. Can you tap a steady beat to it, or do you feel momentum without a fixed pulse?
    Answer

    You feel strong momentum but cannot lock a steady tap — every count you try slips. The drive is real, yet the pulse is implied and internal, carried by the energy of the playing rather than stated by any click or kick. That is the mechanism: forward motion without a grid.

  2. What is recurring — is there small material being developed, or just constant new notes?
    Answer

    Small material recurs. Short shapes — a cluster, a quick run, a leaping interval — keep coming back in altered form: fragmented, transposed, sped up, hammered harder. These are the "units." The density hides them at first, but the form is built from a handful of cells being worked, not endless unrelated invention.

  3. Is the energy constant, or does it build and release?
    Answer

    It builds and releases. Stretches of escalating intensity gather, peak, then thin out before the next surge. The energy curve — not a bar count — is what organizes the time. Following that arc is how you compose with no grid underneath.

  4. Does the piano sound "melodic" or "percussive," and what makes it percussive?
    Answer

    Percussive. Hard attacks, dense clusters struck rather than sung, and fast insistent repetition make the piano read as a tuned percussion instrument. The attack and the cluster, not the line, are doing the work. That is the touch you have to imitate on the rig.

Now build the mechanism you described. Do not build from memory of the notes.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Define two or three percussive energy-unit cells

Phase focus: name the material before you improvise, and make it percussive, not lyrical.

Do this
  1. Choose three short cells and write them down: a hand cluster (three to five close notes struck together), a fast run (a quick burst across registers), and an interval leap (one wide jump repeated). Each should be one to two seconds, no more.
  2. Voice them as percussion — short, hard envelopes, fast attack, little to no release; play them by hand so timing is human, not gridded.
  3. Prepare one or two sampled percussive clusters as accents you can fire by hand to punctuate the cells — not a loop, single hits.
  4. Practice each cell until you can play it cleanly, then mark how you might fragment, transpose, or intensify it later. The cell is fixed; its development is open.

Two or three short, percussive cells you can play by hand and name out loud — your raw units, before any development.

Phase 2 — ~25 minutes

Develop one cell at high density with no grid

Phase focus: momentum that comes from the playing, not from a click.

one cell → repeat with variation by hand (MicroFreak, metronome off) → density and drive, pulse implied
Do this
  1. Confirm metronome OFF and record quantize OFF. Nothing in this take should snap to a grid.
  2. Take a single cell and develop it for a minute straight: repeat it, then fragment it, transpose it, speed it up, hit it harder — never the same twice, always recognizably the same unit.
  3. Push the density up so a felt momentum emerges from your own timing. Let the pulse be implied by the regularity of your attacks, not stated by any external beat.
  4. Record the minute and listen back: the drive should be audible with nothing keeping time underneath. Do not quantize afterward.

Phase 3 — ~30 minutes

Perform one continuous take, colliding the cells on an energy arc

Phase focus: sustain the drive and shape one build-and-release, pulse implied throughout.

Do this
  1. Sketch the energy arc, not the notes: start, build, peak, release — roughly where each happens in a two-to-three minute take. That arc is the only structure.
  2. Improvise the whole arc in one pass: state a cell, develop it, collide it with another, intensify into the peak, then thin out. Fire Digitakt II clusters by hand as accents, never a steady pattern.
  3. Keep metronome and quantize off for the entire take. The pulse stays implied — at no point do you lay down a steady kick or click to hold it together.
  4. Record one continuous take. If you drift into a steady beat or the cells stop developing, stop and start again — do not fix it by quantizing.

Verify against the work

A/B your take against a few minutes of Unit Structures — matched loudness, 15 seconds of yours, 15 of theirs. Then answer two questions in writing.

Can you tap a steady beat to either one — and do both feel driven anyway?

Answer

If you can lock a steady tap to yours but not to theirs, you let a grid form — the give-away is that your hits became perfectly even. The mechanism is momentum with the pulse implied; an audible steady beat means you stated it instead of letting it be felt.

Is your material being developed the way theirs is, or just repeated?

Answer

Theirs keeps transforming its units — you can hear a cell return changed. If yours mostly loops the same shapes, you stopped developing and started repeating. Cell-transformation, not repetition, is what carries the form when there is no beat to lean on.

  • Logbook — the miss list, two or three lines: e.g. "Drifted into a steady beat around the peak." · "Cells stopped developing and just repeated in the middle." · "Energy stayed flat — no real build and release."
  • Note which cell carried the most momentum — that is the unit to open with next time.
