Block 7 · Replication R7 · Study

Rebuild the Set Moment

Take two minutes of the live set you deconstructed in L7 and re-perform it on your own rig — with matching panic paths. The rebuild includes the escape routes, not just the music: every section of your two minutes must have the same kind of exit their analysis claimed theirs had. Then perform it live, in one take, recorded — and trigger one fault on purpose, mid-take, to find out whether your escape routes are real.

Replication lab · R · S4 · E4 M2 F4 · half-cycle — requires the L7 Samples session · filed as study, never portfolio · scaffold

listen → hypothesis → draw the path → calibrate → build → fault → diagnose → print → delta
Start here
  • Put the L7 Samples-session worksheet on the desk; no worksheet, no R7.
  • Save a new set as R7-study and arm a recording path before any rehearsal — performed labs record everything, including rehearsals.
  • Build the two minutes as a performable system: committed elements prepared, performed elements assigned to hands, panic paths wired.
  • Perform it once, live, in one take, with one deliberate fault. Stop at the timebox; the corrected worksheet is the deliverable.
The point

Until now, replication labs tested whether you could hear a record. This one tests whether you could hear an operation. A live set’s sound can be approximated in an afternoon; its structure — what was committed, what was ridden, what the performer would grab when it slipped — can only be verified by performing it yourself, under the same conditions: real time, one take, no undo.

What “close” means here

Your sounds will differ — your rig, your material. The comparison lives one level up, in three things: the committed/performed split (the same elements prepared in advance, the same ones held in the hands), the transition logic (changes happening over the same kind of survivor, at the same rate), and the panic paths (each section escapable the way the worksheet claimed theirs was). And one thing more, which no studio replication has: when the fault hits, does your recovery hold the music together the way theirs did? A verdict on closeness, and a verdict on recovery — R7 files both.

How this lab works

Build the moment as a rig

  • One committed element per worksheet row, prepared exactly as claimed
  • One hand assignment per performed element — if both hands are busy, the analysis was wrong
  • Wire every escape route before rehearsing anything musical

Perform it, one take

  • Live, recorded, two minutes, no stopping
  • One fault, chosen in advance, triggered mid-take
  • Recover musically — the take continues to the end regardless

Two verdicts, one miss list

  • Closeness: A/B your take against the reference passage
  • Recovery: seconds from fault to music, judged from the recording
  • Correct the worksheet — that is what leaves the room

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
Matching panic pathsRebuilding not just what the performer played but what they could have done — the mute they kept armed, the fallback loop idling underneath. Your version must have an equivalent exit for every section, wired and rehearsed, even though the original never visibly used most of theirs. The escape routes are part of the system you are replicating.
The one-take ruleThe replica is performed start to finish, recorded, no edits, no second pass spliced in. A studio rebuild tests your ear; a one-take rebuild tests your ear plus whether the system you built can actually be operated. Falling apart and finishing anyway is a valid — often the most instructive — take.
The deliberate faultYou choose one realistic performance failure in advance — a muted element that shouldn’t be, a runaway level, a clock dropout, a wrong scene — and trigger it mid-take at a pre-decided moment. Block 7’s fault ritual moved on stage: predict, trigger, recover, and only afterwards diagnose at leisure from the recording.
Recovery verdictThe second judgment this lab files, alongside closeness: from the recording, how many seconds passed between the fault and the music being back in control — and would a stranger hearing the take know where the fault was? Judged from the recording, never from memory; memory always flatters the recovery.

1 · Target & analysis

The target is two minutes of the live set you deconstructed in L7’s Samples session — pick the two minutes containing at least one transition, because transitions are where the committed/performed split is most testable. Your worksheet is the blueprint: committed elements, performed elements, escape routes, per section. This is S4 — the worksheet and the brief are all you get; if a row is too vague to build a performable system from, sharpen it against the recording before you start.

  • The firewall. Name the project R7-study. The replica — including its recording — is study evidence, never portfolio output. Nothing from this session migrates into filed work: not the scenes, not the patterns, not the resampled audio. The corrected worksheet and the recovery findings are what leave this room.
  • Worksheet only. No rig-breakdown videos of this performer, no interviews, no gear lists. Someone else’s analysis would test someone else’s eyes. If you get stuck, go back to the footage with the six video questions — not to a search bar.
  • Your rig, their structure. Use your own material and your own machines — Digitakt, MicroFreak, the mixer, whatever lab 031 is certifying. You are not imitating their sounds; you are operating their system.
Before you start
  • The Samples-session worksheet from L7, all three middle columns filled for the chosen two minutes
  • The reference passage available locally for the A/B
  • A live signal-flow diagram of your rebuild rig, drawn before patching — performed labs do not start without one
  • The panic path written down: mute, limiter, bypass, stop, fallback loop — which of these each section uses, and where each control physically is
  • The deliberate fault chosen, with its trigger moment marked in the two-minute timeline
  • A recording path armed and tested — the take that isn’t recorded didn’t happen
  • Phones at the lab-001 mark; if the feedback or monitor path can run away, the limiter sits before your ears
  • Timebox: two sessions, three at most — build and rehearsal is one, the take and verdict is another; write the end date down
Before building

Pre-commit the predictions

In the logbook, before patching: which worksheet rows do you expect to be right — the committed/performed split, the transition logic, the escape routes — and which do you expect the performance to break? Then one more, new to this block: predict your own recovery time for the chosen fault, in seconds. The gap between predicted and actual recovery is this lab’s sharpest finding.

2 · Rebuild & verdict

Setup

The performed A/B rig

The A/B compares the reference passage against your recorded take, not against the live rig — the take is the replica. Reference on a track named REF, gain-matched with a Utility; the printed take on TAKE; one solo press flips between them, serial, never layered. The live rig itself records through the path your worksheet claims the original used — board feed, room, or both. The full setup recipe is forthcoming with Block 7 authoring; R1’s A/B procedure carries you until then.

Expected result

You can solo REF, then solo TAKE, at the same perceived loudness, over the same two-minute structure, hands-free — and the take contains one fault you can point to on the timeline.

live rig (committed + performed elements) → panic path (mute · limiter · bypass · stop · fallback) → record path → TAKE REF (Utility, gain-matched) → solo A/B vs TAKE → phones

The panic path sits between the rig and the recording on purpose: an escape route that isn’t in the signal path is a diagram decoration, not an escape route.

  1. Timebox first. Two sessions, three at the absolute most. The replica is finished when the timebox ends — and a performed lab has a harder stop than most: the take you have when time runs out is the take.
  2. Build the system from the worksheet. Committed elements prepared and saved; performed elements mapped to physical controls; every escape route wired and tested silently before any music is rehearsed. The escape routes are built first — that order is the block’s discipline.
  3. Rehearse the failure before the music. Trigger the chosen fault in rehearsal and walk the panic path once, hands on controls, at low level. This is the failure rehearsal in miniature — lab 031 scales it up; C6 requires it documented.
  4. Rehearse the music — at most twice. More rehearsal would replicate polish, not analysis. The worksheet claims this structure is operable; two passes is enough to test the claim.
  5. Perform the take. One take, two minutes, recorded, fault triggered at its marked moment, recovered as musically as you can, played to the end no matter what. If the take collapses entirely, that is a finding about the worksheet — file it and take one more, but the second take counts as final.
  6. A/B at matched loudness. REF then TAKE, thirty seconds each, one dimension per pass: the committed/performed split first, then transition logic, then density and levels, then the fault window — does their passage contain a comparable wobble, and how does theirs hide?
  7. Write the miss list and both verdicts. Every structural difference assigned to its layer — use the table below, which adds the Human layer this block exists for. Then the recovery verdict: seconds from fault to control, predicted vs actual, and whether a stranger would hear the seam.
  8. Correct the worksheet, close, quarantine. Strike the wrong claims, write the corrections beside them — especially escape routes that turned out to be unreachable in real time. Render the take into the logbook as study, close the project, never reopen it for portfolio work. The miss list is C6’s sharpened hypothesis.
The difference is…LayerExample
A different kind of sound entirelyMaterialTheir bed is a resampled chord; mine is a raw oscillator — a committed-column row was guessed wrong
Same elements, different balance or widthSignalTheir performed lead rides over the bed; mine fights it — the live gain plan, not the material, missed
Same sound, moves differentlyControlTheir filter sweep is one long ridden gesture; mine is steppy — a performed element was actually committed automation, or vice versa
Same moves, land differently in timeTimeTheir transition takes sixteen bars over a survivor; mine jump-cuts — the transition logic was transcribed wrong
Same system, operated differentlyHumanMy hands couldn’t reach the escape route in time, or the recovery took eight seconds against a predicted two — the worksheet’s human-role row was a guess about me, and it missed
Decision check — rig miss or hands miss?

For each item on the miss list: did it fail because your rig cannot do what theirs does — no scene morphing, one stereo bus too few — or because your hands and attention weren’t fast enough on a rig that could? A rig miss is a note for the gear roadmap and a fallback to design. A hands miss is the gold: it is exactly what lab 031’s rehearsals and C6’s failure rehearsal exist to train, located in advance.

Pass gate — R7 complete when
  • The take — two minutes, performed live in one take, recorded, filed in the logbook as study
  • The live signal-flow diagram and the written panic path, as built — corrected after the take if reality disagreed
  • The Samples-session worksheet it was performed from, with corrections marked — especially the escape-route column
  • A/B notes at matched loudness — closeness verdict, one dimension per pass
  • The fault entry: fault chosen, trigger moment, predicted vs actual recovery time, recovery verdict from the recording
  • The miss list — each miss assigned to its layer, rig or hands named
  • One sentence: what can you operate now that you couldn’t before?

No full bundle — this is a half-cycle, but unlike earlier replication labs it does run a fault, because here the fault is the lesson. If the take sounds nothing like the record but the structure held and the recovery worked, the lab passes: the operation, not the sound, is what is being judged.

Replication principle

You haven’t replicated a live set until you’ve replicated its escape routes — the music is what the audience hears; the exits are what the performer built.
