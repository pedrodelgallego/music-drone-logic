Replicate: a rule set against your habits

Copy one mechanism from Derek Bailey — Aida: build an improvisation out of negative rules that forbid your own defaults, then perform a real-time solo take under those bans. You are stealing the decision system — playing from what is forbidden — never Bailey's notes.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill treats improvisation as a real-time decision system. Bailey's non-idiomatic guitar is not "free" in the sense of arbitrary — every note is chosen against a set of bans on anything that sounds like a known idiom. You are stealing that engine: identify your own habits, write rules that forbid each one, and let the playing be generated by what you are not allowed to do.

The system you are replicating
Source workDerek Bailey — Aida (1980), solo guitar
The effectGuitar that refuses to sound like guitar music — atonal intervals, abrupt jumps in register and timbre, placed silences, no groove, no key, no recognisable lick
The mechanismAn anti-pattern rule set: negative rules ("never repeat an interval", "never resolve", "if a phrase sounds like something you know, abandon it") that forbid the player's own defaults, so the music is generated by what is banned rather than what is chosen

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • One live instrument you can play continuously: MicroFreak played by hand (wide register, abrupt timbre via engine/filter) or an actual guitar through Live — pick one and commit to a single real-time take
  • Digitakt II ready for abrupt sample and pitch jumps with no steady kit; free VCV Rack if you want an unpredictable second voice
  • Live open with a recorder armed for one unedited pass; pen and paper for the habit inventory and the rule set, placed where you can read it while playing
  • What you will make: one continuous solo take. Focus on this effect: playing that constantly defeats your own reflexes so it never settles into an idiom.
  • What you will turn off: key, groove, your favourite scale, your comfortable register, your go-to rhythm — every default you can name.
  • What you will prove: your take and REF use the same core mechanism — the playing is governed by negative rules, and habits that slip through are visible because nothing is edited out.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are instrument choices or rig limits, not bans you forgot to apply.
Reference pass · 12 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to three to four minutes of Aida. Write one short answer per question before you touch the gear.

  1. Can you predict the next note, or does each one defeat what you expected?
    Answer

    Each note defeats expectation. The moment your ear leans toward a continuation — a resolution, a step up the scale — the next note goes somewhere else. Unpredictability is not a side effect here; it is the rule the playing is built on.

  2. Does any phrase repeat, or settle into a pattern you could tap or hum?
    Answer

    No. There is no groove, no key, and no figure that returns. Nothing is allowed to become a pattern, because a pattern would be a habit, and the system forbids habits.

  3. What is keeping it from sounding "like guitar music" — what is being avoided?
    Answer

    Avoidance is the technique. No blues licks, no scale runs, no riffs, no chord that resolves — every gesture that would mark it as idiomatic guitar is refused. The negative space defines the music: it sounds like a guitar with all the familiar moves banned.

  4. Are the silences accidental, or are they placed as events?
    Answer

    Placed. The gaps are not pauses to think — they land as deliberate breaks that stop any momentum from building. Silence is one more tool for refusing flow, used as precisely as a note.

Now build the mechanism you described. Do not imitate Bailey's notes — build the rule set that produces this behaviour.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~20 minutes

Write your habit inventory

Phase focus: name your own defaults honestly — you cannot ban what you will not admit.

Do this
  1. Play freely for two minutes on your chosen instrument and just listen to yourself — do not try to be interesting.
  2. Write down your scale habit: the notes or mode your hand reaches for when you stop thinking.
  3. Write down your register habit: the comfortable range you sit in, and the timbre you default to.
  4. Write down your rhythm habit and your one go-to gesture — the run, bend, or shape you fall back on. Be specific; vague entries cannot be banned.

Phase 2 — ~25 minutes

Convert each habit into a negative rule

Phase focus: turn the inventory into an anti-pattern rule set that forbids each default.

habit inventory → one negative rule per habit → rule sheet in view while you play
Do this
  1. Write one ban per habit — phrase each as "never…": never play in your home key; never stay in your comfortable register two notes in a row; never repeat a rhythm.
  2. Add Bailey's general bans on top: never resolve, never repeat an interval, and if a phrase starts sounding like something you know, abandon it mid-phrase.
  3. Add one positive obligation so silence is in play: place at least one deliberate silence whenever momentum starts to build.
  4. Print or write the rule sheet large and prop it in front of you — you must be able to read it without looking away from the instrument.

A visible rule set of five to eight negative rules, each one pointed at a habit you actually have — the engine that will generate the take.

Phase 3 — ~30 minutes

Perform one real-time take under the bans

Phase focus: play continuously while obeying the rules in real time — break habits mid-phrase when you catch them.

Do this
  1. Arm one unedited recording. You get a single continuous take of three to five minutes; no comping, no fixes afterward.
  2. Play slowly. Before each note, glance at the rule sheet and check the note does not break a ban — make abrupt jumps in register and timbre to stay clear of your defaults.
  3. When you catch a phrase turning into something you know, abandon it inside the phrase — jump register, change engine or sample, or drop into a placed silence.
  4. Keep the recorder running through your mistakes. Caught and missed habits both belong on the take so you can hear which bans held.

Expected result

One unedited take where the playing never settles, register and timbre jump, silences are placed, and every habit that slipped through is audible because nothing was cleaned up.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
It settles into a pattern after a minuteYou stopped reading the sheet and the reflexes took overSlow down, keep eyes on the rules, and place a silence the moment you feel flow returning
It sounds random, not refusedYou played to avoid rules rather than choosing real notes that pass themMake a deliberate choice each time — a banned move is still a decision; arbitrary noise is just a different default
Verify against the work

A/B your take against three to four minutes of Aida — matched loudness, 15 seconds of yours, 15 of theirs. Then answer two questions in writing.

Does yours defeat expectation as consistently as theirs, or can you still predict where it goes?

Answer

If a listener can anticipate your next note while Bailey's keeps surprising, a habit is still running the show — usually your scale or register reflex. Consistent unpredictability is the proof the bans, not your defaults, are generating the playing.

Do your silences land as placed events the way theirs do, or do they read as pauses?

Answer

Theirs cut momentum on purpose; if yours feel like hesitation or thinking time, you are not using silence as a tool yet. A placed silence stops a building pattern — that is the obligation, not a rest between ideas.

  • Logbook — the miss list, two or three lines: "Habit that slipped through: …" · "Ban that held best: …" · "Change next: …"
  • Name the one default that was hardest to refuse — that is the first rule to tighten on the next take.
