Block 7 — Performance & Curation

What the human does in real time

Riding, live resampling, audio feedback systems, the long form, and the performance rig — the operational engineering year. Six years of studio time have taught you to build systems that work; this block teaches you to operate systems that are allowed to fail in front of people, and to recover musically when they do.

Engineering focus — operational engineering

Every performance lab requires a live signal-flow diagram, a panic path (mute, limiter, bypass, stop, fallback loop — in that order of preference), a failure rehearsal, a recording strategy, and a post-performance fault report. The question is never “did the set go well?” — it is “when the system became unstable, did the performer understand it well enough to recover musically?” Block 7’s fault emphasis is performance failure and the panic path: faults are rehearsed before they happen, because on stage there is no second take.

Working order

Do these in this order
  1. Programme L7 — run the three sessions first; the video session is the heart of the block, watched for recovery from failure, not just flow
  2. Lab 027 — ride parameters and print the rides as the arrangement; prerequisite for 028 and C6
  3. Lab 028 — play the sampler as an instrument: capture, mangle, redeploy in real time (requires 027)
  4. Lab 029 — build an audio feedback system; requires lab 020 and runs its safety checklist before any sound exists
  5. Lab 030 — curate at scale: a 15+ minute form that survives its own length
  6. Lab 031 — certify the performance rig: full technical rehearsal, emergency procedures, set recording plan
  7. R7 — rebuild two minutes of an L7 live set, panic paths included, performed in one take
  8. Capstone C6 — the Live Set: thirty minutes, performed and recorded
  • Prerequisites in this block: 027 before 028 and before C6 — riding is the grammar everything else here speaks. Lab 029 requires lab 020’s feedback-paths work; its safety checklist is not optional. C6 additionally requires lab 031’s rig certification and a complete L7.

Listening programme L7 — performance practice

Session 1 · Records

Live recordings heard as systems

Live recordings analyzed with the six-question system frame — Tudor’s Rainforest IV and Nakamura’s no-input mixing board among them. What was committed before the show, what stayed live, and what could the performer actually reach in time?

Session 2 · Samples

Deconstruction — one live set’s structure

Take one live set apart minute by minute: what is a backing element, what is performed, where the transitions live, and where the escape routes are. The worksheet becomes the blueprint for replication lab R7.

Session 3 · Video

Watch the hands — the heart of L7

Tudor archival footage, contemporary hardware live sets, rig-breakdown videos — watched for recovery from failure, not just flow. Where are the hands? What is monitored? What is pre-planned vs ridden? What is the failure risk, what is being recorded, and what cannot be recovered?

  • Deliverable: written system analyses + one stolen principle — a structural idea, never material, named in advance and evaluated at C6. Full briefs and worksheets: programme L7

Gear honesty — the no-input practice

The no-input feedback work of lab 029 (Nakamura’s method) ideally wants a mixer that can patch its own outputs back into its own inputs — aux returns into channel inputs, sends folded onto the bus — so the desk can self-oscillate and be steered. The compact aux-send mixer on the bench here cannot fully self-patch; what you build is an approximation of the no-input instrument, not the real thing, and it is fair to say so in the spec. Safety caution: any self-patched feedback loop can jump to a destructive howl in one fader-millimeter — set the limiter and the emergency mute before closing the loop, start with monitoring low, and never close the loop on headphones at level.

Performance-rig depth — beyond the EC4 minimum

The FaderFox EC4 is the minimum ride controller for this block — enough to map the six reachable controls and pass. A deeper performance rig earns more range: a small fader-per-channel mixer (a hand on every level at once, not paged) for true live balancing and dub-style mutes; expression pedals to free a hand by moving filter, send, or feedback depth with a foot; and outboard spring or tape as a dedicated effect to ride for dub and feedback work, with its own return on the desk. None of this is required to pass C6 — it is the next purchase order once the rig is certified.

Voice — a prompt to carry forward

Performance forces the question Block 8 will answer: what are you refusing? Every rig you certify and every panic path you write is also a list of what you will not do on stage. Name one thing — a sound, a move, a crowd-pleasing reflex — that you are deliberately leaving out of your set, and why. That refusal is the first sketch of a voice.

Replication lessons — one per reference

  • Around fifty lessons, one per reference. Every listening reference across labs 027–030 and programme L7 gets its own 60–120 minute step-by-step replication lesson — Tudor’s electroacoustic feedback, sampler-performance moves, long-form curation, live hardware techno, no-input mixing — each rebuilt on your own rig with the lesson’s concept as the lens. Lessons are included here.

Live-performance systems — riding, live-coding, feedback, generative

A strand of replication lessons framed not as arranging but as performing a system live: a take with the hands on a few controls, the panic path written first, no repair afterwards. Each rebuilds the reference’s working mechanism on your own rig.
