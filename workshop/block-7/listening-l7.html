Performance practice — watched for recovery, not flow

Three sessions about what the human does in real time. Until now you have listened to finished objects; here you listen to operations — recordings and footage of people running unstable systems in public, watched for what they monitor, what they ride, and what they do in the seconds after something goes wrong.

Listening programme · 3 sessions · one full cycle · feeds labs 027–031 and capstone C6 · complete

The point

A live set is not a track performed; it is a system operated. The flow you admire in a great live performance is mostly invisible engineering — escape routes built in advance, parameters chosen because they fail gracefully, hands resting near the controls that matter. These sessions teach you to see that engineering, and above all to see recovery: the moment the system slips and the performer turns the slip into music.

Listening is the loop

These sessions are the first step of the operating loop for every Block 7 lab. The panic paths you design in 027–031, the rig you certify in 031, and the set you perform at C6 are all stolen from what you observe here. The video session is the heart of this programme — Block 7 is the one block where watching outranks listening.

  • Programme: complete — the briefs, references, and method below are final in shape; the worksheets are included below.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
Panic path The pre-decided sequence of moves when the system runs away: mute, limiter, bypass, stop, fallback loop — in that order of preference, because each one destroys less music than the next. A performer without a panic path doesn’t have a rig; they have a bet.
Pre-planned vs ridden Every sound in a live set is one or the other: committed in advance (a backing stem, a saved pattern) or held in the hands right now (a filter being ridden, a level being judged). The ratio between the two is the set’s real design — and you can read it off the video by watching where the hands go.
Recovery Not fixing the fault — playing through it. A recovery is musical when the audience cannot tell where the failure ended and the intention resumed. It requires understanding the system well enough to know, mid-collapse, which parts are still trustworthy.
The unrecoverable What no archive will ever contain: an unrecorded set, a feedback voice whose room is gone, a patch that lived only in a device’s volatile state. Live work forces the print-strategy question — what is being recorded, and what is gone the moment it sounds? — to be answered before the first note.

How to listen — the six-question frame

Every recorded work gets the same six questions, answered in writing in the logbook. A few lines per question; vagueness is the only wrong answer.

  1. Inputs — what raw material enters the system, and what was committed before the performance began?
  2. Transforms — what changes it, in what order?
  3. Feedback / variation — what keeps it alive over time, and how much of that is the performer?
  4. Constraints — what prevents clutter — and what prevents disaster?
  5. Human role — what is performed, decided, curated, muted, recovered?
  6. Likely audio path — what had to be routed, monitored, and recorded for this performance to exist — and where were the escape routes?
  • One context prompt, for the strand especially: these practices came from somewhere — postwar European free improvisation, the New York and Japanese underground, club and rave culture, the academy versus the squat. For at least one reference, write a few lines on why it sounded like this here, then: who was in the room, what it was a reaction against, what economic or technological conditions made it possible. A live practice is a social fact before it is a signal flow, and knowing the lineage changes what you can honestly steal from it.

Session 1 — Records: live recordings heard as systems

Two recorded performances at opposite poles of performance practice: a room full of resonating objects with no synthesizer in sight, and a mixing desk producing sound from nothing but its own wiring. Both are systems that could fail at any second — that risk is audible, and hearing it is the assignment.

01 David Tudor — Rainforest IV the instrument is the room

1973, realized with Composers Inside Electronics: audio signals driven through physical objects — metal sheets, barrels, sculptures — whose resonances become the music, picked up again by contact mics and returned to the room. Nothing here is a preset; the piece is a network of unstable electroacoustic circuits, performed by walking among them. It is this block’s founding document: the performer as operator of a system that is partly out of their control by design.

Listen for
  • The double life of each sound: the dry electronic source underneath, and the object’s resonance it wears — try to separate them
  • Instability as material: moments where a resonance starts to run away, and what (or who) reins it in
  • The space itself: how much of what you hear is the room responding, not any single object
After — verify

Could you point to one moment that nobody composed — where the system clearly decided? If everything sounded intentional, the piece’s wager passed you by: relisten to any five minutes and track a single resonance, asking at each swell whether a human chose that or physics did.

02 Toshimaru Nakamura — No-Input Mixing Board feedback as instrument — the 029 reference

A mixing desk with its outputs patched back into its own inputs, and nothing else: every tone, pulse, and texture is self-generated feedback, steered with faders and EQ. This is the purest case of the block’s question — the entire instrument is a controlled failure mode, and the performance is continuous recovery. It is also lab 029’s safety reference: every sound on this record could, one fader-millimeter away, be a destructive howl.

Listen for
  • How a system with no input can still have a vocabulary — the families of tones the desk “wants” to make, and how Nakamura moves between them
  • The threshold riding: how close each sound lives to either silence or runaway, and the micro-adjustments holding it there
  • Dynamics as danger: when it gets loud, what kind of loud is it — chosen, or barely contained?
After — verify

Where does the control live, when there is no input to control? In the routing and the thresholds — gain, EQ, and send amounts are the entire score. If the record sounded like random noise, relisten to one track and count the distinct stable states the system visits; the count is usually surprisingly musical.

Session 2 — Samples: one live set, deconstructed

Session 2 · Deconstruction

Map a live set’s structure, minute by minute

Pick one live set — one of this programme’s video sets is the natural choice — and map its structure: one row per minute or per section. For each row decide what is a committed element (prepared before the show: stems, patterns, saved scenes) and what is performed (ridden, triggered, judged in the moment), where the transitions live, and where you believe the escape routes are — what the performer would reach for if this section collapsed. Finish with the sixth question for the whole set: what rig had to exist, and what was it recording?

Every row needs an entry in all three middle columns — a section with no committed elements, no performed elements, or no imaginable escape route means the analysis isn’t finished. Session 3 — Video: watch the hands

The heart of L7. Two curated live sets — both with the hands fully visible — plus archival and rig-breakdown footage. Watch each set twice: once as an audience member, then again watching only the hands, the meters, and the moments where something nearly goes wrong. YouTube is the canonical source for live sets; that is acceptable here.

03 Surgeon — live at Boiler Room x Dekmantel Festival, Amsterdam, 2014 live hardware techno — this workshop’s hardware

An hour of improvised hardware techno from the era when Surgeon’s touring rig was built around the Elektron Octatrack — the machine family this workshop runs on. Nothing is a playback: patterns are mutated, resampled, and rebuilt continuously, on a rig small enough to carry. This is the most direct model in the whole programme for what your C6 rig will be.

Listen for
  • Where the hands rest between moves — the controls kept within reach are the ones he expects to need fast
  • The transitions: how a running pattern is dismantled and replaced without the floor ever dropping out — what stays constant while everything else changes
  • Near-misses: moments where a mutation lands rough, and the very next move — that move is the recovery vocabulary
After — verify

What is his fallback layer — the element that never stops while everything above it is rebuilt? If you can’t name it, rewatch any transition and listen to what survives it; the survivor is the safety net, and choosing one is the first decision of your own set.

Watch: YouTube (YouTube-only is acceptable for live sets)

Replication lesson — lesson

04 KiNK — Boiler Room Sofia sampler performance, hands visible

A hometown live hardware set famous precisely for what this session is about: the hands are visible the whole time, the crowd is close enough to touch the gear, and the performance is openly physical — pads finger-drummed, parameters ridden hard, the audience shown what each move does. Where Surgeon hides the engineering inside flow, KiNK performs the engineering itself.

Listen for
  • Showmanship vs operation: which gestures change the sound, and which are for the room — learn to tell them apart on sight
  • Risk appetite: the moments he hands a sound to the crowd or to chance, and what he keeps locked down underneath to make that survivable
  • What is monitored: where his eyes go between moves — meters, the crowd, the gear — and what that says about what can fail
After — verify

Name one moment where something audibly misfired and the set absorbed it. There is at least one in any honest live set; if you found none, you watched the crowd instead of the hands — rewatch ten minutes with the sound low, watching only for hesitation and correction.

Archival and rig-breakdown footage

Alongside the two sets, watch Tudor archival footage — the Rainforest performances you analyzed in Session 1, now seen — and at least one rig-breakdown video, where a performer walks through their own live routing. The suggestions below are searches, not curated picks; choose footage where the rig is actually visible and operated.

Search: hardware live set rig breakdownwatch for the routing diagram behind the flow — and ask what their panic path is
Search: Nakamura no-input mixing board livewatch for fader discipline at the feedback threshold — millimeters of travel, total attention
  1. Where are the hands? At rest and in motion — the resting position is the map of expected trouble.
  2. What is monitored? Meters, headphones, the room, the gear — what does the performer check, and how often?
  3. What is pre-planned vs ridden? Estimate the ratio, and find the evidence for your estimate.
  4. What is the failure risk? Which element, failing right now, would be audible to everyone — and which would only the performer notice?
  5. What is recorded? Board feed, room mics, stems, nothing — what will exist tomorrow?
  6. What cannot be recovered? If this performance were lost, what about it is unrepeatable — and does the performer seem to know it?
Watch for the recovery

Every honest live set contains at least one failure. Find it. The performers worth studying are not the ones who never slip — they are the ones whose slips you almost cannot find, because the recovery was already designed. Count the seconds between fault and recovery; that number is what Block 7 trains.

After — verify

For each set watched: can you draw the rig from memory — sources, mixer, effects, what feeds the recording? Can you name its panic path, even as a guess? If either answer is no, rewatch ten minutes watching only hands and cabling.

Strand — free improvisation / EAI

An optional lineage strand. The live, feedback, and recovery practice of this block did not begin with techno — it inherits from free improvisation and electroacoustic improvisation (EAI), where decades of players treated real-time risk, table-top electronics, and listening-as-control as the whole discipline. Read these as the family tree behind your panic paths: musicians who never had a second take and built an art out of it. Each gets a full replication lesson on your own rig.

E1 AMM — selected work collective EAI · listening as the score

The founding London ensemble of electroacoustic improvisation: no themes, no leader, the form decided entirely by what each player will and will not add to a shared, unstable texture. The original model of a system operated collectively in real time.

Replication lesson — lesson

E2 Keith Rowe — selected work tabletop guitar · feedback as instrument

The guitar laid flat and played with objects, radios, and feedback — the direct ancestor of the no-input desk: the instrument is a controlled feedback field, and the performance is continuous steering of what it wants to do.

Replication lesson — lesson

E3 Derek Bailey — selected work non-idiomatic improvisation

The theorist-practitioner of improvisation with no style to fall back on — every gesture chosen live, nothing pre-committed. The purest case of “performed, not played back,” and a hard test of recovery when there is no plan to recover to.

Replication lesson — lesson

E4 Evan Parker — selected work solo saxophone · circular-breathing systems

Solo saxophone built into a self-sustaining system — circular breathing and multiphonics generating continuous, evolving texture from one breath that never stops. A human running a feedback-like process with no machine in the signal path.

Replication lesson — lesson

E5 Ikue Mori — selected work drum machines as improvising instrument

Drum machines re-purposed from timekeepers into a live improvising voice — patterns triggered, mutated, and answered in real time. The closest EAI ancestor to this workshop’s own hardware-as-instrument practice.

Replication lesson — lesson

E6 Otomo Yoshihide — selected work turntables, guitar, no-input

From dense turntable noise to near-silent onkyo: a player who moves between maximal and minimal feedback-based systems, and who has worked directly in the no-input idiom. A bridge between EAI and the Nakamura reference in Session 1.

Replication lesson — lesson

E7 Cecil Taylor — selected work free jazz · energy and large-scale form

Free-jazz piano at full intensity sustained across long spans — the question of how a high-energy improvisation holds shape over time without a chart. The improviser’s answer to Block 7’s long-form problem.

Strand — live-performance systems

An optional strand on performances that are not tracks played back but systems operated in real time — biosignal feedback, live-coding, feedback management, solo laptop noise, generative duos, one-take capture, and semi-autonomous patches. Read them as eight different answers to one question: what does the human actually do, in the moment, when the instrument is partly running itself? Each gets a full replication lesson on your own rig, framed as performing the system live, not arranging it.

P1 Alvin Lucier — Music for Solo Performer amplified brainwaves drive percussion

Electrodes pick up the performer’s alpha waves; the signal is amplified into loudspeakers coupled to drums and cymbals, which resonate only when it arrives. Nothing is struck by hand — the performance is the management of a biological control source. The founding case of operating a system you cannot fully command.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Replication lesson — lesson

P2 Renick Bell — Live TidalCycles Sets live-coding · rules typed in real time

The instrument is code. Bell types and edits short pattern expressions while they play and the rhythm reorganises itself — densities, samples, and probabilistic structures fold over each other because a rule changed, not because a part was triggered. The performance is the editing, and a typo can drop the whole pattern.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Replication lesson — lesson

P3 Robert Henke — Lumière one engine drives sound and image

A custom system generates synchronised sound and laser-drawn vectors from one shared parameter set; Henke performs by riding that engine, and both move together because they are the same data interpreted twice. A model of a rig where a few controls have total, legible reach.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Replication lesson — lesson

P4 Sunn O))) — Live White volume and feedback management

A chord is struck rarely; the room is filled with sustained amplifier feedback, shaped over minutes by where the player stands, by volume, and by pedal switching. The rock cousin of the no-input desk — the instrument is feedback, and the skill is holding it on the edge without it collapsing.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Replication lesson — lesson

P5 Pita — Get Out (live laptop) solo laptop · ride gain and filter

Raw, harsh digital material — sine bursts, distortion, glitch — performed live entirely through gain rides, brutal cuts to silence, and filter sweeps. No song, almost no instrument: the laptop makes ugly raw stuff and the performer rides it into a form, with silence used as a structural cut.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Replication lesson — lesson

P6 Russell Haswell & Florian Hecker — Blackest Ever Black live duo · generative synthesis

Two musicians drive generative synthesis algorithms live — stochastic processes that produce sound on their own — and the performance is the steering and answering of those processes. Improvisation where the instrument partly composes itself and the human role is reaction and constraint, not note-entry.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Replication lesson — lesson

P7 Colin Stetson — New History Warfare Vol. 2 one continuous take · multi-mic · no overdubs

Solo saxophone with no overdubs and no loops: each piece is one continuous performance. The enormity comes from circular breathing and from capture — key-clicks, sung tones, and mechanical noise picked up by an array of mics, each printed as its own layer. The production is the mic placement and the single take.

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Replication lesson — lesson

P8 Kaitlyn Aurelia Smith — Live Buchla semi-autonomous patch · ride a few controls

A Buchla Music Easel patched so its sequencer, random source, and envelopes feed each other — the system produces evolving melody and rhythm largely on its own. Smith performs by riding a handful of controls. Most notes are not chosen; they emerge from the patch. The skill is designing a system worth hearing without you.

Deliverable — the stolen principle

File all worksheets in the logbook. Then write down one stolen principle — a structural idea, never material — before you build anything with it. It will be applied and judged at capstone C6: it must appear structurally in your live set, and you must point to it in the defense.

Pass standard
Minimum passThree sessions filed, six-question notes exist for the records, the set-deconstruction worksheet exists, the six video questions are answered for both sets, and one stolen principle is named.
Solid passThe stolen principle is structural enough that another performer could design a set from it without copying any sound or any rig.
Advanced transferYou can identify the same principle operating in a studio work that was never performed live.
  • Valid: “one element never stops — every transition happens over a survivor” — the fallback layer as form, stolen from the move, not the set
  • Valid: “keep the dangerous control under the resting hand” — rig ergonomics as composition
  • Invalid: “open with a kick like Surgeon’s” — that is material, not structure
Programme L7 — pass gate
  • Two record analyses — six questions each, three passes per record
  • One set-deconstruction worksheet — committed vs performed vs escape route, one row per section, confidence marked
  • Two video worksheets — the six video questions, plus one named recovery per set
  • One stolen principle — structural, named in advance, evaluated at C6
