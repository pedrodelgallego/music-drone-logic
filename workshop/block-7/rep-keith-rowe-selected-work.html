Replicate: a chance signal in a controlled bed

Copy one mechanism from Keith Rowe — The Room: build a texture bed you fully control, then route a genuinely uncontrolled signal (a live radio) into it and improvise only the relationship between the two. Build a short version on the rig and compare it to the reference at the same loudness.

OverviewListeningLabsRebuildCapstoneRep lessonsDashboard
The point

This drill teaches the record's core mechanism as a real-time decision system: a stable, performer-shaped texture PLUS an autonomous chance source you do not author. The music lives in the interaction — when you let chance through, when you mask it. You are stealing the structure (controlled bed + uncontrolled signal + live response), never Rowe's specific sounds or radio fragments.

The system you are replicating
Source workKeith Rowe — The Room (2007), solo tabletop guitar
The effectA quiet, slowly evolving sound-world of non-instrumental sounds — scrapes, ebow drones, prepared objects — with fragments of radio speech and music drifting in by chance, uninvited, against the controlled bed
The mechanismA chance external signal (radio) injected into a controlled texture: the performer shapes the bed entirely but does not choose what the radio does — only how to respond to it in real time (foreground it, bury it, let it collide)

Listen: YouTube · Spotify

Before you start
  • Live open with a controlled texture/drone bed on one or two tracks, plus an empty audio input track armed and monitoring
  • A genuinely uncontrolled chance source ready: a real AM/FM radio into your interface, an internet stream you do not choose the timing of, or a deliberate audio feedback loop
  • MicroFreak set for non-pitched textures (scrapes, ebow-like drones); Digitakt II loaded with object/scrape samples and ready to resample the radio; free VCV Rack if you want an extra texture or feedback path
  • A fader or knob mapped to the radio input level — that one control is the instrument you will actually play
  • What you will make: a short continuous take. Focus on this effect: a controlled quiet bed with an unpredictable signal drifting in and out of it.
  • What you will turn off: beats, song form, any plan for what the radio will say or play, and any urge to edit out collisions afterward.
  • What you will prove: your version and REF use the same core mechanism: a fully controlled bed plus a genuinely uncontrolled source, with the interest in the live relationship between them.
  • What counts as success: remaining differences are sound-source or rig choices, not a missing chance source or a scripted radio.
Reference pass · 12 minutes

Listen before you build

Listen to several minutes of The Room. Write one short answer per question before you touch the gear.

  1. Which sounds were clearly intended, and which were clearly not chosen by the performer?
    Answer

    The bed — the scrapes, drones and prepared-object textures — is plainly shaped and intended. The radio fragments of speech and music are clearly not chosen: they arrive at the wrong moment, in the wrong key, cut off mid-word. That contrast between authored and unauthored sound is the structure.

  2. Does the radio's content matter, or only its texture and timing?
    Answer

    Mostly its texture and timing. You rarely follow what a voice is saying; what registers is that a human grain or a snatch of melody suddenly intrudes on an abstract bed, and when it does. The meaning is the intrusion itself, not the message.

  3. Does the performer fight the chance signal or fold it in?
    Answer

    Both, deliberately. Sometimes the radio is let through almost naked; sometimes the bed swells to mask it; sometimes they are allowed to collide and clash. The performance is that decision, made live and repeatedly — not a fixed treatment of the radio.

  4. Does anything sound like "guitar," and why not?
    Answer

    Almost nothing. The guitar is laid flat and prepared with objects, ebow and scrapes, so it produces non-instrumental textures rather than strummed or picked notes. The instrument is a sound-source, not a guitar — which is why your MicroFreak and Digitakt II can stand in for it.

Now build the mechanism you described. Do not script what the chance source will do.

Steps

Your rig
Phase 1 — ~25 minutes

Build a controlled bed you fully shape

Phase focus: a slow, non-pitched texture that is entirely yours to control.

Do this
  1. Make a quiet, non-pitched texture — an ebow-like sustained drone, a scrape, a filtered noise bed. No melody, no clear pitch you would call a note.
  2. Layer one or two sampled object/scrape sounds, looped or held, low in the mix — the equivalent of objects resting on the strings.
  3. Set this bed at a low, stable level with slow movement only — gentle filter or volume drift over tens of seconds, nothing that grabs attention.
  4. Confirm you can shape every element of it by hand: this is the half of the system you fully author.
  1. Work in Live 12 only for this phase: keep REF looped from the reference and use audio clips, Simpler/Sampler, clip envelopes, and resampling.
  2.   3. Keep every source on named tracks, then use Utility to match the level against REF before judging it.
  4. Record or resample the result to a fresh audio track named PRINT; do not leave the proof only as devices and MIDI.

Expected result

A quiet, slowly evolving texture with no tune and no beat, every part of it under your direct control.

Phase 2 — ~25 minutes

Wire in a genuinely uncontrolled chance signal

Phase focus: route in a source you cannot predict — and refuse to tame it.

live radio / web stream / feedback → Live audio input track (one fader mapped) → into the same space as the bed
Do this
  1. Pick one truly uncontrolled source — a physical AM/FM radio into your interface, a live internet stream you do not curate, or an audio feedback loop. It must produce content you did not choose.
  2. Route it into an armed audio input track and map its level to one fader or knob. That fader is the only thing you will "play."
  3. Optionally resample short fragments of the radio so you can drop unpredictable cut-ups back in later — but never pre-arrange when they land.
  4. Do not EQ, gate, or filter the chance signal into something safe. Its unpredictability is the point; treating it as decoration breaks the mechanism.

Phase 3 — ~30 minutes

Perform the relationship in one continuous take

Phase focus: improvise only when to let chance through and when to mask it — never script the radio, never edit out collisions.

Do this
  1. Start recording one continuous take with the bed running and the chance fader down. Decide everything in real time from here.
  2. Improvise the relationship: raise the radio to foreground an intrusion, swell the bed to bury it, or let them collide. Respond to what the source actually does, not to a plan.
  3. When the radio does something you did not want — a jingle, a clear sentence — react live: fold it in, fight it, or step back. Do not stop the take.
  4. Keep the take in one pass and keep every collision in. The unedited interaction between intention and chance is the deliverable.

One continuous recording where a controlled bed and an unpredictable signal meet, with audible moments of letting-through, masking and collision that you decided as they happened.

If it fails
SymptomCauseFix
The radio behaves like a planned partYou cued fragments on the grid or rehearsed its entriesHand control back to the live source; only your response to it is allowed to be deliberate

Verify against the work

A/B your take against several minutes of The Room — matched loudness, 15 seconds of yours, 15 of theirs. Then answer two questions in writing.

Does your chance signal sound genuinely unauthored the way the radio does?

Answer

If your intrusions land too neatly — on the beat, in key, always at a useful moment — they are not chance, they are parts. Theirs arrive wrong and uninvited. The give-away is convenience: a real chance source is sometimes inconvenient.

Is the interest in the relationship, or only in the bed?

Answer

Theirs keeps your attention on the meeting point of intention and chance. If yours is engaging only as a texture and the radio is mere garnish, you treated chance as decoration instead of a partner — the mechanism is the live negotiation between the two.

  • Logbook — the miss list, three lines: "Matched: …" · "Missed: … (best guess why)" · "Change next: …"
  • Common misses: treated the radio as decoration instead of a partner; pre-chose the chance fragments so nothing was really uncontrolled; only foregrounded, never let the signal threaten the bed.
