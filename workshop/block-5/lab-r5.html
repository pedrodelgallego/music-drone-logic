Rebuild the Dub

Take the send/return choreography passage you mapped in L5 and re-perform it on your own mixer routing — your riddim, their architecture, your hands making their moves. The material is necessarily different, so the target is the choreography: the routing you drew, the throws you transcribed, the way each tail lives and dies. Where your performance lands, your analysis was right; where it doesn’t, you have found what you can’t hear yet.

The point

You cannot re-record Tubby’s riddim — and that is exactly why this lab works. With the material removed from the comparison, everything left to match is the system: the routing you guessed, the throws you transcribed, the hands you watched. A dub mix is a performance, so this replica is one too: wired first, rehearsed, then ridden in a pass. The choreography is the analysis, made audible.

What “close” means here

The material is your own, so the comparison moves up a level — three things must match. The architecture: the same routing as your worksheet claims — what is dry, what is sent, pre or post, what shapes each return. The choreography: throws of the same timing and length, landing at the same places against the pulse, mutes carving the same shapes. The space behavior: tails that live the same way — same decay length, same darkening or regeneration, same way of ending. A stranger hearing both passages should recognize the same hands at the same desk, playing a different song.

How this lab works

Wire the architecture

  • Build a short riddim from your own self-sampled material
  • Patch the worksheet’s routing: sends, returns, what shapes each tail
  • A row too vague to patch is itself a finding

Perform the choreography

  • Rehearse the throws against your worksheet’s timing
  • Record a full pass, hands on sends and mutes — no drawn automation
  • The printed pass is the replica; editing a take is allowed, drawing one is not

Name every miss

  • A/B the choreography, not the material
  • File each miss under the layer it lives in — and routing vs performance
  • Correct the worksheet — that is what leaves the room

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
The choreographyEverything about the passage that isn’t the riddim itself: which sounds get thrown into which space, when, for how long, and what the mutes remove. Two desks playing different songs can perform the same choreography — that sameness is what you are rebuilding.
The throwA send ridden as a gesture: open it for one hit or one phrase, then pull it back and let the tail speak. Throws are timed against the pulse like drum hits — transcribe them the way R1 transcribed notes.
Return level vs feedback amountTwo knobs that both make “more echo”. Return level is how loud the space sits beside the dry signal. Feedback amount is how much of the echo re-enters its own input — too little and tails die early, too much and the system runs away. Confusing the two is the most common worksheet error this lab exposes.
Routing miss vs performance missA routing miss is “the path I drew was wrong” — the filter was inside the loop, not after it; the snare was return-only. A performance miss is “the path was right and my hands were late”. They are corrected in different places: one on the worksheet, one in rehearsal.

1 · Target & analysis

The target is the dub-system passage you mapped in L5’s Samples session. Your choreography worksheet is the blueprint: each element, dry/sent/return-only, what it is sent to, what shapes the tail, and the throws transcribed in time. If a row is too vague to patch or perform from, sharpen it before you start. Finding out a row was unbuildable already counts — that is the lab working.

  • The firewall. Open a new project and name it R5-study. The replica is study evidence, never portfolio output: it is not published, never “finished,” and nothing from this session — no riddim, no printed return, no resampled tail — moves into portfolio work. The corrected worksheet is what leaves this room; the audio does not.
  • Worksheet only. No tutorials, no breakdown videos, no “how Tubby did it” articles. Someone else’s analysis would test someone else’s hearing. If you get stuck, go back to the record with the six questions — not to a search bar.
  • Your material, their system. You may not lift audio from the reference at all. Build the riddim yourself from self-sampled material — a Digitakt kit you recorded, a chord you printed — sketched fast and left rough. The riddim is scenery; the choreography is the subject.
Before you start
  • The Samples-session worksheet from L5 on the desk — routing rows and throw transcription filled
  • The reference passage available as a local audio file — a stream cannot be dragged onto a track
  • The routing drawn on paper before patching: every send, every return, every feedback path, pre/post marked
  • If a feedback path is in the drawing: the lab 020 safety checklist on the desk — limiter location, emergency mute, maximum safe test procedure
  • A new Live set saved as R5-study — the firewall starts at the project name
  • Phones at the lab-001 mark; returns brought up from silence, never from a guess
  • Timebox: two sessions, three at most — rehearsal counts as a session; write the end date down
Before building

Pre-commit the predictions

In the logbook, before you patch anything: which parts do you expect to nail — the routing, the throw timing, or the tail behavior — which do you expect to miss, and why? Writing the prediction down first is the only honest way to find out what you actually believed. The misses you failed to predict are worth double.

2 · Rebuild & verdict

Setup

The A/B rig

Same rig as R1 and R2: the reference on a track named REF, Warp off, gain-matched with a Utility; your rebuild — riddim tracks, returns, and the printed performance pass — grouped as REBUILD; one solo press flips between them, serial, never layered. The comparison loop covers the passage the worksheet describes. The setup recipe below covers A/B between the printed pass and the live-running system.

Expected result

You can solo REF, then solo REBUILD, at the same perceived loudness, over the same passage, hands-free — your hands are needed at the sends.

REF (Utility, gain-matched) → solo → master → phones REBUILD (riddim → sends → returns, performed) → solo → master → phones

Only one is ever audible at a time — the comparison is serial, never layered, because layering hides timing and decay misses, and those are exactly what this lab is judging.

One worksheet row = one routing decision = one rehearsed move = one guess the performance will prove or break.

Worksheet rowYour buildIn the setFirst move

The choreography — the throws and mutes in timeA recorded performance pass, hands on sends and mutesRB-PASSRehearse against the transcription before printing; the printed pass, not the patch, is the replica.
  1. Timebox first. Two sessions; three at the absolute most, and rehearsal counts as one. The replica is finished when the timebox ends, not when it is perfect. The end date is already in the logbook: when it arrives, print a pass and stop, mid-miss if necessary.
  2. Build the riddim fast. One short loop from your own self-sampled material, matching the reference’s instrumentation roles — a low anchor, a chord or stab, a snare-class hit. Two hours maximum: the riddim is scenery, and polishing it is hiding from the choreography.
  3. Wire the architecture. Every routing row becomes a send and return, patched in the worksheet’s order, with whatever shapes the tail placed where the worksheet claims it sits. Calibrate before performing: returns from silence, feedback amounts at the safe mark, one test throw per space.
  4. Rehearse the choreography. Run the passage with the transcription on the desk and make the moves — throw, catch, mute — until your hands know the timing without reading. This is the lab’s center: a dub mix cannot be assembled, only performed.
  5. Print a performed pass. Record the full passage in one pass, hands on sends and mutes, no drawn automation. Keep at most three takes and pick one — picking is part of the verdict.
  6. A/B at matched loudness. Thirty seconds REF solo, thirty seconds REBUILD solo, back and forth — never both at once. Listen for one dimension per pass: the routing’s fingerprint first, then throw timing, then tail behavior, then levels.
  7. Write the miss list. Every audible difference in the choreography, each assigned to the layer where it lives — use the table below — and each named routing miss or performance miss. The layer tells you which worksheet question you answered wrong.
  8. Correct the worksheet. Re-answer the rows the performance proved wrong — strike the old claim, write the corrected one beside it, including every element that turned out to live in a different routing than you guessed. The corrected analysis is the deliverable that outlives the replica.
  9. Close and quarantine. Render the chosen pass into the logbook as study evidence, close the project, and don’t reopen it for portfolio work. Carry the miss list forward: it is C4’s sharpened hypothesis about what send/return decisions actually sound like.

The detailed per-step recipes are included above: riddim sketch procedure, throw-transcription method, rehearsal drills, the performed-pass printing checklist. Use them as the test sequence for the rebuild.

The difference is…LayerExample
A different kind of space entirelyMaterialTheir tail is a boingy spring; my return is a smooth plate — wrong choice of space, not wrong amount of it
Same space, sits differentlySignalTheir echo darkens with every repeat; mine repeats unchanged — the filter was inside the feedback loop, not after the return
Same space, moves differentlyControlTheir throw is one snare hit caught and cut; mine smears across the bar — the send was ridden, not switched, or the mute came late
Same moves, land differently in timeTimeTheir throws anticipate the downbeat and bloom across it; mine land on it — the choreography was transcribed half a beat off
Decision check — routing miss or performance miss?

For each item on the miss list: would redrawing the path fix it, or only more rehearsal? A routing miss corrects the worksheet — and worksheet corrections are the lab’s real product. A performance miss locates the edge of your hands, not your hearing — note it, rehearse it once, and don’t spend the timebox chasing it. If the rig itself can’t make the move — one send where the record clearly has two — that is a gear note for the roadmap, not a failure.

Pass gate — R5 complete when
  • The replica render — one performed pass over the passage, filed in the logbook as study
  • The routing diagram as patched — drawn before, corrected after; the diff marked
  • The Samples-session worksheet it was rebuilt from, with corrections marked — including routing moves
  • A/B notes at matched loudness
  • The miss list — each miss assigned to its layer, routing or performance named
  • One sentence: what can you hear now that you couldn’t before?

No fault ritual, no full bundle — this is a half-cycle. If the replica’s riddim sounds nothing like the record’s, the lab can still pass: the choreography, not the material, is what is being judged.

Replication principle

The riddim was never yours to copy; the desk moves always were. Rebuild the hands, not the song they were playing.
