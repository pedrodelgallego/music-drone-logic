Block 5 — Space & Mix

Effects are routing architectures, not inserts

Delay, reverb, feedback, width, and subtraction — the block where you stop adding effects to tracks and start designing where sound travels. Every space a record puts you in is a path somebody wired; this block teaches you to draw that path, ride it, and prove it survives the trip to a phone speaker.

Engineering focus

An effect is a routing architecture, not a device on a track. Required concepts: insert vs send · pre- vs post-fader · return level vs feedback amount · the wet/dry ambiguity (is the space in the sound, or beside it?) · phase and correlation · mono collapse · room vs headphones · translation across playback systems. Every lab in this block asks the same question first: where does the signal go, and where does it come back in?

Mixing principles, not presets

Compression & EQ — learn the parameters, not one patch

Every compressor and EQ you will ever meet is the same handful of controls wearing different clothes. This block teaches the parameters so that any device — Live's stock, a plugin, an outboard unit — is legible on sight, and no single preset is ever load-bearing. A setting you cannot explain is a setting you cannot move.

  • Knee — how abruptly compression engages at the threshold: a hard knee clamps the instant the signal crosses it (audible, useful for control); a soft knee eases in over a range above and below (transparent, useful for glue).
  • Lookahead — letting the detector see the signal a few milliseconds early so it can catch a transient before it passes, at the cost of latency that must be compensated like any other (see lab 020's round-trip work).
  • Makeup gain — the level added back after compression lowers the peaks, so comparisons stay honest at matched loudness; without it, "the compressor sounds better" usually just means "louder" (lab 022b's whole subject).
  • Q — an EQ band's width: narrow Q surgically notches one resonance, wide Q tilts a whole region; the same boost is a scalpel or a shelf depending only on this one number.
  • Shelf vs bell — a bell lifts or cuts around a centre and returns to flat on both sides; a shelf raises or lowers everything past a corner frequency. Choosing the wrong shape is the most common reason an EQ move "does nothing" or "does too much."
  • Parallel compression — blending a heavily compressed copy under the dry signal (a send/return architecture, exactly like this block's spaces) to add density and sustain without crushing the transients of the original — compression as an addition, not a replacement.

The full hands-on treatment, with the four-system translation test, lives in lab 022b.

  • Bus / group / parallel routing — when to subgroup, when to stay flat. A subgroup (a bus that several tracks feed before the master) earns its place when a set of elements must be processed, levelled, or muted as one — the drum bus glued together, all the wet returns ducked at once, a stem printed for the desk pass. It costs you the per-track grip you had when everything ran flat to the master, so do not bus by reflex: subgroup only where a shared decision exists, and keep the path flat everywhere a shared decision does not. Parallel paths are buses too — a send to a compressed copy or a saturated duplicate is a subgroup that rejoins the mix rather than replacing it.
  • Racks and Macros as routing and abstraction tools. Live's Instrument and Audio Effect Racks are where this routing becomes architecture you can name: chains split a signal into parallel bands or processing lanes inside one device, and Macros hoist the few controls that matter to the surface — the "one knob that moves the system" this block keeps asking you to find. A Rack is a subgroup, a parallel path, and a labelled abstraction in a single object; building one is how a tangle of routing becomes a reusable instrument.
