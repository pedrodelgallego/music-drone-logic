A Dub-System Piece, Translation-Tested

Constraints only, no recipe. You have labs 018–022, R5, and programme L5 behind you; this brief asks for one original piece whose space is a drawn architecture, then makes the mix prove itself on three playback systems. The gate is not the piece — it is whether the system you drew survives contact with a phone speaker.

What a gate is

A block does not end when its labs are done; it ends when you can run the operating loop without a recipe. C4 is deliberately the genre the block has been circling — a dub system — and the only new variables are the absence of instructions and the presence of witnesses: three playback systems that do not care how it sounded on your monitors.

1 — Brief & constraints

Build one original dub-system piece, 6–8 minutes, fully original — steal systems, never material. The space must be a routing architecture you designed: sends, returns, and at least one element whose life happens on a return rather than a track. The stolen principle you named during L5 is now due: it must appear structurally in this piece, and you must point to it in the defense — in the routing, the choreography, or the rules the piece runs on.

  • Support level: C4 runs at S3 — both specs are authored from prompts, nothing arrives pre-filled. Write them before patching, using lab 003’s filled specs only as a model of granularity. A spec written after the piece is a description, not a spec.
  • The drawing is mandatory. The System Spec must include the full send/return/feedback architecture, drawn: every source, every send (pre or post marked), every return, every feedback path with its safe maximum, and where each level is set. Nothing is patched until the drawing exists; the corrected drawing after the build is part of the bundle, and the diff is the lesson.
  • The translation test is part of the brief, not a quality bonus: the finished mix is verified across monitors, mono, and a phone speaker, and the written translation report files with the bundle. A mix that only exists on one system does not pass the gate.

As at every gate, the brief is a constraint menu — pick four, write them at the top of the spec before anything else, and check them for contradictions before patching. The full C4 constraint menu is below; pick from it before patching.

  1. One space only. A single return carries every wet sound in the piece — one delay or one reverb, load-bearing, shared by everything.
  2. The desk pass. The final arrangement is one recorded performance of sends and mutes, R5-style — edited takes allowed, drawn automation forbidden.
  3. Return-only lead. The piece’s most prominent voice is never heard dry — its dry signal exists only to feed the send.
  4. Mono until the last minute. The mix is built entirely in mono; stereo width may be introduced only once, late, and must survive the mono collapse check unchanged in meaning.
  5. One feedback path, performed. The architecture includes one feedback loop whose amount is ridden as a musical voice — with the lab 020 safety checklist in force.
Decision check

Do your four chosen constraints contradict each other anywhere? A return-only lead inside a mono-until-the-end mix is a hard corner — where will depth come from when width is forbidden? Finding the contradiction before patching is part of the spec work; finding it during the build means the spec was incomplete.

2 — Bench delta

Gate capstones renew the bench certification. Block 5 added sends, returns, a hardware aux path, and a feedback-capable loop to your studio; the gate checks whether the map in your head still matches the room — and then puts the room itself on trial with the translation ritual.

  1. Redraw the Studio Signal Map from memory — no peeking at the current version, no opening sessions. Every source, every gain stage, every monitoring path, and now every send, return, and feedback path, as you believe they are.
  2. Compare against the current map. Mark every discrepancy — a return you forgot, a pre/post state you misremembered, a feedback path with no drawn safe maximum. The diff is the lesson.
  3. File the redrawn map as the next version. The map re-versions at every capstone and every gear arrival; the current version is whichever one survives a redraw.
  4. Run the translation ritual below on the finished mix, and file the written report.

The translation ritual

The lab 021 bench, applied with stakes. Play the rendered mix on each system below, at matched moderate level, taking notes during playback — then fix, re-render, and re-test until the piece’s meaning survives all three. Each round is logged; the report is the history, not just the final verdict.

Translation report — one column of notes per system, per round
Monitors (the mixing position)the reference: what is the piece’s spine, space, and widest element here?
Mono (summed, one speaker)what survived, what vanished, what changed level? phase and correlation casualties show up here first
Phone speaker (worst case)does the piece still make sense with no low end and no width? what carries it — and is that what you designed to carry it?
  • For every finding: name the cause in the architecture (a widener on the spine, a return out of phase, a space that only lives in the sides), fix it at the routing — not with a different render setting — and re-run all three systems. The written translation report files the findings, the causes, the fixes, and the rounds it took.
  • Headphone note: the DT 990 Pro is where you built; it is not one of the three witnesses. If the mix only works there, the report must say so — that sentence is worth more than a flattering render.

3 — Report & synthesis

C4 files the full report bundle — the same ten items as a track lab, plus the translation report and the capstone additions below. Capstones run one fault like any track lab: choose a phase, feedback-routing, or translation fault — Block 5’s territory — and run the full ritual: predict the symptom, create the fault, observe, diagnose the location, fix, write the rule learned.

The full bundle — ten items plus one
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, peaking around −3 dBFS
  • Routing diagram — the full send/return/feedback architecture, drawn before patching, corrected after; the diff is the lesson
  • Recording plan — what gets printed, when, at what stage; which returns are printed and which stay live
  • Patch notes — the settings that mattered, including every feedback amount and its safe maximum
  • Session notes
  • Reflection answers, including the listening comparison — which L5 reference contributed what to the hypothesis
  • One removal decision — what was cut from the finished piece and why
  • Fault entry — phase, feedback routing, or translation class: predict, create, diagnose, fix, write the prevention rule
  • Print map — what now exists as audio, MIDI, automation, patch state, or device memory; which spaces are printed and which are unrecoverable
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the system worked like X; after building it, I discovered it worked like Y.”
  • The translation report — three systems, every finding with its cause, fix, and re-test, signed off only when the piece survives all three
  • Minimum pass gate: audible output · routing diagram · fault entry · print map · system diff · translation report. Missing any one of these means C4 is incomplete regardless of how the piece sounds — a musically strong piece cannot bypass the engineering spine.
Pass standard — full rubric
Minimum passThe minimum gate above is complete, the bench certification is renewed, the mix survives monitors, mono, and phone, and the defense questions are answered.
Solid passThe four constraints audibly shape the piece, the stolen principle can be located in the routing or choreography, and the translation report shows findings that were diagnosed at the architecture, not patched at the render.
Advanced transferYou can explain how the same dub system would be rewired on an unfamiliar desk — which paths are essential, which are conveniences — without losing the core principle.
Excellence — beyond the gate

The gate only asks the piece to survive. Excellence asks it to arrive on every system. A piece that clears this tier is one you would publish under your own name, not merely file in the logbook.

  • Nothing was patched at the render. Every translation finding traces to a routing or arrangement decision, fixed at the architecture, and the report shows the round count dropping as the build matured — the mix got easier to translate because the system got cleaner, not because the limiter got busier.
  • The mid/side picture is deliberate. You can name what lives in the side channel and why it is allowed there, and the side could be muted without the piece losing its spine — width is decoration over a mono-proof core, audited, not assumed (lab 021).
  • The full translation test is run against the lab-022b reference standard. The piece passes the four-system pass — monitors, headphones, phone, and mono — with the loudness and master treatment auditioned as principle, not preset (lab 022b — Loudness & the Master), and the report says why each choice serves the music rather than the meter.
  • The stolen principle disappears into the music. A listener hears a coherent piece, not a demonstration of a technique; you can point to the principle in the routing, but it is not wearing a sign.

The seven-layer worksheet

Capstones only — one page, seven answers, written about this piece. Each layer is a question you answer in a few honest lines.

Seven layers — answered for this piece
What is the sound, at the level of oscillator, sample, or noise — and which sounds are really returns?Material
Where does audio travel, get sent, return, feed back, change level, widen, sum?Signal
What is MIDI, automation, modulation, gesture — which sends were ridden, by what?Control
What is clocked, what drifts, what resets — and what do the delay times do to the pulse?Time
Which choices were rules, and which were taste?Decision
What did your hands actually do, and when?Human
What is printed, named, versioned — and which spaces are unrecoverable?Archive

The defense

Answered in the logbook — but out loud first, as if explaining the piece to a colleague who will check your drawings against the session. Gate-scaled: short answers, no essays.

  1. Draw the final audio path from memory — every send, return, and feedback path included.
  2. Draw the control path separately.
  3. Identify the most fragile part of the system.
  4. Explain one failure and its fix.
  5. Explain what was printed, what remains live, and what cannot be recovered.
  6. Name the stolen principle and point to where it appears structurally in this piece.
  7. Name one thing you removed and why.

Block synthesis

One paragraph in the logbook: what does Block 5 claim, in your own words — and where did your build disagree with it? The disagreement is not a failure of the block or of you; it is the most useful sentence you will write this cycle.

  • Passing the gate: bundle complete + certification renewed + translation report signed off + defense answered. Then Block 6 — Decision Systems opens.
  • Year 2 buffer rule: if fatigue shows here, the next cycle is a non-portfolio one — repair, remix, documentation cleanup, or a Studio Signal Map revision. The buffer is part of the plan.
Gate principle

The mix is an architecture, and the architecture is only real if it survives systems that never saw your monitors.
