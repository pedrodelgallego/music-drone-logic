Subtractive Arrangement

Every lab so far asked you to make something. This one asks you to take it away. A deliberately overbuilt session is arranged entirely by deletion — every section change is a removal, the track count only falls, and the piece is finished when nothing left can leave. Six to eight minutes of minimal, written with the mute button.

  • Save a new set as LAB-022-subtraction; lab 021's mono ritual and translation report are standing law — this mix ships with the paperwork.
  • This is an S3 lab: both specs are authored by you, from the guided prompts below, before any sound.
  • Material class is M2 — designed: the loops themselves are simple on purpose; the composition lives in which ones are absent.
The point

Addition is how mixes drown: each new element masks two others, and the cure — another element — is the disease. Removal works in the opposite direction: every deletion makes what remains louder, clearer, and heavier without touching a fader. The best mix move in this block costs nothing and is always available. This lab makes it the only move allowed.

How this lab works

Overbuild on purpose

  • Twelve to sixteen loop-tracks: drums, bass, chords, textures — deliberately too many.
  • Each loop plain, short, and self-sufficient; nothing precious, everything expendable.
  • The full stack playing at once should sound crowded — that crowd is the raw material.

Arrange by deletion

  • Every section boundary is a removal; nothing new may enter after the overbuild is declared closed.
  • The track-count budget falls on a schedule you author — and it never rises.
  • Mutes are written down before they are performed: the score is a list of subtractions.

Stop at the skeleton

  • The piece is finished when removing one more element would kill it — and you test that claim.
  • 6–8 minutes of minimal, fully original, that is emptier at the end than the beginning.
  • The final mix ships with lab 021's translation report; sparseness has nowhere to hide.
Pass standard
Minimum passAudible original piece, routing diagram, deletion score, fault entry, translation report, print map, and system diff exist.
Solid passThe emptiest minute of the piece is also its strongest — and you can name which removal made it so.
Advanced transferYou can listen to any crowded mix — yours or anyone's — and name the two removals that would improve it more than any addition, with the masking reason for each.

New concepts — in plain words

ConceptIn plain words
Subtractive arrangementComposing by deciding what stops sounding. The material is all present from the start; the form is the order and timing of its departures. The question driving every section is not "what does this part need?" but "what is this part carrying that it could put down?"
MaskingThe engineering reason removal works: two sounds sharing a register hide each other, and the ear receives less than the meter claims. Delete one and the other does not just survive — it grows, for free. Most "this mix needs more energy" problems are masking problems wearing a costume.
Track-count budgetA ceiling on how many elements may sound at once — set per section, written before arranging, and only allowed to fall. A budget turns "should this stay?" from a feeling into an accounting question: if the section holds four, the fifth must name which incumbent it evicts.
Muting as writingThe mute button used as a pencil rather than a solo-button's shadow. A mute is a compositional act with a location, a duration, and a reason — performed, logged, and revisable like any written note. The arrangement view of this piece is mostly empty space, and every gap was authored.
Negative spaceWhat absence does to what remains. A gap is not nothing: it is the loudest possible spotlight on whatever sounds next, and the only mix element that costs no headroom, no spectrum, and no translation risk. Minimal works because the silence is doing several instruments' jobs.

Listening — the hypothesis source

Ten works of subtractive minimalism — music made by leaving out, from a piano dissolved into sine tones to a DJ set assembled from deletions. Each gets a brief: what to listen for before you press play, and how to check yourself after.

01 Alva Noto & Ryuichi Sakamoto — “Uoon I” piano reduced to its essence

From Vrioon: Sakamoto plays spare piano figures, and Carsten Nicolai removes — placing the surviving notes in a field of sine tones and near-silence. The collaboration is literally one musician adding and one subtracting, and the subtraction wins.

Listen for
  • How few piano notes there are per minute — and how much weight each carries because of it.
  • The electronics as frame, not content: sine tones and hum that mark the edges of the silence around the piano.
  • What the gaps do to your attention — the next note matters more the longer it withholds itself.
After — verify

Imagine the same piano figures with strings, pad, and rhythm behind them — a film cue, instantly forgettable. The piece's gravity is manufactured almost entirely by what is not played: negative space doing the orchestra's job. That is this lab's core mechanism stated as gently as it will ever be stated. If it read as "background music," relisten and time the longest gap between piano phrases — then notice you were not bored, you were waiting, and waiting is engagement.

02 Monolake — Hongkong few elements, vast room

Chain Reaction dub techno at album length: each track a handful of elements — a chord, a pulse, field recordings, sub — placed in enormous processed space. The arrangement language is presence and absence; almost nothing else ever happens, and nothing else is missed.

Listen for
  • Count the simultaneous elements at any moment — rarely more than four or five, in a mix that feels enormous.
  • Section changes made by departure: a layer leaves, the space it occupied becomes audible, and that is the event.
  • The room as a permanent citizen — reverb and echo so structural that removing an element leaves its space behind like furniture marks.
After — verify

Pick one track and log every arrival and departure for five minutes. The list is short — and the departures outnumber the arrivals in the second half, which is the album quietly teaching this lab's form: build early, then compose the emptying. Notice too how the low count buys translation — with five elements there is nothing to mask, which your own overbuilt stack will demonstrate by counterexample. If it felt static, relisten following only the space between hits; the space is where the writing is.

03 SND — Makesnd Cassette the groove skeleton

Mark Fell and Mat Steel reduce garage and house to clicks, dry chords, and bass — every conventional element removed until only the skeleton grooves. (Their Atavism is homed in another block; this is the earlier, warmer statement of the same subtraction.)

Listen for
  • What is missing, listed honestly: no kick as you know it, no hats, no riser, no fill — and the groove survives all the absences.
  • The chord stabs carrying rhythm, harmony, and hook at once — one element doing three jobs because the jobs' usual owners were deleted.
  • Dryness as a choice: almost no reverb anywhere, so every silence is total.
After — verify

Ask of one track: which standard dance-music element would you add first? Now notice the track already anticipated your addition — the swing of the clicks or the voicing of the stab is covering that role obliquely. Each removal forced a survivor to grow a second function, which is subtraction's compounding interest: fewer elements, each more capable. If it read as unfinished, relisten next to any maximal house track and ask which one you can still hear clearly at minute four.

04 Mika Vainio — Onko almost nothing, fully composed

Vainio's first solo album for Touch: low drones, static snaps, hums, and long silences — the Pan Sonic vocabulary with the beat subtracted too. (Pan Sonic's Vakio is homed in another block; here the lens is how little can carry a piece.) The hardest test in the ten: material at the absolute minimum, attention held anyway.

Listen for
  • How long one sound is allowed to be the only sound — and at what point you stop waiting for company.
  • Events measured in single appearances: a snap that happens once is form, not texture.
  • The loudness illusion: nothing is loud, but with nothing to mask them, tiny sounds arrive like physical contact.
After — verify

Did it hold you? Be honest in the logbook either way — the answer calibrates your own piece's floor. What holds attention here is not density but consequence: with two or three sounds present, any change rearranges the whole world, so the listener cannot doze without missing an event that matters. Your deletion score is aiming at exactly that consequence-per-event ratio. If it lost you, relisten to one track at the marked level doing nothing else — sparseness collapses under divided attention, and that fact belongs in your translation thinking too.

05 Richie Hawtin — DE9: Transitions removal at album scale

Hawtin deconstructs hundreds of tracks into stems and loops, then rebuilds them into one continuous mix — keeping perhaps one element per source. A DJ set as an act of mass deletion: the largest subtractive arrangement in this list. (His DE9: Closer to the Edit is homed in another block; this is the later, deeper cut of the method.)

Listen for
  • How few layers sound at once despite hundreds of sources — the curation is the composition.
  • Transitions made by subtraction: a layer leaves, and its departure is what announces the next section.
  • The consistency of register: every borrowed element trimmed to fit one spectral plan, so nothing ever masks.
After — verify

Try to find a moment where two elements fight for the same register. You will struggle — with the whole history of minimal techno to choose from, Hawtin's main act was saying no to almost all of it, and the mix's cleanliness is that refusal made audible. The lesson for your overbuild: the value of sixteen loops is not playing them, it is having enough to refuse. If the mix blurred into wallpaper, relisten across any ten-minute span and log each element in and out — the turnover is constant; it is just never crowded.

06 Ryoji Ikeda — +/- the threshold of audibility

Sine tones, clicks, and bands of noise at the edge of perception — subtraction taken past arrangement into the material itself, until what remains is barely more than the fact of sound. (His Dataplex is homed in another block; this is the starker, earlier limit case.)

Listen for
  • Tones so reduced they have no envelope, no vibrato, no width — a frequency and a duration, nothing else.
  • Rhythm emerging from bare alternation: on/off as the only percussion instrument left.
  • Your playback system joining the piece — at this sparseness, every speaker's character becomes audible content.
After — verify

What is left to remove? Almost nothing — and the album works anyway, which fixes the far end of the scale your piece sits on: somewhere between Steely Dan's density and this. Notice what the reduction exposed: the system, the room, your own hearing — at the limit, subtraction stops revealing the music and starts revealing the listener. Your piece should stop before that line; knowing where the line is requires having heard someone cross it. If it was unbearable, log how many minutes you lasted — that number is data about your own floor, not a verdict on Ikeda.

07 Studio 1 — “Grün” the mute button as score

Wolfgang Voigt's color series: a handful of loops per record, and the entire arrangement is which of them sound in any given bar. No fills, no risers, no automation drama — mute states as the complete score. (The series' “Rot” is homed in Block 3 as a time study; here the lens is the arrangement method.)

Listen for
  • The full inventory: list every distinct loop you ever hear — the count stays in single digits.
  • Form as combinatorics: each section is a subset of the inventory, and the piece walks through subsets.
  • What a single unmute does after two minutes of its absence — the cheapest climax in electronic music.
After — verify

Chart one track as a grid — loops down the side, bars across, filled cell means sounding. The grid is the piece; you have just transcribed it completely, which you could not do for any other record in this list. That grid is also, almost exactly, the deletion score this lab asks you to write before arranging. If the music felt featureless, relisten watching for the subset changes — the form is real, it is just wearing no costume.

08 Thomas Brinkmann — Klick music from the leftovers

Tracks built from clicks, pops, and run-out grooves — the sounds every other producer edits out. Brinkmann subtracts the music and keeps the residue, then proves the residue grooves. Subtraction applied one level deeper: not fewer elements, but the elements nobody else would keep.

Listen for
  • The sources: locked-groove artifacts and surface noise, looped until pattern becomes pulse.
  • How much funk lives in timing alone — material with no pitch and no sustain, swinging anyway.
  • The bass as the lone conventional citizen, giving the clicks a floor to dance on.
After — verify

Name what was subtracted before the first sound existed: melody, harmony, timbre, sustain — the whole orchestra was refused at the door, and rhythm alone was admitted. The track still works, which radically lowers the floor on what your own loops need to be: if run-out crackle can hold a groove, your plainest loop is over-qualified. If it read as a gimmick, relisten and try to stop your foot — the groove is doing real work with no materials, which is the least gimmicky thing in this list.

09 Arvo Pärt — “Spiegel im Spiegel” subtraction before electricity

Piano triads and a single sustained violin line — Pärt's tintinnabuli style, arrived at by stripping his earlier complexity down to rules simple enough to state in a sentence. The proof that subtractive arrangement predates the mixer: this is mixing by removal performed with a pencil in 1978.

Listen for
  • The rule set's smallness: rising and falling scale fragments against arpeggiated triads — you can hear the entire system in one minute.
  • What never happens: no modulation, no crescendo, no development section — every standard device refused.
  • How the two voices never mask each other — register, role, and timing fully separated, an orchestration lesson in two instruments.
After — verify

Ask what a third instrument could add. Every candidate you audition in your head makes the piece worse — which means the piece is at its skeleton: the state your lab's pass standard calls finished, where one more removal kills it and one more addition bloats it. Pärt found that point by years of subtraction from a maximal style, the same direction this lab walks in one session. If it read as merely pretty, relisten tracking only the violin's relationship to the piano's triads — the rule is audible, and hearing a rule is this workshop's oldest pleasure.

10 Frank Bretschneider — Curve precision as space

Raster-Noton minimalism with dub's production grammar: one Nord Modular as the sound source, every element placed with machine precision, and the space between events treated as carefully as the events. Subtractive minimalism as engineering practice — the closest record in the ten to this lab's actual session.

Listen for
  • The single-source discipline: every sound from one synth, differentiated by patch, not by plugins — coherence bought by constraint.
  • Dub moves at miniature scale: echoes and dropouts measured in milliseconds, the Tubby grammar shrunk to a click's size.
  • How clean each element's spectral slot is — nothing overlaps, so the quietest click survives the busiest bar.
After — verify

Take one bar and assign every sound a register: sub, low-mid, mid, high. No collisions — the spectrum is allocated like the track-count budget this lab makes you write, one tenant per slot. That is why the record sounds composed rather than empty at five elements: emptiness is even, allocation is deliberate, and the ear can tell. If it read as cold, relisten for the dub gestures — the swing in the delays is warmer than its surface admits, and it survives the minimalism because nothing masks it.

Requirement

Your written hypothesis names what your piece will refuse — gravity from gaps, departures as events, survivors growing second jobs, consequence per event, curation as composition, the limit where the listener becomes the content, mute states as the whole score, residue admitted and orchestras refused, the skeleton point, or one spectral tenant per slot — and what was stolen from the comparison. The target idea across all ten: removal is the only mix move that makes everything else better for free. What you steal is a structure, never a sound.

1 · The two specs — author from prompts

This is an S3 lab: no spec is provided. Answer each prompt in writing in the logbook — one or two lines per row — before touching gear, then correct every row against reality after the build.

System Spec — prompts (author, then verify)
InputsWhat fills the overbuild — Digitakt patterns, MicroFreak loops, printed tails from lab 019, prints from labs 018 and 020? Twelve to sixteen loops, each deliberately plain. What makes a loop expendable enough to qualify, and what disqualifies a loop as too precious to delete?
TransformsAlmost none — this lab's transform is the mute. What minimal processing does each loop get before the overbuild closes (a filter, a level, a register trim so nothing collides), and what is frozen from then on?
Feedback / variationThe variation engine is the deletion schedule (D3): write the track-count budget per section — e.g. 14 → 9 → 6 → 4 → 2 — and the rules for what may leave when. Does anything ever return after leaving, and if so, what does a return cost the budget?
ConstraintsNothing new enters after the overbuild closes · the track count never rises (state your return rule if returns exist) · every section change is a removal · 6–8 minutes · the final section holds ≤3 elements. Add one constraint of your own and defend it.
Human roleThe arranger as editor: who decides what leaves — a written schedule followed exactly, or judgment exercised at each boundary against the budget? Name the standard a removal must meet, and what you do when the mix audibly improves past the planned budget.
Engineering Spec — prompts (author, then correct after building)
Signal pathDraw it before patching: sources → Live tracks → register-trim processing → groups (how are the 12–16 organized — by register, by role?) → master. Where do the mutes physically live — clip grid, track activators, a mapped controller — and can one hand reach them all?
Gain planPredict in writing: as tracks leave, what happens to the master level — and to the perceived level? (Masking means they diverge.) State the rule for what you do when removal makes the survivors feel louder: ride nothing, and let the headroom open. Nominal and peak for the full stack and for the final ≤3 elements.
Clock / syncWho is tempo master for the overbuild, and are all loops the same length? Different loop lengths drift into polymeter (Block 3's lesson) — decide whether that is your variation engine or a fault, before it happens.
LatencyIf loops are printed from hardware, the lab-002 number decides whether they sit on the grid. State it, and state how you verify a printed loop's downbeat before the overbuild closes.
Stereo / phaseLab 021 is standing law: which elements are allowed width (tails, air) and which are forbidden it (kick, bass, anything in the final ≤3)? A sparse mix exposes every width decision — predict the mono sum of your final section in writing, then test the print.
Noise budgetSubtraction exposes the floor: under fourteen loops your noise is invisible; under two it is a performer. Which stage's hiss will the final section reveal, and is it kept (a citizen, as in lab 005) or fixed at the source?
Print strategyPrint the full overbuild once (the "before"), print the finished arrangement (the "after"), and keep both — the diff is the lab. When is the deletion score frozen, and does the final print follow the score or the session's best judgment, with the difference logged?
Failure modePredict one and write its symptom: the removal that kills the groove (you deleted a masker that was secretly the glue), the final section that collapses on the phone speaker (this lab's fault), or the budget abandoned at minute four because empty felt frightening.
SafetySparse music invites volume creep — with less sound, the hand reaches for the knob. Phones at the lab-001 mark, off every fifteen minutes, and the mark does not move because the mix got quieter; that quiet is the piece.

2 · Bench preflight

Pre-flight — before the first note
  • Both specs authored in the logbook — every prompt answered in writing
  • The deletion score drafted: track-count budget per section, removal order, return rule (if any) — written before arranging
  • All 12–16 loops named and register-trimmed; the overbuild declared closed in a dated logbook line
  • The full-stack print (the "before") rendered and filed
  • Lab 021's kit armed: mono check mapped on the master, correlation meter visible, the translation report blank copied
  • Gain ledger applied · phones at the mark, off every fifteen minutes

3 · Build

Build the lab in five passes. Each pass produces an artifact, not just a better-sounding session: a written rule, a printed comparison, a fault entry, and a corrected system sentence.

Build recipe · Subtractive Arrangement

Make the space and translation path audible

Do this in order
  1. Separate dry and wet. Create DRY, SPACE, and PRINT tracks in Live. Keep the dry path audible and route the wet path separately.
  2. Set the safe level. Record Digitakt II from MOTU M4 inputs 3/4 and MicroFreak from input 1 around -12 dBFS before adding space.
  3. Build one space path. Use one delay, reverb, feedback, width, or subtractive move. Map one control only so the change can be repeated.
  4. Check collapse. Use Utility for mono and a second playback system. Write what survives, changes, or vanishes.
  5. Run the fault. Create a translation or feedback-path fault on purpose, then repair it with level, routing, or subtraction rather than adding material.

4 · Diagnosis & report

  • The fault ritual (faultType: translation). Predict the symptom in writing, then create the fault: take your finished, emptied mix and run lab 021's full gauntlet — mono sum, laptop, phone speaker — on the final section, where ≤3 elements have nowhere to hide. Observe which survivor fails where (the wide tail that vanishes in mono, the sub that the phone never receives, the click that was riding a masked resonance). Diagnose why subtraction exposed it — the element was always broken; thirteen neighbors were covering for it. Fix at the element, not with an addition, and write the rule learned. A sparse mix is a translation test with the excuses removed.
The full report bundle — ten items, filed in the logbook
  • Rendered piece — 24-bit WAV, master peaking −3 dBFS, 6–8 minutes, plus the full-stack "before" print
  • Routing diagram — drawn before patching, corrected after; the diff is the lesson
  • Deletion score — budget per section, removals as planned and as actually performed, every deviation dated and defended
  • Translation report — lab 021's grid on the finished mix, with the final section's verdict line
  • Session notes
  • Reflection answers, including the listening comparison — which record contributed what to the hypothesis
  • One removal decision — the single deletion that improved the piece most, and the masking reason why
  • Fault entry — symptom, cause, fix, prevention rule (faultType: translation)
  • Print map — both prints and every loop of the closed inventory, including the loops that never survived to the final arrangement
  • System diff sentence — “I thought the system worked like X; after building it, I discovered it worked like Y.”
  • Minimum pass gate: audible output · routing diagram · deletion score · translation report · fault entry · print map · system diff. Without these, the lab is incomplete regardless of audio quality — a musically strong piece cannot bypass the engineering spine.
Track-lab principle

Every element you remove makes every element you keep louder, clearer, and heavier — for free. Arrangement is deciding what stops sounding; the mute button is a pencil, and the gaps are the writing.
