Loudness & the Master

A finished mix is not a finished master. This lab teaches the four ways "how loud is it?" can be answered — peak, RMS, LUFS, and true-peak — shows you which one the streaming and club worlds actually use, and walks one of your own pieces to a named, defended loudness target with a metering reading you can show.

Start here
  • Bring one finished piece of your own — ideally a master candidate from C4, or anything you intend to deliver. This lab masters a real piece, not a test tone.
  • Save a new set as LAB-022B-loudness with that piece imported on the master.
  • Install a LUFS meter: Live 12 Suite ships a metering display with LUFS, or install Youlean Loudness Meter 2 (free) — either works. Have one ready before step 1.
  • Choose one reference track in the same world as your piece, in lossless form if possible.
  • Expected time: two sessions — measure & reference (steps 01–04), then limit, fault, translate, deliver (steps 05–08).
The point

"Make it loud" is not a target — it is a wish. A target is a number with a meter behind it: "−14 LUFS integrated, true-peak under −1 dBTP, delivered as 16-bit dithered." Once you can read those numbers, loudness stops being a vibe you chase by ear and becomes a decision you defend.

1 · New concepts — in plain words

Six terms run through this whole lab. Come back to this table whenever one appears.

ConceptIn plain words
Peak (sample peak) The single highest sample value in the file. Tells you about clipping headroom, almost nothing about loudness — a quiet click and a wall of sound can share the same peak.
RMS The average energy over a window — closer to "how loud does it feel" than peak, but it ignores how the ear actually weights frequencies. A useful old tool; LUFS is its modern, ear-matched replacement.
LUFS Loudness Units relative to Full Scale — RMS, but filtered to match human hearing, and the unit every streaming platform uses. Three time-windows: momentary (≈400 ms, the instantaneous feel), short-term (≈3 s, the section feel), and integrated (the whole piece averaged — the one number that defines a master's loudness).
True-peak (dBTP) The real peak between samples, after the file is reconstructed into a continuous wave on playback or conversion. A file can read 0.0 dB on a sample-peak meter and still overshoot to +0.8 dBTP when decoded — inter-sample clipping no sample-peak meter ever showed you.
Normalization What streaming platforms do to your master: they turn it down (or up) to hit their own target, so a track mastered at −6 LUFS and one at −14 LUFS play back at the same loudness — but the over-loud one arrives squashed and pre-distorted. This is why chasing maximum loudness backfires on streaming.
Dither Tiny shaped noise added when you reduce bit depth (24-bit → 16-bit). It trades inaudible hiss for the removal of audible quantization distortion in quiet tails. Apply it once, on the final 16-bit export — never on a 24-bit render, never twice.
peak RMS LUFS (M/S/I) true-peak normalization dither

2 · Listening — same masters, two destinations

Before metering anything, do a structured listen. The goal is to hear what a loudness target costs and buys, so the numbers later mean something.

01 A loud club master vs. a quiet ambient master loudness vs. dynamics

Pick one track built to be loud (a recent techno or club release) and one built to breathe (an ambient or modern-classical piece). Level-match them by ear as best you can, then compare.

Listen for
  • How much the loud master moves — or doesn't. Loudness is bought with dynamic range; the wall has no quiet to fall back to.
  • How the quiet master uses its headroom: transients that actually hit, tails that actually decay.
After — verify

State the trade in one sentence: louder integrated LUFS buys impact-on-first-listen and costs dynamic contrast. Which does your piece want? That answer is your target.

02 One track, streaming vs. the original file normalization, heard

Play a loud master from a streaming app with normalization on, then from a lossless file at full level. The app turned it down toward its target.

Listen for
  • That the "loud" master is no louder than anything else once the platform normalizes — its loudness war was unwinnable.
  • Any squash or pumping that survived the turn-down: the platform removed the volume but not the damage.
After — verify

Conclude why ~−14 LUFS is a sane streaming target: above it, the platform only turns you down, keeping the dynamics you sacrificed for nothing.

03 Your chosen reference — across four systems translation

Play your reference track on monitors (if present), the DT 990s, your phone speaker, and in mono. A real master survives all four.

Listen for
  • What stays constant: the broad spectral balance, the relative loudness of bass to top.
  • What collapses on the phone or in mono — that is what your own master must protect.
After — verify

Note one thing the reference keeps everywhere. Your translation test in step 07 must clear the same bar.

Write a one-sentence hypothesis naming your target before measuring: something like my piece wants ≈ −14 LUFS integrated for streaming, true-peak under −1 dBTP, because its dynamics matter more than first-listen impact. The bench will test whether you can hit it and defend it.

4 · Procedure

Step 01

Route the LUFS meter as the last device

Goal: a meter that reads exactly what leaves the system — so it sits after every device that can change level.

Do this
  1. Put your piece on the master (or a print track feeding the master). Master fader at 0 dB.
  2. Insert the LUFS meter as the last device in the chain. If using Live 12's built-in metering, switch its display to LUFS; if Youlean, drop it last on the master.
  3. Enable, where the meter offers it: integrated, short-term, momentary, and true-peak readouts.
Expected result

Four numbers available at once: M, S, I, and true-peak. If the meter is placed before the limiter later, it lies — it must always be the final device.

Step 02

Measure the piece as-is

Goal: the honest baseline — what your unmastered mix actually reads, end to end.

Do this
  1. Reset the integrated reading. Play the piece from the very start to the very end without stopping — integrated LUFS is only valid over the whole piece.
  2. Record three numbers: integrated LUFS, maximum short-term LUFS, maximum true-peak (dBTP).
  3. Compare to the four units in your head: note how far the peak is from the LUFS — that gap is your dynamic range, and it is what limiting will spend.

Step 03

Name the target — and say why

Goal: one committed number, chosen from the destination, not from ambition.

Do this
  1. Choose the destination: streaming (≈ −14 LUFS integrated, the loudness platforms normalize toward) or a louder club / file master (−9 to −6 LUFS, where the system plays the file at full level and loudness is not normalized away).
  2. Write the target with its true-peak ceiling: e.g. −14 LUFS I, ≤ −1 dBTP.
  3. Write the why in one line: what the destination does with loudness, and what your piece values (dynamics vs. impact).
Why the two worlds differ

Streaming normalizes: master above its target and the platform turns you down, so you keep none of the loudness and lose the dynamics you traded for it — hence ≈ −14 LUFS. A club PA or a downloaded file plays your level as-is, so a louder integrated LUFS genuinely arrives louder — hence club masters push harder, accepting the dynamic cost on purpose.

Step 04

Reference-match: loudness, then balance

Goal: compare your piece to the reference fairly — at matched loudness — so you judge tonal balance, not volume.

Do this
  1. Import the reference on a track that bypasses the master chain. Measure its integrated LUFS the same way (whole track).
  2. Trim the reference's track gain so it plays at the same integrated loudness as your piece — louder always sounds better, so an unmatched A/B always favours the louder file.
  3. A/B in short loops. Note where your broad balance differs: too much low-mid mud, a dull top, a hollow midrange. Correct with gentle broad master EQ moves (≤ 2–3 dB), not surgical cuts.
Decision checkpoint

At matched loudness, does your piece sit in the same broad tonal world as the reference? If it only sounded worse because it was quieter, you skipped the match — redo it.

Step 05

Limit to target — and stop there

Goal: raise integrated LUFS to the target with a limiter, set the true-peak ceiling, and resist going louder than the target requires.

Do this
  1. Insert a limiter before the meter. Set its ceiling / output to −1 dBTP and enable true-peak limiting if offered.
  2. Lower the threshold (or raise input gain) in small steps, re-measuring integrated LUFS over the whole piece each time, until you reach the target.
  3. Watch gain reduction: a few dB on peaks is healthy; constant heavy reduction means you are over-limiting — back off and reconsider the target.
  4. Log the after-limiting row in the measurement table.
Expected result

Integrated LUFS at target, true-peak at or under −1 dBTP, and the piece still breathing. If reaching the target required crushing the life out of it, the target is wrong for this piece — that is information, not failure.

5 · The fault & diagnosis

Step 06

Fault ritual — break the master two ways

Deliver, on purpose, the two masters you must learn to recognize: one that clips between samples, and one that pumps from too much limiting.

over-push → export → re-measure → hear the damage → fix → write the rule
  • Inter-sample clip: set the limiter ceiling to 0.0 dB sample-peak with true-peak limiting off, push loud, and export. Re-import and measure true-peak: it will read over 0 dBTP even though the sample-peak meter said 0.0. That overshoot is the distortion a phone DAC or MP3 encoder will reconstruct and clip.
  • Pumping: drive the limiter 6–10 dB into heavy gain reduction to chase a club-loud number. Listen on the bass and the tails: the whole mix breathes against the kick, transients flatten, sustains duck. That is over-limiting.
  • Fix both: restore true-peak limiting at −1 dBTP and back the threshold off to your real target. Re-measure to confirm true-peak ≤ −1 and the pumping is gone.
SymptomCauseFix
Crackle/harshness on a phone or after MP3, though the file looked clean at 0.0 dBInter-sample (true-peak) clipping — the between-sample overshoot the sample meter never showedEnable true-peak limiting; set ceiling to −1 dBTP; re-export and re-measure dBTP

6 · Translate and deliver

Step 07

Translation test — four systems, one mono

Goal: confirm the master holds up where it will actually be heard, not just on the bench.

Do this
  1. Play the limited master on: monitors (if present), the DT 990s, your phone speaker, and collapsed to mono (a Utility set to Width 0 % on the master).
  2. On each, ask: does the balance survive? Does anything vanish in mono (a phase-cancelling element)? Does the bass disappear on the phone, or overwhelm it?
  3. Compare each pass against the reference's behaviour on the same system from the listening section.
If it fails

Step 08

Export the archive and the delivery

Goal: two files done right — a clean 24-bit archive and a correctly-dithered delivery at the named target.

Do this
  1. Archive: render 24-bit WAV, normalize off, dither off. This is the master of record.
  2. Delivery: render to the destination format. If 16-bit, set dither on; apply it once. Normalize off — your limiter already set the level.
  3. Re-import the delivered file and measure it one last time. Log integrated LUFS and true-peak in the final row of the table — the reading you will defend.
Logbook

The completed measurement table, all four rows, plus the one-paragraph defended decision. Carry the same metering discipline into C4 and C7, where finished pieces are delivered under these same rules.

7 · Pass gate & report bundle

The report bundle — filed in the logbook
  • The delivered file at the named LUFS target, true-peak ≤ −1 dBTP
  • The 24-bit undithered archive
  • Measurement table — mix, reference, after-limiting, delivered — all four numbers each
  • Reference-match notes — what broad balance changed and why
  • Fault entry — the inter-sample clip and the pumping master, with the re-measured proof of each fix
  • Translation notes — four systems + mono, pass/fail each
  • One-paragraph defended decision (the pass gate proper)
  • System diff sentence — "I thought loud meant X; the meter showed it meant Y."
Pass gate

One piece delivered at a named LUFS target, with the metering reading recorded (integrated LUFS + true-peak) and a one-paragraph written defence of the decision: why this target, for this destination, with this trade against dynamics. "I made it loud" fails. "−14 LUFS integrated, −1.2 dBTP, for streaming — the piece is ambient and platform normalization would erase any extra loudness while costing me the tails" passes.

Pass standard
Minimum passThe bundle above exists; the delivered file hits the named target and stays under the true-peak ceiling.
Solid passYou can read all four loudness units off the meter and say what each is for; the fault entry proves you found and fixed inter-sample clipping by measurement.
Advanced transferYou can master the same piece to two destinations (streaming and club) and defend the different target each got.
  1. How far apart were your mix's peak and its integrated LUFS — and what did that gap tell you before you limited anything?
  2. How much loudness did limiting buy before the piece started to suffer? Where was your limit?
  3. Did the reference change your broad balance, or only confirm it?
  4. Which system in the translation test was hardest to pass, and why?
Bench principle

Loudness is a target, not a wish — a number with a meter behind it and a destination in front of it.
