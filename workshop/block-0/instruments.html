Block 0 — The Rig · optional

The three instruments, in depth

Three boxes do the music-making in this rig: the Elektron Digitakt II, the Arturia MicroFreak, and the Novation Launchpad Pro MK3. This page is a slow, opinionated tour of each one. Not a manual — those exist. This is what each instrument is for, the few controls that matter most, and the habits that make it sing. Read it once, then keep it open while you play.

Optional · Setup & systems · Digitakt II · MicroFreak · Launchpad Pro MK3

The opinion up front

You have three very different brains here: one for rhythm, one for a single voice, and one for your hands. Learn what each one is for before you learn what each button does. The Digitakt keeps time and shapes beats. The MicroFreak makes one expressive sound. The Launchpad turns your fingers into a controller. Mix those jobs up and the rig fights you. Keep them clear and the rig disappears.

The three, side by side

Before any detail, hold the whole picture in your head. Each instrument has one job. When two of them could do the same thing, the last section on this page tells you which to pick.

Three instruments, three jobs
Its one jobDigitakt II — rhythm & sampling. MicroFreak — one melodic voice. Launchpad — playing & launching by hand.
Best atDigitakt — beats, chopped samples, tight grooves. MicroFreak — leads, basses, evolving drones. Launchpad — clip launching, scales, screen-free sequencing.
How it sequencesDigitakt — deep step sequencer with per-step locks. MicroFreak — small built-in sequencer + arpeggiator. Launchpad — a standalone 4-track step sequencer.
Role in the rigDigitakt — the timekeeper and beat heart. MicroFreak — the single voice you sample and ride. Launchpad — your hands on top of everything.

Digitakt II — the rhythm brain

The Digitakt II is a drum machine, a sampler, and a sequencer in one box. A sampler just means it plays back recordings of sound. It gives you 16 audio tracks (each one plays a sample) plus 16 MIDI tracks (each one can play other gear). Every audio track has its own sample slot, an amp envelope (it shapes how the sound fades in and out), a multimode filter (it removes high or low frequencies), an LFO (a slow automatic knob-mover that adds movement), overdrive, and sends to the on-board delay, reverb, and chorus. It samples in stereo and stores everything on its +Drive.

Sample slot → Amp envelope → Filter → Overdrive → FX sends → Delay · Reverb · Chorus

That chain runs on every one of the 16 audio tracks. On the back are two audio inputs (A and B) for sampling outside gear like the MicroFreak, a main output (L/R), and a headphone output.

  • [TRK] + [TRIG 1-16] — hold [TRK] and tap a step pad to pick which track you are editing.
  • [SRC] — the sample / source page. Choose which sample the track plays and tune it.
  • [FLTR] — the filter page. Cutoff, resonance, and the filter envelope live here.
  • [AMP] — the amp page. Volume, pan, and the fade-in / fade-out shape.
  • [FX] — the send page. How much of this track goes to delay, reverb, and chorus.
  • [RECORD] — grid recording. Turns the 16 step pads into a place to draw a pattern.
  • [FUNC] + [YES] — saves your work. Do this often.
  • [SETTINGS] — opens system menus. You need MIDI Config → Sync to set who keeps time.
  • Hold a trig + turn a knob — this is the magic move. It saves that knob's value to that one single step. That saved value is a parameter lock (p-lock).
First setup
  • Load a project — turn on, pick a project from the +Drive (or start a fresh one).
  • Set Sync — open [SETTINGS], go to MIDI Config → Sync, and decide whether the Digitakt leads (sends clock) or follows (receives clock). For the labs it follows Live.
  • Set a sample — press [SRC] and load a kick onto track 1, a closed hat onto track 2.
  • Mark your inputs — note that inputs A and B on the back are where the MicroFreak goes when you want to sample it.
  • Save it — [FUNC] + [YES]. You now have a project to build on.
Hands on · 10 minutes

Your first pattern, with one p-lock

A four-on-the-floor kick with off-beat hats, then one step that breaks the rule. This is the whole Digitakt workflow in miniature.

Do this
  1. Digitakt II Press [RECORD] so the step pads light up as a 16-step grid.
  2. Digitakt II Hold [TRK] and tap pad 1 to select the kick track. Tap steps 1, 5, 9, 13 to place a kick on every beat.
  3. Digitakt II Hold [TRK] and tap pad 2 for the hat. Tap the off-beats — steps 3, 7, 11, 15.
  4. Digitakt II Press play. You have a groove. Now the trick.
  5. Digitakt II Hold hat step 15 down and, while holding, turn the [FLTR] cutoff knob. You just made a p-lock — that one step now opens the filter, and only that step. Let go and listen.
What just happened

You changed a single step without changing the rest of the pattern. That is the Digitakt's superpower. A whole evolving beat can be built out of dozens of these per-step changes, with no automation lanes and no mouse.

  • Let the sequencer do the sound design. p-locks and conditional trigs (rules like "play this step only 50% of the time", or "only on fills", or "first loop vs. not-first") are where a flat loop becomes alive. Reach for them before you reach for new samples.
  • Use per-track lengths for polymeter. Each track can have its own length. Make the kick 16 steps and the hat 15. They drift against each other and never line up the same way twice. That is polymeter — different loop lengths running together — and it is free movement.
  • Sample the MicroFreak through inputs A/B. Run the synth into the back, grab a few seconds, and now that sound is a Digitakt voice you can chop, p-lock, and play across the pads. This is the fastest sound-design loop in the rig.

MicroFreak — the single voice

The MicroFreak makes one melodic sound at a time. It can stack up to four notes (that's called paraphonic), but they all share a single filter, so think of it as one expressive voice rather than a full chord machine. Its design is unusual and that is the point: a digital oscillator (the part that makes the raw tone) with many swappable engines, feeding into a single analog filter (the part that sculpts the tone). Digital flexibility into analog character.

Digital oscillator · pick an engine → Analog filter · 12 dB/oct SEM-style → Amp / envelope → Mono out

The engines include Basic waves, Superwave, Wavetable, Harmonic, Karplus-Strong (plucked / physical), Virtual Analog, Waveshaper, two-operator FM, Formant / Speech, Modal, and Noise-Engineering bass/lead. The one analog filter switches between low-pass, band-pass, and high-pass. The keyboard is a flat touch surface (capacitive) with poly-aftertouch and a pressure strip, so how hard you press becomes a control.

  • Oscillator + Type / Wave — pick the engine with Type, then shape its raw tone with Wave and the timbre knobs. This is the source of the sound.
  • Filter Cutoff / Resonance — the single analog filter. Cutoff opens and closes the tone; Resonance adds a vocal peak right at the cutoff point. Switch low-pass / band-pass / high-pass here too.
  • Envelope — shapes how the sound rises and falls over one note (attack, decay, sustain, release). Long attack and release make pads and drones.
  • Cycling Envelope — a second, loopable envelope you can run as an extra movement source on its own.
  • LFO — the slow automatic knob-mover. Its real power shows up in the matrix below.
  • The 5×7 mod matrix — a grid that wires your movement sources (envelope, Cycling Envelope, LFO, pressure, keyboard) to destinations (cutoff, pitch, wave, and more). This is where the synth comes alive.
First patch
  • Pick an engine — turn Type to Basic waves or Virtual Analog. Hold a note and explore Wave until you like the raw tone.
  • Set the filter — choose low-pass, bring Cutoff down until the tone goes dark, then back up to taste. Add a touch of Resonance for character.
  • Set a long-ish envelope — raise attack and release so notes swell in and fade out instead of clicking on and off. You now have a pad you can hold.
Hands on · 8 minutes

Make it move

A held note is static. Two small moves give it life: an LFO sweeping the filter, and a second voice slightly out of tune.

Route the LFO to the filter
  1. MicroFreak Open the 5×7 mod matrix. Find the row for the LFO and the column for filter Cutoff.
  2. MicroFreak Turn the matrix encoder to set an amount at that crossing point. Now the LFO opens and closes the filter on its own.
  3. MicroFreak Slow the LFO right down and hold a note. The tone breathes. That slow filter sweep is the heart of a drone.
Two detuned voices for beating
  1. MicroFreak Set the voice mode so two voices sound at once and detune them slightly.
  2. MicroFreak Hold one note. The two close-but-not-equal pitches fight, and you hear a slow throb. That throb is called beating, and it is the warmest, cheapest movement a synth can make.
  • Keep it mono on purpose. One voice, one cable, one channel. A single sound you fully control beats a chord you half-control. Width comes later, from effects, on purpose.
  • One engine mastered beats ten skimmed. There are many engines and the temptation is to scroll. Don't. Pick one, learn exactly what every knob does to it, and you will get more music out of it than from a tour of all of them.
  • The filter is the most expressive control. If you only ever automate or play one thing by hand on this synth, make it the Cutoff. It is the MicroFreak's voice box.

Launchpad Pro MK3 — your hands

The Launchpad Pro MK3 is an 8×8 grid of 64 RGB pads. The pads are velocity-sensitive (how hard you hit matters) and have poly-aftertouch (pressing harder after the hit matters too). It does several different jobs depending on which mode it is in. Learn the modes and it becomes the most direct, screen-free way to touch your music.

The modes, plainly
Session — launch Live clips and scenes by tapping padsWhen you are playing your Live set by hand
Note — a playable instrument with scale and chord helpersWhen you want to play melodies and stay in key
Chord — build and trigger chords from single padsWhen you want full chords without a real keyboard
Custom — four user-defined MIDI layouts you designWhen you want your own controls under your fingers
Sequencer — a built-in 4-track step sequencer that runs standaloneWhen you want to drive hardware with no computer
Using it in Live
  • Connect over USB-C — Live recognises the Launchpad and lights it up. It runs on USB power, so no extra adapter.
  • Session mode — the grid mirrors your Live clips. Tap a pad to launch a clip, tap the side to fire a whole scene. The dynamic track controls let you mute, solo, and arm tracks from the grid.
  • Note mode — switch to it and play. Turn on a scale so every pad is in key, and use the chord helpers to play fuller parts with one finger.
  • Capture MIDI — if you play something good without recording, Capture MIDI grabs what you just played and turns it into a clip.
The Config-D superpower · 12 minutes

The standalone sequencer — no computer at all

Most people never find this. The Launchpad has a 4-track step sequencer built in, and it runs with no computer. It sends MIDI out of its own port straight into the Digitakt and MicroFreak. This is the heart of the screen-free rig (Config D in the block).

Do this
  1. Launchpad Run a MIDI cable from the Launchpad's MIDI Out (3.5mm TRS, Type A, via the included adapter) to the gear you want to sequence. Power the Launchpad from any USB charger — no computer needed.
  2. Launchpad Enter Sequencer mode. Program a step pattern on one of the four tracks and set it to send to the channel your Digitakt or MicroFreak listens on.
  3. Launchpad + Digitakt II Press play. The Launchpad now drives the hardware directly. No screen, no DAW, no latency.
  4. Live Later, if you like what you built, Print to Clip captures that standalone sequence straight into a Live clip so you can keep it.
  • The standalone sequencer is underused. People buy this for Live clip launching and never touch the most interesting thing it does. A Launchpad plus a USB charger plus the Digitakt is a whole studio with no computer in it. Try it once and it changes how you work.
  • Build Custom Modes for your most-used controls. You get four. Put the knobs and pads you reach for constantly onto a Custom layout so your hands stop hunting. This is the move that makes the Launchpad feel like yours.

Who should sequence what?

Three instruments and Live can all sequence. That freedom causes paralysis, so here is a plain answer. For each kind of part, this is the tool to reach for first and why.

The partBest sequenced byWhy
DrumsDigitakt IIIts step sequencer is built for rhythm — p-locks, conditional trigs, and per-track lengths give grooves that breathe.
Bass / lead melodyMicroFreak sequencerKeep the melody on the synth that plays it. Its sequencer records knob moves too, so the part and its sound design travel together.
Long evolving texturesLiveSlow, minutes-long change is easiest to draw and recall with Live's automation lanes and long clips. The hardware sequencers are too short for it.
Live performanceLaunchpadYour hands launching clips, playing scales, or running the standalone sequencer — built for playing in the moment, with or without a computer.
Now put it to work

You know what each instrument is for, the few controls that matter, and who should sequence what. That is enough. A fourth box — the Digitone II — is on its way; see how it joins the rig next.
