Workshop/Block 0 — The Rig
Block 0 — The Rig · optional

Set up your studio once, then stop thinking about it

A deep, opinionated guide to your exact gear — how the boxes connect, who keeps time, where the sound is mixed, and how the whole rig should grow from your first hour to your first year. None of this is required to start Block 1. It is here so that when a lab says "wire the Digitakt II to the M4," you already know three ways to do it and why you picked one.

The opinion up front

You already own a complete, professional studio. Buy nothing else for a year. Every block in this course can be built on these six boxes. The work is not acquiring gear — it is learning the rig you have so well that it disappears.

  • Do you need this block? No. Block 1 walks you through the minimum wiring step by step. Come here when you want the why behind those steps, the alternatives, and a plan for how your setup should evolve.
  • Deep pages: Signal & clock — every cable and who keeps time · Live, Max & sample packs — the software rig · The three instruments — Digitakt II, MicroFreak, Launchpad in depth · Adding the Digitone II — routing & the two-Elektron workflow · Digitone FM — FM in depth.
  • New to synthesis? Do Block 00 — Foundations first: oscillators, filters, envelopes, and LFOs taught one control at a time.

The six boxes, and the one job each does

A rig only makes sense when every box has a single clear role. Here is the role each one plays in this course. When two boxes can do the same job, the table further down tells you which to pick.

BoxIts one jobWhat it is, in plain words
Arturia MicroFreakThe single voiceA small synth that makes one melodic sound at a time (it can stack up to four notes — paraphonic — but shares one filter). Many digital oscillator engines into one analog filter. Your "one oscillator, one filter" instrument.
Elektron Digitakt IIRhythm, sampling & the sequencer brainA drum machine and sampler with a deep step sequencer: 16 audio tracks, 16 MIDI tracks, stereo sampling, two inputs, on-board delay/reverb/chorus. The heart of every beat and the place per-step changes (p-locks) live.
MOTU M4The hubThe audio interface — sound in and out of the computer (4 in / 4 out), two preamps, a headphone output with its own knob, and a pair of real 5-pin MIDI DIN sockets. Everything meets here.
Launchpad Pro MK3Your handsAn 8×8 grid of pressure-sensitive pads. Plays and launches things in Live, and also works as a standalone four-track sequencer that can drive the hardware with no computer at all.
Ableton Live 12 SuiteThe DAW & the labThe software where you record, arrange, process, mix, and run every lab. "Suite" includes all the built-in instruments and Max for Live, the toolkit for building your own devices.
Ableton sample packsRaw materialThe Core Library plus downloadable Packs — drums, textures, multisampled instruments. A stocked larder. Use them as ingredients, never as finished meals.

The whole rig, drawn

This is the audio path of the full rig — the sound you can hear. Solid lines carry audio. Every box you own has exactly one place its sound goes. Control and clock (the messages that tell machines what to do and when) run on a separate layer, shown below.

The Launchpad is drawn dashed because it never carries audio — only your gestures. Here is the control and clock layer that the audio diagram leaves out:

Control & clock — two ways to keep time

Computer · Live → USB MIDI → MicroFreak + Digitakt II + Launchpad
Live → M4 MIDI DIN out → Digitakt II DIN in → thru → MicroFreak
  • The M4's secret weapon: it has real 5-pin MIDI sockets on the back. That means you can send a rock-solid clock to the Digitakt II over a DIN cable instead of over USB — often tighter, and it works even with the computer doing nothing. Most M2/M4 owners never plug these in. You will. Full detail on Signal & clock.

Four rigs in one — pick by intent

The same six boxes can be wired into very different studios depending on who is the brain. You will not pick one forever — you will switch between them by mood and task. Here they are drawn, then side by side.

A · DAW-centric

Live keeps time; every box lands on its own channel. One mix, total recall.

B · Hardware jam

The Digitakt clocks and sums everything; the M4 just feeds your ears. No screen.

C · Hybrid

Live clocks and records separate stems while the Digitakt drives the drums. The course home base.

D · Standalone

Unplug the computer entirely. The Digitakt and Launchpad lead; the M4 sums straight to your ears.

Filled box = the brain (keeps time + sequences). ◆ = where the audio sums to one mix. Dashed line = clock or control, not audio.

Recommendation

Start in Config A for your first week — it is the simplest to reason about and the labs assume it. Grow into Config C (Hybrid) as your home base by month three. Keep B for flow days and D for when you want to disappear from the screen. The rig is the same; only the brain moves.

Three experimental rigs — when you want to break the frame

The four rigs above cover most of the course. These three bend the same six boxes toward sound that partly makes itself — held drones, self-feeding loops, machines that improvise while you steer. Reach for them once Config A feels automatic: in the later blocks, or any day you want to be surprised. Same legend as above — filled box is the brain, ◆ is where audio sums.

E · Drone

Nobody plays notes — slow LFOs lead. The MicroFreak holds a chord, the Digitakt adds slow texture, a long reverb in Live binds it. Modulation is the composer.

F · Feedback / resample

The Digitakt records its own output back in, layer on layer. A no-input-style loop that grows texture from itself. Each pass mutates the last.

G · Generative

Max for Live and probability decide what fires; you set the rules and the odds, not the notes. The dashed lines are triggers out; audio returns to be mixed. The machine improvises, you steer.

These are not on the Day-1-to-Year-1 path — they are detours worth taking. E suits Block 3 (time) and any ambient interlude; F belongs with Block 2 sampling and Block 4 transformation; G is the natural home of Block 6 decision systems.

Where does the audio get mixed?

One real decision worth understanding early: does the MicroFreak go straight into the M4 as its own channel, or into the Digitakt II first? Both are valid. Here is the trade.

Two ways to route the MicroFreak
 Into the M4 directlyThrough the Digitakt II first
Channels in LiveIts own — full separate controlShares the Digitakt's stereo pair
Effects on the synthOnly Live'sThe Digitakt's delay/reverb too, played live
Resampling the synthIn LiveStraight into the Digitakt sampler — fast, hands-on
MixingYou set the balance in LiveYou set it on the Digitakt, before it ever reaches Live
Best whenYou want clean, separate, editable tracksYou want to jam, resample, and treat the synth like another Digitakt voice

The course default is direct into the M4 (Config A/C), because separate channels make the early bench and gain labs clear. The Digitakt route is a wonderful Block-2-onward toy. Diagrams for both are on Signal & clock.

How the rig grows — Day 1 to Year 1

Do not connect everything on day one. A studio is learned one box at a time. This is the progression that keeps you making sound the whole way instead of drowning in cables.

Day 1
One sound, start to finish. Plug in only the MicroFreak → M4 → headphones. Open Live, set the M4 as the audio device, make one note, record eight bars. Save it as a template. Leave the Digitakt and Launchpad in the box. Goal: hear yourself, end to end, with nothing to debug.
Days 2–7
The full audio path + one clock. Add the Digitakt II into M4 inputs 3/4. Make Live the clock master over USB. Mark your headphone level. This is exactly Block 1, Labs 001–002: draw the signal map, fill the gain ledger, prove the timing. You now have Config A, minimal and clean.
Weeks 2–4
Hands and a template. Add the Launchpad in Live (Session and Note modes). Build a starter Live set: two return tracks (a reverb, a delay), a Drum Rack, an Operator. Learn the Digitakt sequencer and its sampler. Start resampling. You can now build a whole loop without the mouse.
Month 3
Hybrid home base. Move to Config C: Live clocks and records separate stems while the Digitakt drives the drums. Add a few Max for Live devices (an extra LFO, an envelope follower). Curate your sample packs into one personal folder. Commit to a single clock-master setup and stop fiddling with it.
Month 6
Flow and reuse. Try Config B (Digitakt-led jams) on days you want no screen. Build Launchpad Custom Modes for your most-used controls. Save Instrument Racks with Macros so a built sound becomes a tool you keep (Block 2, lab 007b). Your template now loads a working studio in one click.
Year 1
The rig disappears. You switch between all four configs by intent without thinking about cables. You can unplug into Config D and play a set with no computer. You have your own templates, racks, a tagged sample library, and a documented signal map. You think in music; the gear is just where your hands are.

Opinions, stated plainly

One clock master, chosen and forgotten

  • Pick who keeps time and commit. Two devices both trying to lead is the number-one cause of sloppy timing.
  • For the computer-based configs, make it Live. For screen-free jams, the Digitakt.

Use the M4's MIDI DIN ports

  • They mean you never need a separate MIDI interface, and a DIN clock to the Digitakt is steadier than USB.
  • Most owners ignore them. Plugging them in is a free upgrade.

Keep the MicroFreak mono on purpose

  • One cable, one channel. Width, when you want it, is made deliberately with effects — never by accident with a second cable.
  • A single voice is a feature here, not a limit. It is the whole point of Lab 004.

Build sounds before you borrow them

  • Reach for Operator and Wavetable before the sample packs. Make the kick, then sample one if you must.
  • Packs are ingredients, not finished dishes. Treat them as raw material to deconstruct.

Commit early, resample often

  • A sound printed to audio is a sound you can stop second-guessing. Limits free you.
  • Resampling — into Live or the Digitakt — is the engine of half this course.

Overbridge later, not now

  • Elektron's Overbridge gives you the Digitakt's tracks as separate channels over USB, with full recall — powerful, but more CPU and more to break.
  • For Year 1, analog audio into the M4 plus a DIN clock is simpler and more reliable. Add Overbridge when you actually need per-track stems.
