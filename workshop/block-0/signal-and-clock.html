Signal & clock — every cable, and who keeps time

This page is the deep version of "wire it up." It draws every cable in your rig, shows you three different ways to make all the machines play in time together, and explains the small delays that creep in when a computer is involved. You do not need any of this to start Block 1. Read it when you want to understand the why, and pick your setup on purpose instead of by accident.

Optional · Signal & clock · MicroFreak · Digitakt II · MOTU M4 · Ableton Live 12 · DT 990 Pro

The one big idea

Two layers run through your studio at the same time. One is audio — the sound you actually hear, travelling down cables to your ears. The other is control and clock — silent messages that tell each machine what to play and when. They use different cables and follow different rules. Keep them separate in your head and almost everything else gets simple.

  • Audio = the noise. It flows one way, from each instrument into the M4, then to your headphones and computer.
  • Control & clock = the instructions. "Clock" is a steady tempo pulse one device sends so the others march in step. "Transport" is the play / stop / where-are-we message. Neither makes a sound on its own.
  • The golden rule, said once: only one device gets to be the clock leader. Two machines both trying to set the tempo is the number-one cause of sloppy timing.

Every cable, drawn

This is the audio layer only — the sound you can hear. Every instrument has exactly one place its sound goes: into the MOTU M4. The M4 then sends everything to your computer and to your headphones. Solid lines carry audio.

  • TS vs TRS — why two different cables. A TS cable has one signal wire (tip) and a ground (sleeve). It is "unbalanced" — fine for short runs, but it can pick up hum. A TRS cable has an extra wire (tip, ring, sleeve), which lets the gear cancel out hum it picks up along the way.
  • So: the MicroFreak's output is unbalanced, so use a TS (mono) cable into input 1. The Digitakt II's main outs are impedance-balanced, which means a TRS cable will reject hum — use TRS for the L and R into inputs 3 and 4. Using TRS on the Digitakt is a small, free upgrade in cleanliness.
  • Keep audio cables away from power. Do not run an audio cable alongside a power brick, a wall-wart, or a USB hub. Cross them at a right angle if they must meet. A power supply lying on top of an audio cable is the most common cause of a faint buzz.

Who keeps time — three clock setups

This is the heart of the page. Clock and transport do not travel on the audio cables above — they travel on MIDI. You have a choice about who leads. Here are three real setups, drawn as chains. The leader is on the left of each; everyone to the right follows.

A · Live leads, over USB MIDI

The computer is the brain. Each machine is connected to Live by its own USB cable, and Live sends clock and transport to all of them at once. Simplest to set up.

Computer · Live → USB MIDI → Digitakt II + MicroFreak

B · Live leads, over hardware MIDI DIN

Live still leads, but the clock leaves the computer through the M4's real 5-pin MIDI socket and travels down DIN cables, machine to machine. Tighter timing, and it leans on the computer far less. This is the one to grow into.

Live → M4 MIDI DIN out → Digitakt II DIN in → DIN thru → MicroFreak

C · Digitakt II leads — screen-free jam

No computer needed. The Digitakt is the brain. It sends clock and transport out of its own MIDI socket to the MicroFreak. Press play on the Digitakt and both start together. Best for flow and ideas.

Digitakt II → MIDI DIN out → MicroFreak · computer off

Recommendation

Start with A — Live over USB is the fastest to get running and the labs assume it. For any work where timing has to feel rock solid, move to B: send the clock out of the M4's real MIDI DIN socket. Most M4 owners never plug those sockets in; you will, and it is a free upgrade in steadiness. Keep C for the days you want to jam with the screen off. Whichever you pick — pick one, set it once, and stop fiddling.

  • How the DIN chain in B is wired: the M4 has 5-pin MIDI sockets, but the Digitakt II and MicroFreak use the smaller 3.5 mm TRS MIDI jacks. Both ship with the little adapters you need. So the M4's MIDI OUT goes (via the included TRS-to-5-pin adapter) to the Digitakt's MIDI in, then the Digitakt's MIDI THRU passes the same clock on to the MicroFreak. One leader, passed down the line.
  • What about Ableton Link? Link keeps Live in time with other software and apps over a network — phones, tablets, a second laptop. It is great for that. It is not a way to clock your hardware. The Digitakt and MicroFreak need a real MIDI clock (USB or DIN), so do not reach for Link to solve a hardware-timing problem.

Clock vs transport

These two get muddled all the time, so here it is plainly. They are two separate messages.

Clock — the tempo pulse

  • A steady stream of ticks that says "this is how fast we are going." It sets the BPM.
  • A follower listening to clock alone knows the speed, but does not know where in the bar it is.

Transport — play, stop, position

  • The "press play now," "stop," and "jump to bar 1" message. It starts everyone at the same moment.
  • Without transport, a follower might know the tempo but start in the wrong place, so the groove lands off.

A picture helps: imagine a marching band. Clock is the drummer's steady beat — everyone knows the pace. Transport is the conductor shouting "go!" — everyone steps off together, on the same foot. You usually want both, from the same leader.

  • Where you switch this on the Digitakt II: go to SETTINGS → MIDI Config → Sync. Turn on Clock Receive so the Digitakt follows the tempo, and Transport Receive so it also starts and stops with the leader. Turn both on when the Digitakt is a follower (setups A and B). Turn them off when the Digitakt is the leader (setup C).

Latency, and why your kick can drift

"Latency" is the small delay a computer adds while it processes sound. You play a note, the computer crunches it, and you hear it a few milliseconds late. That is normal. The trouble starts when that delay is not steady — when it slowly slides — because then your kick and your hi-hats stop lining up. That slide is called drift. Latency and drift are different problems with different fixes.

One setting controls most of the latency: buffer size. The buffer is a small waiting room where audio is gathered before the computer works on it. A small buffer means less delay but more strain on the computer (more chance of clicks and dropouts). A large buffer is smoother and safer, but adds more delay. You pick a number, like 256 samples, that feels responsive without crackling.

Buffer size · the trade-off
Small buffer (e.g. 64–128)less delay · more CPU strain · risk of clicks
Medium buffer (e.g. 256)a good middle for most work
Large buffer (e.g. 512–1024)more delay · smoother · safest while mixing
  • Fixing a constant offset. If the delay is steady — always the same few milliseconds — Live can cancel it out. You measure the real number with a loopback test (send a click out and record it coming back in), then enter it as the driver error compensation. Live then nudges everything by exactly that amount and the offset disappears. You do this once in Block 1, Lab 002.
  • What that cannot fix. Driver error compensation only corrects a steady offset. It cannot fix drift — a delay that grows or shrinks over time. Drift comes from clocks slowly disagreeing, which is exactly why the one clock leader rule above matters so much.
  • Hear the hardware directly. The M4 can pass your instrument inputs straight to your headphones with effectively no delay (hardware monitoring). Use it so you are not listening to a laggy, computer-delayed version of yourself while you play.
  • One listening path only. Never hear the same input twice at once — once direct from the M4 and again coming back from Live. Two copies a few milliseconds apart create a hollow, swooshing sound called comb filtering. Pick one path: monitor through the M4, or through Live, not both.

Noise & grounding

  • Balanced vs unbalanced, in one line: a balanced connection (TRS, XLR) carries the signal in a way that lets the gear cancel out hum it picks up; an unbalanced one (TS) does not, so it is more prone to buzz on long runs.
  • Why TRS on the Digitakt: its main outs are impedance-balanced, so a TRS cable into the M4 gives you that hum rejection for free. Use TRS there even though the cable still carries a mono signal per side.
  • Ground loops and hum: a low, steady buzz usually means two devices disagree about "ground." The most common real culprit, though, is simpler — an audio cable lying across a power supply or a USB hub. Move the cable first before you blame anything fancy.
  • Phantom power: the M4's 48V button is only for condenser microphones. Leave it off for the MicroFreak, the Digitakt, and any line input. Sending 48V into gear that does not want it is needless risk.
Set it once

Wire the audio the way the diagram shows, pick one clock leader and commit to it, set your buffer to 256 and sample rate to 48000, run the loopback once in Lab 002, and choose a single listening path. Do all of that one afternoon and then forget it. The whole point of this page is that you should never have to think about cables and clock again — you just make music.
