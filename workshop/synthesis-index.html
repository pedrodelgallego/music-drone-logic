Synthesis Index by Method

Synthesis is the process of building a sound from scratch, using math and electronics instead of recording something in the real world. There are many different methods — each one has a different character, a different set of strengths, and a different tool in your rig that does it best. This index maps every method covered in the workshop to the lab where you learn it and the tool that produces it.

Start here if synthesis is new to you

Before using this index, complete Synth from Scratch in the Foundations block. That page explains what an oscillator, a filter, and an envelope are in plain English. This index assumes you know those three terms. If you do not, read that page first — everything in the table below will make more sense.

Methods at a glance

Each row is a synthesis method. Find the sound character you want in the second column, then follow the lab link to learn the technique on the tool listed.

Method Good for Where to learn it Tool in your rig
Subtractive Warm bass tones, classic leads, anything that needs to feel full and round. You start with a rich sound and use a filter to remove the parts you do not want — like carving a sculpture from a block. Lab 004 MicroFreak (VCO + filter engine), Live Analog
FM (Frequency Modulation) Metallic bells, electric piano, sharp digital tones, and complex harmonic textures that change dramatically as a single number is turned. One oscillator modulates the pitch of another at audio speed. Lab 013b · Digitone FM guide Live Operator, Digitone II
Wavetable Evolving pads and leads that morph between different timbres as a knob or LFO moves through a table of stored waveforms. Smooth and modern-sounding. Block 2 / Sources Live Wavetable, MicroFreak wavetable engines, Digitone WAVETONE mode
Physical modelling Sounds that behave like real-world objects — plucked strings, struck bars, resonating bodies. The synthesis simulates the physics of how those objects vibrate. Block 2 / Sources MicroFreak Karplus-Strong engine, Live Corpus
West-coast / function-generator Folded, complex, non-traditional tones that do not sound like standard East-coast synths. Uses waveshaping and complex envelopes instead of a filter. Harder to predict, more unusual in character. Lab 024b VCV Rack (software modular)
Granular Clouds of texture built by chopping a recording into tiny pieces (grains) and scattering them. Works on any source — voice, field recording, synth — and produces washy, atmospheric, sometimes alien results. Lab 015 Live Granulator II
Sample-based Any sound you can record becomes an instrument. Pitch it, chop it, layer it, loop it. Essential for concrete textures, field recordings, and anything organic mixed with electronic material. Block 2 / Sources Digitakt II, Live Sampler / Simpler
Feedback Self-oscillating, unstable, and unpredictable tones produced by routing a signal back into itself until it howls. Used in no-input mixing and some noise practices. Requires careful gain management. Block 7 / Performance No-input technique (any mixer or effect with a feedback path)
Tuning systems Not synthesis in the traditional sense, but a method for choosing which pitches your instrument plays. Moving outside standard 12-tone equal temperament opens up new harmonic colours and micro-pitch textures. Lab 026 Scala (software tuning tool)
Choosing a method for a new piece

Describe the sound you want in one sentence — its weight, its movement, and whether it feels mechanical or organic. Then scan the "good for" column. The row that matches your description is your starting method. Do not pick a method because it sounds advanced. Pick the one whose description matches what you heard in your head.

Synthesis fundamentals

Before diving into a specific method, make sure you have the basics. These two resources cover the shared vocabulary that every method uses.

Synth from ScratchThe Foundations entry point. Oscillator, filter, envelope, LFO — explained in plain English with your actual tools. Start here. Foundations block →
FM from scratch (Digitone II)A focused guide to FM synthesis using the Digitone II specifically. Covers operators, algorithms, and the ratio controls that shape FM timbres. Digitone FM guide →
CV, Gate & Voltage ControlHow synths are wired and modulated by voltage — the four signal types, 1V/oct pitch, and routing one control into another. Underlies west-coast, modular, and every analog instrument. Practise free in VCV Rack. CV / gate page →
  • You do not need to master every method. Most strong pieces use one or two synthesis approaches deeply rather than all of them shallowly. Use this index to find your primary method and your one backup method, then go deep on those two.
  • Methods combine. Granular synthesis applied to an FM output, or wavetable through a feedback loop, are both valid techniques. Once you know each method on its own, combining them is a natural next step — but only after you understand each one separately.
