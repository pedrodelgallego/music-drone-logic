Lab Note B3-004
Euclidean · 03 — Decisions

The theory, and the choices it forces

Distribute N hits across M steps as evenly as the integers allow. You choose N and M; the spacing is computed. Before you build, you need to understand what the algorithm actually does, where its patterns come from, what rotation changes, and which decisions are yours to make.

The idea in one rule

Principle

Place N hits across M steps so the gaps between them are as equal as the numbers permit. When M does not divide cleanly by N, the algorithm interleaves the long and short gaps in the smoothest possible order. You set two numbers; the rhythm falls out.

Concept 01

Bjorklund's algorithm — even distribution

Where it comes from
  • In 2004, Godfried Toussaint showed that the routine Euclid wrote for the greatest common divisor (around 300 BC) — when used to space hits across steps — produces the most even possible distribution. The same algorithm, attributed to E. Bjorklund, had already been used to time the firing of spallation-neutron-source pulses.
  • Notation: E(N, M) means N hits spread over M steps. The hits are placed so the gaps are as equal as the integers permit.
  • You do not compute it by hand in performance — you set two numbers and the generator fills the steps. The point is to think in N and M instead of in individual taps.
How the algorithm actually runs — E(3,8) by hand
  • Start by writing N "hit" buckets and (M−N) "rest" buckets as two groups. For E(3,8): three hits [x][x][x] and five rests [.][.][.][.][.].
  • Repeatedly pair each item in the larger group onto an item in the smaller group — like Euclid's subtraction — folding remainders in until you cannot pair any further. Each "hit" bucket gathers rests behind it.
  • The buckets, read left to right, become the pattern. For E(3,8) you get x . . x . . x . — three hits with gaps of 3, 3, 2. That string is the Cuban tresillo.
  • The maths guarantees you cannot make the gaps more equal than the algorithm does. That is what "maximal evenness" means, and it is why so many independent traditions land on the same strings.

  • E(4,16) is four-on-the-floor — the trivial even case where 4 divides 16 cleanly, all gaps equal.
  • E(5,16) spreads five hits with mixed gaps of 3 and 4 steps — a syncopated, restless pulse.
  • E(7,16) packs seven hits into sixteen — busy but still even, the gaps alternating 2 and 3.
Three facts worth memorising
Properties of E(N,M)
DensityN/M is the density. E(5,16) ≈ 31 % full; E(7,16) ≈ 44 %. Pick density by ear, then let the algorithm place it.
ComplementE(N,M) and E(M−N,M) are mirror images — the rests of one are the near-hits of the other. Useful for call-and-response between two voices.
Trivial casesWhen N divides M (E(4,16), E(2,8), E(3,12)) every gap is equal — you get a plain even pulse with no syncopation. Reach for non-dividing pairs when you want life.

Where these patterns come from

E(4,9)The Turkish aksak and a Greek rhythm.
E(2,5) / E(3,7)Persian and Balkan aksak "limping" meters.
  • The insight that made this famous: rhythms developed independently across continents over centuries land on the same mathematically-even distributions. The maximal-evenness rule appears to be something the ear seeks out on its own.
  • Practically, this means a Euclidean generator is not producing "computer rhythms" — it is producing the deep grammar traditional music already discovered. Trust the unfamiliar ones; they have ancestors.
  • The terms worth knowing: tresillo (the 3-in-8 cell underpinning much Latin music), clave (the two-bar timeline key of Afro-Cuban music), and aksak (Balkan/Turkish "limping" meters of unequal beats). All are Euclidean strings or rotations of them.

Rotation — the second dial

Concept 03

Spinning the pattern to a new start

What rotation does
  • Once N and M are fixed, the rotation control shifts which step the pattern starts on — like spinning a necklace of beads so a different bead sits at the top.
  • The gaps stay identical; only the phase relative to the downbeat changes. E(5,8) rotated by one step is a different-feeling groove with the same DNA — and is often exactly how one traditional clave relates to another.
  • Use rotation to decide whether your line leads with the kick or pushes against it. Rotating a hit off step 1 instantly makes a part feel syncopated without changing N or M.

Euclidean as rule-based placement

Tapping by feel

  • You place each hit by ear and habit
  • Tends toward patterns you already know
  • Hard to vary systematically

Setting a rule

  • You choose two numbers and a rotation
  • The placement is even by construction
  • Vary the whole groove by changing one integer
  • The power move: run a different M per voice. Kick on E(4,16), hat on E(7,16), perc on E(3,11). The 11-step perc is now both Euclidean and a polymeter against the 16-step grid — two rules stacked on one part, generating a long evolving groove from three pairs of numbers.

The decisions that are yours

Decision check — before you touch the rig
  • What is the length M of my master grid? 16 is safe; 12 invites triplet-feel ancestors; 15 or 11 force polymeter against a 16-step kick.
  • Which voice is the "bell" — the unchanging rule everything answers to — and which voices are allowed to evolve?
  • For each voice, what density (N/M) do I want, and have I picked a non-dividing pair so it is actually syncopated?
  • What rotation puts each line in the right relationship to the kick — leading, answering, or pushing against it?
  • Am I changing one integer over time to evolve the track, or am I tempted to add new elements? (Change the integer.)
Before you move on
  • I can read E(N,M) and know the difference between hits, steps, and rotation.
  • I can run E(3,8) by hand and recognise it as the tresillo.
  • I know what density, complement, and trivial cases mean.
  • I know which traditional rhythm my chosen pattern echoes — it has an ancestor.
  • I decided which voice is the unchanging "bell" of my track.
