Lab Note B3-004
Euclidean · 06 — The build

Build a track where the rule is the author

Now we touch the gear. Every voice in this track is an E(n,m) string and nothing else — kick, hat, perc, texture, and bass are all chosen as pairs of integers, placed, rotated, and then left alone. Movement comes from one drifting length and from slowly changing a single integer over the arrangement. Keep the setup page and the pattern palette open alongside this one.

Step 01

Set the project and decide the grid

Open the set
  • ⌘ N for a new Live Set. Tab to Arrangement view. Tempo 124, signature 4/4.
  • Right-click the ruler → Time Ruler Format → Bars. The whole point of this build is counting steps and bars, so keep the ruler in bars.
  • ⌘ , → Audio: device MOTU M4, 48 kHz, 256 samples. Enable mono input 1 (MicroFreak) and stereo input 3/4 (Digitakt II).
  • ⌘ , → Link, Tempo & MIDI: enable Track and Sync on the Digitakt II output row and the MicroFreak output row. Ableton is the only clock master.
Commit to one master grid
  • This track's master grid is M = 16 — one bar of sixteenth steps. Every dividing voice locks to it; one odd-length voice will drift against it on purpose.
  • Write the plan down before sequencing anything. The discipline of this Lab Note is that the integers are decided first, on paper, then entered:
The five voices — chosen as integers, on paper, before any sound
KickE(4,16), rotation 0 — the anchor
HatE(7,16), rotation 0 — the even offbeat bed
PercE(3,11), rotation 0 — the drifting polymeter line
TextureE(5,12), rotation 0 — a 6/8-feel bell ancestor
Bass (MicroFreak)E(5,16), rotation 2 — pushed off the downbeat
Create the tracks
  • ⌘ T for audio tracks: DT DRUMS, MF BASS.
  • ⌘ ⇧ T for one MIDI track: MF EUCLID, MIDI To → MicroFreak ch 1.
  • ⌘ ⌥ T for one return: A — ECHO (Echo device, sync 3/16, feedback 40 %, HP 250 Hz, 100 % wet).
  • ⌘ S → save as euclid_b3_004.als in a dedicated project folder.
Decision check
  • Have I written down all five integer pairs before opening a sequencer? (If not, do it now — the rule must precede the sound.)
  • Is exactly one of my five lengths odd (11), so the groove will drift without new material?
  • Does the Digitakt II start, stop, and read 124 from Ableton?
Step 02

Place the kick — the anchor E(4,16)

Why the anchor is trivial on purpose
  • E(4,16) is a dividing case: all four gaps are equal. That is exactly what an anchor needs to be — the unmoving rule every other voice is heard against.
  • On Digitakt II, load a short dark kick on T1 KICK. Set track length to 16. Grid-record trigs on steps 1, 5, 9, 13.
  • Tune the kick's fundamental to A or E so it agrees with the bass to come. Keep the decay short — the tail must be gone before the next step.

Lock it and leave it
  • No parameter locks on the kick beyond a tiny pitch drop on step 1 to mark the bar. The anchor's whole value is that it does not change.
  • Loop the kick alone for one full minute. If you are already bored, good — an anchor you notice is an anchor doing too much.
Decision check
  • Are all four gaps audibly equal — a true four-on-the-floor with no accidental swing?
  • Is the kick tuned so it will not blur with the bass below 100 Hz?
  • Is the kick boring on its own? It should be.
Step 03

Place the hat — the even bed E(7,16)

Read the string off the palette, then place it
  • E(7,16) places seven hits with gaps alternating 2 and 3 steps. From page 05: x.x.x.x.x.x.x... — hits on steps 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13.
  • On T2 HAT, length 16, load a tight closed hat. Grid-record those seven trigs by hand. High-pass the hat around 500 Hz on the Digitakt FLTR page.

Make the even line breathe
  • Parameter-lock alternating velocity: hold every other hat trig and lower its volume a few dB. The distribution stays E(7,16); the accents make it roll instead of tick.
  • Micro-time: nudge each hat a hair late (hold trig, micro-timing right by 1–2 ticks). A consistent lag is the difference between mechanical and human-played evenness.
  • Add 51–53 % swing — subtle, on the edge of perception. Euclidean placement plus a touch of swing is the core of the groove.
Decision check
  • Against the kick, does the hat sit as an even bed or does it fight the downbeats? It should weave through them, busy but never dominant.
  • Is the last gap (steps 13→1) audibly longer? That breath at the bar's end is the E(7,16) fingerprint — keep it.
Step 04

Place the perc — the drifting E(3,11)

The polymeter superpower
  • On T3 PERC, switch to per-track length and set it to 11 steps while the pattern stays 16. This is the move that makes the track evolve with no new material.
  • E(3,11) places three hits: x..x..x.... — steps 1, 4, 7 of the 11-step track. Load a rim, conga, or click.
  • Because 11 and 16 share no factors, the perc line lands on a different position in the bar every cycle and only fully realigns with the kick after 176 steps (11 bars). That slow drift is the whole arrangement engine.
Perc E(3,11)
1
4
7
Keep it sparse
  • Three hits is enough. The drift does the work; adding more hits buries the phasing under density.
  • Optional: a 1:4 trig condition on one hit so it fires only every fourth cycle — a slow heartbeat layered on top of the drift.
  • A small Echo send (via p-lock on chosen trigs only) lets the drifting hit smear into the gaps as it moves.
Decision check
  • Loop kick + hat + perc for three full minutes. Does the perc appear to move around the bar without you changing anything? That is the polymeter working.
  • Is the perc sparse enough that you can hear it relocate, or has density hidden the drift?
  • Does the realignment moment (every 11 bars) feel like an event? If you can sense it land, leave it as a natural section marker.
Step 05

Place the texture — the 12-grid bell E(5,12)

An ancestor inside the machine
  • On T4 TEXTURE, set per-track length to 12. E(5,12) is the venda / West-African bell line: x..x.x..x.x. — steps 1, 4, 6, 9, 11.
  • Load a short resampled fragment, a dusty bell, or a metallic tick. This is the voice the rep lessons pointed at — the bell that implies a 6/8 swing inside the steady cycle.
  • 12 against 16 drifts more gently than 11 against 16 (they share the factor 4 and realign every 4 bars). The two odd lengths together — 11 and 12 — never line up with each other within the track, so the composite is in constant slow motion.

Sit it under the groove
  • Low-pass the texture mostly closed on the Digitakt FLTR page, then open it very slowly with a track LFO (MOD page: LFO → Filter Frequency, slow speed, small depth).
  • Keep it quiet — this is the room the rule lives in, not a fourth rhythmic lead.
Decision check
  • With all four Digitakt voices running, can you still hear four distinct rules, or has it become a wall? If it is a wall, thin the texture or the hat — never the anchor.
  • Does muting the texture make the track feel smaller rather than missing? It should.
Step 06

Generate the bass — E(5,16) rotated by 2

Let Ableton compute this one
  • On MF EUCLID, create a 1-bar MIDI clip. Generate → Euclidean: Steps 16, Pulses 5, Rotation 2.

Decision check
  • Does the rotated bass answer the kick rather than land on top of it? Solo kick + bass to confirm they interlock, not collide.
  • Is the bass leaving the sub region (below 100 Hz) to the kick on the downbeats?
  • If the bass feels too on-the-nose, try rotation 3 or 4 in the generator and listen again — that is a one-number edit.
Step 07

Hear the stack — five rules at once

The composite signal flow
E(4,16) kick + E(7,16) hat + E(3,11) perc + E(5,12) texture + E(5,16)+2 bass → one evolving groove
Listen for the interlock
  • Loop the full stack for at least five minutes. Two of the five lengths (11 and 12) are not 16, so the composite never repeats exactly within the loop — that is the Konono / Burundi interlock from page 02, built from numbers.
  • Name what each voice is doing: anchor, bed, drift, bell, answer. If two voices are doing the same job, change one integer until they separate.
  • Do not add a sixth voice to fix boredom. If it is flat, the fix is a different rotation or a different length on a voice you already have.
Why this stack works — the maths under the feel
  • The kick at E(4,16) is the only voice that repeats identically every bar. It is the metronome the ear measures everything else against; without one stable rule, drift reads as a mistake rather than as motion.
  • The hat at E(7,16) shares the 16-grid, so it never drifts — but its uneven 2/3 gap pattern keeps it from sounding like a simple division of the kick. It is the texture that proves a same-length voice can still feel alive purely from its distribution.
  • The perc at E(3,11) and texture at E(5,12) are the moving parts. Eleven is coprime with sixteen, so the perc takes the full eleven bars to return to its starting phase; twelve shares a factor of four with sixteen, so the texture cycles back every four bars. Two different return periods means the composite of perc-plus-texture has a period of their least common multiple — long enough that a four-minute loop never quite repeats.
  • The bass at E(5,16)+2 is back on the grid but rotated, so it interlocks with the kick's gaps instead of doubling its hits. Rotation, not a new rhythm, is what makes one note feel like a melodic answer.
Decision check
  • Can I follow each rule separately while hearing them as one groove?
  • Is the low end clear — kick owns 40–80 Hz, bass owns 80–180 Hz, nothing else below 200?
  • Does the groove move on its own over five minutes, with my hands off everything?
Step 08

Evolve by changing one integer

The arrangement is a sequence of integer edits
  • This track does not add and remove elements — it changes the numbers. Map the eight minutes as a series of one-integer moves, each landing on a bar line:
Arrangement by integer — times at 124 BPM
0:00 INTROTexture E(5,12) alone, low-passed. The bell establishes the room.
1:00 KICK INAdd kick E(4,16). The anchor arrives under the drifting bell.
2:00 BASS INUnmute bass E(5,16)+2. The interlock with the kick begins.
3:00 HAT INAdd hat E(7,16). Full density; the groove is now five rules.
4:00 PERC DRIFTAdd perc E(3,11) — the polymeter drift becomes the centre of attention.
4:30 PEAK — integer movePush hat from E(7,16) to E(9,16): one number, more density, peak energy with no new instrument.
5:30 STRIP — integer moveHat back to E(7,16), then to E(5,16): the bed thins by one number at a time.
6:30 OUTRODrop kick and hat. Bass E(5,16) then perc fade; texture E(5,12) closes alone, as it opened.
How to perform an integer move
  • On Digitakt II: prepare the change on a copied pattern (A02 with hat re-placed as E(9,16)) and switch to it on a bar line, or re-place trigs live. The change must land cleanly on the downbeat.
  • On the Ableton bass: automate the Euclidean Generator's Pulses or Rotation via a Max for Live version, or duplicate the clip with new generator settings and trigger it at the section.
  • Record one long performance into DT DRUMS and MF BASS while you perform the integer moves. Performance first, surgery second.
Decision check
  • Does the PEAK arrive because a number grew, not because an instrument was added? That is the thesis of this Lab Note.
  • Is every integer move landing exactly on a bar line, or are some lurching mid-bar?
  • Could I narrate the whole arrangement as a list of number changes? If yes, the rule is genuinely the author.
Step 09

Record, mix, and verify the rule survives

Record the passes
  • Arm DT DRUMS (Ext. In 3/4, Monitor Auto). Record the full 8-minute drum performance with your integer moves.
  • Arm MF BASS (Ext. In 1, mono, Monitor Auto). Record the bass pass with the generator driving the MicroFreak.
  • Optional muted-kick pass into a DT KICK track for a clean sidechain source.
Mix to keep the rules legible
Starting balance and separation
DT Drums-10 dB. EQ HP 25–30 Hz. Saturator Soft Sine +2 dB.
MF Bass-12 dB. Compressor sidechained to the kick: 4:1, attack 1 ms, release 120 ms, ~3 dB reduction per kick.
Hat / texture EQHP the hat at 500 Hz, the texture at 250 Hz — keep the low end for kick and bass only.
Return A (Echo)-10 dB. Fed only from perc and texture p-locks, so the drift smears, not the anchor.
MasterSpectrum + Utility, peaks ≤ -3 dBFS. No limiter for this exercise.
  • The sidechain is what lets the bass and kick share the low end while interlocking — watch the gain-reduction meter pulse with the E(4,16) anchor.
Verification passes
  • Drift check: play 4:00–5:00 and confirm the perc and texture are still relocating against the kick. If they have locked, a length got reset to 16.
  • Legibility check: solo each voice for 8 bars. Each should read as one clean Euclidean string, not a smear of effects.
  • Quiet check: listen once at low volume. The interlock should still be felt; if it collapses into mush, the densest voice is too loud.
  • Mono check: flip Utility width to 0 % — nothing essential should vanish.
Export and archive
  • File → Export Audio / Video: WAV, 48 kHz, 24-bit, Normalize OFF. Filename euclid_b3_004_v1.wav.
  • File → Collect All and Save. Save the Digitakt project (with all per-track lengths) and the EUCLID BASS patch.
  • Write a 10-line notes file — timestamped observations only: 4:30 hat E(9,16) too dense, not peak feels off.
Decision check
  • Does the printed audio still drift, or did the recording flatten the polymeter?
  • Can a listener who knows nothing of Euclidean rhythm still feel the groove evolve?
  • If I had to rebuild this track from scratch, could I do it from five integer pairs and a list of integer moves? That is the deliverable.
