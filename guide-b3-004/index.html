Euclidean · 01 — Overview

Euclidean rhythm as the engine

Give a rule two numbers — how many hits, how many steps — and let it space the hits as evenly as the integers allow. That single instruction generates a startling share of the world's traditional rhythms and a deep well of placements you would never program by hand. This Lab Note treats Euclidean distribution as the engine of a track, not a novelty button.

The goal

A rule-driven original

  • Built from E(n,m) distributions before any sound design choice
  • Several voices, each its own pair of numbers, stacked into one groove
  • Fully original — the rule is the author, no reference track is copied

Fundamentals you'll practice

  • Bjorklund's algorithm: even distribution of N hits across M steps
  • Rotation as the second dial; the world-music ancestors of each string
  • Stacking E(n,m) across kick, hat, perc, and bass for long-form evolution
Core objective

Understand how choosing two integers — and a rotation — can place a groove more evenly, and more interestingly, than your fingers ever would.

Why this exercise matters

The beginner instinct

  • Tap each hit by feel, landing on patterns you already know
  • Reach for a fill the moment the loop feels static
  • Treat the unfamiliar machine-spread pattern as a mistake to fix

The opposite discipline

  • Choose N and M; let the spacing be computed, not drawn
  • Vary an entire groove by changing one integer
  • Trust the even-but-uneven pattern — it has ancestors

Lab Note B3-004 sits behind Block 3. It takes the rhythm-as-system thinking of that block and narrows it to one engine: the Euclidean distribution. The skill transfers anywhere a part needs to feel even yet alive — techno, polyrhythmic percussion, generative patches, modular sequencing.

Principle

The rhythm is the output of a rule, not a row of taps. Think in N and M.
