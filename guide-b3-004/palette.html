Euclidean · 05 — Pattern palette

A working table of E(n,m) patterns

A reference you can keep open while building. Each row gives the string, its ancestor, and the job it does best in a track. Steal a pair, place it, and only then decide whether to rotate it.

The short, dividing cases — even pulses

Trivial E(N,M): every gap equal — use for anchors, not for life
E(4,16) — x...x...x...x...Four-on-the-floor kick. The unmoving anchor everything answers.
E(2,8) — x...x...Half-bar pulse. Clap or rim on the backbeat feel.
E(8,16) — x.x.x.x.x.x.x.x.Straight eighths. A driving closed-hat bed; deliberately dull, by design.
E(3,12) — x...x...x...Even in a 12-grid — a triplet-feel pulse for swung or 6/8 sections.

The classic syncopated cells — the workhorses

Non-dividing E(N,M): even-but-uneven — this is where grooves live
E(3,8) — x..x..x.The tresillo. The single most useful cell — bass, clap, or perc. Instantly Latin/African weight.
E(5,8) — x.xx.x.xThe Cuban cinquillo / West-African bell. Busy but rolling; great for a shaker or conga line.
E(5,16) — x..x..x..x..x...Bossa-nova clave family. The default hypnotic bassline distribution — sparse, restless, never resolving.
E(7,16) — x.x.x.x.x.x.x...Samba bell. A full, even offbeat hat or ride that still leaves a breath at the end of the bar.
E(9,16) — x.xx.x.x.xx.x.x.Dense driving layer. Use sparingly — close to a wall of hits; thin it with velocity.
E(6,16) — x..x.x..x..x.x..A loping perc line; sits between tresillo sparseness and a full hat.

The 12-grid family — triplet and 6/8 ancestors

E(N,12): the West-African / Afro-Cuban bell world
E(5,12) — x..x.x..x.x.The venda / West-African bell line. A timekeeper that implies 6/8 swing inside a steady cycle.
E(7,12) — x.xx.x.xx.x.The "standard pattern" relatives — the densest common bell. A full, rolling ride for a 12-step feel.
E(4,12) — x..x..x..x..Even quarter-pulse in 12 — a calm kick or downbeat marker for triplet sections.
E(2,12) — x.....x.....A sparse half-cycle accent; good for a low tom or sub stab.

The odd-length engines — for polymeter

E(N,M) where M ≠ 16: place on a track of its own length to drift against the kick
E(3,11) — x..x..x....An 11-step perc against a 16-step kick. Phases every bar, realigns slowly — drift without new material.
E(4,9) — x.x.x.x..The Turkish aksak. A 9-step part that limps beautifully against straight 16.
E(5,13) — x..x.x..x.x..A prime-length texture that effectively never repeats its phase against 16 within a normal track.
E(2,5) — x..x.The smallest aksak cell. A tiny 5-step motif for a blip or click that wanders.
E(7,15) — x.x.x.x.x.x.x..Near-full 15-step ride that slips one step early of the 16-grid each bar.

How to choose a pair

Pick density first

  • Sparse anchor: N/M around a quarter (E(3,16), E(5,16))
  • Rolling layer: around a half (E(7,16), E(5,12))
  • Driving bed: above half (E(9,16)) — thin with velocity

Then choose length M

  • M = 16 to lock with the grid
  • M = 12 for a triplet / 6-8 ancestor
  • M = 11, 13, 15 to force polymeter drift
  • Stacking recipe to start from: kick E(4,16), hat E(7,16), perc E(3,11), bass E(5,16) rotated by 2. Four pairs of numbers; one of the four lengths is odd, so the whole groove evolves for free.
  • Complement trick: if a voice is E(5,16), a second voice on its complement E(11,16) fills exactly the gaps — instant interlock, used carefully so it does not become a wall.
Before you move on
  • I chose a sparse anchor pair and a busier answering pair.
  • At least one of my pairs has an odd M for polymeter drift.
  • I know the ancestor of each pattern I picked.
  • I have not stacked so many dense pairs that the groove is a wall.
