Resonant patch recipes

Concrete starting points for every resonant voice the build needs: the lead filter ride, the excited-resonance pluck, the self-oscillating sine, the key-tracked acid line, and the supporting parts that stay out of the filter's way. Dial these, save them, then mutate. Numbers are starting positions, not laws — but start exactly here.

Recipe 1 — The lead: excited resonance acid line (MicroFreak)

This is the voice that carries the track. A bright, short source excites a high-Q filter; the cutoff is the melody. Key-tracking keeps it in tune as you play notes; the sequencer or your hand moves the cutoff between frets.

MF LEAD — oscillator
EngineWavetable — rich partials give the peak something to grab
Wave~ 55 % — bright; the brighter the excitation, the clearer the ping
Timbre / Shape50 / 45
ParaphonicOff — strictly monophonic, like a 303
Glide8 – 15 % — the acid slide, audible only on slid steps
MF LEAD — filter (the instrument)
TypeLow-pass, state-variable analog filter
Resonance~ 75 – 85 % — rung four: peak dominates, transients ping, no constant scream
CutoffStart at a fret in your scale (see the fret table below); this is the pitch knob now
Keyboard trackingOn, full — the peak tracks the played note so the line stays in tune across the range
Filter envelope amount~ +30 — each note blooms the cutoff upward then settles: the acid "wow"
ENV — Attack / Decay / Sustain / Release0 / 35 / 15 / 25 — fast pluck, short body, silence between
  • The bloom is the sound. Envelope-to-cutoff plus high resonance is the entire acid voice. If it sounds flat, the envelope amount is too low; if it screeches, the resonance is too high or the decay too long.

Recipe 2 — The self-oscillating sine (MicroFreak, for the peak)

MF SING — the filter as a tuned sine generator
Oscillator levelLow to zero — at full self-oscillation the input is only excitation, and you may want pure sine
ResonancePast ~3 o'clock until a steady tone appears with no note held — self-oscillation engaged
CutoffNow the pitch control — tune it to a scale tone by ear against the sub
Keyboard trackingOn — so the sine plays in tune when you sequence notes
Cycling envelope → cutoffLoop mode, slow rise/fall, small depth — the sine glides between two pitches forever, theremin-like
Level disciplineThis can be the loudest thing in the mix. Pull the patch volume and meter the peak per the setup page.

Recipe 3 — The slow arc: Auto Filter on a flat source (Ableton)

ARC — resonant filter as the long-form journey
SourceA flat, partial-rich printed clip: filtered noise, a sustained drone, recorded room hum. Print it before filtering.
Auto Filter — typeLow-pass, 24 dB. Try the analog-style model for grit, Clean for a politer self-oscillation.
Resonance~ 60 – 70 % for the arc; it should add pitch without stealing the lead's job
FrequencyThe automated parameter — draw it on a scale-quantized lane over many bars
LFO → FrequencyVery slow, small depth — a sub-audible drift so the arc never sits perfectly still

The fret table — cutoff positions that land on A minor

A resonant peak's pitch equals its cutoff frequency. These are the approximate frequencies where the peak lands on tones of A natural minor across the lead's working range. Sweep slowly, confirm the peak matches the note by ear against your sub, and note the knob position — those are your frets.

Resonant peak frequency → scale tone (A natural minor)
~ 220 HzA3 — root, low fret
~ 262 HzC4 — minor third
~ 294 HzD4 — fourth
~ 330 HzE4 — fifth, the tension fret
~ 392 HzG4 — minor seventh
~ 440 HzA4 — octave root, the resolve
~ 523 HzC5 — high color, use sparingly
~ 659 HzE5 — peak-section reach, the brightest fret
Tuning the frets

These frequencies are a guide; the actual cutoff position that produces a given pitch depends on the filter model and key-tracking. The reliable method: hold the root on your sub, sweep the lead's cutoff until the resonant peak beats against it and then locks in tune, and mark that spot. Repeat for each scale tone. Now your cutoff automation has real targets, not guesses.

Recipe 4 — Digitakt resonant rhythm (per-track filter)

DT TEXTURE — a filter melody from one drum track
SampleA short noise burst or resampled MicroFreak fragment — flat, no inherent pitch
FLTR — ResonanceHigh enough that the peak has pitch (40–70 % region on the source)
FLTR Freq — p-locksHold each trig, set FLTR Freq to a different fret — a 3–4 note filter line locked to the sequence
FLTR envelope depthPer-step depth so only chosen trigs ping; others stay dull
Sound lockLock alternate cutoff values per step for a sung two-note counter-line

The supporting parts — staying out of the filter's way

MF SUB — clean low anchor
EngineWavetable or Harmo, Wave low for a near-sine
Filter resonanceLow — the sub has no business resonating; that is the lead's job
CutoffClosed; the sub is felt, not heard. Root on every kick or held under the line
RoleGives the filter ride a tonal floor to be in tune against. Sidechain it to the kick.
DT DRUMS — dry, restrained foundation
KickShort, dark, tuned to A or E; no FX sends — a dry kick is the hypnotic signature
HatTight, high-passed ~ 500 Hz; offbeat, p-locked velocity so it breathes
PercOne sparse hit with a probability condition; gives space, not a fill
Resonance disciplineKeep drum filters low-Q. Only the lead and the arc are allowed to ring.
The one-resonance rule

At most two voices in the whole track should have audible resonance: the lead and, briefly, the arc. Everything else stays low-Q. Two singing filters at once is a duet; three is a mess. Restraint is what lets the lead read as the lead.

  • Save every patch before you change it: MF LEAD, MF SING, MF SUB. The build re-uses them and you will not want to re-dial a high-Q sweet spot from memory.
  • Carry forward: note the level each resonant patch peaks at — that number is the input to saturation, sidechain, and the master. Resonance and gain-staging are one decision.
