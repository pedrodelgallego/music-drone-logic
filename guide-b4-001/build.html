Lab Note B4-001
Resonance · 06 — The build

Build a track the filter carries

An exhaustive, step-by-step build of an original track whose lead voice is a resonant filter ride. Every step is small, has a decision checkpoint, and spells out exact values. Keep the setup page open for the wiring and the palette open for the patches — this page assumes both. The sequence: foundation first, then the filter voice, then tune it, then make it a melody, then build the arc, then mix the peak, then arrange.

dry source → high-Q filter cutoff = pitch resonant tone → sequenced cutoff = the lead line
Step 01

Set the project and the bare foundation

Open the set
  • ⌘ N, Tab to Arrangement. Tempo 126, 4/4. Set the time ruler to m:ss (right-click the ruler → Time Ruler Format → Time).
  • Audio: MOTU M4 in/out, 48000 Hz, buffer 256. Enable mono input 1 (MicroFreak) and stereo 3/4 (Digitakt II).
  • Confirm clock: Ableton is the only master; Digitakt II and MicroFreak receive clock + transport over USB. Press play — Digitakt should start and read 126. Fix this before anything else.
Create the tracks
  • ⌘ T for audio tracks: MF FILTER (the lead), ARC SOURCE, DT DRUMS, MF SUB.
  • ⌘ ⌥ T for returns: A — REVERB (Hybrid Reverb, decay 3 s, HP the input at 400 Hz) and B — ECHO (Echo, 3/16 ping-pong, feedback 38 %, HP 300 Hz).
  • Save as resonance_lab_b4001.als in a dedicated project folder. Color the lead track distinctly — you will be staring at it for hours.
Why foundation before filter
  • The filter ride must be in tune against something. Build a minimal kick + sub first so you have a tonal reference to tune the resonant peak to. Without it you will tune the filter to silence and it will float.
  • Program the Digitakt II foundation: T1 KICK on steps 1, 5, 9, 13, tuned to A. T2 HAT offbeat, high-passed. One sparse T3 PERC hit with a 35 % probability condition. Keep it dry — no FX sends on the kick.
  • MicroFreak MF SUB patch (palette Recipe: supporting): near-sine, low resonance, cutoff closed, root A1 held or struck on each kick.
Decision check
  • Is the foundation deliberately boring? It should be. The filter is the star; the foundation is the stage.
  • Is the kick dry and the sub low-Q? Any resonance down here will fight the lead later.
  • Can you hold the root sub and clearly hear its pitch? That is your tuning reference for every step that follows.
Step 02

Dial the resonant lead voice

Initialise and build MF LEAD
  • Shift + press Type to init a fresh patch — never sculpt on a factory preset. Engine Wavetable, Wave 55 %, Paraphonic off.
  • Filter: low-pass. Set the ENV → cutoff amount to +30 and the amp/filter envelope to A 0 / D 35 / S 15 / R 25 — fast pluck, short body.
  • Now bring resonance up slowly while striking the root. Stop at the moment a clear ping appears on each note but before a constant scream: about 75 %. That is rung four.
Engage key-tracking
  • In the filter section enable keyboard tracking, full. Play the root, then an octave up. The resonant character should stay the same — the peak follows the note. If the high note sounds thin and the low note muffled, tracking is off; fix it now.
  • Key-tracking is what makes the next step (tuning) hold across the range. Skip it and the line drifts out of tune the moment you change notes.
Climb the ladder on purpose
Audition each rung against the held sub — do not skip to the top
40 – 70 %Confirm you can hum the peak. If you can't, the filter isn't a voice yet.
70 – 85 %The working home for the lead. Peak dominates, transients ping clearly, source still present.
Near self-oscReserve this for the peak section in Step 06. Note the knob position so you can return to it.
Decision check
  • On a single struck note, do you hear a clear "wow" bloom — envelope pushing the cutoff up then settling? That bloom is the acid voice. No bloom = raise envelope amount.
  • Does the same patch sound in tune on a low note and a high note? If not, key-tracking is wrong.
  • Save the patch as MF LEAD before touching anything else.
Step 03

Find the frets — tune the peak to the scale

Sweep and lock
  • Hold the root sub (A1) so you have a constant pitch reference. On MF LEAD, hold a note and slowly sweep the cutoff.
  • Listen for the beating between the resonant peak and the sub. As the peak approaches a scale tone the beating slows, then locks. Mark that cutoff position. That is a fret.
  • Work through the scale tones from the palette fret table: A, C, D, E, G, A-octave. Note each knob position physically — masking tape on the panel is not beneath you.
Confirm the line is singable
  • Play your intended 3–5 note line by hand, hitting only the frets you marked. Record a quick scratch pass and listen back.
  • If it doesn't sound like a melody, it isn't one yet — simplify to fewer notes, or widen the intervals so the ear can track the movement.
Decision check
  • Can you sing the line the filter is playing? If not, the resonance is too low — it is still timbre, not pitch.
  • Does the resonant peak stay in tune across the range, or drift? If it drifts, you need key-tracking, not more automation points.
  • Are all your cutoff targets on frets, or are some between notes? Between-note cutoffs are out-of-tune notes — move them.
Step 04

Sequence the filter into a melody

Program the note sequence
  • MicroFreak sequencer: 16 steps, scale A natural minor, root A2 for the lead (an octave above the sub). 3–5 notes, root on step 1, gaps left open.
  • A starting shape: A2 . . A2(ghost) . . C3 . . . G2 . . E3(slide) . . — the slide into the high tension note gives one acid gesture per bar.
  • Set Glide on the slid step only. The slide makes the cutoff portamento between frets — the most recognizable acid move.
Two ways to move the cutoff — pick the phrasing layer
  • Let key-tracking do it: because tracking is on, each sequenced note moves the resonant peak to that note's fret automatically. The sequence is the filter melody. This is the cleanest method — start here.
  • Add a hand ride on top: over the sequenced notes, also move the cutoff knob by hand for the accent gesture — small, per-bar, the "talking" of the line. This is printed movement; it cannot be undone, so keep it subtle.
  • Wire one mod route for life: Cycling Env (loop, slow) → cutoff at depth ~ 12. Two slow movements at different rates never repeat exactly — the hypnosis mechanism inside the lead itself.
Record the lead take
  • Arm MF FILTER, Monitor Auto. Drop a Limiter at -6 dBFS ceiling on the track as the peak safety net (setup page).
  • Loop and listen for at least 3 minutes before committing — the line that bores you at 30 seconds and locks you in at 3 minutes is the right one.
  • ⌘ ⇧ R, record 4 minutes. Keep the resonance and the note sequence static for this take; the long-form cutoff arc comes later as drawn automation. A static melodic core can be reshaped forever.
  • Re-save the patch as MF LEAD in case you nudged anything while performing.
Decision check
  • Is the line genuinely a melody, or just rhythmic ticking? Hum it back without the track — if you can't, it failed.
  • Does it create tension instead of resolving every bar? If it lands "finished" on the root each bar, displace a note.
  • Is the resonant peak clipping the Limiter on the loud notes? If the safety net is working hard, the take is too hot — pull the MicroFreak volume and re-record.
  • Is any step decorative? Delete it, listen two minutes — if nothing is lost, it stays deleted.
Step 05

Build the slow arc with a second resonant filter

Print a flat source
  • On ARC SOURCE, record a flat, partial-rich source: MicroFreak Noise engine, or a sustained low-resonance drone holding the root and fifth. Record 60 s, print it flat — no filtering yet. Loop the clip across the arrangement.
  • The richer the source, the more the resonant peak has to grab onto. Print it dry so the filter does the melodic work, not a pre-pitched sample.
Auto Filter as the journey
  • Drop an Auto Filter on ARC SOURCE: low-pass 24 dB, resonance ~ 65 % — enough to add pitch, not enough to compete with the lead.
  • Add a slow LFO → Frequency, small depth, so even when the automation is flat the arc drifts a few cents — never perfectly still.
  • This is the layer the whole long-form journey hangs on. The lead is the melody; the arc is the tide under it.
Scale-quantize the cutoff lane
  • A to show automation, click Auto Filter Frequency, draw breakpoints. Snap each to a fret frequency from the palette table so the arc, too, plays in tune.
  • Keep ramps long: no movement shorter than 8 bars; most are 16–48. Bend the ramps to S-curves (right-click segment → Curve) so they read as intention, not a ruler line.
Decision check
  • Does the arc sit under the lead, or does it compete? If two filters are fighting for "the melody", pull the arc's resonance and level down — one lead only.
  • Is the arc in the same key as the lead and sub? A resonant peak on a non-scale frequency is an out-of-tune drone.
Step 06

Mix the resonance — tame the peak, ride it for the climax

Carve the lead so the peak fits
Effects chain on MF FILTER — in this order
1 — EQ Eight HP90 Hz, 24 dB/oct — clear the sub's territory so the lead and sub don't blur
2 — Dynamic EQ / de-esser on the peak bandA narrow node that ducks only when the resonant peak spikes — catches the harsh overshoot without dulling the whole line
3 — SaturatorSoft Sine, drive +3 dB — the hot resonant peak drives saturation hard; this is where resonance and nonlinearity fuse (the acid grit)
4 — LimiterCeiling -6 dBFS, catching only rare peaks — the safety net, not a sound
  • The level leaving a resonant filter is not the level that entered it. The saturator sees the 12–18 dB peak, so a "gentle" +3 dB drive is plenty — push more and the line turns to fizz.
Reverb and delay without the whistle
  • Send the lead to B — ECHO at -12 dB — the dotted-eighth echoes turn single pings into a rolling phrase. This is the acid space.
  • Send to A — REVERB sparingly (-18 dB), and only because the reverb input is high-passed at 400 Hz. A high-Q peak into a long reverb tail will ring at one frequency forever; keep decay short (3 s) and the send low.
Sidechain so the filter breathes
  • Compressor on MF SUB: sidechain from the kick (Pre FX), ratio 4:1, attack 1 ms, release 120 ms, ~ -3 dB reduction per kick. The sub pumps so the kick and resonant lead both have room.
  • Tune the release by ear: the sub should swell back between kicks like breathing.
The climax — ride the resonance, add nothing
Peak-section automation (times → bars at 126 BPM)
Lead resonance @ 4:00 – 4:4575 % → 92 % over 24 bars — the peak narrows and screams; this is the only fast-ish gesture and even it spans bars
Lead cutoff @ 4:00 – 4:45Climb the frets upward toward E5 — the brightest, highest tension note
Arc resonance @ 4:30Brief bump to near self-oscillation, then back — the tide rises with the lead
Echo feedback @ 4:3038 % → 60 % → 38 % — the pings briefly self-regenerate
Saturator drive @ 4:30+3 → +6 → +3 dB — density without a new element
All values @ 5:15Back toward home — the release that makes the climb worth it
Decision check
  • At the climax, did energy come from the filter opening and ringing harder, not from new parts entering? That is the thesis of this Lab Note.
  • Does the resonant peak ever clip the master? Watch the master meter through the whole peak section — the narrow spike is sneaky.
  • Is the reverb whistling on one note? High-pass harder or shorten the decay.
  • Flip the master to mono for ten seconds — does the lead survive? A resonant peak is mono-strong, so it should.
Step 07

Arrange the ride into a journey

Mark the map first
  • Right-click the scrub area → Add Locator at each boundary: INTRO, FILTER IN, FULL, PEAK, STRIP, OUTRO.
Section → time → bar at 126 BPM
INTRO — 0:00Bar 1 — sub + dry kick only, no resonance audible
FILTER IN — 1:00Bar 33 — lead enters low and closed; the filter wakes up
FULL — 2:30Bar 80 — arc joins, cutoff climbing, the ride is established
PEAK — 4:00Bar 126 — resonance and cutoff rides from Step 06; nothing new enters
STRIP — 5:15Bar 166 — resonance pulled back, lead thinned; the climb made retroactively big
OUTRO — 6:15Bar 197 — lead closes down, sub + kick carry the exit, slow fade
Place clips against the map
  • INTRO — sub + kick. The resonant lead is silent; the listener settles before the voice arrives.
  • FILTER IN — lead clip from bar 33 with cutoff low (closed end of its range). Draw a fade-in on the first 4 bars so it surfaces rather than arrives.
  • FULL — arc source enters; the lead's cutoff arc begins its long climb. The track is now two resonant voices and a foundation, nothing more.
  • PEAK — no new clips. Energy is entirely the Step 06 resonance/cutoff rides.
  • STRIP — pull lead resonance back to ~ 55 %, drop the arc, thin the hats. The filter goes quiet so its return lands harder.
  • OUTRO — cut the lead around 7:00, sub + kick fade over the final 30–45 s via master volume.
Perform the ride live, then simplify
  • ⌘ M MIDI map: map a Launchpad control to Auto Filter Frequency, another to lead resonance. Set Min/Max to your fret range so the full control travel stays musical and never overshoots into screech.
  • F9 Arrangement Record. Play all ~7 minutes and ride the mapped controls slower than feels natural. Two takes; keep the calmer one.
  • Right-click each lane → Simplify Envelope to thin breakpoints while keeping the shape.
Decision check
  • Play 4:00–4:45 blind: does the peak arrive because the filter opened and rang, not because parts were added? That is the whole test.
  • If you can hear a cutoff move as an "event" outside the peak section, it is too fast.
  • Can you remove the arc lane without weakening the journey? If yes, remove it — the lead alone may be enough.
  • Does the strip section make the earlier fullness feel bigger in hindsight? If not, strip more.
  • Carry forward: you have proven a resonant filter can be a lead, a melody, and a climax mechanism. The level leaving that peak drove every downstream stage — saturation, sidechain, master. Resonance was never an isolated tone choice; it was the gain-staging, the arrangement, and the hook at once. Keep both the MF LEAD patch and the marked fret positions — they are the seed of the next track.
