Lab Note B4-003
Granular · 05 — Grain palette

Four recipes from one set of dials

The same four controls produce wildly different material depending on where you set them. These are four reference recipes — pad, cloud, stutter, glitch — written as concrete Granulator III settings. They are starting points, not law. Dial one in, then move a single dial and listen; the gap between two recipes is itself a usable sound.

The four dials at a glance

Where each recipe sits on the four controls
RecipeSize · Density · Spray · Position
PadLong · High · None/tiny · Frozen
CloudMedium · High · Moderate · Slow drift
StutterShort · Low · None · Stepped
GlitchVery short · Low–medium · High · Jumping

Recipe 1 — Pad

Recipe 1

A frozen instant becomes a held chord

Granulator III settings
Pad — the spine of the track
Grain Size90–120 ms — long enough that the source timbre survives intact
DensityHigh — many overlapping grains, fully continuous, no gaps
Spray0–8 ms — just enough to hide the loop point, not enough to blur pitch
PositionFrozen on the richest, most stable instant of the source
WindowSmooth bell — no grit
PitchPlay polyphonically; the decoupled engine turns one instant into a chord
  • Use this as the foundation layer. Hold a triad and the frozen point sustains as a pad indefinitely.
  • For life without movement: add a slow LFO to Spray (one cycle per 15–20 s, depth ~5 ms) so the chord shimmers without anyone hearing a change.

Recipe 2 — Cloud

Recipe 2

A slow scan through the source

Granulator III settings
Cloud — evolving texture
Grain Size50–80 ms — on the line between texture and pad
DensityHigh, but slightly lower than the pad — a little air between grains
Spray20–40 ms — a moving haze, periodicity gone
PositionSlow drift — unfrozen, or an LFO moving Position one full pass per 30–60 s
WindowSmooth bell
  • This is the layer that evolves. As Position scans, the timbre changes continuously — different instants of the source become the sound, one after another.
  • Keep the scan glacially slow. If you can hear the timbre "move" as an event, halve the LFO rate. The listener should notice it is different, never that it changed.
  • Send generously to the Grain Haze return for depth; it doubles the cloud at an offset pitch if you set Grain Delay Pitch to +12 st.

Recipe 3 — Stutter

Recipe 3

Sparse grains become rhythm

Granulator III settings
Stutter — rhythmic seasoning
Grain Size25–40 ms — short, each grain is a discrete event
DensityLow — gaps between grains, so you hear them individually
Spray0 ms — keep it tight and repeatable
PositionStepped — frozen, or moved in discrete jumps synced to the grid
  • This is where granular meets rhythm. Sync the grain rate (or an LFO on Position) to 1/16 and the cloud becomes a pulse made of source fragments.
  • Best made on the Digitakt: slice a printed cloud, then use Retrig on one slice to machine-gun it. The sequencer resolution gives you tighter, more musical stutter than dragging an LFO.
  • Use sparingly. Stutter is the accent, never the bed — one stutter event every few bars reads as intent; constant stutter reads as a broken file.

Recipe 4 — Glitch

Recipe 4

Position jumps and high spray dissolve the source

Granulator III settings
Glitch — the source coming apart
Grain Size10–20 ms — very short, the grain itself is audible as a click or chirp
DensityLow to medium — sparse enough to hear the chaos
Spray60–90 ms — heavy scatter, the source dissolves toward noise
PositionJumping — fast random or stepped position changes; the read point never settles
WindowSharper edges add the grit; soften it if it becomes painful
  • This recipe deliberately destroys pitch and intelligibility. Use it as a transition, a tear in the fabric, or a one-off event — never the spine.
  • Pairs well with a hard pitch shift (−12 or random) and a short reverse on the Digitakt slices.
  • Resample a glitch pass and you have a brand-new noisy source to granulate again — a generation whose origin is unrecognisable.

Combining recipes in one track

Stack by role

  • Pad as the bed, frozen and constant
  • Cloud above it, slowly scanning, carrying the evolution
  • Stutter and glitch as rare events that puncture the calm

All from one source

  • The same source file feeds every recipe — only the dials differ
  • Print each as its own generation so they can coexist
  • The shared DNA is what makes four layers sound like one piece
Decision check
  • Can I move from Pad to Cloud by turning one dial (Position from frozen to drifting) and hear the layer come alive? That single move is the whole arc in miniature.
  • Is my stutter coming from the grid (Digitakt retrig) or from a wobbling LFO? The grid version will sit in time; the LFO version usually won't.
  • Have I kept glitch rare? If glitch is present more than a few seconds at a time, it has become the bed — which is not what this track wants.
  • Carry forward: these four recipes are the vocabulary. Page 06 sequences them into a full track — record the source, freeze a pad, scan a cloud, slice on the Digitakt, glitch a transition, and resample generation after generation into a 6–8 minute textural piece.
