Lab Note B4-003
Granular · 02 — Listen

Hear the grain before you build with it

Granular is easy to misuse and hard to hear correctly the first time. Before you generate a single cloud, listen to three records that treat grains and slices as the material itself. Train your ear to accept the smear, the stutter, and the frozen instant as composition, not as a sound waiting to be cleaned up.

Listen for time coming apart from pitch

What to listen for

In all three pieces a sound is held still while something inside it keeps moving — or a recording is taken apart and put back on different terms. The common thread: the audio is no longer obliged to play forward at its own speed. Notice how little source material each piece actually needs.

Listen 01

Curtis Roads — Point, Line, Cloud

Why it is here
  • Roads literally wrote the book on granular synthesis (Microsound), and this is the sound of the theory made audible: individual grains as points, lines of grains, and dense clouds — the full vocabulary of page 03 demonstrated in order.
  • Listen at the openings, where you can still hear single grains as discrete events — clicks and chirps. Then hear the density rise until those same grains fuse into a continuous wash. That transition is the density control made into a piece.
  • Take away: the grain is a compositional unit at every scale. Sparse is rhythmic and pointillist; dense is texture. You choose where on that line your track sits.

Listen 02

Fennesz — Venice

Why it is here
  • Guitar fragments granulated and stretched until the instrument is barely recognisable, yet the harmony is intact and even tender. This is the texture-into-harmony move that page 06 builds toward — clouds that resolve into chords.
  • Listen for how the grain haze sits over a clear tonal centre. The processing is heavy but never erases the pitch; spray and grain size are tuned to shimmer the chord rather than dissolve it into noise.
  • Take away: granular does not have to mean abstract. A frozen, sprayed cloud can be the warmest, most melodic thing in a track. Decide whether you want the source to survive or disappear — Fennesz keeps it just barely alive.

Listen 03

Kara-Lis Coverdale — Grafts

Why it is here
  • A long-form piece grown from a small set of vocal and synth fragments, granulated, layered, and re-sequenced into something that evolves continuously over many minutes. This is the one-source, many-generations structure of this whole Lab Note.
  • Listen for how few actual source ideas there are, and how far they are stretched. Fragments reappear transposed, frozen, sliced, and reassembled — the same DNA wearing different masks.
  • Take away: a textural piece does not need a wide palette. It needs one source and the patience to keep transforming it. Coverdale's restraint — letting a single cloud breathe for a long time — is the discipline page 06 asks of you.

A listening method

Find the source

  • Ask what the original recording probably was — a voice, a string, a synth
  • Then ask how little of it you are actually hearing, stretched how far
  • Train yourself to hear seconds of source behind minutes of result

Is it frozen or scanning?

  • A frozen cloud sits on one timbre and shimmers in place
  • A scanning cloud slowly moves through the source like scrubbing tape
  • Decide which each moment is — that is the position control by ear
Before you move on
  • I heard single grains as discrete clicks, then heard them fuse into a continuous cloud as density rose.
  • I heard a granulated source where the pitch survived intact under the haze.
  • I can tell the difference between a frozen cloud and one slowly scanning through its source.
  • I picked one piece whose relationship to its source — survived or dissolved — I want my own track to behave like.
