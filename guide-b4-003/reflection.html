Lab Note B4-003
Granular · 07 — Reflection

Criteria & reflection

Most of the learning happens here, not while making the track. Granular work especially rewards reflection, because the temptation to over-process is constant and only audible in hindsight. Answer slowly. Write the answers down next to generations.txt.

How you know it worked

Success signals
  • The whole piece grew from a single short source — you can name it.
  • A frozen instant became a recognisable held chord.
  • The track evolves over minutes from movement baked into the audio, not drawn on top.
  • At least one cloud slowly scanned and transformed without any audible "event".
  • A sliced cloud became rhythm without becoming a drum beat.
  • The glitch was a brief tear, not a bed.
  • You can trace every layer back through its generations.
  • generations.txt would let you rebuild the track from scratch.
  • Position — not level — was your main sound-design control.
  • The piece sits in one mood and deepens it, rather than touring effects.
Failure signals
  • The track needed new sources added to stay interesting.
  • The clouds are noise — the source's pitch never survived.
  • Everything is frozen and static; nothing scans, so nothing evolves.
  • The glitch runs so long it became the texture.
  • The low-mids are a fog of overlapping grain build-up.
  • You can't say how many generations deep any layer is.
  • The piece sounds like a granular plugin demo touring its presets.
  • The automation is doing the work the printed grain movement should have done.

Where this usually goes wrong

The fault

  • Granulating a noisy or busy source and wondering why it never becomes harmony
  • Spraying so hard the source vanishes, then missing the pitch you destroyed
  • Resampling without logging — ending up with material you can't reproduce or explain
  • Letting grain build-up turn the whole piece to low-mid fog

The fix

  • Choose a pitched, harmonically rich source; lengthen grains until pitch returns
  • Treat spray as seasoning — add until the loop hides, then stop
  • Log every generation the instant you print it; the file is irreversible
  • High-pass each layer and carve the shared 250–400 Hz band before adding more

Reflection questions

  1. Which generation surprised you most — where did the source stop sounding like itself?
  2. Did the frozen pad genuinely become harmony, or did it stay texture?
  3. Which dial taught you the most: size, position, density, or spray?
  4. How many generations deep did your deepest layer go, and was it worth it?
  5. Did slicing add something granular synthesis alone could not?
  6. Was the glitch transition a tear or a crutch?
  7. Did the piece evolve from baked-in grain movement, or did you rescue it with automation?
  8. Could you rebuild the track from generations.txt alone? If not, what's missing?
  9. Did you want the source to survive or dissolve — and did the result match that intent?
  10. What would you change in the next session?
Carry forward

Granular made change cumulative across generations — each resample baked the last transformation into the file, exactly like bouncing to tape. The next idea is to make that history itself the subject: degradation you can hear accumulating, pass after pass. The grain taught you to decouple time from pitch; the tape and degradation work teaches you to let the medium leave its own marks.

Final principle

Granular synthesis is not an effect you add — it is a way of seeing every recording as raw material with no fixed timeline. One well-chosen second, frozen, scanned, sliced, and resampled, is a deeper well than a folder of samples. Do less, but transform it further.
